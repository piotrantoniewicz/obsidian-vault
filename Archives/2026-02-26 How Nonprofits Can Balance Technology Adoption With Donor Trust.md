Business professional holding a smartphone and extending an open hand beneath glowing digital icons representing analytics, global connectivity, marketing, financial growth and community engagement, illustrating how nonprofits can adopt technology tools while maintaining donor trust, transparency and mission integrity.

Technology is giving nonprofits new tools and strategies to fundraise, communicate and deliver services. However, while digital tools can amplify reach, streamline operations and surface powerful insights, in mission-driven organizations, trust is the true currency.

Donors expect transparency, data security and authenticity, and digital enhancements shouldn’t come at the cost of human connection or organizational values. Below, members of [Forbes Nonprofit Council](https://councils.forbes.com/forbesnonprofitcouncil) share lessons they’ve learned about aligning digital adoption with mission integrity and donor trust.

## 1\. Protect Donor Data Like You Protect The Mission

Donors want to know their info will be kept private and secure. Nonprofits absolutely need modern software to stretch resources and amplify impact, but giving shouldn’t mean donors’ details can end up in miscellaneous apps or AI tools. Transparency, privacy and security are table stakes in the age of AI, and the organizations that win trust can clearly explain how donor data is protected and used. - [Logan Brown](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f8a30041-542e-4b61-8e2d-f6ede48d4e5d), [The Third Place for Kids](https://www.thethirdplaceforkids.org/)

## 2\. Use Technology To Support Care, Not Replace Connection

Our mission in healthcare must always center on patients. Technology can enhance care, improve outcomes and expand access, but it should never replace human connection. Digital tools should support person-centered care, not overshadow it. When technology reinforces trust and compassion, it strengthens both mission and impact. - [Christine Schuster](https://councils.forbes.com/u/76b1ea92-7a9e-41bb-8e79-aca1fb478fe3), [Emerson Health](https://emersonhealth.org/)

## 3\. Own Digital Missteps Fast, Clearly And Publicly

In the fast-evolving landscape of communications and tech, trust is our most critical currency. When a glitch occurs or a misstep happens, the instinct is often to pause and strategize, but silence in the digital age is quickly interpreted as indifference. Our philosophy is simple: Own it immediately and transparently. We are humans building and managing these systems, and errors are inevitable. - [Erin Mote](https://councils.forbes.com/u/abaafc15-f517-41b5-a0e6-d558c52dbb73), [InnovateEDU](https://www.innovateedunyc.org/)

## 4\. Choose Tools That Directly Serve The Mission

As the founder of a nonprofit, I’ve learned that technology must always serve the mission, not replace it. I focus on tools that expand access, strengthen outcomes for students, and clearly demonstrate impact. Transparency about how we use technology has been key to maintaining donor trust as we work to grow our nonprofit. - [Celeste Warren](https://councils.forbes.com/u/d42a958d-1b52-4607-bdd4-d22ffc4afcb3), [Destination STEM, Inc.](https://destinationstem.org/)

## 5\. Use Technology To Amplify People, Not Replace Them

The resource constraints nonprofits face make technology essential for them to deliver on their mission and continue to scale. Technology cannot replace human connection because philanthropy and giving are built on it. Technology must be focused on amplifying human potential, not replacing it. - [Scott Brighton](https://councils.forbes.com/u/db261305-586a-4cb5-9e41-6d58dbb0c17c), [Bonterra](https://www.bonterratech.com/)

## 6\. Anchor Innovation In Values And Accountability

One key lesson is that technology must serve the mission rather than reshape it. Digital tools are most effective when they strengthen transparency, accountability and meaningful engagement while preserving human judgment and institutional values. This balance builds donor trust by showing that innovation deepens impact without distancing the organization from its purpose. - [Ekramy El Zaghat](https://councils.forbes.com/u/a92c15af-4664-4a09-81c9-2359815efca5), [World Fund for Development and Planning](https://wfdp-igo.org/)

## 7\. Stay Mission-First When Evaluating Digital Investments

Maintaining the balance of acting from a mission-driven perspective while being efficient and innovative is a constant battle. Staying mission-first is critical to incorporating digital tools and maintaining donor trust. - [Kathryn Schubert](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f8171d1c-03cd-4f5f-8435-48a72312670c), [Society for Women’s Health Research](https://www.swhr.org/)

## 8\. Design For Privacy And Anonymity From Day One

Thoughtful use of technology creates a safe entry point for connection. In our work, stigma remains one of the greatest barriers to seeking help, so we design our tools to be anonymous and privacy-first. Even simple, low-tech solutions can be highly effective when trust is built into the design. When anonymity is protected, people are far more willing to seek information and support. - [Justin Phillips](https://councils.forbes.com/u/787a6968-b04b-4729-87c3-8139743a0f9f), [Overdose Lifeline](https://www.overdoselifeline.org/)

## 9\. Don’t Let AI Replace The Organization’s Voice

Don’t allow artificial intelligence to become an “artificial” voice for the core values, mission and efforts of your nonprofit. The relationships you have built and will build center around the authentic and genuine voice of your nonprofit’s mission. Technology can assist our efforts, but don’t allow it to replace your unique voice, tone and message. - [Aaron Alejandro](https://councils.forbes.com/u/3c585a1a-0084-4ae5-8737-f9c0d9b7818f), [Texas FFA Foundation](http://www.mytexasffa.org/)

## 10\. Match New Platforms With Stewardship And Governance

Adopting digital tools too quickly can dilute confidence if stewardship doesn’t keep pace. Every new platform forces decisions about accuracy, lifecycle management and transparency. Trust holds when technology advances alongside clear rules for quality, ownership and long-term responsibility. - [Alan Thomas](https://councils.forbes.com/u/aa425549-ad12-497f-89a8-6e09f160e629), [Association for Materials Protection & Performance](http://ampp.org/)

## 11\. Ensure Tech Decisions Are Mission-Led, Not Growth-Led

One lesson I’ve learned is that technology must remain mission-led, not growth-led. Digital tools earn donor trust when they enhance transparency, protect data and deepen human connection. When tech decisions are clearly anchored in values and impact, not efficiency alone, authenticity is preserved and trust grows. - [Yujia Zhu](https://councils.forbes.com/u/fe239d67-7d78-40af-9466-6509cc112a0d), [FASSLING.AI](https://fassling.ai/)

## 12\. Define And Prove The ROM

Mission authenticity in the AI era requires more than adding tech to old processes; it demands a shift in how we deliver impact. Trust is built by defining the return on mission (ROM)—for example, proving that manual data analysis during a crisis costs lives, whereas AI can cut that time to minutes. Mission thrives from adopting tools that unlock scale so people can focus on the most critical tasks. - [Jacek Siadkowski](https://councils.forbes.com/u/894fd369-b576-4f32-9c0d-6d31e211a9b8), [Tech To The Rescue](https://techtotherescue.org/)

## 13\. Adopt Tech That Scales Output Without Changing Character

Technology for technology’s sake is a waste of resources. If it makes us more removed from our human mission or causes us to appear more generic in our field, then it is actively harmful to what we’re trying to accomplish. Good technology should expand our output without changing our character. It should be as invisible as possible. - [Anne Marie Dougherty](https://councils.forbes.com/u/db9190fa-861c-4f3d-baff-f96e6610d209), [Bob Woodruff Foundation](https://bobwoodrufffoundation.org/)

## 14\. Use AI To Scale The Message, Then Show Up As Humans

Technology works best when it frees people to focus on relationships, not when it replaces them. AI can help clarify, scale and extend your message, but it should never flatten your voice or rush your thinking. When everything starts to sound the same, donor trust erodes. Use technology to amplify your mission and reach new audiences, then slow down and show up as humans where trust is built. - [Karen Cochran](https://councils.forbes.com/u/ef2a7a6a-3a79-476a-b428-5274b0822dbf), [Philanthropy Innovators](https://www.philanthropyinnovators.com/)

## 15\. Let Strategy And Story Lead, Not The Tools

Our messages still need to be simple, consistent and actionable. Tech will hopefully help many nonprofits, especially those with small teams, increase the efficiency and reach of their communications. But the core strategy behind the message, our narrative arc, is something that should be driven by our team. Consistency is critical when audiences engage on multiple platforms. - [Matthew Gayer](https://councils.forbes.com/u/63c2351d-6601-4eff-baf8-d9b5bbd450a3), [Spur Local](https://www.spurlocal.org/)

## 16\. Meet Supporters Where They Are

We need to meet our supporters where they are. For many, that means engaging on a digital stage, but using technology to collaborate, not just push information out. For older supporters and donors, we still need to keep traditional channels open so others feel appreciated and prioritized. It is about what is best for supporters, not the organization. - [Patrick Riccards](https://councils.forbes.com/u/898b58dc-cef6-43bd-a82b-67a754c84b57), [Driving Force Institute](https://drivingforceinstitute.org/)

## 17\. Use Digital Tools To Build Recurring Giving

Digital tools can be effective in connecting with donors, helping to build a strong recurring gift program. Recurring donors have a retention rate year over year of 87.3% compared with 19.2% for one-time donors, according to the [Fundraising Effectiveness Project’s Quarterly Fundraising Report, Q3 2025](https://publications.fepreports.org/). Nonprofits should embrace digital platforms as key tools to acquire and cultivate donor relationships for sustainable revenue. - [Arvetta Jideonwo](https://councils.forbes.com/u/2ee80d1b-ac5b-48f6-a03e-388551f6c7c6), [Wheeler Mission](https://wheelermission.org/)

## 18\. Do The Human Work First, Then Choose The Tools

Technology does not fix your mission. It just makes it louder. If you are clear, it amplifies clarity. If you are kind, it scales kindness. If you are messy, it helps everyone see that, too. My rule is simple: Do the human work first. Then pick tools that make it easier to listen, follow up and say “thank you” on time. Better tech, same heart, fewer dropped balls. Keep a real person on replies. - [Michael Bellavia](https://councils.forbes.com/u/0ac4f8a0-0fd4-4547-b39b-f10f5d48ad4d), [HelpGood](http://www.helpgood.com/)

## 19\. Use Tech To Reflect The Mission’s Heart, Not Redefine It

Technology can act as both a microphone and a mirror for your mission. When used without balancing human connection, it can distance organizations from their purpose and people. True impact happens when technology amplifies—not replaces—authentic relationships and reflects the heart, values and humanity behind the mission. - [Jamee Rodgers](https://councils.forbes.com/u/99e80e99-c9ac-44c6-9fe0-49b6685455bd), [Community Foundation of Rappahannock River Region](https://www.cfrrr.org/)

## 20\. Pair Smart Systems With Empowered People

Coupling people with technology is the winning recipe for efficient and effective outreach. Complete a tech audit to be sure you have the right systems, producing the results your team requires for integrated, seamless production. The technology should support the staff members who are ultimately creating connections with your constituents. - [Victoria Burkhart](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f6d331b9-6f5a-4314-b8b6-e4ccc169316c), [The More Than Giving Company](https://www.morethangiving.co/)

## How HR Leaders Can Elevate Employee Voices, Beyond The Survey

Traditional employee engagement surveys offer useful snapshots, but they often miss the nuance and real-time insight needed to shape meaningful change. As workplaces evolve, HR teams and department leaders must find new, more dynamic ways to ensure unheard voices become central to decision-making. That means building intentional listening systems that foster trust, psychological safety and ongoing dialogue—not just periodic feedback.

Below, [Forbes Human Resource Council](http://councils.forbes.com/forbeshumanresourcescouncil) members share innovative strategies for moving beyond surveys and creating channels where employees feel seen, heard and empowered to influence the future of work.

## 1\. Identifying Trusted Influencers And Expanding Leadership Access

I use two effective strategies. The first is identifying influential employees in each department, regardless of hierarchy—these highly trusted peers provide candid insights that strengthen decisions and drive adoption. The second is creating effective access to leaders, formally through forums like “Breakfast with the CEO” and informally through an authentic, open-door policy by line managers to ensure everyone is heard. - [Helal Hamdanieh](https://councils.forbes.com/u/dacf25eb-f5ef-48e9-b8b3-8818423c9020), [PwC](http://www.pwc.com/m1/en.html)

## 2\. Embedding Diverse Employee Voices In Leadership Forums

Invite new and diverse voices into the room by design, and not by exception. Create employee representation seats on leadership forums—rotating, frontline roles with real agenda-setting power—so decisions are shaped where the work actually happens. Pair this with small ongoing listening circles and shadowing, not one-off feedback exercises, to surface lived experiences in real time. - [Anthony Cheong](https://councils.forbes.com/u/94c9b47d-20cc-4dad-986e-df574043eab3), [Boston Consulting Group](https://www.bcg.com/)

## 3\. Establishing A Diversity Representation Board

A new way of listening to employees could be through establishing a diversity representation board. Allow them to be a source of information for management and a source of support for employees like them. This board can convey and make recommendations to management in a collective way to ensure all employees are heard—and feel a sense of belonging they deserve. - [Tiersa Smith-Hall](https://councils.forbes.com/u/eec636fc-af6b-4709-b662-d05eba99d25c), [Impactful Imprints, Training & Consulting](https://tiersahall.com/)

## 4\. Combining Shared-Experience Groups With Real-Time Feedback Tools

While surveys are a good tool, there is always a challenge of adoption and participation. Listening must be proactive and reactive. We've seen great insights from groups built around shared experiences, not labels. Pair anonymous digital channels and real-time feedback with programs like "mood-meters" and "one-question-a-day"—less noisy ways to truly hear employees. When employees see impact, trust grows. - [Smiti Bhatt Deorah](https://councils.forbes.com/u/06de5f14-201b-4220-b3d1-3bf1429cf547), [AdvantageClub.ai](https://www.advantageclub.ai/)

## 5\. Create Rotating Employee Advisory Groups

Move beyond surveys by creating rotating employee advisory groups that meet directly with leaders to unpack challenges and test decisions before they’re finalized. When people have the chance to talk openly in real time, unheard voices become validated and strengthened collectively. Act on what you hear, and employees will see their input shaping real outcomes. - [Kathy George](https://councils.forbes.com/u/391e2ce7-d025-429d-bf86-24c342107183), [Spherion Staffing and Recruiting](https://www.spherion.com/)

## 6\. Including Quietly Insightful Contributors In Ongoing Assessments

As part of an ongoing brand health assessment, regular one-on-one or small-group check-ins help ensure brand promises are being lived out inside and outside the company. As they perform these assessments, HR and marketing can work with department leaders to identify and include “quietly insightful” contributors to get the fullest picture. - [Jill Shedek](https://councils.forbes.com/u/370c72ba-cbbe-458f-9138-d48bf39d7eb8), [Bank Iowa](https://www.bankiowa.bank/)

## 7\. Leveraging Peer Recognition As A Listening Signal

Employee recognition is an invaluable form of peer-validated feedback. It's impossible for managers to see everything their people do to make a difference in the lives of customers and colleagues. Employee recognition that is personal, specific and impact-oriented sends a signal to employees and to the entire organization about what's worth repeating and the gap between good and great. - [David Bator](https://councils.forbes.com/u/7c9b7e4f-63df-435f-9d3f-ea148e4a8e85), [Achievers](https://www.achievers.com/)

## 8\. Using Energy Checks To Surface Hidden Friction

Listening must move beyond periodic surveys to everyday signals. One powerful approach is “energy checks”—leaders routinely asking teams what is energizing or draining them in their work. Over time, these patterns reveal hidden friction, workload imbalances and leadership blind spots. Energy often signals organizational health earliest, turning employee voice into insight for better decisions. - [Bernie Yong](https://councils.forbes.com/u/079f5c62-898c-4ec5-910a-ef76a0d196ad), [Averis](https://www.averis.com/)

## 9\. Implementing Reverse Mentoring And Employee Councils

Create "reverse mentoring" programs where junior and underrepresented employees advise senior leaders. Establish rotating employee councils with decision-making authority on key issues. Host listening sessions in small groups where leaders only ask questions, not defend. Use anonymous idea platforms with transparent follow-up. Make skip-level conversations routine, not rare. - [Jonathan Westover](https://councils.forbes.com/u/00b3630f-0c6f-4f98-9115-89b259df82fa), [Human Capital Innovations](https://www.innovativehumancapital.com/)

## 10\. Replacing Surveys With Structured Employee Panels

Replace surveys with structured employee panels. Invite frontline team members to respond to pre-shared questions from senior leaders in a facilitated forum. Preparation builds confidence; structure builds trust. When listening is intentional, voices move from anecdotal to influential. - [Nicole Brown](https://councils.forbes.com/u/5d438538-a6f8-4154-a496-b8d15683e966), Ask Nikki HR

## 11\. Formalizing Informal Listening Loops And Theme Tracking

There are multiple ways to listen to employee voices beyond surveys, such as one-on-one check-ins, team chats, focus group summaries and informal listening loops that can provide valuable insights. It’s not always about finding new ways to listen, but using the channels you have now to identify and document themes, share insights across teams and ultimately as an input into decision-making. - [Jennifer Rozon](https://councils.forbes.com/u/7709e78c-8702-4af5-87fb-b1a336615c63), [McLean & Company](https://hr.mcleanco.com/)

## 12\. Embedding Employees Directly In Decision-Making Processes

The real shift is moving from collecting feedback to embedding employees in decision-making. Rotating advisory groups tied to real business issues, leader-led listening sessions and visible follow-through signal that voice equals influence—not just input. - [Michelle Mahaffey](https://councils.forbes.com/u/1aa11680-5d5b-4dee-a816-35b28799fa53), [Community Health Network](https://www.ecommunity.com/)

## 13\. Embedding Micro-Forums Into Daily Work

Create real-time listening by embedding "micro‑forums" into daily work: small, facilitated spaces where employees speak directly to decision‑makers without hierarchy. Paired with skip‑level dialogues and anonymized voice channels, these touchpoints surface unheard perspectives long before surveys ever do. - [Britton Bloch](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f2bfe2b3-ce78-421a-b8e5-3824b98b5f2b), [Navy Federal](https://www.navyfederal.org/)

## 14\. Prioritizing Inverted Feedback Loops And Cross-Functional Circles

To modernize employee listening, HR teams should transition from static surveys to inverted feedback loops that prioritize the insights of those closest to the daily work. By replacing top-down town halls with small, cross-functional listening circles, leaders engage in active dialogue that uncovers specific friction points and innovations often hidden from high-level data for better decisions. - [Sherry Martin](https://councils.forbes.com/u/4dd55d86-5a5d-4620-8be6-3d2525d6de2f)

## 15\. Integrating One-To-One Dialogue And Inclusive Data Practices

One-to-one or monthly meetings challenge leadership resistance to establishing effective listening systems that integrate employee voice into decision-making. Transitioning from surveys to shared platforms elevates previously unheard voices to a key role in organizational strategy and culture. Employing demographic HR data to ensure the inclusion of historically underrepresented groups is essential. - [Dr. Nara Ringrose](https://councils.forbes.com/u/c5f062c2-2a5e-41e3-b63b-3778edc55c2e), [Cyclife UK Limited](https://cyclifeaquila.com/)

## 16\. Building Continuous Dialogue Through Reverse Town Halls

Move from periodic surveys to continuous dialogue. Build structured listening forums, small group roundtables and reverse town halls where leaders listen more than they speak. Create safe, facilitated spaces where frontline and underrepresented voices shape real decisions. When feedback loops are visible and acted on, listening becomes power, not performance. - [Nicole Cable](https://councils.forbes.com/u/c09f0242-8949-4ef9-beac-3fb59a185ed6), [Blue Zones Health](http://bluezoneshealth.com/)

## 17\. Capturing Real-Time Feedback Through The Work Itself

An impactful new way to listen is through the work itself, not by launching another questionnaire. HR teams can create simple moments where employees react to real decisions, workflows or changes as they are happening. When feedback is grounded in lived experiences, quieter voices naturally emerge. Listening becomes impactful once people see what they say reflected in the decisions that follow. - [Dr. Timothy J. Giardino](https://councils.forbes.com/u/e9979047-2958-4614-8ced-8f6b519e59d4), [myWorkforceAgents.ai](https://myworkforceagents.ai/)

## 18\. Establishing A 'Shadow Board' For Inclusive Governance

Replace surveys with a "Shadow Board": a diverse group of non-executives who review the same strategic data as the C-suite. Why? It injects fresh perspectives into high-stakes decisions and identifies blind spots leaders miss. By giving them the "Board Deck" to review and provide formal inputs, you move from passive sentiment to active, inclusive governance that improves decision quality. - [Sonia Vora](https://councils.forbes.com/u/b80eaf14-bb8a-4ce6-bd81-1795e032d11b)

## 19\. Building Organization-Wide Mentoring As A Listening Strategy

Build mentoring as a formal, organization-wide listening system and strategy. When leaders engage regularly across roles, geographies and hierarchy—including reverse mentorship—they hear unfiltered perspectives that surveys miss. Those direct relationships surface overlooked ideas and issues and bring previously unheard voices into real decision-making. - [David Satterwhite](https://councils.forbes.com/u/63adadc6-ee34-40cd-8eec-9e26b43272f3)

## 20\. Launching Monthly 'Listening Labs' With Visible Follow-Through

Build monthly “Listening Labs,” rotating small-group sessions run by trained peer facilitators, with leaders only as observers. Capture themes in a public backlog, vote on priorities in real time and assign an executive sponsor and a deadline for each action. Close the loop within 30 days and report back. Empowering people to come together, share and problem-solve is the most powerful and impactful aspect. - [Sheena Minhas](https://councils.forbes.com/u/bedade7d-28a1-4985-9bfa-0a21023817bc), [ST Microelectronics](https://www.st.com/)
