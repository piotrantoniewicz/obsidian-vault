[Mamdani v. Cuomo: A Critical Feminist Political Moment](https://substack.com/home/post/p-177802044)

Mamdani's economic platform- in stark contrast to Cuomo- isn’t just progressive, it’s a roadmap for feminist power-building and a fairer New York.

---

When we talk about feminist power, we don’t mean token representation or new slogans. True power means structural change—control over resources, care work, decision-making, and how our bodies and lives are governed. That’s exactly what the New York City Mayoral campaign is putting front and center.

The contrast couldn’t be starker. On one side stands Mamdani, a 34-year-old Democratic socialist whose campaign centers care work, housing stability, and economic redistribution. On the other is Andrew Cuomo, attempting a political comeback after resigning as governor in 2021 because a state investigation found he sexually harassed eleven women and created a hostile work environment that “corroded the very fabric and character of our state government.”

The Department of Justice later confirmed that Cuomo subjected at least thirteen women to a “sexually hostile work environment” and that his senior staff retaliated against four women who spoke out.

The other part of this story, too often left out of the feminist narrative, is Andrew Cuomo’s record as governor of New York. While it includes some high-profile progressive wins (paid family leave, a $15 minimum wage), when you look beneath the headlines, several of his economic and fiscal policies **actively harmed poor and working-class women**, especially Black, Latina, immigrant, and caregiving women. Examples are Medicaid cuts, underfunding education and child care, tax policies that favored the wealthy. His housing policies left women — particularly single mothers and domestic-violence survivors — disproportionately represented among those priced out or made homeless.

For voters who believe feminist power means more than words, this election poses a fundamental question: Can New York reject a candidate whose treatment of women violated federal law while he pursued economic policies that favored the ultra-wealthy, and instead choose a vision that places care, dignity, and economic justice at the center of governance led by a man who who values equity and justice for everyone?

Mamdani isn’t a singular hero or idol without flaws, but he is challenging more than just the mayor’s office, and in success, he will fundamentally transform the lives of women. He’s challenging who gets to hold power and who the economy serves. His platform—rent freezes, free buses, universal childcare, city-owned grocery stores funded by taxing the ultra-wealthy—makes a statement: the issues that most affect women (especially poor women, women of color, immigrant women) are citywide priorities, not niche side issues.

By centering affordability, care, and redistribution, the campaign shows that feminist politics is not just about identity—it’s about economics. A win for Mamdani means a win for the kind of feminist agenda that means more than words. **It means policy. It means power.**

Feminist movements have long fought for recognition of unpaid care, equal pay, access to healthcare, and autonomy. But too often those fights stop at language and culture. Mamdani’s campaign is a model for moving beyond symbolism to **material transformation**.

In a city where housing costs are crushing families, transit is strained, and wages haven’t kept up, his proposals aim to shift power toward ordinary people. That matters because power in the economy *is* power in democracy. When women—especially those at the bottom rungs—don’t have control over their time, pay, health, or housing, their ability to shape civic life and policy is limited.

Mamdani’s message offers a blueprint: policy that treats economic justice as gender justice, racial justice, and community justice all at once. His campaign proves that when you center care and redistribute wealth, you’re not dividing voters—you’re building a coalition.

If Mamdani wins, it won’t just be a victory for one candidate—it will be a signal that New York is ready for leadership that serves *everyone*, not just the well-connected or the wealthy.

For renters, transit riders, working parents, immigrants, women of color, transit workers, small business owners—his win means someone who understands daily life and plans to act on it. For city governance, it means a shift away from the elite playbook toward a government that sees care, affordability, housing, and living wages as central, not optional.

It means New York could become a model for how cities build shared power—across gender, race, class, and identity—not by default, but by design. And when a major city does that, it sends a message far beyond its borders: democracy can be shared, economic power can be redistributed, and feminist victories are not just about rights—they’re about **power.**

*This isn’t just theoretical. Here are five ways Mamdani’s agenda builds feminist power in practice:*

1. **Universal Childcare and Paid Care Work**  
	City-funded childcare and higher wages for care workers tackle one of the deepest gender inequities: unpaid and undervalued labor. It recognizes care as essential infrastructure and expands freedom for working families.
2. **Housing as a Human Right**  
	Expanding social housing, freezing rents, and curbing evictions turn housing from a speculative asset into a public good. Stable homes mean safety, autonomy, and political participation—especially for women and families.
3. **Public Transit for the Public Good**  
	Free city buses and expanded service are feminist policies because mobility equals opportunity. Women, immigrants, and low-income New Yorkers rely on transit; affordable movement is freedom.
4. **Taxing Extreme Wealth to Fund Care and Housing**  
	Feminist economics asks who pays and who benefits. Taxing the ultra-wealthy to fund childcare, housing, and infrastructure challenges extractive systems and redistributes burdens away from women and the poor.
5. **Democratizing Power: Participatory Budgeting and Worker Ownership**  
	Expanding participatory budgeting and supporting cooperatives invite ordinary New Yorkers—especially women and immigrants—into decision-making. It’s lived democracy, not symbolic representation.

> **Why it matters:** Each of these proposals connects moral clarity to material change. They link economic survival to democratic renewal. They prove that *care, fairness, and redistribution aren’t side issues—they’re how we build a city that works for everyone.*

Mamdani’s campaign is what it looks like when feminist theory becomes public policy. It’s what happens when we stop treating care as invisible and start treating it as infrastructure. When we stop rewarding cultural fluency and start rewarding courage.

If New York chooses this path, it won’t just elect a mayor—it can drive a movement. A movement that redefines what feminist power looks like in the twenty-first century: collective, structural, and fearless.
