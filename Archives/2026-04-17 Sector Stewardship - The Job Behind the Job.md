### TEAMS! Newsletter | Stewardship Edition | April 17, 2026

I spent three hours last month at a policy meeting that wouldn't change a single line of my organization’s budget. Twenty of us were in that room. CEOs of organizations that, on paper, we occasionally compete with for the same donors and grants. And somewhere in the middle of it, I caught myself running the old calculation of what was waiting for me back at the office: *board reports, gala planning, staff vacancy, what are you doing here?*

If we’re honest, I think most of us run some version of that calculation. I’ve been guilty of it. But the more I thought about that narrative, the more I realized it's worth reexamining. The job description we inherited as CEOs or Executive Directors almost exclusively rewards us for one thing: stewarding our own organization. Managing donors and fundraising. Keeping our programs running. Growing our impact. These are all important responsibilities, and I'm not suggesting we abandon any of them. But now that I’ve been in this role for 10 years, it’s become clearer that the biggest risks to our mission don't originate within our organization. They originate in the sector around us, including the policy environment, workforce pipeline challenges, shared infrastructure challenges, and the public's confidence in nonprofits as a whole. Here’s where I’ve landed on this: Stewardship that stops at our organization’s front door doesn't actually protect our mission. It just makes us feel that way. We have to be active, engaged stewards of the *communities* we live in. Our collective work improves outcomes for all of us.

### Executive Summary

The traditional definition of nonprofit stewardship, which typically focuses on internal health and organizational growth, is insufficient in today’s environment. Leadership now requires Sector Stewardship: the intentional investment in the health of the *entire ecosystem*. By expanding our focus to policy, peer support, shared infrastructure, and talent pipelines, we support the long-term health and survival of our missions and strengthen the broader social services ecosystem.

### The TEAMS! Takeaways:

- ***The "A-Grade" Illusion:*** You can earn a perfect internal evaluation while the sector crumbles around you. Expand your horizons to see the interconnectedness of the entire system.
- ***Policy as Infrastructure:*** Single-organization advocacy is a whisper; coalition advocacy is a roar that can change the system for everyone.
- ***The Fragility Factor:*** When your peer fails, your waitlist grows, yet your collective credibility shrinks.
- ***Shared Goods:*** Infrastructure such as finance, HR, and data systems can often be too heavy for many organizations to shoulder alone. We need to foster more collaboration in this area.
- ***Leadership as a Legacy:*** Your "bench strength" should be measured by the leaders you prepare for the entire sector, not just your executive team.

### Redefining the Boundaries of Stewardship

***The Limitation of the Internal "A":***Most CEO or Executive Director job descriptions and board evaluations are designed around a "Fortress Mentality". We are rewarded for programmatic integrity and mission fidelity. These are non-negotiables, but they're incomplete. If we excel only at internal management while ignoring the collapsing workforce pipeline or the shifting policy environment, we may leave the entire sector weaker than we found it, ultimately harming our own organization's health. We must move beyond "my organization" to consider "our sector".

***Policy Stewardship: Showing Up for Others:*** Policy stewardship means showing up in rooms where decisions about your community are made, even when your organization isn't the direct subject of a bill or policy proposal. Whether it is H.R. 1, Medi-Cal pressures, or a Vehicle License Fee funding shortfall in our own county, the policy environment is a shared responsibility. When we provide testimony for a peer organization or join a coalition, we are stewarding the legal and financial ecosystem that enables all of us to serve our missions.

***Peer Stewardship: Refusing the Zero-Sum Game:***In a world of limited donor or grant funding, the temptation is to compete at all costs. Peer stewardship, for example, through the Thrive Alliance lens here in San Mateo County, calls for sharing what works and mentoring emerging leaders. It’s about the quiet confidence that when a strong operator wins a contract, the entire sector wins because clients are better served. Collaboration is a strategic investment that helps prevent sector-wide failure.

***Infrastructure Stewardship: Building Shared Goods*****:** No single nonprofit can build robust, proprietary systems and infrastructure from scratch without depleting vital resources. Infrastructure stewardship means investing your organization’s learning into shared “goods.” This includes contributing your knowledge and experience to other organizations. When we share our "blueprints," we lower the cost of entry and operation for every peer organization. As the saying goes, *“Sharing is caring”*.

***Successor Stewardship: Developing the Sector’s Bench:*** We often view staff turnover to peer organizations as a failure, but *successor stewardship* reframes it as a contribution. When you develop a high-potential leader who later leaves to lead another organization, you haven't "lost" talent; you have exported excellence to the sector. Stewardship means building the leadership capacity the entire community needs, even if you don't get to keep it all.

### The Tension

Sector stewardship costs something. It costs time, attention, and sometimes even a competitive advantage. Your board may ask why you are spending hours on a coalition call that doesn't directly fund your budget. The answer is that we are stewards of something far larger than what we directly manage. If the ecosystem fails, even the most "well-stewarded" organization in the world will be negatively impacted. Call this enlightened self-interest. We’re all connected through our various missions. Looking beyond our own four walls, we can strengthen our individual mission by supporting other organizations.

### Putting It into Action:

- ***Revisit Your Calendar:*** Identify one recurring meeting or coalition that serves the sector but not your "direct" bottom line. Re-commit to it as a core responsibility.
- ***Share the Playbook:*** Take one internal document, a policy, a training manual, or an AI guideline, and share it with three peer organizations.
- ***Cross-Pollinate Leadership:*** Invite a leader from a peer organization to sit in on a strategy session or provide feedback on a project.
- ***The "Plus-One" Testimony:*** The next time a policy issue arises that affects your clients but not your funding, show up to testify anyway.
- ***Board Re-Education:*** Dedicate 15 minutes of your next board meeting to discussing "Sector Health" as a component of your long-term organizational viability.

### Closing Thoughts

That crowded room, full of "competitors" fighting for the same cause, wasn't a distraction from my role as a CEO. It *was* my role. We are the keepers of a collective promise to our communities. This quarter, I challenge you to pick one dimension of sector stewardship you’ve treated as optional and move it to the "essential" column. Our missions don't live in a vacuum; they live in the strength of our connections.
