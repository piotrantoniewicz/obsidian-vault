Power

08.05.2026

The politicisation of 'big tech', social media, and artificial intelligence (AI) is well documented, with the political right seemingly taking to this new battlefield much more adeptly than their opponents, and governments across the globe struggling with questions of regulation and safety versus freedom and progress. But where do the public sit on all of this?

Are certain social or demographic groups more open toward these recent technological advancements? What do regulatory, access and availability, and political neutrality demands look like among global publics?

\-

Crucially, publics distinguish between narrow, practical uses of AI (often welcomed) and systemic deployments in politics, media and security (viewed as dangerous without strict oversight), and they expect governments to regulate all three domains: AI systems themselves, the Big Tech firms deploying them, and the social media platforms that shape political discourse. The political right has so far weaponised these arenas more effectively than its opponents, especially around migration, culture and identity, and voters across ideologies have noticed. Unless mainstream actors move quickly to define credible, citizen‑centred frameworks for AI control, the emerging politics of artificial intelligence will calcify along partisan lines, with populist movements claiming the mantle of “AI scepticism” while tech‑aligned elites defend a loosely‑governed status quo.

#### Anxious adoption: why AI isn’t as “everywhere” as it looks

Strip away the conference jargon and the first finding from YouGov’s cross‑country survey is deceptively simple: most people are not actually using AI very much, at least not in ways they recognise. Weekly personal use ranges from roughly one in five adults in Italy to about one in three in the Netherlands, a far cry from the breathless claims of a fully “AI‑driven” society. Workplace use is similarly uneven, clustered in white‑collar and technical professions, and steeply stratified by age and education. Beneath this patchy adoption, however, sits a dense layer of social concern: respondents report seeing concrete upside in productivity and convenience while simultaneously fearing job displacement, widening inequality, hyper‑targeted persuasion and the erosion of democratic choice. That duality matters politically because it undermines the simplistic narratives currently on offer, the techno‑optimist growth story and the catastrophist “robots will rule us” line, and creates space for more nuanced, citizen‑driven demands.

In practice, the public seems to draw a line between AI as a tool and AI as an infrastructure of power. Automating tedious office tasks or translation wins cautious approval; embedding opaque systems in welfare eligibility, policing, immigration, or news distribution triggers alarm, particularly where people feel they have no recourse when the system gets them wrong. This is the political terrain on which AI will be fought over in the next decade: less about sci‑fi scenarios, more about whose judgment is displaced, whose data is harvested, and who carries the risk when “smart” systems fail.

#### The quiet consensus: regulate it, even if it hurts

Perhaps the most politically explosive finding in YouGov’s work is how strong the appetite for restriction has become. Across the 12 countries surveyed, clear majorities want tougher rules not only on AI systems, but on Big Tech and social media platforms, and they are remarkably willing to trade off economic or geopolitical benefits to get them. That runs directly against a favourite talking point in many capitals and boardrooms, that voters will punish any move that “slows innovation” or undermines “national competitiveness”. In the data, the opposite appears: large shares of citizens say they prefer stricter controls even if it makes their country less attractive to investment or strains relations with more permissive allies. For governments, this is both a warning and an opportunity: the political cost of doing nothing now likely exceeds the cost of moving fast and tightening the rules.

The regulatory demands themselves fall into three broad buckets. First, substantive safeguards: limits on high‑risk deployment, human oversight in sensitive decisions, strong liability when harms occur. Second, structural controls on Big Tech’s political role: transparency over algorithms, caps on data hoarding, interoperability and real penalties for repeated violations. Third, a recalibration of social media’s political function, from the virality‑driven feeds that reward outrage and disinformation to more accountable, deliberative spaces. Voters are not asking their leaders to pick winning business models; they are asking them to restore some symmetry of power between citizens, states and the handful of global platforms mediating everything from news to elections.

#### The new ideological fault‑line: AI and the rightward drift online

If regulation is where there is latent consensus, political identity is where the fractures are already visible. YouGov’s team flagged a pattern that seasoned digital campaigners know instinctively: the organised right has been faster, more disciplined and more imaginative in turning Big Tech’s architecture to its advantage. On issues like migration and “cultural replacement”, right‑wing actors have learned to exploit recommendation algorithms and engagement incentives to push formerly fringe slogans, “remigration” foremost among them, into mainstream feeds, where they now outperform bread‑and‑butter topics like jobs, inflation or defence on a per‑post engagement basis. That doesn’t mean such narratives dominate overall volume, but it does mean they punch far above their weight, shaping agenda‑setting and forcing centrist parties onto terrain they did not choose.

Citizens across the spectrum are aware of this imbalance. In many countries, conservatives and right‑populist voters are more likely to see AI and Big Tech as tools that can be bent to their political project, whereas liberals and left‑leaning voters are divided, some seeing technology as a route to mobilisation and inclusion, others as a vector of corporate and authoritarian control. The risk, as YouGov’s researchers noted, is the hardening of an “AI cleavage” in which scepticism or support for AI clusters tightly with partisan identity, making cross‑party governance much harder. It is not far‑fetched to imagine a near‑future campaign in which one bloc promises to “take back control from the algorithms” while another warns that high regulation will hand the future to rival powers.

If that happens, the question “who owns AI?” becomes less about patents and more about narrative. Right now, the side that talks most confidently about using AI and platforms to reshape politics is not the one most invested in liberal‑democratic checks and balances. Progressive and centrist actors, meanwhile, have been slower to occupy the same terrain, often preferring defensive regulatory fights to proactive experiments in citizen‑centred, transparent use of AI in campaigning, participation or service delivery. Bridging that gap will require more than better messaging; it will demand institutional imagination.
