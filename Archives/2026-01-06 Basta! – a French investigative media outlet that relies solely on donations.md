Basta! – a French investigative media outlet that relies solely on donations

With nearly 400,000 euros collected in donations in 2025, the French media outlet Basta! is targeting a loyal audience to counter the decline in social media traffic. The Fix spoke with its development manager

*Basta!*, which could be translated as “enough” in English, is a French independent investigative media outlet launched in 2008, which focuses on social and environmental issues.  

Their ambition is to offer as many people as possible, in free access, keys to understand the world and levers to change it. The publisher operates solely on a donation-based model, and it has seen for the past ten years an annual growth of 10 to 15% in revenue from donations. With this model, which was groundbreaking when they launched it, they have now managed to generate nearly 400,000 euros in donations in the past year.

To reduce the dependence on large platforms and algorithms, *Basta!* has just also launched an online platform to offer a news feed dedicated only to articles from reliable independent media, selected every day by hand by their team of journalists. Named “ [**Portail des médias indépendants**](https://portail.basta.media/?ref=thefix.media) ”, it has received a good reception since its launch.

The Fix spoke with Nicolas Camier, development manager at *Basta!*.

## How did everything start?

*Basta!* is a media outlet that was founded in 2008. It was one of the first independent online media outlets in France, operating on a completely new model at the time: free and open access, funded by donations. Editorially, the idea was to address social and environmental issues from multiple perspectives.

Today we are a team of 10 journalists, and 3 more are joining us this winter because we just merged with *Rapport de Force*, a French media outlet specialising in social news and social struggles.

## What is your assessment 17 years after its creation?

> Initially, *Basta!* benefited from foundation support, and then donations gradually took over. We gradually implemented this donations strategy, and for about ten years now, we have recorded annual growth in donations of 10 to 15%. We're aiming for €400,000 in donations for 2025

*(Editor’s note: the interview was recorded in late 2025).*

We've managed to achieve growth and consistency, which is really satisfying because it allows us to plan ahead and to have some stability, and ultimately we realise that donations are very similar to subscriptions in terms of income stability compared to a grant-based model where we're very dependent on public authorities or private foundations.

## How do you explain this increase?

It's quite surprising because at the same time our traffic and the number of visits to our website are decreasing. We benefited greatly in the 2010s from social media, where link sharing was the foundation of Twitter and Facebook. But today, Facebook completely changed its algorithm, and all media outlets suffered somewhat.

I have the feeling that media outlets that produce political content or that deal with social or environmental issues have been even worse off, and we've essentially been rendered invisible by Facebook. Today we have 240,000 followers on our page, but only 1,000 or 2,000 people actually see our posts. We've also left Twitter.

The only explanation is that I think we're reaching fewer people than we used to, but we're better at building loyalty. People probably find a unique tone and a difference with us. When you have a media outlet that relies on audience revenue, you have to create attachment, a sense of connection, and a distinct editorial identity to add value compared to others.

## Has the culture of donations also changed in recent years?

Yes, I think there's been a cultural shift around donations. It was quite complicated about ten years ago. We were dealing either with very committed activists, people very close to our editorial line, or with older people who were used to donating. Today, that's completely changed. There are so many things we can fund with donations.

We also thought for a long time that it is difficult to get some donations from young people, but this also changed. There are plenty of examples around us of media outlets that have seen a surge in support, especially among the 25-40 age group.

## Are you trying new platforms to distribute your content?

Our first thing was to improve our Google ranking because what still works is search. But it remains complicated. We publish one or two articles a day, and in fact, facing major news producers, it's difficult to stand out. Google tends to confuse quantity and quality; the more you publish, the higher you rank. It's not the only criterion, but still.

Like everyone else, we're also trying to make more videos, because we see that the social media that are still gaining traction are Instagram, TikTok, and those that create videos. Even Facebook has started to increasingly favour these videos.

We try to incorporate video a bit, even though it's more complicated for us, because we're a paper-based business, we mostly produce news reports of 15,000 characters.

## Do you find it more difficult to produce independent journalism today?

Well, the answer isn't simple, but let's say that in France specifically, the threats facing the press affect everyone, except for the biggest players.

Among all the changes happening in the press, some are quite favourable to independent media. A few years ago, we had to explain what independent media is, how it's different, and what we contribute, whereas today it's quite obvious.

I think people clearly see that there's a difference when a media outlet isn't owned by a group or an individual with strong industrial or private interests, who would then restrict the media outlet from speaking freely about certain topics.

## You recently launched the platform Portail des médias indépendants. What does it consist of?

There are many independent media outlets, and we suffer from this fragmentation of small media, each with its own niche, interest, and format. It's difficult to stand out. The idea behind our first platform, launched 10 years ago, was to bring together newly created independent media outlets that lacked visible ideas and also fight the rise of far-right ideas in the media at that time.

Then, two or three years ago, we wanted to relaunch this platform with a focus on quality, given the changes in social media algorithms. We thought there was an opportunity with an aggregator that would only offer articles from these independent media outlets and provide a real, timely service. We monitor, curate, and highlight a wide range of media that people don't know about.

## One of the goals with this platform is also to reach a broad audience?

Currently, we're getting around 40,000-50,000 visits per month. We want to go further, but we're starting from a fairly low base. Essentially, there's a challenge in terms of brand awareness.

The platform needs to become known, and for this, it depends on the same constraints we face as a media outlet: the constraints of the web and the average impact of our posts and videos. The only thing we can do is leverage the network of independent media outlets and hope they gradually get on board and promote the project.

It’s a win-win situation. The more traffic there is on this platform, the more beneficial it will be. And it's in your best interest to talk about it with your audience, because if others do the same, it will help you break out of your bubble. This platform can serve as a crossroads.
