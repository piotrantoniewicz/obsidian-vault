Governance 2.0: How AI Is Reprogramming the State

Artificial intelligence is reshaping how governments design policies, deliver services, manage public resources and uphold accountability. As AI moves from pilots to system-level use, it offers major gains in productivity, responsiveness and transparency, but also raises questions around ethics, skills, oversight and cross-border coordination.

\-

There is an uncomfortable truth at the heart of today’s AI enthusiasm: the smartest models in the world are useless if the organisations deploying them are badly designed. As one former Amazon Web Services and European Commission official put it on the Berlin stage, “we can have the smartest technology we can invent, but if we have stupid organisations, we’re not going to make it valuable.”

The reference was to Melvin Conway, the computer scientist who observed in 1968 that systems tend to mirror the communication structures of the organisations that create them. That insight travels neatly from software to the state. A government that is fragmented, slow and inward‑looking will not magically become efficient because it plugs ChatGPT into its workflows. It will simply reproduce its dysfunction faster.

The question, then, is not “how do we get more AI into government?” It is “what does a future‑ready state look like in an AI world, and what does it keep constant?”

One answer, drawn from the private sector but deeply relevant to the public, is to focus on the things that will not change. For governments, two functions deserve particular attention: how they hire and how they buy. Everything else, policy areas, political constellations, even the technologies themselves, will continue to shift.

#### Procurement: the most underrated lever in AI politics

In most capitals, AI has been treated as a regulatory puzzle. Drafting acts, issuing guidelines, debating bans: this has been the Brussels reflex. But, regulation is only one of three ways governments interact with the world. The other two, communication and procurement, are at least as powerful.

Procurement, in particular, is a sleeping giant. Across Europe, public authorities spend billions every year buying software, infrastructure and services. Yet surveys run with APolitical show that even senior managers in charge of purchasing AI systems “don’t know how to do it”. The risk is obvious: instead of strategic investment, procurement becomes a box‑ticking compliance exercise, where vendors with slick templates and legal teams win by default.

A future‑ready state would approach this differently. It would treat procurement as a core frontier of AI policy, not an afterthought. That means three shifts.

First, building capacity. Buying AI is not like buying laptops. It requires understanding data governance, model risk, bias, vendor lock‑in, and integration into existing systems. That knowledge cannot be outsourced entirely to consultants or left to a handful of digital units. It has to be spread across finance ministries, line departments and local authorities.

Second, using public purchasing power to shape markets. If European institutions collectively spend billions on AI‑enabled tools, they can demand open standards, interoperability, ethical safeguards, and create viable markets for home‑grown providers who meet those conditions. Right now, Europe is happy to announce €5 billion in “scale‑up” funds for startups while spending more than half a trillion euros on procurement that largely goes to US hyperscalers. Rebalancing that equation is not protectionism; it is industrial policy.

Third, treating contracts as partnerships, not vending machines. AI systems evolve over time. So should the relationship between state and supplier. That implies iterative development, shared testing environments, and the ability to terminate or adapt contracts when evidence of harm emerges. When procurement is stuck in multi‑year, fixed‑specification frameworks, governments lock themselves into yesterday’s assumptions about both technology and citizens.

#### The Global South is not waiting

The most interesting innovations in AI‑enabled governance may not be happening in Brussels, London or Washington at all. They are emerging from the so‑called Global South.

A new “Global South City Tech Competitiveness Rating”, developed by her institute, assessed 50 cities across metrics ranging from macro‑economic conditions and digital infrastructure to liveability and talent attraction. The top tier features an almost equal mix of “North” and “South”: Singapore, Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, Beijing, Shanghai. The pattern is instructive. The cities that perform best are those that have chosen a clear archetype for themselves and aligned governance, investment and technology accordingly.

Dubai thinks of itself as a “global gateway”, so it builds regulatory frameworks, visa regimes and infrastructure to attract flows, of capital, people, data. Bengaluru leans into its role as a deep‑tech innovator, shaping physical planning and funding to support that identity. Several Chinese cities assume their future lies in industrial production and configure AI deployment to optimise logistics, energy use and manufacturing.

That clarity stands in sharp contrast to many Western metropolises, which still behave as if incremental digitisation of existing structures is sufficient. It also complicates the comfortable narrative that only liberal democracies can produce “ethical” AI. Authoritarian regimes are aggressively harnessing frontier technologies to optimise their own governance models, from predictive policing to credit scoring. As another recent book by Taiwan’s former digital minister Audrey Tang and economist Glen Weyl argues, “authoritarian governments are making more use of these technologies” today, while democracies lag, constrained by their own values and institutional inertia.

The implication is not that democracies should mimic illiberal practices. It is that they cannot afford to pretend they are competing only on regulatory virtue. If they abdicate the terrain of technological innovation and institutional experimentation, they will find themselves norm‑takers, not norm‑makers, in the next phase of digital globalisation.

#### Use cases that matter: beyond faster forms

AI is already making public institutions more capable, when used thoughtfully.

Policy design is one. In Singapore, “regulatory sandboxes” use AI‑assisted modelling to test the effects of new rules in controlled environments before rolling them out at scale. Estonia’s “Kratt” system acts as an AI assistant that helps civil servants draft policy briefs, model scenarios and distil stakeholder input. The European Union is experimenting with AI to synthesise the avalanche of documents, amendments and multilingual feedback that accompany legislative processes across 27 member states.

Public service delivery is another. Argentina’s Prometea system uses AI to triage court cases, identify relevant precedents and generate draft decisions for simple matters, reportedly saving up to 90% of human labour time on routine tasks while leaving final judgement in human hands. Singapore employs AI chatbots to guide citizens through complex processes like tax filing, answering questions in natural language and reducing the need for in‑person visits. In the UK, government departments are piloting generative AI tools to help civil servants summarise large evidence bases, draft initial versions of documents, and sift through public feedback.

Financial stewardship may be the least glamorous but most transformative domain. AI systems can flag anomalies in procurement data that suggest corruption or collusion, optimise allocation of funds according to changing needs, and simulate the long‑term budgetary impact of policy options. The OECD has estimated that up to 84% of repetitive public transactions in countries like the UK could be automated, saving the equivalent of 1,200 full‑time years of labour.

The key distinction is between “making inefficiency faster” and redesigning the underlying process. A badly designed benefit system fed into an AI engine will still produce unjust outcomes, only with more streamlined cruelty. A well‑governed system that uses AI to increase transparency, adaptability and citizen oversight can shift the relationship between state and society.

#### Europe’s strategic drift

For Europe, the conversation around “future‑ready government” quickly becomes existential. The continent has long relied on what former central banker Mark Carney recently called the “power of values”: the idea that by enshrining human rights, privacy and non‑discrimination in law, it could set global standards that others would follow.

That strategy, sometimes dubbed the “Brussels effect”, has delivered tangible wins, from GDPR shaping data protection norms worldwide to the new AI Act setting early benchmarks. But, as Leonardo Quattrucci argued on stage, it has also masked a more uncomfortable reality: Europe has under‑invested in the hard infrastructure and industrial capacity that underpin those values.

Seventy per cent of cloud infrastructure used in the EU is controlled by three or four US hyperscalers. The continent is significantly outspent on R&D by both the US and China. Critical technologies like advanced semiconductors, photonics and quantum computing remain dominated by non‑European firms. In that context, values become harder to project. Dependencies on “allies” can quickly turn into vulnerabilities, as disputes over data access, extraterritorial regulation and sanctions regimes have shown.

Quattrucci’s prescription was not to abandon values, but to recalibrate aspirations. Strategy, he reminded the audience, is about aligning “unlimited aspirations with ever limited resources.” Europe cannot credibly aim to match the US and China on every frontier technology. It must choose niches where it has comparative advantage, say, in photonics or specific industrial applications, and concentrate resources there, including through Airbus‑style joint ventures rather than fragmented national efforts.

At the same time, the EU needs to complete its own single market. Internal barriers to services and data, the equivalent of triple‑digit tariffs, are hampering the diffusion of innovation. And it must use the levers it already has more intelligently: redirecting some of its enormous procurement budget towards European AI providers that meet ethical and technical benchmarks, rather than funnelling most contracts to foreign incumbents.

Finally, Europe needs to rediscover institutional imagination. The last time the world re‑architected its economic and governance structures at scale was the Bretton Woods moment after World War II. There is, as yet, no equivalent “Bretton Woods for AI”. Nor is there a ministry of “moral innovation” anywhere with the mandate to think systematically about the ethical and philosophical foundations of machine‑augmented governance. If any polity has the intellectual and normative resources to lead on that front, it is the EU. But that will require more than risk registers; it will require genuine experimentation, new forms of participatory oversight, cross‑border public digital infrastructure, and institutions designed for agility rather than permanence.

#### Democracy as security infrastructure

The geopolitical stakes of AI‑enabled governance hung heavily over the Berlin conversation. One audience member asked bluntly whether AI will accelerate the erosion of democracies and undermine security in Europe and the United States. The answer was nuanced but clear: if democracies continue to move slowly while authoritarian regimes weaponise AI for control and influence, the balance will tilt.

At the same time, democracy itself is a security infrastructure. Polities that can process dissent, adjust policies in response to feedback, and replace leaders without violence are more resilient than those that rely on coercion and opacity. AI can either strengthen or weaken that resilience.

Used to monitor citizens, manipulate information and entrench incumbents, AI will become an authoritarian accelerator. Used to broaden participation, enhance transparency and help institutions learn faster, it can buttress democracy against both external and internal shocks.

That choice is not abstract. It is being made now in budget lines, hiring decisions and procurement contracts. It is being made in whether local authorities use AI to listen better to their residents or merely to deflect complaints. It is being made in whether parliaments invest in their own analytical capacity or outsource legislative drafting to vendors. It is being made in whether governments are honest about the trade‑offs they face and the limits of what technology can do.

#### The real test of “future‑readiness”

In the end, “future‑ready government” is not about having an AI strategy document or a fleet of chatbots. It is about whether public institutions can do three things at once.

First, hold onto the non‑negotiables: the rule of law, fundamental rights, the principle that people affected by decisions should have a say in them. Those are the constants.

Second, be brutally honest about what is broken in their own architecture: siloed departments, archaic procurement rules, talent systems that repel technologists, a political culture allergic to admitting mistakes. Without that self‑diagnosis, AI adoption will only harden dysfunction.

Third, cultivate the courage to change course. That may mean admitting that some regulations, trumpeted as civilisational achievements, need to be adjusted or repealed when they undermine competitiveness without delivering the intended protections. It may mean confronting “degrowth fantasies” when they clash with the empirical link between economic growth and the sustainability of liberal democracy. It may mean making enemies, of entrenched bureaucracies, of comfortable industries, of foreign partners who prefer a docile Europe.

Governments that manage that trifecta will not look sleek or futuristic. They will likely be messy, contested, full of arguments between lawyers, engineers, civil society and citizens. But they will be alive to their time.

The rest will discover, too late, that you cannot regulate your way out of irrelevance, and that in an AI age, a state that does not value its own power will soon find that it has very little of either left.
