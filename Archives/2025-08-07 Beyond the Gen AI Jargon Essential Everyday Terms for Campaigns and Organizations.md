Gen AI is exploding—and it can feel like everyone’s already speaking a new language. For those working in social change and politics, the daily demands are already heavy. Finding the time and headspace to keep up with new tech—let alone understand the buzzwords—can feel impossible.

That’s exactly why we made this glossary. It’s not a complete dictionary. You won’t find every single term here, and we’ve left out the more obvious ones like “AI ethics” or “prompt engineering.” Instead, we focused on the core, often-confusing concepts that campaigners, organizers, and communicators actually need to know to get started with AI—and to start using it with clarity, confidence, and purpose.

## Two Essential AI Terms

Before we dive into deeper terms, we thought we’d start off with the division between Machine Learning and Generative AI. Lots of people use ‘AI’ broadly these days, but it's helpful to distinguish between the two:

##### Machine Learning

The foundational technology where computers learn patterns from data rather than following pre-programmed rules. Instead of explicitly coding "if X then Y," ML systems discover these relationships by analyzing examples, improving their performance with more data and experience. Machine learning has diagnostic or predictive functions and is generally quantitative.

##### Generative AI

AI systems that create new content—text, images, videos, or audio—based on patterns learned from training data. Unlike traditional AI that analyzes or classifies, generative AI produces original outputs that didn't exist before, though always based on patterns from its training. Generative AI is generally qualitative in its outputs and includes subsets like LLMs.

## Generative AI Glossary

The following terms are the AI key concepts that will shape digital campaigning in 2025 and beyond.

##### Agents

**What it is:** Software programs that can perform tasks independently, make decisions, and interact with other systems without constant human supervision - like having a digital team member who never sleeps. **Agential/Agentic AI** proactively monitors situations, initiates workflows, and adapts in real-time to achieve objectives.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Deploy AI agents to handle voter inquiries 24/7, manage social media responses, coordinate volunteer schedules, and monitor campaign mentions across the internet. For example, an AI agent might automatically respond to common questions on your Facebook page while flagging complex issues for human review.

**Why it matters:** Extends your campaign's reach and responsiveness without hiring more staff, particularly valuable for resource-constrained organizations.

##### AI-Augmented Organizations

**What it is:** Organizations that integrate artificial intelligence into workflows to transform decision-making, information flows, and resource coordination, aiming to enhance efficiency and adaptability while redefining traditional organizational structures

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Organizations should start with data infrastructure and governance, begin with low-risk, high-impact use cases, leverage open-source solutions and strategic partnerships, then gradually scale through pilot programs in content creation, donor analysis, and volunteer management before full workflow redesign.

**Why it matters:** The transformation to AI-augmented organizations requires strategic planning, adequate resources, and commitment to fundamental change. Success comes from viewing AI as a transformative force for mission achievement rather than merely a set of tools to adopt.

##### AI Opacity

**What it is:** AI opacity or the "black box problem" refers to AI systems making decisions through processes impossible for humans to understand or explain.

**Example of why a campaign or organization might care about it:** Opaque AI systems may perpetuate biases in voter outreach, amplify misinformation without clear attribution, enable undetected foreign interference, and cause unknowing violations of campaign finance laws. For NGOs, risks include mission misalignment, donor trust erosion, ineffective advocacy targeting, and legal liability from unexplainable decisions.

**Why it matters:** Organizations should implement clear AI disclosure policies, conduct regular bias audits, train staff on transparency requirements, and establish human oversight for AI-generated content.

##### AI Orchestration

**What it is:** AI systems that coordinate your campaign across all digital channels - social media, email, SMS, web - ensuring consistent messaging and optimal timing.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Ensure consistent messaging whether supporters encounter you on Instagram, in emails, or on your website. An education advocacy campaign might coordinate information about school board meetings across all channels, adapting the format but maintaining core messages.

**Why it matters:** Eliminates silos between different digital channels and ensures every supporter interaction is coordinated and effective.

##### Chain of Thought

**What it is:** Chain of Thought prompting guides AI models to "show their work" by breaking down complex problems into step-by-step reasoning processes. Instead of jumping to conclusions, the AI explains its thinking, similar to showing mathematical work, dramatically improving performance on complex reasoning tasks.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Organizations should use this approach for complex strategic decisions, multi-factor analyses, and situations requiring transparent justification.

**Why it matters:** This approach enhances accuracy by reducing logical errors in multi-step analysis, provides transparency showing how AI reached conclusions, enables human oversight and fact-checking of reasoning, and proves critical for high-stakes political decisions requiring clear justification.

##### Constitutional AI

**What it is:** Constitutional AI embeds explicit ethical principles and behavioral guidelines directly into an AI system's decision-making. Instead of learning values implicitly, the AI follows a written "constitution" of rules to guide responses, using these principles to self-critique and revise outputs.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Content moderation systems navigating political speech guidelines. Voter engagement chatbots remaining nonpartisan while helpful if the organization cannot officially endorse a party/candidate. Social media tools balancing campaign messaging with platform standards. Internal systems maintaining professionalism with sensitive information.

##### Custom GPTs

**What it is:** Custom GPTs are specialized versions of ChatGPT created for specific purposes without coding knowledge. They function as personalized AI assistants that understand your mission, audience, communication style, and specific needs - like having a tireless staff member with perfect institutional memory.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Organizations can use custom GPTs for 24/7 assistance, personalized email campaigns, and volunteer coordination.

##### Data Augmentation (Synthetic Data)

**What it is:** Data augmentation is the process of creating new data from existing data, often using generative AI. It is used to expand datasets for training models or to simulate scenarios for analysis.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Create educational animations explaining complex issues, generate accessible content in multiple formats, or produce training materials efficiently. Always with clear disclosure and ethical guidelines about AI-generated content.

**Why it matters:** Data augmentation enhances model training and decision-making. Synthetic data also lets teams test campaigns in simulated conditions, improving strategy without risking real people data.

##### GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer)

**What it is:** You have probably heard of GPTs with ChaGPT but might not know what it stands for or what it is. GPT is a type of artificial intelligence model developed by OpenAI that uses deep learning to generate human-like text. It’s trained on large datasets of text from the internet. It works by predicting the most likely next word in sequences, but does so sophisticatedly enough to produce human-like results across diverse communication needs.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** NGOs can use GPT to create interactive education and training assistants, grant writing support, create campaign content, provide natural language access to internal documents, and grant data analysis support.

##### Edge AI

**What it is:** AI processing that happens directly on devices like smartphones or tablets rather than in the cloud, enabling instant responses even without internet connection.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Power offline-capable apps for field organizers working in areas with poor connectivity. Enable real-time translation at rural canvassing without internet dependence. Process sensitive data locally on devices to protect privacy while still leveraging AI capabilities.

**Why it matters:** Ensures campaign tools work everywhere, reduces privacy concerns by keeping data local, and enables real-time responses.

##### Federated Learning

**What it is:** A privacy-preserving way for multiple organizations to train AI models on their combined data without actually sharing the sensitive data itself.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Coalition campaigns can analyze combined supporter data without exposing individual contact lists, enabling better targeting while maintaining privacy and organizational independence.

**Why it matters:** Allows unprecedented collaboration between allied organizations while respecting data privacy and organizational boundaries.

##### Human-Centered Design

**What it is:** Human-Centered Design for AI systems ensures technology serves human needs through user agency, transparency, accessibility, ethical alignment, and iterative improvement.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Start with user needs rather than technology capabilities, test with diverse stakeholder groups, provide clear help systems and human support options, ensure equitable access regardless of resources, and maintain focus on enhancing human decision-making rather than replacing it.

**Why it matters:** Well-designed systems achieve better outcomes through improved human-AI collaboration, reducing training time and increasing effectiveness.

##### Human in the Loop (HITL)

**What it is:** Refers to AI systems where humans actively participate at key stages to guide, review, and refine AI outputs. This creates a feedback loop combining machine efficiency with human judgment - think of it as having an AI assistant that flags uncertain decisions for human review while handling routine work automatically.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Human oversight becomes essential for content moderation of sensitive materials, donor communications involving financial data, voter data handling for privacy compliance, crisis communications requiring nuanced judgment, and legal compliance.

**Why it matters:** Builds public trust through perceived human oversight, prevents AI from making inappropriate promises or misstatements, and ensures compliance with evolving regulations while maintaining efficiency gains from automation.

##### Large Language Models (LLMs)

**What it is:** Massive AI systems trained on enormous amounts of text that can understand and generate human-like language, power tools like ChatGPT, and form the foundation of most modern Generative AI writing tools.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Analyze policy documents to extract key points, answer supporter questions about complex issues, create educational materials, help draft grant proposals, and translate content into multiple languages.

**Why it matters:** Provides campaigns with an intelligent writing assistant that can dramatically accelerate content creation and communication tasks.

##### MCP (Model Context Protocol)

**What it is:** Open standard by Anthropic allowing AI systems to securely connect to external data sources through standardized interfaces. Acts as a "universal adapter" for AI accessing databases and services, replacing fragmented integrations with a single protocol across platforms.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Connect AI tools to your organization's various data sources without complex custom integrations. An environmental organization might use MCP to let their AI assistant access their research database, event calendar, and supporter CRM simultaneously, enabling it to answer questions with current, organization-specific information.

##### Model Drift

**What it is:** Performance degradation when AI encounters data different from training or when learned relationships become invalid. Includes concept drift (meaning changes), data drift (input characteristics change), and upstream drift (source changes). Models can become inaccurate within days without monitoring.

**Example of why a campaign or organization should know about it:** Understand when your AI tools stop being reliable due to changes in social context, language, or behavior. For example, if your sentiment analysis model begins misreading public opinion because of evolving slang or shifting political discourse, your messaging could miss the mark or even backfire. By proactively detecting and addressing model drift, your campaign ensures that AI-driven decisions stay aligned with current realities—maintaining trust, relevance, and impact.

##### Model Hallucination

**What it is:** AI hallucination occurs when language models generate false, misleading, or nonsensical information while presenting it confidently as fact. This happens because models predict statistically likely responses based on training patterns, not because they understand truth or have real-time fact access.

**Example of why a campaign or organization should know about it:** Protect your campaign’s credibility by recognizing when AI tools generate false or misleading information. Hallucinations often sound confident and convincing, making them hard to spot. By implementing fact-checking processes and training staff to identify hallucinated content, your organization ensures accuracy and accountability in all public-facing materials.

##### Multimodal AI

**What it is:** AI systems that work with multiple types of content simultaneously - understanding and creating combinations of text, images, audio, and video from a single prompt.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it** It is useful for a campaign when deciding which LLM to use considering that there are agents that are text-only, text + partial modalities like image input but lack image generation or voice capabilities (Claude), and fully multimodal which can understand and generate across multiple modalities, including voice, image, and video (ChatGPT-4).

**Why it matters:** Streamlines content creation by eliminating the need for multiple specialized tools and ensures consistency across different media types.

##### Multipurpose AI

**What it is:** Multipurpose AI refers to artificial intelligence systems designed to perform a wide range of tasks across different domains, rather than being specialized for a single function.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** streamline operations, boost engagement, and make data-driven decisions more efficiently.

**Why it matters:** In a competitive and fast-moving political landscape, multipurpose AI empowers smaller teams to operate with the scale and sophistication of larger organizations, enhancing their ability to influence outcomes and mobilize support.

##### RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)

**What it is:** AI technique that combines a language model's generative capabilities with external knowledge sources by retrieving relevant information from a database and incorporating it into the model's response. Instead of relying solely on training data, RAG searches a knowledge base for relevant context before generating answers, dramatically improving accuracy for domain-specific questions.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** A volunteer training chatbot can answer questions using real campaign materials. A content assistant for staffers can draft emails, tweets, or press statements based on the latest campaign messaging and media appearances.

##### Unstructured Data

**What it is:** Unstructured data encompasses information lacking pre-defined organization - social media posts, emails, videos, audio recordings, images, survey responses, and news articles. For campaigns, this represents the vast majority of incoming information that traditional databases cannot easily process.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Campaigns use unstructured data analysis for hyper-personalized voter outreach through "look-alike audiences," real-time monitoring in campaign war rooms, and multilingual constituent engagement. Environmental groups analyze social sentiment to identify key influencers, while civil rights organizations process protest footage to document activities and measure impact.

**Why it matters:** Verification challenges intensify with AI-generated content making it difficult to distinguish authentic from synthetic data. Organizations must balance the value of unstructured data insights against verification costs and potential misinformation risks.

##### Vibe Coding

**What it is:** Vibe coding refers to the process of building digital experiences—such as websites, mobile apps, or interactive content—through intuitive, visual interfaces or prompts without writing traditional code.  
As vibe coding platforms are still in early development stages it’s highly recommended to supervise the results by an expert who can ensure the quality of the generated solution to minimize risks.

**Example of how a campaign or organization might use it:** Campaigns use vibe coding tools to rapidly prototype and launch landing pages, donation platforms, volunteer sign-up tools, event apps, or storytelling microsites—without needing full-time developers.

**Why it matters:** It allows campaigns to move faster, test more ideas, and involve non-technical staff in the creation of digital assets.
