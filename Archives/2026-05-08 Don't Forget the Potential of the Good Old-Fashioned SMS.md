Power

08.05.2026

In this current era of daily digital innovation, AI-optimised content and algorithm-chasing, there remains an effective tool in the campaign arsenal that is consistently under-leveraged: the good, old-fashioned SMS or text message.

It may seem quaint to talk about SMS’s in 2026 – and that one is nostalgically looking back to the early 2010s - but SMS still delivers what some digital channels struggle to achieve: near-universal reach, high open rates and a degree of potential interactivity that social media has lost.

While we all constantly seek the next frontier in political tech, SMS messaging remains a useful tool in the execution design of any serious campaign.

\-

By Jonathan Moakes (SABI Strategy Group)

At the heart of SMS's effectiveness is something simple we tend to forget: the phone is personal and the old-fashioned notable. And outside of calls, which appear to have all but vanished for much of today's (younger) generation, SMS is the most personal you can get. Unlike social media feeds, display ads or even email inboxes, a text message lands in a space that can command genuine attention. And genuine attention translates into reach and campaign cut through.

SMS open rates consistently sit above 90%. Many of us live across WhatsApp, Signal and Telegram groups, drowning in hundreds of group threads. A busy professional might be sitting on 200 or 300 unread messages at any given moment, and that's before they've opened their email. Against that backdrop, an SMS is a curiosity. It has become, in its own way, what a handwritten letter can now be in direct mail: it is interesting enough to be read.

So, how and where can SMS be used today in campaigns? The answer is in more places than we think.

GOTV and GOTR (Get Out to Register) are where I have personally found SMS to be the most effective. A personalised election-day reminder, with the voter's name, their polling location and closing time, delivers solid ROI. Providing young people with personalised, clear information to register can be groundbreaking in terms of achieving differential turnout and building a new voter market.

Fundraising is where the immediacy of SMS messaging works. A deadline-driven donation ask lands in real time and can prompt an immediate decision. Text messages can convey an urgency that can be difficult to manufacture in an email that often gets read much later than when it was sent.

Rapid response is perhaps where SMS can be used a lot more. In the middle of a debate, for example, SMS allows a campaign to reach its most engaged supporters instantly. As we often say, speed in politics kills, and SMS is one of the fastest triggers a campaign has.

Event mobilisation, whether it be driving people to attend a manifesto launch or visibility event, benefits from SMS's read-within-minutes reality. Peer-to-peer texting also adds a layer of authenticity that broadcast SMS, WhatsApp channels, or emails cannot replicate. A message that feels like it came from a real person lends itself to interaction and response rather than the usual “delete” or ignore.

Finally, persuasion sequences are something I have been experimenting with recently. These allow campaigns to build multi-touch SMS journeys for soft supporters that move voters along a conversion funnel with space for genuine interaction and addition to groups on WhatsApp and Facebook.

SMS is, of course, not without its challenges.

Regulatory and data protection compliance is a natural yet important hurdle. In the UK and across Europe, we are all familiar with what GDPR requires in respect of data handling and opt-in requirements. To reach the point of being able to serve SMS messages to thousands of voters, significant on-the-ground and social media funnel work is required. In many jurisdictions where regulations have historically been more lax, the landscape is evolving rapidly, and campaigns must keep pace.

Opt-out and the risk of irritation is a challenge to watch carefully, especially when you have worked hard to curate your list. SMS earns its effectiveness precisely because it feels personal, but that can work against a campaign just as easily. Sending out messages too frequently or contacting someone who never asked to hear from you can lead to immediate and very public backlash. An irritated voter doesn't just unsubscribe; they form an opinion, and they share it. Ask any campaign manager who has found themselves on local radio fielding questions about "invasive" text messages.

Format constraints are a creative limitation worth acknowledging. SMS is a medium that demands brevity and discipline. This can be a genuine strength, but it requires a different kind of copywriting skill.

List quality and ageing data are an operational reality that too many campaigns gloss over. Mobile numbers change hands and go inactive considerably faster than email addresses do. A list that was clean twelve months ago may be of significantly lesser value today. Regular sample testing is absolutely key to preventing wasted spend.

Even though I have worked with SMS send-outs for more than fifteen years, there are some key foundational principles I always return to. The first is to start building your list early and never stop. Good SMS lists are the product of months of on-the-ground organising, solid digital funnel operations, and consistently capturing quality data at events. Secondly, personalisation and specificity drive optimal results. Insert the voter's name and ensure messaging is segmented by constituency and issue. Finally, ensure every effective SMS has a single, clear call to action. Do not give the voter or donor multiple options.

It would, of course, not be right to leave out how AI can enhance SMS campaigns. LLMs can assist with predictive targeting, specifically identifying which existing or potential supporters are most likely to respond to a particular message and when. AI can also help assess in real time how an SMS campaign is landing: has the message struck the right tone, is it cutting through, and where is it falling flat. And while it may feel unsexy compared to the more glamorous applications of AI in campaigning, some of the most valuable work LLMs can do is in list optimisation: identifying aged and inactive numbers, cleaning data and ensuring that every send is as efficient as it can be.

Overall, SMS campaigns are not a relic of times gone by. And they are not just something that unsophisticated campaigns in the developing world just use. They can be of immense value in raising money, getting volunteers out and critically registering and mobilising voters. As ever with most tools, SMS should always be used as part of an integrated campaign communications operation. No single channel can be over-relied on. But the old humble text message does deserve a seat at the table.
