## Facilitation in the AI era: a new report from Jigsaw

If you speak with people who are experts in leading dialogues and structured conversations, professional facilitators who know how to make public discussions meaningful and effective, they will share with you a counterintuitive truth about the state of civic discourse. “People feel frustrated because they feel benched, not because they're apathetic,” one leading expert on deliberative democracy told Jigsaw. In other words, members of the public want to have their voices heard and to shape their collective future, but existing institutions aren’t making it easy.

> “People feel frustrated because they feel benched, not because they're apathetic.”

Innovative methods from fields like [deliberative democracy](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/innovative-citizen-participation-and-new-democratic-institutions_339306da-en.html) and [participatory peacebuilding](https://berghof-foundation.org/library/participatory-methods-in-peacebuilding-work) are rising to the occasion, responding to this growing desire for more impactful public discourse by offering people new ways of shaping decisions through dialogue and deliberation. According to one expert, professional facilitators are trying to challenge “the most critical and debilitating syndrome in democratic societies… the inversion that has led us to see the public as a risk we need to manage rather than a resource we should tap.” Novel AI technologies have emerged against this backdrop, offering exciting opportunities and potential challenges for facilitators and the work of empowering the public through conversation. *How might AI most effectively support, enhance, or scale the work being done by facilitators to lead better conversations?*

Over the past few years, Jigsaw has been exploring ways of leveraging AI for public dialogue, to improve our ability to have voice and choice within the systems that affect our lives. We call this [structural agency](https://medium.com/jigsaw/a-new-perspective-on-human-agency-for-the-ai-era-cd785faab026). To deepen our understanding of what makes conversations constructive and meaningful, we reached out to expert leaders of dialogues and deliberations, often known as facilitators. We held in-depth interviews with 22 facilitators from 6 continents who have led a wide range of dialogue types, from local citizens’ assemblies to multinational conflict resolution and peacebuilding. We explored how facilitators design and deliver successful conversations, how they are already experimenting with technology, and where AI would be most (and least) helpful in enhancing their work.

**Today we’re publishing a** [**report**](https://services.google.com/fh/files/blogs/jigsaw_facilitation_in_the_ai_era.pdf) **that shares our full research findings and identifies the most promising opportunities for AI to improve collective dialogues by enhancing and augmenting this important work, rather than replacing or automating it. And we’re proud that more than a dozen professional facilitators have joined us as coauthors, crafting this community roadmap for AI & facilitation together for the benefit of the wider ecosystem.**

Many facilitators we spoke with are optimistic that novel AI technologies will be able to support them and enhance their work in exciting new ways. But as one facilitator explained, “it’s critical to have a say in designing the system itself, not just being passive participants.” Jigsaw works by doing deep research and design together alongside users and communities – and we hope that this report can spark new collaborations and investment with AI for collective dialogues.

## For designers & developers, an actionable typology of dialogue processes

What makes a structured conversation or dialogue successful? Facilitators across geographies and traditions agreed that what occurs “in the room,” or during a discussion is just the tip of the iceberg – what makes or breaks a structured conversation is process design. As one facilitator explained, “you really have to understand what your objective is… and then, are the tools you’re using to get there efficient and appropriate?”

Facilitators told us that the best process design emerges when everyone is crystal-clear about the desired aims of the conversation. Through our research, we found that dialogue processes can be categorized according to 3 different primary objectives:

- **Transformative.** Here, the primary goal is to affect participants as individuals or as a group, and no decision or consensus is necessarily required. Outcomes might include, e.g. learning, growth, empathy or capacity-building, or in some cases opinion change. Imagine a trust-building dialogue between urban residents and indigenous groups, or a process designed to build empathy for other people’s policy views.

- **Generative.** Here, the primary goal is to gather or generate ideas from the public, and no decision or consensus is necessarily required by participants. Traditional town halls requesting policy feedback could fall in this category, or perhaps a large conversation using digital tools asking participants to submit ideas. Examples include Jigsaw’s AI-enabled town hall for local leaders and residents in [Bowling Green, Kentucky](https://medium.com/jigsaw/how-one-of-the-fastest-growing-cities-in-kentucky-used-ai-to-plan-for-the-next-25-years-3b70c4fd1412) and an AI-powered national conversation on freedom and equality, [We The People](https://medium.com/jigsaw/we-the-peoples-first-national-conversation-freedom-and-equality-409dedfb02cd).

- **Deliberative.** Here, the primary goal is for the group to achieve a task together through active learning and discussion. The tasks might include making a judgment or decision, or issuing a set of policy recommendations. A classic example of this type is a [citizens’ assembly](https://assemblyguide.demnext.org/) or deliberative mini-public.

These objectives are really ideal types, highlighting the key distinguishing features that set one kind of conversation apart from another – and they don’t capture everything that happens in a given process. And multiple types are often sequenced or combined to achieve more complex goals. This framework is intended to help clarify design decisions, especially for technologists building digital tools or scaled hybrid processes. See the full report for more details.

## 5 untapped opportunities for AI to support dialogue facilitators

During this research, many facilitators told us about their experiments with technology and AI. Using lo-fi concept “thought starters,” we brainstormed novel applications of AI together with facilitators and analyzed where this technology might be most (and least!) helpful. Five ideas rose to the surface as the most promising ways for AI to enhance collective dialogues.

*An important note:* Jigsaw is not actively developing these product opportunities. But we are excited to share them with the wider ecosystem, as validation and inspiration for those working on technology for large-scale conversations. We also believe, with many facilitators, that these AI opportunities should be co-designed with both practitioners and publics. This will help build trust in the technology and ensure that AI features *augment* the transformational experience of dialogue and deliberation, but do not replace it.

Five untapped opportunities for AI to support dialogue facilitators

**1\. Multilingual, multimodal tools for tailored recruitment and improved participation.** It can be difficult to make sure that the right people participate in a conversation: often expensive and challenging for organizers to reach potential members, while barriers like cost, time, resources, or language can hold people back from getting involved. To drive access and better participation, facilitators would benefit from more tailored outreach tools, translation features for language and cultural context, and multimodal features that allow public contributions in different formats (e.g. audio, video, text).

> *“\[We need\] an AI that is culturally sensitive to certain terms we use, understanding the power dynamic of the language itself… for sensitive dialogues, that could be helpful. ” —Facilitator P8*

**2\. Multimodal education and evidence features for dynamic learning.** In structured dialogues, many people find it hard to process the information they’re given: because it’s too technical and complex, or because the volume of data is overwhelming. AI tools built to complement dialogues could help provide information to people in the interactive or multimodal formats best suited to their schedules or learning styles, for instance, short video summaries to convey key expert insights or technical reports.

> *“We need to get better at more engaging materials that are easier, but it’s also the conversational element of \[AI\] that encourages inquiry… some kind of chat interface where you could query it and learn about \[the topic\].” —Facilitator P20*

**3\. Real-time conversation analysis and visualization tools for live sensemaking.** It’s crucial for facilitators to know the evolving “temperature of the room” during a dialogue, but analog sensemaking can be a bottleneck – and in large-scale digital conversations, reporting is often made available only at the end of a process. Live sensemaking could be powerful for facilitators and participants alike, provided the tools could be quickly trained on the nuances of a specific conversation, and with chances for participants to review or challenge the AI summaries of their discussions.

> *“I think \[using AI chatbots to summarize participant questions\] is hugely powerful for us as facilitators, but for participants as well. It enables \[us\] to interact with feedback data in real time in a way we haven’t been able to prior.” —Facilitators P16/P17*

**4\. Scenario planning and tradeoff extrapolation tools for futurecasting.** During our research, we observed that people participating in deliberative processes were often hungry for more information about the implications of decisions they were being asked to make, and found it difficult to keep track of relevant trade-offs. AI features could help paint a picture of different decision outcomes 2, 5, or even 10+ years into the future that might be hard to otherwise imagine or articulate. Facilitators agreed that AI features like this could be valuable, but stressed that these tools would need to be transparent and adjustable, to maintain trust – and avoid accidentally limiting people’s imaginations.

> *“I think that \[a tradeoff wizard\] would be useful for really complex topics… if we had a way of visualizing and thinking about the future, and the trade-offs that we make right now and \[what\] that means, that would be really helpful.” —Facilitator P13*

**5\. Interactive, public-facing analysis and reporting for participatory sensemaking.** Turning the data or recommendations from a dialogue into a compelling report for stakeholders can be time-consuming and expensive. And it often requires participants to rely on the professional expertise of facilitators to translate their inputs faithfully. One powerful use of generative AI would be to empower participants to create their own reporting assets from deliberations or dialogue data in multimedia formats. AI-enabled sensemaking tools that include interactive data visualizations could also help the public interpret outcomes from large-scale conversations, building a sense of wider collective investment and understanding beyond dialogue participants themselves.

> *“Our reports are just written reports. How can they be more engaging, without having a big comms budget and a million cameras in the room? How can people talk to people to tell the stories?” —Facilitator P11*

## Collaboration & co-design is the way forward

Together with our coauthors, we believe that these 5 AI technologies could enhance dialogues in meaningful and inspiring ways – if they are built collaboratively by facilitators, technologists, researchers, and the public. Many of the opportunities in this report are already being explored by developers as well as facilitators themselves. We hope this report can complement ongoing work and help validate certain directions for further exploration and investment, by confirming how deeply they are grounded in facilitators’ own needs and expertise. We are optimistic, too, that the findings might inspire others to create entirely new AI products, to help everyone have more meaningful conversations.
