Key Takeaways

- **Impact is more than attendance and activity.** Outputs matter, but they are only the starting point. Real impact moves beyond “how much we did” to “what actually changed.”
- **Outcomes describe personal transformation.** Skills gained, beliefs shifted, confidence built — these are meaningful markers of progress and part of the depth we’re seeking.
- **Impact is the ripple effect beyond your organization.** When personal change reshapes families, neighborhoods, or systems, that’s where long-term community transformation begins.
- **You don’t have to reinvent the wheel.** Connecting your work to established research frameworks, like a Theory of Change or socio-emotional learning models, strengthens credibility and sustainability.
- **Impact flows both ways.** Donors and organizations are part of the community too. When they experience transformation alongside participants, trust and long-term investment deepen.
- **Depth builds trust.** When nonprofits clearly articulate outputs, outcomes, and long-term impact, they move from reporting activity to demonstrating meaningful change.

Recently, our Nonprofit Hive community wrestled with a simple but uncomfortable question:

**[Are we in a crisis of depth?](https://thenonprofithive.com/nonprofit-crisis-of-depth/)**

In a sector that often prioritizes performance reports and fast metrics, it’s worth asking whether we are measuring activity or meaningful change.

Before you read further…think back through your day, your week, your month…

*How many times did you hear or [read the word “impact?”](https://thenonprofithive.com/reframing-the-nonprofit-narrative/)*

For many of us, impact is the current *word-de-jour*, buzzword, or even intricate to our own organizational brand and messaging.

But what exactly is impact? And, more importantly, what is **I** mpact, with a capital “I”?

Does any of this sound familiar?

- Tonight’s program was so impactful!
- Here is our annual impact report. (Full of attendance stats)
- Your gift made an impact on our organization.

While the above statements may refer to the feeling of impact, the emotion of change, or even the initial stages of transformation, do they truly reflect the full depth of impact? Do they describe the highest degree of transformation? Do they describe measurable results that prove your organization matters?  
**I** mpact is a journey, both of breadth and depth. Understanding the different stages of change deepens the way we tell our story and build relationships of trust within our community.

![](https://thenonprofithive.com/wp-content/uploads/Outputs-700x700.jpg)

## First Things First

If you want to capture the true depth of the word **I** mpact, a Theory of Change framework is a valuable way to understand progressive change.

First, we have **outputs**. Outputs are always based on individual participation. They are our initial results based on the direct services we are providing. Common examples of outputs are:

1. How many hours were spent in a particular service area?
2. How many people participated in a program?
3. How much money was spent on a program?

[![](https://thenonprofithive.com/wp-content/uploads/Hive-Events-and-Gatherings.png)](https://luma.com/thenonprofithive)

Outputs are very important! To quote the favorite musical of many, *Hamilton*, outputs are describing “the room where it happened.” And for many of us, people need to be in the “room” where our programs are happening before ever receiving the benefits. Outputs answer the questions:

- How much did we do?
- Are we doing the right things?

[Outputs are the first level of change.](https://thenonprofithive.com/outcome-reports-for-maximum-impact/) It’s a *change of position*. Someone is in your program, instead of not being in your program. Often this is the only data ever reported by nonprofits. Yet there is so much more to discover!

## The Flow of Change:

So, now that we know the “room” where it’s happening…the people, the hours, the cost, let’s describe the change happening in the room. These are **outcomes**.  These are the goals of our programming. Outcomes describe the *personal change.* They are intermediate results, based on new skills, beliefs, or habits. You can also look at the outcomes of a group of people such as a cohort or class.

Common examples of outcomes are:

1. Students’ reading levels have risen to grade level or above.
2. Parents feel more equipped and empowered to care for their children.
3. Youth are growing in their creative identity and developing leadership skills.

Outcomes help answer the questions:

- How well did we do it? (Internal Evaluation)
- Is there a difference in our *participants*? (External Evaluation)

But, don’t stop there. We’ve still only scratched the surface of **I** mpact

---

## Change Beyond You:

After achieving initial participation results and describing personal change within your program, now it’s time to look at what’s happening *outside* of the walls of your organization.

**I** mpact is the ripple effect of personal change. Once someone has adopted new patterns, beliefs, or skills, their world is bound to change in some other way. This may be seen first among their family, classmates, co-workers, friends or neighborhood.

**I** mpact asks:

1. Did our programs *transform* anyone else?
2. Can we see changes that *transcend* our immediate efforts?
3. Are our efforts creating *sustainable* proof that our approach works?

If outcomes are personal change, **I** mpact is *corporate change*. **I** mpact extends beyond the initial group of people your organization served and asks:

- Is anyone *else* better off?
- Is there a difference in our *community*?

**I** mpact answers those questions. **I** mpact achieves long term results. **I** mpact demonstrates the ability to affect the greatest systemic challenges we face. **I** mpact influences the community. **I** mpact offers the highest possible return on investment for our funders. Simply put, **I** **mpact is proof that your way works.**

**And *that* is depth.**

Here are some examples of how **I** mpact may look in your context.

- Young girls participate in dance class and learn skills such as confidence, time management and creative expression (outputs and outcomes). Over time, students are experiencing higher academic scores, attend college (first in their family) and ultimately begin a career. Their career success elevates them out of generational poverty and interrupts related systemic issues. Not only is her life changed, but her family, and future family is also transformed. **This is Impact. This is Depth.**
- Families experiencing homelessness receive transitional housing and supportive services and gain personal budget tools, career development coaching and holistic healing (outputs and outcomes.) Equipped with the tools needed to remain housed, children benefit from stable school and supportive relationships, neighborhoods thrive, and local business experience economic boosts. **This is Impact. This is Depth.**
- Women participate in health education and advocacy training programs, experiencing empowerment and improved access to the care they need (outputs and outcomes.) Healthier women are now coaching others in their community on safer practices and building coalitions to address needs on a larger scale. Over time, as education and advocacy prevail, community health scores increase. **This is Impact. This is Depth**

And aren’t results like these the ones we all long for? The ones that propelled us into this work in the first place? The ones that keep us going through long days, countless emails, ever-shrinking budgets, and any number of other challenges?

## So, Now What?

At this point you are either energized, overwhelmed, or both at the thought of capturing your **I** mpact.

The good news, no, great news, is that **I** mpact can and *should* be linked to existing research. You don’t have to reinvent the wheel. You just have to connect your wheel to someone else’s.

Let me explain.  
Socio-Emotional Learning (SEL) can be used as the theoretical foundation for most evaluation work. Growth is tracked over three domains: Being, Doing and Relating. In other words: identify formation, skills, and community relationships. SEL has *already* been linked to **I** mpacting significant aspects of one’s personal life and the community at large.

When we demonstrate SEL outcomes (personal change), we connect that change to the **I** mpact on greater areas of corporate change. This correlation is supported by any data you have: pre/post-assessment, quotes, stories, etc.

  
When you connect your **I** mpact to existing research you are connecting donor investment to your organization’s activities and subsequent transformation.

![](https://thenonprofithive.com/wp-content/uploads/Impact-Funnel-arrow-down-700x700.jpg)

When change is experienced by one person and backed up with supporting data from a larger group, it’s not hard to imagine the kind of change that the whole community will see in the near future!

And this is the kind of change that sticks. This is the change that transforms the world.

This is **Impact**.

This is **Depth.**

**And your donors will LOVE it.**

**But Wait, There’s More**

Earlier we said, “ **I** mpact is a journey, both of breadth and depth.”   **I** mpact is not one-directional.

While we tend to think of **I** mpact as ultimately driven by donors and organizations, it should not only be a top-down relationship.

If **I** mpact is defined as change in the community, and your organization and donors are part of the community, **I** mpact can extend to them, through your participants. This can look like:

- A culture of humility, listening, and learning among donors and stakeholders
- A robust alumni network that resources your model (think Jim Collins’ Fly Wheel)
- Investment based on relationships, not simply a transaction

![](https://thenonprofithive.com/wp-content/uploads/Impact-Funnel-up_down-arrow-700x700.jpg)

When donors experience their own transformation through their investment, that’s the kind of depth that no one else can replicate.
