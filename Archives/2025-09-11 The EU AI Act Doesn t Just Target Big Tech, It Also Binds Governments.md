## Once only regulators, public institutions are now risk owners of AI systems.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*uW8XJ5n371MO0RWZyQkT-A.png)

When people hear about the EU AI Act, they often picture Silicon Valley giants and European startups scrambling to comply. But there’s another, less-discussed actor in the spotlight: **the state itself**.

In 2020, the Dutch SyRI system, an algorithm designed to detect welfare fraud, was struck down in court for violating human rights. Under the EU AI Act, public bodies using such systems will now face strict obligations too.

European governments are not just regulators under the AI Act, they are also **providers and deployers** of AI systems. Ministries, municipalities, law enforcement agencies, and public institutions will now face the same obligations as private companies when they use AI. In rare cases they may also act as **importers** if they procure AI directly from outside the EU, but this is the exception rather than the rule.

And yes: **the AI Act applies to them too,** except in the areas of national security and military use, which are outside the scope of the regulation.

Whether it is a chatbot at the job center, an algorithm scoring visa applications, or a predictive tool used by social services, if the system carries risk, the AI Act demands compliance.

## 1\. Governments Are More Than Just Regulators

European governments play a **dual role**: they oversee AI compliance across society *and* they deploy AI systems themselves.

This is not theoretical. Public bodies already use AI for:

- **Border control and migration** (e.g. risk profiling at Schengen borders)
- **Social security fraud detection** (e.g. risk scoring of benefit recipients)
- **Predictive crisis warning** (e.g. strategic conflict forecasting)

Under the AI Act, many of these fall into the “high-risk” category, meaning they must meet strict requirements on transparency, risk management, documentation, and human oversight. Public authorities do **not** receive a blanket exemption.

The message is clear: **governments are also risk owners** and they will be held accountable.

Even the use of chatbots in communication with citizens falls under the AI Act. Most are “limited risk,” but if a chatbot is used in high-stakes contexts such as access to public benefits, migration, or education, it may be classified as high-risk.

## 2\. Key Obligations for Public Institutions

If a public agency develops, deploys, or buys an AI system, it must understand its responsibilities under the law. These obligations depend on the risk category.

### If the AI System Is High-Risk

This includes many public sector use cases such as border control, education, welfare fraud detection, and employment screening.

🔎 **Risk classification**

- Determine whether the system is high-risk.
- If the public body is the provider, it must register the system in the EU AI database and undergo a conformity assessment.

🔍 **Transparency and citizen information**

- Inform individuals when decisions are automated, especially if those decisions affect their rights (e.g. eligibility for benefits, visa approvals).

🛠 **Governance and documentation**

- Implement a risk management system
- Ensure quality and unbiased training data
- Guarantee human oversight
- Enable logging, record-keeping, incident reporting, and documentation

🧾 **Procurement responsibilities**

- If procuring the system externally, ensure the provider complies with the AI Act and that contracts cover compliance, liability, and updates.

### If the AI System Is Limited-Risk

These are systems that do not make high-stakes decisions but still interact with citizens, such as most chatbots or AI-generated content.

**Transparency obligations only**

- Citizens must be informed that they are interacting with AI, e.g. a notice like “This conversation is being handled by an AI system.”
- AI-generated content must be clearly labeled as such.

## 3\. Challenges for the Public Sector

While the AI Act provides clear rules, implementation will not be easy, especially for public institutions.

- **Legacy systems**: Many agencies rely on outdated IT that makes AI integration and monitoring difficult.
- **Decentralization**: In federal systems like Germany, responsibilities are spread across multiple levels. Who ensures compliance at each level?
- **Limited resources**: Compliance requires expertise, documentation, testing, and monitoring, which many municipalities or agencies lack.
- **Vendor dependence**: Many AI systems are developed externally. If a system fails compliance, liability is often unclear.

## 4\. What Comes Next?

Despite its ambition, the AI Act leaves some critical questions unanswered:

**Will small and mid-sized public bodies be overwhelmed?** Most do not have an AI ethics officer or compliance team.

**How will enforcement work for public institutions?** Will national regulators audit ministries, and what happens if they fail?

**Will fear of penalties chill innovation?** Agencies might avoid using AI entirely to escape risk.

**Where are the resources?** Without funding and training, many public sector bodies will struggle to meet the requirements, even with the best intentions.

## Conclusion: The State Must Walk Its Talk

For years, the public sector has encouraged ethical, responsible AI in society. Now, with the EU AI Act, governments must walk their own talk.

Public institutions now have the chance to lead by example. If they do not, they risk undermining both compliance and public trust.

The Act will test whether governments can practice what they preach. If they succeed, Europe could set the global standard for trustworthy public sector AI. If they fail, it will not only stall innovation but also erode trust in democracy itself.
