Europe’s Next Blackout? The Political Price of AI’s Energy Appetite

As Europe accelerates its AI ambitions, political conflict is shifting from abstract ethical debates to concrete infrastructure battles over data centres, electricity demand, land use, and the fear of AI-driven labour displacement. Communities already worry about rising energy prices, while local leaders struggle to balance investment, jobs, and environmental pressures.

What is now the biggest AI policy debate in the US is coming to Europe fast, and political actors, campaigns, and regulators are unprepared for how quickly it could reshape public opinion.

Public media in a platform world

In a media ecosystem rewired by AI, social platforms, and creator economies, can public broadcasters still shape the public sphere, or will they become niche heritage brands shouting from the sidelines? For Bundred, who leads policy and public affairs at the BBC, the answer begins with a reminder of why these institutions exist at all. The BBC, he noted, was born as an R&D innovation “the country coming together” to publicly own radio and has always tied its legitimacy to universality: being “for all not just for some.”

That mission is under direct threat from the fragmentation of attention. Linear television and radio no longer guarantee reach; iPlayer, the BBC’s streaming platform, was the fastest‑growing video‑on‑demand service in the UK last year, yet even that is not enough when audiences live on TikTok, YouTube and Instagram. Bundred said the BBC has set itself a hard target: at least 80 percent of UK households using its services weekly, “four out of five,” a goal that simply cannot be met within the “walled garden” of BBC‑owned platforms.

For ORF’s Kollinger, the challenge is brutally operational. ORF remains “a fully fledged broadcaster” with four TV channels, 12 radio channels, streaming, news websites and multiple digital offerings, but “our organisations are not growing,” he said. Every new platform demands development, testing, audience engagement, analytics and the same finite pool of staff and budget must cover ever more ground. The past decade has been less about glamourous tech than about “keeping up with the pace of change” without burning out teams or hollowing out journalism.

#### **Partnering with the platforms that ate TV**

Both speakers agreed that you cannot ignore the platforms that have already “rewired” modern media. Bundred recounted the BBC’s deepening partnership with YouTube first leaked by the Financial Times under which the corporation will not only distribute more content on the platform but also create tailored formats and collaborate more closely with YouTube’s creator ecosystem. YouTube reached an estimated 52 million people in the UK in December 2025, compared to 51 million for the BBC; “YouTube says themselves they are the future of TV,” he noted.

The deal, as he presented it, is not a capitulation but a strategic bet. The BBC is already the number‑one global media news brand on Instagram and has a massive YouTube news channel with tens of millions of subscribers, but its reach on YouTube and TikTok overall still lags its ambitions “only three in five” users, compared to the four‑in‑five target. The partnership is meant to close that gap while preserving the BBC’s distinctive role: delivering “trusted impartial news, fearless, fair, independent news” in feeds dominated by content people like rather than content they need.

Yet the risks are obvious. As the moderator pressed, is this not making the BBC more dependent on a platform whose economic incentives and moderation policies it does not control? Bundred acknowledged the danger but argued that the alternative refusing to go where audiences are is worse. The trick, he suggested, is to make the relationship genuinely two‑way: “bringing creators onto our platforms themselves” while using YouTube’s reach to keep a publicly funded universal service visible to a generation that might otherwise never encounter it.

#### **Learning from creators without becoming them**

One of the more revealing exchanges centred on creators: the independent YouTubers and TikTokers who now command audiences bigger than many traditional channels. Bundred called them the “new indies,” successors to the independent production houses that transformed UK television in the 2000s. They are “doing some really innovative and exciting stuff” in storytelling, format and audience engagement, often without the weight of legacy systems.

For public broadcasters, the temptation is either to ignore these outsiders or to mimic them badly. Bundred argued for a different approach: partnership. Just as the BBC once partnered with independent producers, it now needs to treat creator businesses some with 10, 20, even 100 employees as peers, not curiosities. That means inviting them to co‑produce, commissioning series that bridge their audiences and the BBC’s, and offering them something they do not have: editorial standards, brand trust, and access to institutional resources.

But there is an ego check involved. Do twenty‑something creators see a “hundred‑year‑old brand like the BBC” as relevant? Part of the YouTube deal, Bundred suggested, is explicitly about signalling that the BBC wants to learn from them, not lecture them that it can be, in his phrase, “interesting to them” rather than simply important in its own mind.

Kollinger, for his part, saw the creator boom as another reminder that innovation is at least as much about culture and process as about tools. Big organisations, he said, are hard to move: “I can get more done at the BBC than I could at Google,” Bundred quipped, but both acknowledged the drag of hierarchy and compliance. That is precisely why the outside pressure of creators can be healthy: it forces broadcasters to rethink formats, workflows, and hiring, lest they lose a generation entirely.

#### **Human editors in an AI newsfeed**

Running underneath the discussion was a more recent shock to the system: AI. From automated transcription and translation to generative video and synthetic anchors, newsrooms are being flooded with tools that promise efficiency but threaten to flood the information space with cheap, plausible fakes.

Bundred saw an opportunity amid the noise. Recent BBC audience research, he said, shows that around 78 percent of people prefer to get their news from sources “where there’s been a human involved.” In a world of AI‑generated everything, the presence of a human editor, reporter or presenter becomes a differentiator, not an anachronism. Public broadcasters can position themselves as “human curators and editors” whose judgement and accountability stand between audiences and the algorithmic torrent.

That does not mean rejecting automation outright. Both speakers acknowledged that AI‑assisted workflows fact‑checking, subtitling, archive searches are already boosting productivity, much as legal‑tech tools like Protea are doing in courts. The question is not whether to use AI, but how to embed it in editorial processes that keep humans in charge of what is true, what leads the bulletin, and what corrections are issued when mistakes are made.

In that sense, public media’s challenge is the inverse of platforms’. Where platforms are under pressure to prove they are not letting machines run wild, broadcasters must prove they are using machines intelligently to serve human judgement and public value not to chase the same engagement spikes that drive the rest of the attention economy.

#### **Cutting through the algorithmic clutter**

Both Bundred and Kollinger returned, in different ways, to the same strategic dilemma: public broadcasters are caught between a licence‑fee or taxpayer‑funded mandate to inform everyone, and an information environment in which reach is increasingly mediated by companies whose primary obligations are to shareholders.

On one side, advertising markets are shifting: TV ad prices are falling while online ad prices grow, threatening the commercial competitors that shape the broader ecosystem and, indirectly, the political space in which public media operates. On the other, platforms like YouTube and TikTok are pushing long‑form video onto living‑room screens, not just phones, making them “more TV‑like competitors” that erode the natural monopoly of the broadcast spectrum.

The answers offered were necessarily partial. Continue to invest heavily in first‑party streaming like iPlayer and ORF’s online services, to retain some control over distribution. Expand on third‑party platforms to maintain relevance, but negotiate partnerships that acknowledge public media’s unique role rather than treating it as just another channel. Use AI to improve speed and verification, but double down on human‑edited, accountable journalism as a brand. Bring creators into the tent where possible, instead of dismissing them as unserious or treating them purely as competition.

What none of the panellists suggested was retreat. If anything, “breaking the broadcast mold” is about leaning into the messy, rewired reality and insisting that public value can be delivered within it not just on a cosy linear schedule. The risk, of course, is that in adapting to the logic of platforms and creators, broadcasters lose the very distinctiveness that justifies their funding. The task now is to do two things at once: master the tools and terrains of modern media, and remain the place where citizens can still find journalism that is accountable to them, not to an algorithm.
