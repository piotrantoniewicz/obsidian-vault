## Freelance Writing vs Ghostwriting

This point of view will change your life—literally.

When you hear the term “freelance writer,” what do you think of? Better yet, WHO do you think of? Chances are, the person that comes to mind is a young professional, working out of a coffee shop, sporadically bouncing between their phone (scrolling on Instagram) and their laptop, churning through another blog post assignment.

Without even talking to them, you probably assume a few things:

They have a small handful of clients (they’ve gotten from referrals).

They are earning enough to pay their bills, but not enough to be comfortable (by any means).

They are stressed out, overworked, and burned out from writing the same old blog articles over and over again.

Does this sound like someone you know?

(We know tons of people who fit this archetype.)

#### However… it’s not their fault.

There’s almost no education around how to be a successful, highly profitable freelancer.

Worse, the term “freelancer” has become so commoditized over the years, that when someone says they’re a “freelancer,” they might as well say they drive Uber. It just sounds “indie.”

And yet, millions and millions of writers (and designers, and project managers, and all sorts of other career professionals) willingly call themselves “freelancers.”

We’re here to tell you: this is a mistake.

Do not call yourself a Freelance Writer. It sends the signal you are “just like everyone else.”

#### Want to immediately unlock higher-tier opportunities? Call yourself a Ghostwriter.

The term “ghostwriter” is still relatively new.

But let’s talk about some of the differences between Freelance Writing & Ghostwriting.

#### Freelance Writing

More competition: Millions of people call themselves “freelance writers.” When you call yourself the same exact thing, you are making it HARDER for yourself to stand out.

“Hire me and I’ll write whatever you need”: Most people “fall into” freelance writing. They don’t really know who they’re servicing or why, so they just say, “Hire me to write!” And then they end up in very reactive situations where they’re constantly adjusting to the client’s wants and needs.

#### Ghostwriting

Less competition: Simply by calling yourself something other than a “freelance writer,” you immediately stand out. Yes, that’s literally how it works. Calling yourself a “ghostwriter” is the fastest way to start separating yourself from the competition.

Write for business leaders: When you’re a “ghostwriter,” it’s implied that you are not an average freelance writer. You work directly with business leaders, and as a result, unlock a different level of access to opportunities. And once you become friends with a business leader, they will inevitably send more specialized work your way.

“Hire me for 1 specific purpose”: Most ghostwriters specialize in 1 thing. As a result, they are seen as “premium,” which gives them much more pricing power. While freelance writers are stuck “reacting” to the client’s wants and needs, ghostwriters set the agenda and make recommendations to the client.

Gain pricing power and charge a premium ($$$$$): All of these things together are what allow ghostwriters to earn so much more money than freelance writers. They are seen as “different,” they work directly with decision-makers, and they have complete and total clarity over the 1 specific value they provide—allowing them to charge a premium for their work.

Now, here’s the central mistake you want to avoid…

#### Most people “fall into” Freelance Writing.

Here’s how it starts:

This first client usually leads to a second. A second leads to a third.

And then 1 or 2 years goes by, and all of a sudden, the person has found themselves trapped: writing “lots of different types of things” for “lots of different types of people.”

Sound familiar?

#### You aren’t just “ghostwriting.” You’re starting a Ghostwriting Business.

This is the big takeaway:

Mentally, you do not want to think of it as you’re “falling into” ghostwriting.

No—you’re starting a business. Even if it’s a 1-person business, you should think of it as “starting a business.” And when you start a business, you don’t just let customers walk through the door and say, “What should I sell here? You tell me!” No, when customers walk through the door, you already know what you sell. And then you sell it to them!

And we’re going to show you exactly what you should be selling—so you skyrocket your monthly earnings as a ghostwriter.

## Mistakes Ghostwriters Make

But first, we want to point out the 4 big mistakes new ghostwriters make.

(Steer clear of these!)

#### Mistake #1: Trying to ghostwrite “anything” for “anyone.”

In our Ghostwriting Intensive, we are going to help you niche down as a ghostwriter.

The reason is because one of the WORST things you can do for yourself & your “business” as a writer is to fall into the trap of writing “anything” for “anyone.” Again, this puts you in a very reactive state—constantly adjusting your work style, your schedule, your processes, and your expertise to fit the client’s wants and needs (and let us tell you: clients are fickle. They don’t know what they want!)

Instead, you want to solve…

1 specific problem

For 1 specific type of person

In 1 specific type of way

If this sounds “too niche,” guess what? That’s a signal you’re headed in the right direction!

The most successful ghostwriters specialize.

(And inevitably have more opportunity than they know what to do with.)

#### Mistake #2: Treating every client as a blank canvas.

Ghostwriting is mysterious.

Ghostwriting is sexy.

As a result, a lot of writers think they need to “start from scratch” every time they work with a client. They think they’re producing “art.” Newsflash: you’re not. Sure, you might be creating important work. But you are not writing The Next Great American Novel. You are providing a specialized service, to solve a specific problem, for a very specific type of person.

After ghostwriting for hundreds of people, we can tell you that the idea “everyone’s voice is unique & different” is also a myth.

People like to believe they are different. But the truth is, many of us are the same.

In fact, 99% of clients can be reverse-engineered into 5 different categories.

#### The 5 Archetypes Of Voices

The Storyteller: Here’s the difference. “In 1982.” Moment in time. That’s it. Or, 1 single sentence of description: “They had on a bright blue blazer.” Literally. You would be shocked how many clients will say to you, “This is so vivid!” when all you do is name a date or describe one object.

The Opinionater: This is someone who likes to share their opinions on things, usually newsy topics. It’s a sliding scale here. There are 2 types of Opinionaters: on one end of the spectrum, you have Hedgers—the person who hedges everything they say (I’ve had TONS of clients like this). They cannot say a strong opinion without acknowledging the other side. On the other end of the spectrum are the Betters—they will bet the house on any of their opinions. Your job is to figure out which one of these 2 the client is, and cater the writing accordingly. And here’s a very simple rule to live by: Hedgers, just acknowledge both sides of everything. Betters, pretend the other side doesn’t exist. That’s it.

The Fact Presenter: Above story, this client just wants stats. Trends. Studies. NYT, WSJ, HBR, and peer-reviewed journal sources. This is the single point of failure—the rest of the writing almost doesn’t matter. When in doubt here, more is better. If you can, put a stat or a trend or a study in every section. They’ll be happy.

The Frameworker: Listen close and this person always reveals themselves with a very subtle cue. Steps. Anytime someone puts something into steps, you should immediately realize who you’re working with. This person thinks carefully about how to move from point A to point B, which means what you’re writing better be organized into “steps” or “a guide.” If you don’t give them this format, they are going to be unhappy.

The F Bomb Dropper: Any client that says, “Nobody sounds like me” and drops tons of expletives is this archetype. And, newsflash, they aren’t as special as they think. You can create this voice literally by just adding 1 expletive per section, or even per paragraph (you’ll learn “how many” expletives they feel best represents their persona).

#### Mistake #3: Not practicing on yourself.

“How am I supposed to find clients when I can’t talk about my work?”

Simple: you use yourself as the case study.

Here’s the golden rule: Whatever “type” of ghostwriting you’re selling (whatever service you’re providing), you should have a version (or multiple!) of that same asset for yourself:

If you’re selling “ghostwriting someone’s Twitter account,” you should be actively writing & proving YOU can build an audience on Twitter.

If you’re selling “ghostwriting someone’s LinkedIn account,” you should be actively writing & proving YOU can build an audience on LinkedIn.

If you’re selling “ghostwritten educational email courses,” you should have YOUR OWN educational email course actively building your own email list.

If you’re selling “ghostwritten eBooks,” you should have YOUR OWN eBook (or eBooks) for sale.

Etc.

This is such a simple thing, but so many ghostwriters overthink it.

Getting clients isn’t about having a huge audience, fame, or a 10+ year track record.

It’s literally just about demonstrating you can create the thing you are saying you can create.

#### Mistake #4: Not starting with the Free Work Pitch.

Finally, so many aspiring ghostwriters underestimate the ROI of working for free.

Let us be clear:

We are NOT advocating for you to work for free forever.

We are NOT saying you shouldn’t care about earning money.

We are NOT saying you should run your business like a non-profit.

We are NOT saying you should let people take advantage of you.

In fact, the “Free Work Pitch” isn’t even really “free work.”

It’s literally just you taking the time to paint a vivid picture for your prospective client.

Take a look at these two different DM pitches, for example:

Pitch #1: “Want to buy this service?”

Hey John! I was just reaching out to see if you were looking for someone to build you an educational email course?

I create educational email courses for FinTech startups. They are 5 emails long, sent over 5 days, and are very effective at keeping readers engaged.

For this service, I charge $5,000.

Let me know!

Pitch #2: “Don’t you want to build your email list faster?”

Hey John! Quick question.

I was poking around your site, and then I got hit with your pop-up offering me a 10% discount on my first purchase.

And that got me thinking…

I bet the opt-in rate for that is probably pretty low. I’ve seen a lot more success (instead of doing a discount) offering people a free 5-day educational email course—because it demonstrates free value AND educates them before pitching them to buy something.

I feel like this is a giant missed opportunity for you—and I know exactly how to solve it.

No pressure, but if I put together an outline of what a really compelling 5-day educational email course looked like for your target customers, would you take a look?

#### Which one of these 2 DM pitches do you think is more likely to get someone to say, “Yes!”

The second one—without a doubt.

And here’s the thing: it will probably take you 20 or 30 minutes to create this free “outline” for anyone who says, “Sure.” But if you do a good job, the likelihood the person says, “You know what? You’re hired!” goes up dramatically.

So, are you “working for free?”

Not really.

All you’re doing is front-loading the work, and getting them excited to pay you.

Whereas trying to convert someone cold on your service is much, much harder.

## Most Lucrative Ghostwriting Services

OK, now remember: the trap is you trying to write “anything” for “anyone.”

Which means it’s worth understanding what the most lucrative ghostwriting services are—so you can narrow down what it is you provide.

This is usually where most ghostwriters start.

Again, they “fall into it.” Someone asks them to ghostwrite their Twitter, they say yes, and next thing they know, they’re a “Twitter ghostwriter.”

Pros List:

It’s fairly easy to do: The vast majority of clients have low expectations here. People aren’t editing Tweets like they’re editing a book. As a result, you can get by with above-average content (and even outsource a good chunk of it to ChatGPT!).

It’s easy to sell & there’s pent-up demand: Anytime something is “easy to sell,” it’s because people already KNOW they want it. In fact, there are many more people in the world actively looking for someone to ghostwrite for them on Twitter/LinkedIn than there are ghostwriters actively pitching themselves to prospective clients. Think about that.

Cons List:

Since it’s easy, there’s low pricing power: Most clients looking for ghostwriters know their expectations are low, which also means they aren’t really interested in hiring a “premium ghostwriter” for this service (unless you convince them otherwise!). As a result, most ghostwriters in this category tend to charge “less.”

#### Service #2: Educational Email Courses

In our opinion, this is the single greatest opportunity for ghostwriters today.

Most companies and creators live and die by the strength of their email list. Their email list is their “pool” of prospective customers, and in order to build that pool, they need to tell people: “Hey! Hop on our email list!” Which is what most companies and creators do.

However, the reason why their email lists grow so slowly is because they are literally asking the reader (prospective customer) for something—and offering nothing in return.

…which means less than 1% of people who visit their website type in their email.

The next step, and what most companies & creators do to build their email list, is they offer a “discount.”

This is their way of saying: “Fine. You give us your email, we’ll give you 10%-off.”

#### Service #3: eBooks & PDF Downloads

Ghostwriting eBooks and other “free downloads” is another highly lucrative form of ghostwriting.

But there’s an important nuance to understand here.

Both email courses & eBooks are digital assets. However, a client is much more likely to be picky and overly critical of an “eBook” than they are a 5-day educational email course. Why? Because when someone hears the word “book,” even if it’s a 10,000 word Ultimate Guide you’re giving away for free, something hiccups in their brain. They associate the word “book” with “This is my life’s work,” and all of a sudden, they’re obsessing over it.

(Trust us. We’ve seen this happen hundreds of times.)

So yes, while ghostwriting eBooks and other digital downloads can be lucrative, they usually take more work and invite more criticism and involvement from the client.

Which is why we recommend niching down on Educational Email Courses.

Similar end result, but a much lower-pressure vehicle.

## Least Profitable Ghostwriting Services

Now let’s go to the other end of the spectrum and talk about the LEAST profitable ghostwriting services.

#### Bad Service #1: Books

Building on the above: the idea of ghostwriting books (especially for CEOs, celebrities, and professional athletes/musicians) seems sexy… until you do it. And what you’ll discover (speaking from experience here) is that these projects are far from simple.

Here are some of the big problems with ghostwriting books:

Problem #1: Books take a very long time, and people’s expectations are very high. Especially when you’re dealing with someone who will probably only write one book in their life, they are going to obsess over every single little detail. This is their “magnum opus,” after all. (By the end of the project, divide the amount you got paid by the number of hours it took you and you’ll realize this project was a financial net-negative.)

Problem #2: There are a lot of cooks in the kitchen. Especially when you’re ghostwriting a book for someone noteworthy, who is also publishing through a traditional publishing house, you don’t just have to make the client happy. You have to make their agent happy. And their editor happy. And their editor’s boss at the publishing house happy. And their significant other (who will somehow end up part of the project) happy. And so on. All of these voices telling you which direction to take the book adds escalating layers of complexity.

Problem #3: There are two types of books that get ghostwritten. There are books in the $10,000 to $30,000 range, and there are books in the $100,000 - $500,000 range. There’s very little middle-ground. You’re either way undercharging relative to the workload, or you’re asking for a premium but you can only take on 1 project at a time (and there are much, much easier ways to earn six figures as a ghostwriter).

#### Bad Service #2: Speeches

Speeches are next, and they are very similar to books.

Very subjective.

Very time-consuming.

Very difficult to “templatize” for yourself.

As you start to land ghostwriting clients, it’s inevitable that one of your clients (usually someone who has worked with you for a while and trusts you) will ask you for help writing a speech. This is up to you. However, if you say “Yes,” we recommend going into it with the right expectations. See it as an opportunity to learn. See it as an opportunity to deepen the relationship. And don’t tell yourself, “This is going to be easy money!” because it won’t be. It’ll be a time suck, and by the end, you’ll want to never do it again.

So, in our opinion, just steer clear of speech writing from the beginning.

#### Bad Service #3: Fundraising Decks (And Anything Else)

Lastly, it’s very common for business leaders to call you up when they need help ghostwriting other assets.

This can include:

Landing Pages

Press Releases

Investor Memos

Fundraising Decks

Product Descriptions

Etc.

Here’s the short answer: “No—that’s outside my wheelhouse.”

Every time you say “Yes” to a different form of ghostwriting, you are creating 3 big problems for yourself:

Problem #1: You’re not specializing anymore. Over time, this will chip away at you, until suddenly you’ll find yourself speaking with new prospective clients saying: “I ghostwrite a lot of different types of things for lots of different types of people!” Whoops. You’re back at square one.

Problem #2: You’re reinventing the wheel. For every new service, you have to create a whole new process. A whole new approach. A whole new mental model for how you’re going to complete this project successfully. You literally have to re-think everything you do on a day-to-day basis, which is exhausting.

Problem #3: You’re increasing switching costs. In the short term, this doesn’t seem like a big deal. But compounded over several clients, and all of a sudden you have zero efficiency in your business anymore. One minute, you have to think about how to perform Service A, and the next, you have to completely pivot your thinking to perform Service B. Even with two different services, this destroys your ability to focus & create efficiencies. Imagine how much worse it gets when you offer 7 different things!

## 2 Ways To “Scale Yourself” As A Ghostwriter

Everyone always wants to know where the “scale” comes from, so we figured we’d answer this out the gate.

There are 2 very specific ways to “scale yourself” as a ghostwriter.

#### Horizontal: 1 specific asset for lots of different clients.

This means you do 1 thing (ghostwrite Educational Email Courses) and sell that same service, over and over again, to a long list of different clients in your niche & surrounding niches.

For example, you might ghostwrite an Educational Email Course for an education startup this month, a course creator next month, and a college the month after. Same service, but sold to 3 different “archetypes,” but all within the realm of “education.”

“Scale” then is just your ability to push & pull these 3 levers:

Sell more clients

At higher & higher price points

While getting the work done in less & less time

Usually these 3 levers get tackled one at a time (first you focus on getting more clients, second you raise your prices, and third you work on creating efficiencies so you don’t have to work as hard). But this is how you “scale.”

#### Vertical: Lots of different services for 1 client.

What will inevitably happen when you start ghostwriting is a client will love your work and then ask you to do “other things.”

We’ve already explained why this is a bad idea.

HOWEVER…

The 1 scenario where this becomes a positive is when 1 client has the potential to become your ONLY client. There’s an element of risk here, because if that 1 client goes away, well, so does your entire business (and you have to go back to knocking down doors). However, there’s also a lot of comfort and luxury in having 1 client that essentially pays for your entire life.

This is a subjective decision, but it’s a different example of what “scale” can look like.

(And sometimes, what ends up happening is the client ends up working with you in such a full-time capacity that they even offer you a full-time position. Again, this is up to you.)

## How To Think About Pricing Your Ghostwriting Service

“But how much should I charge?!”

Great question—let’s talk money.

There are 5 levels to increasing your income as a ghostwriter (and these 5 levels are generally true for just about any type of Solopreneur):

Level 1: Charging Per Word. We have already discussed why this is a huge mistake. Unfortunately, this is the “accepted” form of monetization for the vast majority of writing gigs—ranging from journalism to PR to general “freelance writing.” It doesn’t even matter if the client is paying you a “good per-word rate.” The fact your value is tied to the number of words you write means you inherently have very little long-term pricing power. You want to escape Level 1 as quickly as you can!

Level 2: Charging Per Hour. This is the next most commonly accepted form of monetization for writing gigs, and while it’s better than being paid per word, it’s still fundamentally broken. You do NOT want to be in the business of your value being tied to “effort.” Because then you aren’t incentivized to get better, or to create any efficiencies for yourself. (Why would you if it means you actually make less money?). So while charging hourly can be a simple way of being compensated in the beginning, the goal is to ultimately elevate yourself out of Level 2 and monetize in more lucrative ways.

Level 3: Charging Per Month. This is usually what’s known as being paid “on retainer.” Now, is this better than being paid per word? Yes. Is this better than being paid per hour? Usually. But there is a “right” way of being paid on retainer, and there is a “wrong” way of being paid on retainer. The wrong way is accepting a lump sum per month in exchange for you to do “anything” the client needs. This is a giant mistake—and almost always ends up earning you less than if you had just charged hourly. So if you are going to charge per monthly retainer, you want to make sure you are very specific about what that retainer includes. (Hint: If it’s a retainer that includes X amount of hours, then you aren’t really charging a retainer. All you’re doing is selling a month’s worth of your hours and being paid upfront.)

Level 4: Charging Per Asset. This is where you start to unlock real pricing power as a ghostwriter. You do not want to be compensated per word, per hour, or “per month.” You want to be compensated for your ability to successfully create an asset. Because then, the measure for success has nothing to do with a) your time, or b) your effort. The client shouldn’t care whether it takes you 1 hour or 10 hours, and the client also shouldn’t care whether it was challenging or easy for you. All they should care about is whether or not you successfully did what you said you were going to do. If it’s a one-off asset (like selling a 5-Day Educational Email Course), you would charge a fee for the creation of that asset. And if it’s a repeated asset (like 1 ghostwritten newsletter per month, or 20 ghostwritten Tweets per month), then you would charge a monthly retainer for those repeated assets. This is the correct, and most lucrative, way to monetize your skills as a ghostwriter.

Level 5: Charging Per Outcome. This is the ultimate goal, and where you end up making the most money as a ghostwriter (or Solopreneur of any kind). At a certain point, you can only create so many efficiencies for yourself, and you can only charge “so much” for any given asset. Which means the only way to increase your pricing power above Level 4 is to share in the OUTCOME you generate. There are two ways this typically plays out. Either you participate in some sort of profit-sharing model where you earn a percentage of the outcome, or you own some equity in the business you are helping to accelerate. It’s worth noting both of these vehicles are rare, and likely not something you’re going to unlock in the early stages of your career. Pro tip: early in, the equity or profit-sharing deals you want, you aren’t going to get (they’re too lucrative and you won’t have enough leverage). And the ones you can get, you aren’t going to want (because they aren’t lucrative enough and are too risky).

Our recommendation would be to accelerate yourself to Level 4 as quickly as possible, and then stay there and optimize for several years. Raise your prices. Build efficiencies. Get your footing and building a foundation of financial security for yourself, your life, and your family. And then, once you’ve pushed Level 4 as far as you can, start looking for specific, advantageous opportunities to reach up to Level 5, take some risk, and try to elevate yourself further by being rewarded for the outcome you successfully generate for yourself and whomever you partner with.

## How To Get Clients As A Ghostwriter

Ah, the best section of all: how to get clients!

This is the bulk of what we’re going to dig into, and do together, in our Ghostwriting Intensive.

The reason is because, when it comes to increasing your income as a writer & ghostwriter, most people think increased income is a byproduct of increasing your skill as a writer. And yes, that’s sort of true, but it’s not the whole truth. In fact, you could be a fairly mediocre writer. But if you understand how to find clients, package up a desirable service, and sell them on the benefits of that service, you will make more money than 99% of other writers.

Remember: as a ghostwriter, you are running a business.

And the “lever” for making that business grow is sales.

So, to get your wheels spinning, here’s a high-level overview of how to get clients as a ghostwriter.

#### There are really only 3 ways to get clients:

Word-Of-Mouth: Every client you work with, and make happy, is most likely going to refer you to someone else. Why? Because it makes them look good. When their friend (another successful entrepreneur) comes to them with a problem that you can solve, your client is going to look good introducing them to you. So keep that in mind for every person you work with.

Warm Lead Attraction: The second way to get clients is to build an audience of your own. The more you write about the service you provide, the more likely you are to attract people saying, “Hey! I need that!” This is very hard for people to wrap their heads around, but it’s very simple: write online about the benefits of the service you provide, and point them to an example to show how it works, and voila. You will be shocked at your ability to generate business.

Cold Lead Outreach: The third, and most important way, is to literally just DM and email as many prospective clients as you possibly can. There’s no other way around it. The more people you reach out to (who you believe fit the bill for who would need your service), the more likely you are to land clients. Period. Everyone wants to avoid this step, but it’s the key to growing your business. And, after a few months, what you’ll start to learn is exactly how many messages you need to send in order to land 1 new client. Which means, in any given month, if you want to land 3 clients, well then you know you need to reach out to 3x as many people.

The single most important skill you can build as a ghostwriter isn’t even the writing.

It’s sales.

## The 3 Skills You Need To Master To Make $10,000+ Per Month As A Ghostwriter

With all of this in mind, here is how you can very easily start generating $10,000 per month as a ghostwriter.

#### Step 1: Provide 1 specific service, for 1 specific person, in 1 specific industry.

Niche down.

From there, it’s your decision who you want to reach out to, in what industry.

#### Step 2: Build your own Educational Email Course to attract Warm Leads.

We show you exactly how to do this in our Ghostwriting Intensive.

For “proof” of the service you want to provide, it’s important for you to build an Educational Email Course for yourself. Because not only will this asset work for you (24 hours per day, 7 days per week, attracting potential clients), it will also be the core of your “portfolio.” When a client asks, “How do you know this is going to work?” You say: “Because I built one for myself, and I’m collecting more emails than I ever have before.”

Rule of thumb: whatever service you want to sell, be sure to build a version of it for yourself to prove it works.

#### Step 3: Master the art of Cold Outreach.

You need to bake into your weekly schedule a certain time of the week to do nothing but cold outreach.

Send people DMs.

Send people emails.

Ask people if they’ll hop on a call with you.

Offer free 30-minute “consulting” power-hours where you educate them on the benefits of Educational Email Courses.

Do whatever you have to do to get in front of prospective clients.

Why?

Because you literally only need 2 people to say yes, per month, to start making 6 figures.

Think about that!

#### The Path To $10,000+ Per Month:

It’s very simple.

First, get 2 new clients per month. You should be able to easily create an Educational Email Course, start to finish, for 2 people (or more) each month. That’s $5,000 each.

Upsell time.

You’re making a comfortable six figures per year as a ghostwriter.

Anyone can do this.

## Where You Go From Here: The 5 Ways To Scale From $10,000 to $100,000 Per Month

Now, where do you go from here?

We will get into “scaling” yourself as a ghostwriter, or turning your ghostwriting services into a full-blown agency, at the end of our Ghostwriting Intensive, but here’s a high-level overview so you get a sense of where you’re headed.

#### Here are the 5 paths you can take to scale yourself as a ghostwriter:

The High-End Ghostwriter: This is usually reserved for novelists & NYT best-selling writers who then start unlocking high-end ghostwriting deals. These are books that pay anywhere from $100k to $500k+ to ghostwrite. This “seems” the sexiest, but it’s actually one of the least lucrative, most time-intensive, and most difficult to “scale” of the 5 models.

The Guru Model: This is the hardest of them all (because it requires the most valuable, hardest thing to replicate: highly differentiated thinking), and is where people pay you $10k-$50k/month just for access to your expertise. But this “tier” rarely gets unlocked in the first decade of your career. (Usually takes a decade or more to unlock this level.)

The Lifestyle Boutique Model: If you’re going to go the agency route, this should be your goal: $500,000 - $1.5 million in revenue, 5 employees or less. Once you get above 5 employees and over $1.5 million in revenue, things start to get shaky—and you basically need to decide whether to “stay small” or risk it all and try to hit $10 million in revenue. All agencies between $2M and $10M “go to die.” So instead, we recommend staying small and obsessing over creating efficiencies and increasing your prices to make your agency as easy & lucrative as possible.

The Mass Market Agency Model: If you don’t want to stay small, then you need to swing for the fences. There’s very little in-between. If you go this route, you better believe you are going to need to get to $8-10M+ in revenue per year for it to be “worth it” for you. Otherwise, you’re going to be working insanely hard for the same or less money you could make just keeping your agency small.

The Education Model: Finally, you can take all your learnings from your agency and pivot to a totally different sort of business: online education. You can create a course, a suite of digital products, or a community teaching other people everything you just figured out for yourself. [Jack Butcher](https://visualizevalue.com/) did this for design with Visualize Value. We did this for writing with Ship 30 for 30. Etc.
