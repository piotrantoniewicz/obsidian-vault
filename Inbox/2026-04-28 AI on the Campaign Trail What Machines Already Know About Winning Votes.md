Power

28.04.2026

For people who work in politics every day, AI is no longer a futuristic add‑on. It has become part of the plumbing of campaigns. AI tools generate first drafts of speeches and emails, suggest segment‑specific messages, help plan field operations, and create quick‑turn images and videos tailored to social media formats.

In the United States, where federal regulation has lagged badly, the environment is almost completely open: national‑level rules for political use of AI are minimal, leaving campaigns free to experiment with synthetic imagery, voice cloning and automated persuasion. Constraints exist mainly at the state level, where some jurisdictions are beginning to regulate spoofed voices, fake endorsements and AI‑generated content in election communications.

Within this largely unregulated space, AI is present “everywhere from planning to research to communications and organising,” as one practitioner put it. It drafts fundraising copy, generates variations of ads for A/B testing, helps micro‑target messages, and even structures internal strategy documents and polling summaries. The popular image of AI as a one‑off gimmick, from a deepfake video or viral meme, misses the point: the more consequential impact lies in speeding up and scaling the everyday work of campaigns.

\-

The deepfake illusion, and the real power of humour

The most visible AI interventions in recent cycles have been spectacular but often crude deepfakes: synthetic images of Donald Trump being arrested; over‑dubbed videos of Joe Biden delivering lines he never said; awkward composites that break down under closer inspection, with extra fingers, strange lighting or unnatural details.

These incidents generate headlines and panic but, in practice, many voters recognise them as suspect. In one anecdote, a voter’s mother called in distress after seeing the AI‑generated Trump arrest images circulating on social media, only to be reassured that the pictures fell apart under scrutiny. The problem wasn’t that the tools were perfect, but that the information environment made verification feel urgent and difficult.

The session’s more provocative claim is that the “killer app” for AI in campaigns may not be lying, but laughing. Humour and political satire have always been powerful tools in democratic politics, and generative AI now makes it trivial to create parodies, memes and remix content that lampoon opponents in visually inventive ways.

In European contexts, where regulation and public norms make deception riskier, the case was made for progressive and pro‑European campaigns to embrace AI‑driven satire aggressively, precisely because far‑right actors are already exploiting the technology. Ethical lines can be drawn by ensuring the satire is clearly exaggerated or absurd enough that no reasonable viewer would mistake it for documentary fact. The aim is to harness AI for creativity and mobilisation, not for covert manipulation.

#### Persuasion at scale, and its ethical edge

Beneath the jokes lies a more unsettling frontier: AI‑assisted persuasion. Academic studies have begun to compare AI‑generated political messages with human‑crafted ones and find, at least in controlled settings, that machine‑authored content can be similarly persuasive and, in some designs, even slightly more effective.

For campaign professionals, that is both an opportunity and a warning. On the one hand, AI can help generate tailored messages for specific segments, rural young men, urban older women, swing‑state independents, far more quickly than human teams alone. On the other hand, the ability to produce fine‑grained, personalised narratives at scale risks crossing lines into manipulation that voters cannot easily detect or contest.

Practitioners are already using AI to build “campaigns in ten prompts,” as one exercise in the session showed: from defining a fictional party and its values, to outlining target voter groups, drafting core narratives, and generating social media content and imagery. In workshop settings, participants can watch as AI tools take a handful of descriptors and build out a plausible campaign plan in minutes.

The caveat is crucial: no serious campaign would or should simply deploy such an auto‑generated plan without human revision and contextual judgment. AI can propose slogans, identify generic voter concerns and mimic tone, but it lacks the local knowledge, emotional feel and ethical compass needed to navigate real‑world political conflicts. Used blindly, it risks producing tone‑deaf, inauthentic or outright offensive messaging.

#### Reluctant democrats, eager extremists

One persistent tension highlighted in the discussion is the asymmetry of adoption. Pro‑European, progressive and centrist actors often approach AI with caution, worrying about ethics, legality and public backlash. Far‑right and authoritarian‑leaning movements are under far less internal pressure to restrain themselves and have thrown volunteers, bots and resources into exploiting these tools as aggressively as possible.

In Germany, for example, some analyses suggest that the far‑right AfD has used generative tools to produce social media content at a volume comparable to all other major parties combined. That doesn’t automatically translate into persuasion, much of the content may be low‑quality or only resonate with existing supporters, but it does shape the visual and narrative environment in which more moderate actors must operate.

Within this landscape, some campaigners argue that progressive reluctance to use AI is no longer tenable. If one side fights with memes, synthetic speech and micro‑targeted narratives while the other confines itself to hand‑crafted leaflets and slow, human‑only messaging, the imbalance will deepen. The normative challenge is to use powerful tools without collapsing into the same race to the bottom on truthfulness.

#### Literacy as a democratic skill

The session connects back to a broader theme running through the summit: AI literacy as a democratic skill, not just a technical one. For campaign teams, that means everyone, from field organisers to communications staff, understanding both what AI can do and where it fails, so they can integrate it into workflows without surrendering strategic control.

Practically, that involves exercises like the “ten prompts” workshop, where participants see, in real time, how different models respond to political questions, where they hallucinate, and how sourcing and reasoning can be interrogated. Some newer models will show their chain‑of‑thought, list sources and allow users to challenge or refine outputs, making them more suitable for research and complex writing.

At the citizen level, AI literacy means teaching people to recognise synthetic media, to question viral content, and to understand that not all “personalised” messages are the product of human care or attention. It also means demystifying the tools: when voters see how easily a workshop of non‑experts can spin up a believable campaign brand in a half hour, they may be less overawed by polished digital propaganda.

The line between empowerment and exploitation will be fought over in code, law and culture. In jurisdictions where national regulation has failed, citizen‑initiated measures and state‑level experiments may become the main route to setting guardrails. But campaigns do not have the luxury of waiting for perfect rules before deciding how to act.

The strategic choice facing democratic actors is stark. They can either treat AI as a marginal curiosity, used occasionally for a clever meme or press release, or as a core part of their infrastructure, subject to internal norms, audits and training. The first path cedes the field to less scrupulous players. The second demands more work but offers a chance to shape how these tools are normalised in democratic practice.

What has AI done for campaigns so far? It has made them faster, more visual, more experimental and, potentially, more manipulative. What it does next will depend less on the technology itself than on the choices of those who wield it.
