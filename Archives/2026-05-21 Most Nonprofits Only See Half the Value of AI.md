I have a confession. I am a sucker for shiny tools.

A new AI app drops, someone mentions it in a group chat or a webinar, and I’m already signing up. Sometimes I even pay for it. I tell myself it’s research. And then, almost without fail, I find myself drifting back to the same two or three tools I use every day. The ones that actually do the work.

I mention this because I think it reveals something about how most of us, myself included, approach AI. We get drawn to the new thing. The next tool, the next feature, the next capability. And in that rush, we often miss the more important question: what kind of value are we actually chasing?

Because in my experience, AI offers nonprofits two fundamentally different kinds of value. And most organisations only ever see one of them.

### The first bracket: efficiency

This is where almost everyone starts, and for good reason. You’ve got tasks that eat hours every week. Drafting emails, summarising meetings, writing thank you letters, pulling together board reports. The kind of work that’s necessary but not exactly what gets people out of bed in the morning.

AI can compress a lot of that. Voice dictation alone is worth pausing on. We speak roughly three times faster than we type. If you’re spending two hours a day on emails and you switch to speaking them, you’ve just found time that didn’t exist before. Not theoretically. Actually.

There’s a useful way to think about which tasks to start with. Map them on two axes: how often you do them, and how much time each one takes. The sweet spot is in the corner where frequency and time are both high. Emails, meeting notes, reports, donor acknowledgements. These aren’t glamorous wins. They’re not going to make anyone’s conference highlight reel. But they are the tasks where the return is immediate and measurable.

I ran a webinar recently where I asked people to share in the chat which AI features they were already using in their existing tools. The answers were telling. Rewriting emails for different literacy levels. Converting paper records to digital databases. Using project management tools that auto-generate subtasks from a description. One person mentioned that the AI in their HR platform had become genuinely useful for surfacing policy information that nobody ever read in document form.

None of that is flashy. All of it is valuable. And most of it was happening inside tools these organisations were already paying for.

### The second bracket: imagination

Here’s where it gets interesting. And where most nonprofits stop short.

The efficiency bracket is about doing existing work faster. The imagination bracket is about doing work that was never possible before. Not because the ideas weren’t there, but because the cost of execution was prohibitive.

I was on a call with a funder recently, walking through some basics. Partway through, I pulled up their website and started rebuilding it. Not a mockup. The actual site, functional, in real time, while we were chatting. When I said we could go live with it in a couple of minutes, the silence on the other end told me everything.

That’s not an efficiency gain. That’s a category shift. The gap between having an idea and having a working version of it has collapsed. Time from ideation to something usable is now measured in minutes, not months.

I know a small nonprofit overseas that couldn’t find a CRM that suited their workflows. Nothing on the market quite fit the way they operated. So they built one. Using AI to write the code. When I spoke to them, they hadn’t even considered that they could potentially sell it to similar organisations in their sector as a revenue stream. The possibility hadn’t occurred to them, because six months earlier, building software wasn’t something organisations like theirs did.

This is what I mean by imagination. It’s not about doing the same things with less effort. It’s about looking at problems you’ve walked past for years, the ones that felt too expensive or too complex or too far outside your capabilities, and realising the barrier has dropped.

The fundraising series nobody had time to write. The onboarding guide that’s been on someone’s list for three years. The campaign landing page you couldn’t justify paying a developer to build. The data that could tell a powerful story if anyone had the hours to shape it.

### Why most organisations get stuck in bracket one

There’s a natural gravity toward efficiency. It’s concrete. It’s measurable. You can point to hours saved and feel confident something happened. Leadership can report on it. It fits neatly into existing ways of thinking about productivity.

The imagination bracket is harder to justify because it requires a different mindset entirely. You have to stop thinking about what you currently do and start thinking about what you could do. And that means stepping outside the constraints that have defined how your organisation operates, probably for years.

I see this all the time. Organisations that are genuinely under-resourced, running on tight budgets with stretched teams, and they approach AI as a way to make the current situation slightly less painful. Which it can do. But they never get to the point of asking the bigger question: **if we could do anything, what would we build?**

That question feels indulgent when you’re drowning in operational demands. It’s not. It might be the most strategic question your organisation can ask right now.

### The rabbit hole, though

I’d be dishonest if I didn’t flag the other side of this. Because the imagination bracket has its own trap, and I fall into it regularly.

A few weeks ago, some friends in a group chat were arguing about which coding tool was better. Harmless enough. Somehow this turned into me using AI image generation to create Mortal Kombat-style fighting game portraits of their faces. One of them told me later he’d spent most of that evening turning himself into a full character instead of doing the work he was supposed to do.

It’s genuinely easy to get pulled down the rabbit hole. Every new capability is interesting. Every new feature invites experimentation. And before you know it, you’ve spent three hours on something that was fun but produced nothing of value for your mission.

The discipline isn’t in avoiding the imagination bracket. It’s in being intentional about which problems you point it at.

### A practical way to hold both

One exercise I’ve been recommending to organisations lately is dead simple, but I think it captures the right way to approach this.

Create two documents. The first is an efficiency register. For each team member, list the repetitive tasks they do regularly and estimate how much time AI could save on each one. Emails, reports, meeting summaries, data entry, whatever it is. Be specific. This becomes your baseline for tracking actual time savings over the coming months.

The second document is an imagination list. And this one requires a different conversation entirely. Ask your team: if our organisation could do anything, with no constraints on time, budget, or technical capability, what would we build? What would we try? What problems have we walked past because solving them felt impossible?

Write everything down. Don’t filter it. Don’t assess feasibility yet. Just capture what people wish they could do.

Then look at that list through the lens of what’s now actually possible. You’ll be surprised how many of those items have moved from fantasy to feasible. Not all of them. But enough to change what your next twelve months look like.

### The leverage point

Most organisations approach AI as a productivity tool. Get things done faster, free up some hours, maybe reduce a bit of the operational burden. That’s real value, and I don’t want to diminish it. For teams that are genuinely stretched, even saving a few hours a week can be the difference between burnout and breathing room.

But if that’s where the conversation stops, you’re capturing half the value at best. The other half lives in the imagination bracket, in the work that becomes possible once your team starts seeing AI not just as a faster way to do what they’ve always done, but as a way to do what they never could.

The efficiency register will save you time. The imagination list might change what your organisation becomes.

Both documents take an hour. Start this week.
