Escaping the Click Trap: Can Democracy Survive the Attention Economy?

Advertising money has migrated from newsrooms to global platforms optimised for engagement, starving public‑interest journalism of the revenue it needs to hold power to account.

The core issue is not only fake news or bad content, but a broken market in which the attention economy rewards clickbait and algorithmic outrage rather than verification, context and civic value.

Saving democracy’s information ecosystem requires designing new platforms, incentives and ownership models that channel digital advertising back into quality media and use recommendation algorithms to maximise trust and diverse exposure, without returning to state dependency or charity as a permanent crutch.

\-

For a century, journalism rested on an uneasy but workable business model: a mix of subscriptions, cover prices, advertising and, in some cases, public subsidies. Advertising paid for investigative desks and foreign bureaus because readers’ attention was scarce and could be sold to marketers who needed newspapers and broadcasters as intermediaries.

The internet broke that bargain. In a little over 15 years, the bulk of global advertising revenue shifted from publishers who care about journalism to digital platforms whose business is extracting and monetising attention. Scaled to the global level, one snapshot shared in the session emphasised the imbalance: roughly 35 billion in advertising going into traditional print media versus around 860 billion flowing to Google, Facebook, TikTok and, increasingly, AI platforms that will sell ads on conversational interfaces.

That imbalance isn’t just about numbers on a chart. It has emptied newsrooms, forced surviving outlets to churn out cheap, traffic‑chasing content, and left fewer reporters with the time and resources to scrutinise governments, corporations and organised crime. In many localities, no reporters remain at all, creating “news deserts” where rumours and partisan propagandists fill the void.

#### The algorithmic turn: convenience without conscience

On the audience side, behaviour has shifted just as dramatically. Data from the Reuters Institute show that younger cohorts rarely visit news websites or apps directly; their news diet arrives primarily through social media feeds and mobile aggregators. For many under 35s, the brand carrying the story matters less than the platform delivering it, or disappears entirely behind screenshots and re‑uploads.

There are rational reasons for this. Feeds are convenient, device‑native and responsive to user preferences. The sheer volume of content makes some kind of algorithmic curation unavoidable; no human editor can hand‑pick headlines for billions of people in real time.

The problem is that the prevalent algorithms are built to optimise metrics that are orthogonal, if not hostile, to public interest journalism. Click‑through rates, watch time, comment activity and share velocity reward content that provokes quick reactions, reinforces pre‑existing beliefs and taps into outrage, curiosity or fear. A carefully reported piece that takes ten minutes to read and requires reflection struggles in that environment against a sensational meme or misleading headline that can be consumed and spread in seconds.

This is the extractive attention economy: a system in which platforms harvest behavioural data and attention from users, sell targeted access to advertisers, and pass only residual crumbs back to the organisations doing the expensive work of reporting. The costs, polarisation, declining trust, the spread of conspiracy thinking, are borne by societies, not by the actors profiting from engagement.

#### Broken incentives and adversarial relationships

The current relationship between platforms and publishers is structurally adversarial. If users spend time on social media, they are not on news websites; platforms want to keep them “in‑app” and show them platform‑managed ads, not send them away to publishers’ pages. Outbound links become a problem, not a feature.

Publishers, in turn, have every incentive to create headlines and thumbnails that maximise clicks from feeds, even at the expense of nuance or accuracy, because the click is the only event they can monetise under traditional models. If a user taps a misleading headline, realises the article does not match expectations, and bounces after a few seconds, the attention has still been “counted” as success. The system rewards bait, not depth.

Attempts to claw back power through regulation have had mixed results. The Canadian experiment with forcing platforms to pay for news led major players like Facebook and Google to simply remove news content for users in Canada. Surveys suggest many citizens still say they “get news from social media,” but in practice they are now more exposed to fake outlets and low‑quality information that mimic the branding of real newspapers without any of the editorial standards.

The session pointed to examples like the “Boston Times”, a non‑existent newspaper with a name designed to evoke the reputable Boston Globe, used as a front for Russian disinformation campaigns. On platforms where brand recognition is weak and fact‑checking is rare, such fakes can thrive, especially once real outlets have been de‑indexed or downgraded in response to regulatory fights.

#### A pro‑democracy attention economy: design principles

If the attention economy is not going away, the question becomes: what would a pro‑democracy version look like? The answers emerging from the discussion fall into a few design principles.

First, follow the money back to journalism. Instead of having platforms and publishers compete over the same eyeballs and ad budgets, new intermediaries could explicitly exist to feed money back into newsrooms. One example discussed is a “broadsheet social” platform structured to share a significant share of advertising revenue, on the order of 30 percent, with participating publishers, while hosting their content in full within the app.

This changes the calculus. If a Boston Globe article appears on such a platform with its branding intact, the user never leaves the app, but the publisher is still paid based on time spent reading, not just the fact of a click. The platform can then safely down‑rank obvious clickbait: when users quickly bounce from a misleading headline, the system learns to treat that as failure, not success, protecting readers from low‑quality content and publishers from incentives to mislead.

Second, make the algorithm serve civic value, not just personal preference. Recommendation systems could be tuned to understand topics and relationships between pieces, and then deliberately expose users to diverse but related content: someone reading about Greek archaeology might be shown a piece on Peruvian archaeology, or someone consuming local political news might be nudged toward national or international context.

The goal is not to impose editorial lines, but to break pure filter bubbles and the rabbit‑hole dynamics where engagement metrics push users toward ever more extreme or narrow content chains. Diversity of exposure, combined with clear branding and quality signals, can help rebuild a shared information space without returning to a monolithic mass‑media model.

Third, treat public-interest media as a market service, not a permanent charity case. Institutional funders, foundations and states will remain vital for kick‑starting new models and supporting media in fragile contexts. But over the long term, media independence depends on revenue flows that come from the public and the market, not from political goodwill or philanthropic priorities that can change with the economic or electoral wind.

A platform that can, for every 20,000 daily users, channel roughly one million euros per year into quality media, as one projection suggested, would offer a sustainable base for professional journalism without direct state control. That is the scale at which investigative units and foreign desks can be rebuilt.

#### Avoiding the twin traps: state capture and platform dependency

Any redesign of the media economy has to navigate between two dangers. On one side lies overt or covert state capture: when governments fund media directly, they often expect deference, even if it is never openly stated. Public broadcasters and subsidised newspapers can function well in strong rule‑of‑law contexts, but in many countries they become extensions of ruling parties.

On the other side lies total platform dependency: as news organisations discovered when Facebook changed its algorithm or when Canadian platforms dropped news entirely, outsourcing distribution and monetisation to private platforms leaves media at the mercy of opaque decisions made in Silicon Valley or Shenzhen.

The solution sketched in the session is a hybrid. Platforms designed from the outset as utilities for democracy, not as attention extractors, would be governed by transparent rules, audited recommendation systems and contractual revenue‑sharing arrangements with publishers. States and foundations could seed such initiatives but would not control day‑to‑day editorial decisions or algorithmic tuning; those would be guided by publicly debated principles around diversity, quality and civic relevance.

#### Rebuilding trust, one scroll at a time

Underneath the economic and technical proposals lies a normative ambition: to rebuild trust, not only in media but in public debate itself. When users know that platforms are working with, not against, quality publishers, and that algorithms are trying to maximise understanding rather than outrage, the experience of consuming news can change.

It will not mean the end of partisanship or conflict, nor should it. Democracies need argument. But they also need shared baselines of verified information and professional scrutiny of those in power. Those baselines cannot survive in an ecosystem where most of the money flows to systems that reward whatever keeps thumbs moving, regardless of its truth or public value.

A pro‑democracy attention economy would still be convenient, personalised and mobile‑first. The difference is that under the hood, its incentives would align with democratic needs: sustainable funding for watchdog journalism, exposure to diverse perspectives, and resistance to manipulation by hostile actors hiding behind fake mastheads.

The hardest work may not be coding algorithms or signing contracts, but persuading users to care. In a world habituated to “free” content, asking people to value, and indirectly pay for, public interest journalism via their attention and data is a political act. Yet without that act, media will continue to wither into either state‑dependent outlets or click‑chasing shells.

The session’s quiet provocation is that we should stop treating this as a marginal tech policy question and start seeing it as constitutional plumbing. Who funds journalism, and on what terms, will shape whether democracies have the information they need to remain democracies.
