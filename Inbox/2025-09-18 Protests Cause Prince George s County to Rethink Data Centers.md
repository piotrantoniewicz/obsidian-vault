A data center in Northern Virginia.

The old Landover Mall site has been empty for years, a tangible reminder of the economic struggles in Prince George’s County. But when residents learned of plans to build a data center where families once shopped for back-to-school clothes and holiday gifts, the vacant lot sparked community backlash. Dozens of protesters showed up at the county council’s data center task force meeting last week.

The protests got the attention of county lawmakers. The Landover project is one of three currently proposed for the county, which has few restrictions on data center development under laws intended to fast-track such projects. Now, county leaders are scrambling to reconsider their approach to using data centers for economic development. This week, they hit ‘pause’ on data center projects, promising to assess the impacts and get resident input.

**“** I’m not a proponent of data centers,” says At-Large Councilmember Jolene Ivey, who represented the Landover Mall site when laws were eased for data centers. “They made me uncomfortable from the beginning. However, I am a proponent of having a strong tax base and one that doesn’t keep burdening the homeowners and that we can pay for the things that we need so desperately in this county. We need more money for schools, better roads, more social services.”

Ivey was one of several council members fielding concerns from protesters last week. They demanded answers about air quality, traffic impacts, environment racism, and what they saw as a secretive approval process that seemed designed to exclude residents.

But Ivey says the process was transparent. In 2020, the county was desperately in search of new economic drivers. There was consensus among councilmembers and the county executive’s office about the need to compete for data center projects. Nearby jurisdictions, like Frederick County, offered a quicker, streamlined approval processes for developers; in Prince George’s county, the approval process could take over a year. In order to be competitive, county officials felt that they needed a process that would allow developers to put a shovel in the ground in 90 days.

In 2021, the council [voted](https://princegeorgescountymd.legistar.com/LegislationDetail.aspx?ID=4746434&GUID=36BE88B1-F04E-43A5-824E-E04F652285FF&Options=ID%7CText%7CAttachments%7CCode%7COther%7C&Search=data+centers) unanimously to allow data centers to be built ‘by right’ in most places in the county, meaning developers could go straight from the planning board to the permitting office without council approval or public hearings. Areas where data centers can be built under current rules encompass much of the county, including areas zoned for commercial offices, rural residential areas, and shopping centers. The laws also give [developers tax](https://princegeorgescountymd.legistar.com/LegislationDetail.aspx?ID=4629473&GUID=FB5A5F23-75E6-4439-973B-2583FB6A0B03&Options=ID%7cText%7cAttachments%7cCode%7cOther%7c&Search=cb-55-2020) breaks as an extra incentive.

This week, County Executive Aisha Braveboy issued an order halting the permitting process for data centers. And several councilmembers [introduced a resolution](https://princegeorgescountymd.legistar.com/LegislationDetail.aspx?ID=7658373&GUID=558AF087-8E60-47B4-B003-C94F4D67B68D&Options=&Search=) to halt the permitting process for six months to allow the council’s task force, formed earlier this year, to study the issue and make recommendations.

But it’s unclear whether the council can retroactively change the rules and halt the Landover project; the county may have fast-tracked itself into a corner. In Northern Virginia, which has the largest concentration of data centers in the world, proposed developments have sparked numerous [lawsuits](https://www.wusa9.com/article/news/local/data/residents-lose-appeal-against-devlin-park-data-center/65-3431b7c1-e570-4da5-847d-e07d2e8480e6),

Several newer council members say they were blindsided by the proposed projects and the protests; some even claim they weren’t aware of the laws the county currently has in place.

“Well, I think that first we have to get our newer members up to speed, because if they don’t know about what was done in 20 and 21, they don’t know what to do in 2025,” Councilmember Ivey told WAMU.

Residents in Landover, a majority-Black community, see the data center proposal as the latest example of pollution being dumped [in their neighborhood](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DOGq_eOjZjd/?igsh=dG5ubmhsdXRwbWU2) while wealthier areas remain untouched.

“I don’t agree with fast-tracking a data center because of all the infrastructure that is required,” says Councilmember Wala Blegay, who sits on the council’s data center task force.

At the moment, the county does not have any data centers, which despite the protests, some county leaders see as cause for concern. Despite being home to one of the nation’s largest Black middle-class suburban communities, the county has long struggled to generate revenue, relying heavily on property taxes. Now, in addition to ongoing federal layoffs, the county has lost the Washington Commanders stadium, Six Flags amusement park, and likely the FBI headquarters. And the U.S. Department of Agriculture recently announced it would close the Beltsville USDA Agricultural Research Center.

Across the Potomac River, Northern Virginia localities are using data center revenue to fully fund schools and reduce property taxes. Those economic benefits are clear to Prince George’s lawmakers, even as [those same communities](https://wamu.org/story/24/12/12/jlarc-report-virginia-data-center-growth-energy/) now grapple with similar community pushback, including lawsuits over environmental impacts and residents facing higher energy bills to power the massive servers.

Prince George’s Councilmember Blegay has promised community forums this fall to gather resident input before the task force makes its final recommendations, likely next year.
