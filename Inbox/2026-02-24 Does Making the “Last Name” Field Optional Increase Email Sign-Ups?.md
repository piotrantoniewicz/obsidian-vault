Experiment ID: #202511

### Luther Seminary

#### Experiment Summary

Ended On: 02/24/2026

Luther Seminary uses its Illustrated Book of Acts and Enter the Bible Exodus content offers as ongoing acquisition tools to introduce new audiences to its ministry and grow its email list. These resources consistently attract individuals interested in biblical scholarship and theological education and serve as an important entry point into long term engagement.

As the team reviewed the landing page experience for these offers, they identified a potential source of friction within the sign up form. The form required three fields to be completed: first name, last name, and email address. While collecting a full name supports personalization and database management, required fields can introduce subtle resistance, especially for individuals who are early in their relationship with the organization.

This raised an important motivational question. When someone is downloading free theological content, are they willing to provide only the minimum required information, and does requiring a last name create enough additional effort or privacy concern to reduce completion rates?

Rather than removing the last name field entirely, Luther Seminary tested making the field optional while still requiring first name and email address. This approach preserved the opportunity to collect full names from those willing to provide them while lowering the minimum amount of required information needed to complete the form.

Two split URL tests were conducted, one for Illustrated Book of Acts and one for Enter the Bible Exodus. In each case, the page experience was identical except for the required fields on the form.

Control

- First name required
- Last name required
- Email required

Treatment

- First name required
- Last name optional
- Email required

Because both offers tested the same structural change with similar acquisition audiences, the results were aggregated to evaluate the overall impact of reducing required form fields across content campaigns.

The primary metric was email acquisition measured by form completion rate. Secondary metrics included overall transactions, one time gifts, recurring gifts, and revenue to ensure that any improvement in top of funnel performance did not negatively affect downstream donor value.

#### Research Question

We believe that making the “Last Name” field optional on content offer sign-up forms will increase email acquisition because reducing required effort lowers friction at the moment of decision and makes the form feel simpler and less intrusive to complete.

#### Key Learnings

This test reached 77.2% statistical confidence, which does not meet the 95% level of confidence threshold for a valid learning. Therefore, we cannot conclude that making the “Last Name” field optional had a meaningful impact on email acquisition. The observed differences between control and treatment may be due to normal variation rather than a true behavioral effect. **Email Acquisition Results** Control

- 119 conversions out of 432 visitors
- 27.5% conversion rate
Treatment
- 104 conversions out of 434 visitors
- 24.0% conversion rate
While there is a numerical difference between the two versions, the confidence level does not allow us to determine whether requiring or not requiring last name meaningfully influences form completion. At this stage, the appropriate conclusion is that this test did not produce a statistically valid learning and should be retested before making a permanent change. **Transaction Results** Transaction data was also reviewed as a secondary metric. Control
- 11 transactions out of 432 visitors
- 2.55% transaction rate
Treatment
- 16 transactions out of 434 visitors
- 3.69% transaction rate
Transaction volume is low, and no statistical conclusion can be drawn from these numbers. These results should not guide decision making without additional testing. What This Means: We cannot confirm whether reducing the required fields meaningfully affects donor behavior on these pages. It is possible that requiring a last name does not create significant friction for this audience, or that any true impact is small and requires more volume to detect. What we do know is that this test did not reach a valid learning and should be repeated to reach a more confident conclusion. Next Steps: This experiment should be retested on pages with higher traffic volume so that stronger statistical confidence can be achieved. Aggregating additional acquisition pages into a single test may also help generate clearer results. Only after reaching sufficient confidence can we determine whether making the last name field optional meaningfully impacts email acquisition or downstream giving behavior.
