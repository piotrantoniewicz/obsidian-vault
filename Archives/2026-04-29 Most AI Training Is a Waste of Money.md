I spend a lot of time talking to organisations about AI training. The conversations tend to follow a familiar pattern. They’ve sent a few people to a conference, maybe done a maturity assessment, and come away with a clear realisation: they’re behind.

So they do what most organisations do next. They start planning training.

It’s a sensible instinct. And it’s exactly where most organisations go wrong.

Not because training isn’t important. It is. But because training without strategy is just an event. People show up, they nod along, they learn a few things. Three weeks later, nothing has changed. Leadership wonders why adoption isn’t sticking. And somebody starts googling the next workshop.

I’ve watched this pattern play out for three years now. The organisations that get value from training aren’t the ones that book the best facilitator. They’re the ones that do the unglamorous work beforehand — the work that makes training land.

### Training is step four, not step one

In the last two pieces, I’ve talked about [policy](https://kylebehrend.substack.com/p/what-happens-when-a-donor-asks-if) and [assessment](https://kylebehrend.substack.com/p/you-cant-train-a-room-you-dont-understand). There’s a reason they come first, and it’s not because I like a tidy sequence. It’s because training doesn’t work without them.

Think about what happens when you skip straight to a workshop. You’ve got a room full of people at wildly different capability levels. Some have been experimenting with AI for months. Some tried ChatGPT once and found it underwhelming. Some are quietly terrified. And you’re pitching to the middle, hoping something sticks for enough of them to call it a success.

Meanwhile, you might not have agreed on which tools people should actually be using. You haven’t defined what good looks like at different levels. You haven’t established a shared vocabulary — and this one matters more than people think. I see it constantly: someone calls something a “project,” someone else calls it a “folder,” people throw around the word “agents” when they mean something else entirely. If your team can’t talk about AI in a common language, they can’t learn about it together in any meaningful way.

And underneath all of that, there may not even be a policy that tells people what’s okay to do and what isn’t. Which means even if someone walks out of training excited and ready to experiment, they hit a wall of uncertainty the moment they sit back down at their desk.

Policy. Assessment. Tools. Then training. Not because it’s a rigid framework, but because each step creates the conditions for the next one to actually work.

### The problem with generic

Here’s the thing I keep coming back to: information is not the problem anymore. If someone wants to learn how to prompt, they can go to YouTube. There are courses, tutorials, webinars, entire academies dedicated to teaching AI fundamentals. The information is abundant and mostly free. You can even access a whole range of these on the [AI Impact Hub](https://aiimpacthub.com/).

So when an organisation spends money bringing someone in for training, the question should be: what are we getting that people can’t get on their own?

The answer has to be specificity. Training built around the actual tools your organisation uses, the actual tasks your people do, the actual capability levels in the room. Not “here’s how to write a good prompt” in the abstract, but “here’s how to use this tool for the specific challenges your fundraising team faces every week.”

That’s a fundamentally different offering. And it requires all the groundwork I just described — knowing your tools, knowing your people, having a strategy that connects the training to something larger.

Without that, you’re paying for something people could get for free. And in the nonprofit sector, that’s not a small thing.

### Shared language is the unlock most people miss

I want to stay on this point about vocabulary for a moment, because I think it’s one of the most underrated barriers to AI adoption.

It can be genuinely intimidating for people to talk about AI if they don’t feel they have the correct understanding or the correct terminology. Especially in a room where a few colleagues are clearly further ahead. People go quiet. They stop asking questions. They nod along in meetings and then go back to doing things the way they’ve always done them.

One of the main goals of training is to enhance the communication around AI within your organisation. You can’t really do much unless you’re talking about it. Unless people share their concerns, their experiments, the things that worked and the things that didn’t. And that only happens when everyone feels they have the language to participate.

This is partly a training problem and partly a policy problem. Your policy can include a glossary of terms, clear definitions that everyone works from. Your training can reinforce those definitions and make sure that from this point forward, when someone says “agent” or “hallucination” or “prompt,” everyone in the room means the same thing.

It sounds basic. It’s not. It’s the difference between a team that can learn together and one where a few people race ahead while the rest quietly disengage.

### The case for training your own people

On a recent call, I did something I probably shouldn’t admit to doing regularly: I talked myself out of potential work.

The person I was speaking with was looking for external training and consulting. And look, consultants absolutely have value, especially around strategy, frameworks, the architecture of how all these pieces fit together. But when it comes to the ongoing work of training and supporting a team through AI adoption, I keep coming back to the same conclusion: you’ll often get more value from someone internal.

The people who understand your organisation best are the ones inside it. They know the workflows, the politics, the unspoken constraints, the actual problems that need solving. If you’ve got someone who’s already showing initiative, someone with a natural pull towards this technology and a willingness to help others, that person is worth more to your AI adoption than any external consultant doing a fly-in engagement.

I know an organisation overseas, a small nonprofit, where the founder used AI to compress his own work down to three days a week. He now spends the other two days supporting his staff and even training similar organisations in what he’s learned. That’s not a consultant’s framework being applied from outside. That’s someone who figured out what works in their context and is sharing it from lived experience.

So before you default to looking outside, consider what’s already in front of you. Could someone’s role be pivoted, even part-time, into an AI implementation or support function? Could your existing champions be given formal permission and time to do what they’re probably already doing informally?

There’s a real problem right now with hiring for AI roles externally. Everyone can claim expertise. It’s genuinely difficult to verify. But the person who’s been quietly experimenting in your team for the past six months? You already know what they can do.

### Make it an experience, not a lecture

One idea I keep coming back to is the internal hackathon.

Not the intimidating, technical kind. Something simpler. Take your AI policy, the document everyone was supposed to read but probably didn’t, and give teams an hour to transform it into something else using AI. A song. An infographic. A slide deck. An interactive website. A video.

What happens in that exercise is remarkable. First, people actually engage with the policy, because they have to understand it to transform it. Second, they’re building practical AI skills, prompting, iterating, working with different tools, without it feeling like a training session. Third, and this is the part I think matters most, you ask them to proof the output at the end. Because AI hallucinates. The content might be wrong. And that proofing habit, the instinct to check, to verify, not to trust the output blindly, is arguably the single most important skill in AI literacy.

Three outcomes from one exercise: policy comprehension, practical AI skills, and the critical thinking habit that makes all of it safe. And you’ve surfaced capability levels organically, because you can see where people are by watching what they build, without anyone having to self-report on a survey.

You don’t need an external facilitator for this. You need a few internal champions who understand the basics, a clear brief, and permission to have fun with it. If you’ve got more advanced people on the team, let them take the outputs further. Show what’s possible at the next level. Let the room see the gap between where they are and where they could be, and let that gap be exciting rather than threatening.

### The leverage point

Here’s where this connects to the bigger picture.

Training is where most organisations put their energy when they decide to “do something about AI.” It’s visible, it’s actionable, it feels like progress. And it can be, if it’s embedded in something larger.

But training in isolation is an intervention at the wrong point in the system. It’s like trying to improve water quality by cleaning the water at the tap, when the problem is upstream. The upstream work is policy, assessment, tool decisions, shared language. Get those right, and training becomes transformative. Skip them, and training becomes an event people attended once.

The leverage point isn’t the training itself. It’s the strategy that makes training specific, relevant, and connected to everything else your organisation is trying to do with AI. Without that, you’re paying for something people could find on YouTube. With it, you’re building genuine capability that compounds over time.

If you’re planning AI training for your team, pause before you book anything. Ask yourself: Do we have a policy? Do we know where people are? Have we decided on tools? Do we have a shared vocabulary? If the answer to any of those is no, that’s where your time and money should go first.

The training will be better for it. And so will everything that comes after.
