Manipulation is moving from the visual user interface such as hidden buttons or misleading navigation flows into the conversational layer itself, where AI utilizes language, tone, and simulated empathy to capture and retain our attention.

Global awareness of these threats is growing, and regulators and courts around the world are beginning to hold tech giants accountable. Recent developments in Los Angeles, New Mexico, Brazil, and India perfectly illustrate how different nations and legal systems are tackling the challenge of dark patterns and addictive algorithms.

#### Firm Judicial Action in the United States: Los Angeles and New Mexico

In the US, the debate over addictive interface design has aggressively shifted to the courtrooms, evidenced by landmark rulings.

In Los Angeles, a jury delivered a precedent-setting verdict against Meta (owner of Instagram) and Google (owner of YouTube), ordering them to pay $6 million in damages to a 20-year-old woman. The plaintiff demonstrated that her childhood addiction to these platforms led to severe depression, body dysmorphic disorder, and self-harm. The court recognized that these corporations had effectively built “addiction machines,” utilizing deliberate design choices like infinite scroll to maximize the time young users spent on their screens.

#### Preventative Law and the Role of AI in Brazil

Brazil has adopted a strictly preventative legislative approach. Sparked by public outrage including a viral video exposing the sexualization of children online the government enacted the Digital Statute of Children and Adolescents.

This law directly targets dark patterns by prohibiting platforms from using addictive features such as infinite scroll and the automatic playback of videos for minors. Furthermore, users under the age of 16 are required to have their social media accounts linked to a legal guardian to ensure proper supervision. Digital services must also implement effective age verification mechanisms. In response to this new legal framework, Google announced it will use artificial intelligence to estimate whether a user is a minor or an adult in Brazil, allowing the system to automatically block inappropriate content.

#### Recommendations and “Soft Law” in India

India, on the other hand, illustrates an earlier stage in the fight against deceptive design. The Indian Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) recently issued an advisory for e-commerce platforms to identify and eliminate dark patterns.

The CCPA identified 13 specific types of dark patterns, such as “basket sneaking” (secretly adding items to a cart), “false urgency” (pretending an item is about to sell out), and “confirm shaming” (guilting a user into agreeing). The authority directed platforms to conduct self-audits and submit declarations of compliance. However, legal experts point out that India’s current framework is heavily reliant on “soft law”. Unlike the massive financial penalties seen in the US or strict regulations in the EU, the CCPA’s advisory lacks enforceable penalties, mechanisms for formal investigation, and mandatory rulemaking, meaning compliance relies largely on voluntary corporate behavior.

#### Artificial Intelligence as a New Vector of Manipulation

Our work is focused on supporting product teams, designers, and product managers - especially in small and medium-sized technology companies that are rapidly adopting AI without fully developed responsible AI frameworks.

BehaviorAI is designed to bring structure to a fragmented landscape of knowledge by combining legal insights, behavioral science, and practical design guidance. Rather than promoting ready-made solutions to be copied blindly, we aim to foster critical thinking and informed decision-making. Our goal is to equip teams with tools, patterns, and materials that not only improve product design but also strengthen communication with stakeholders and help justify responsible choices.

Through an accessible knowledge library, curated design patterns we seek to raise awareness of cognitive biases and their influence on AI systems, while building stronger competencies in human-centered AI. Ultimately, BehaviorAI is about improving the quality of both products and the decision-making processes behind them - so that technology serves users in a transparent, trustworthy, and ethical way.
