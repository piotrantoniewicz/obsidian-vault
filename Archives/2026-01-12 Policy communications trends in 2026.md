Happy new year!

As is tradition for this time of year, I want to share the policy communications trends I’ll be watching most closely in 2026. They’re not predictions in the sense of “this is coming”. They’re already visible if you work closely enough to the day-to-day reality of institutional, advocacy and public affairs communications.

As ever, I’m interested in hearing what others are seeing from their corner of the EU communications world. Feel free to message me and let me know what you think!

---

## 1\. Social media distribution now follows interest, not authority

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!f8Z8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbee135c8-99d0-4a9a-becc-a0a5f29051ab_500x641.jpeg)

Me, looking out over the “For You” page to see what nonsense memes it will serve me today.

Social platforms increasingly function as “discovery engines”, surfacing content based on inferred interests rather than who users follow. [TikTok’s For You Page uses an interest graph](https://beatstorapon.com/blog/tiktok-algorithm-the-ultimate-guide/) to prioritise videos users may enjoy regardless of follower count, while Meta has built similar [recommendation logic into Facebook’s and Instagram’s feeds](http://socialmediaexaminer.com/facebook-content-strategy-2025-whats-actually-working-right-now).

For EU policy communicators, this marks a structural shift. Visibility is now shaped less by institutional authority and more by how content is classified, tested and recommended mid-scroll. Policy messages are now often encountered incidentally, outside a trusted institutional context.

That raises the bar on clarity and relevance. Understanding distribution logic is no longer a purely technical concern — it directly affects whether policy communication is seen at all. We now need to ask some practical questions earlier in the process - how will this be discovered, by whom and in what context?

## 2\. Audience first, channels second

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KD0M!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9192d628-f37f-4bcc-8051-295fb19b60dc_910x686.webp)

Analysing audiences sometimes requires getting your hands dirty.

Audience profiling in EU policy communications is becoming more of a priority. Broad categories still exist, but they are increasingly complemented by a clearer understanding of who actually needs to be reached, why and how.

This year, conversations with [Rowan Emslie](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rowanemslie/) sharpened my thinking on the specific, unusual and entirely knowable nature of EU policy audiences. They are discoverable, surveyable and focus-groupable. We can learn about their goals, constraints and preferred ways of consuming information. At the same time, working with [Aitza Burgess-Reynolds](https://www.linkedin.com/in/aitzab/) highlighted more practical ways of uncovering this insight — through qualitative signals, behavioural patterns and subtle testing rather than rigid segmentation alone.

For several years, I have been tracking [platform fragmentation as a big feature](https://tommoylan.substack.com/p/communication-trends-2024) of the information environment. As this accelerates, audience understanding is less of an input and more the understanding that everything else is built on. It is also what enables meaningful community building — moving beyond one-off messaging towards sustained, relevant engagement with the same small but critical groups over time.

## 3\. Credibility over certainty

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Zccc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff1a58767-bdf3-49de-8609-1dfef56bcfb7_1370x1094.png)

“Yes, I am absolutely certain I can paint a cat.”

In a low-trust information environment, projecting certainty at all costs is increasingly counterproductive. Policy audiences — particularly in Brussels — are highly attuned to spin, oversimplification and overly controlled narratives.

In 2025, [criticism around the Commission’s presentation of the post-2027 EU budget](https://www.api-ipa.org/2025/07/18/api-ipa-deplores-breaches-of-press-agreement-during-presentation-of-eu-budget/) was a good example of this in action. The backlash focused less on the proposal itself than on perceptions of opacity and the sense that uncertainty was being smoothed over rather than acknowledged under Ursula von der Leyen’s leadership.

What audiences appeared to be asking for was not less ambition, but more candour and clearer signals. Naming what is known, what is contested and what remains unresolved increasingly functions as a credibility signal in EU policy communications.

## 4\. Not just the message, but the messenger

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5-UR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4d98c100-ca1e-45d4-a489-1a4c2b4439d6_1176x1536.png)

Pictured: An untrustworthy messenger delivering an untrustworthy message.

Another shift is the renewed importance of who explains policy. In previous years, I’ve written about the [growing role of micro- and nano-influencers in policy communications](https://tommoylan.substack.com/p/policy-communication-trends-2025). What’s emerging now is a natural extension of that logic.

Audiences increasingly trust recognisable humans more than anonymous institutional voices, particularly on complex or contentious files. Officials, experts, analysts and negotiators bring a form of legitimacy that logos alone no longer guarantee.

In practice, this often shows up as structured employee advocacy: not everyone saying everything, but the right people speaking clearly and consistently. A noticeable middle ground is emerging between grey institutional comms and influencer theatrics — expert-led, personality-aware communication that feels human without being performative.

## 5\. Video earns attention through explanation

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3XWD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e884b90-e5ee-461a-a0c2-36242c0bc85a_647x335.webp)

Me, preparing to produce a 9-second video on global trade for LinkedIn.

Video isn’t new. What’s changing is how platforms — especially LinkedIn — evaluate it. [Recent LinkedIn guidance and algorithm updates](https://www.linkedin.com/business/marketing/blog/marketing-collective/2025-b2b-marketing-benchmar-the-video-influence-effect-starts-with-trust) increasingly prioritise video relevance, watch time and meaningful engagement over volume.

In policy communications, simply “showing up” with video no longer works. Visual content earns attention when it helps audiences understand something faster or more clearly than text — whether that’s a short explainer or a focused piece of context.

This pushes us to think like editors, not producers. The useful question is no longer whether we want a video, but how video adds explanatory value.

## 6\. Physical presence as policy signal

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yrOu!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05cdd401-3572-4cc3-8a4d-bf71badc6a17_2801x4001.jpeg)

Just a few professional connections coming together around a shared interest.

Alongside continued digital evolution, the value of being in the room is returning. This is particularly true for the EU policy bubble, where audiences are limited and highly networked.

In-person events, workshops and roundtables are not mass-reach tools. They function as credibility signals and content opportunities in themselves. In a small, repetitive ecosystem, showing up consistently and creating tangible moments communicates seriousness and anchors digital visibility with narratives that would otherwise feel abstract.

In many ways, the [work of the agency BUMP](https://www.bump.nu/) — combining high-quality production with a physical presence at Place Luxembourg — represents a natural conclusion of this trend.

## 7\. Traditional media as a credibility layer

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fRIY!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F45d0bcb5-0e07-43c3-8f77-6814592e2dfa_3840x2160.webp)

Pictured: A journalist continuing to dodge your laser-focused pitches.

This isn’t new, but it continues to harden. Traditional media remains important in policy communications, but it no longer functions as a primary reach channel. For many organisations, getting policy issues onto traditional media agendas requires a disproportionate amount of time and effort — while policy content is increasingly encountered first via social platforms, video or direct sharing.

In this context, its role has become more specific. The [2025 Reuters Institute Digital News Report](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/digital-news-report/2025/dnr-executive-summary) shows continued growth in social and video news consumption alongside declining engagement with TV, print and news websites. However, trusted outlets remain a reference point (with geographic variability in trust levels) where audiences go to verify information, anchoring narratives that then circulate elsewhere.

The implication is not that media relations matters less, but that it needs to be approached differently. It becomes a question of judgement and timing rather than volume. Selective engagement and clear briefing matter more than persistent pitching.

## 8\. Human judgement, AI support

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Mq1q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F61c185aa-208f-49ef-8684-8ff69209e27e_512x351.jpeg)

At least until human judgement is uploaded to the mainframe I suppose.

AI is increasingly embedded in many [policy communications workflows](https://tommoylan.substack.com/p/ai-public-affairs-and-the-eu-bubble) — monitoring, synthesis, drafting support, formatting and analysis. That’s a trend already underway and that will continue to gather steam.

What’s interesting is not the technology itself, but the [audience reaction](https://www.pewresearch.org/global/2025/10/15/how-people-around-the-world-view-ai/). People want to know when AI is being used — [2025 Ipsos AI Monitor](https://www.agencyreporter.com/trust-and-transparency-emerge-key-cornerstones-of-ai-usage-among-indians-ipsos-ai-monitor-2025/) shows that people expect disclosure of AI use and some agencies have included disclosure [as part of their AI policies](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/moylato_moylan-communications-ai-policy-activity-7379072923833376768-hd2E?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAAsbWDMBaAmKLDJ72_eQMPvde7neyhPOkEs). Trust remains higher when communication feels clearly edited, curated and accountable. Human judgement is becoming visible again, precisely because AI is everywhere.

The organisations that benefit most from AI are the ones that use it to support human work —and can convincingly demonstrate the humanity of their work.
