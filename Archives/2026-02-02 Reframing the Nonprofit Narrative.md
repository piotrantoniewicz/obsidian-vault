## Reframing the Nonprofit Narrative: Telling the Real Story of Impact

  

There is a story the nonprofit sector has been telling for a long time.

It shows up in fundraising appeals and board conversations. In job descriptions and performance metrics. In the quiet ways we talk about ourselves when we think no one is listening.

It is a story built on scarcity. On sacrifice. On the belief that impact must come at the expense of sustainability, and that caring deeply means carrying everything quietly.

During our recent LinkedIn Live, *Reframing the Nonprofit Narrative: Telling the Real Story of Impact*, we invited nonprofit leaders into a different kind of conversation. One that moved beyond outward messaging and into the stories shaping how we lead, work, and value ourselves inside the sector.

The conversation featured **Alistair Scott**, Co-Founder of Diaspora Legacy Collective, **Julie Despaties**, Founder and Executive Director of Interwoven Connections and **Bri Gerzevske**, Director of Managed Contributor Care at The Lutheran Church and Founder of Oddball Leadership each bringing lived experience navigating the tension between mission, sustainability, and care. The session was facilitated by **Nicole Jamrozinski** and made possible with support from our session sponsor **[Story Gardening by Rachel Zant](https://www.storygardening.com/quiz)**, whose work is rooted in strengthening nonprofit leaders and organizations through values-aligned support.

Early in the conversation, Nicole named the quiet tension many nonprofit professionals carry.  
“We talk a lot about changing the narrative externally,” she said. “But we don’t always stop to ask how those same stories are shaping how we lead and work internally.”

What followed was not a marketing exercise. It was an honest reckoning with the narratives we’ve inherited, internalized, and now have the opportunity to change.

## Why This Conversation Matters Right Now

Nonprofit professionals are exhausted, but they are also paying attention.

Funding is shifting. Expectations are rising. Many organizations are being asked to do more with fewer people, fewer dollars, and less margin for rest or reflection. At the same time, public narratives still frame nonprofits as passion projects rather than essential economic and social infrastructure.

That disconnect takes a toll.

As the speakers reflected during the conversation, the danger is not just how the sector is perceived externally, but how those perceptions quietly shape internal culture. When scarcity becomes normalized and overwork is praised, burnout starts to feel inevitable instead of preventable.

Reframing the nonprofit narrative is not about spin. It is about truth. About naming the real value nonprofits create and the real cost of pretending that impact should come without care for the people doing the work.

Alistair spoke to how deeply embedded these assumptions can be. *“Scarcity becomes the water we’re swimming in,” he said. “And when that happens, we stop questioning systems that were never designed to support sustainability in the first place.”*

## Core Themes from the Conversation

### Scarcity Is a Story, Not a Fact

Early in the conversation, Alistair named how scarcity thinking shapes both nonprofit strategy and identity. *“We’ve inherited a system that tells us there’s never enough,” he said. “Enough money. Enough time. Enough capacity. And over time, we start believing that’s just the way it has to be.”*

One of the clearest throughlines was how deeply scarcity thinking runs in nonprofit culture. Scarcity of funding. Scarcity of time. Scarcity of capacity.

But the panel challenged the idea that scarcity should define how nonprofits operate internally. While resource constraints are real, treating them as a permanent identity shapes decisions in subtle but damaging ways. It can justify underinvestment in staff, normalize constant urgency, and limit imagination.

Reframing this narrative does not mean ignoring financial realities. It means refusing to let scarcity dictate values.

### Impact Is More Than Outputs and Optics

Julie challenged the idea that impact can be reduced to numbers alone. *“So much of what actually creates change is relational,” she shared. “It’s trust. It’s consistency. It’s people staying long enough to build something meaningful.”*

Another theme that surfaced was the narrow way impact is often measured and communicated. Dollars raised. Programs delivered. Numbers that look clean in a report but rarely capture the full picture.

The panelists spoke about impact as something relational and long-term. Trust built with communities. Consistency of staff. The quiet stability that allows programs to work over time.

When nonprofits are forced to perform impact rather than live it, storytelling becomes disconnected from reality. Reframing the narrative means aligning what we measure, what we fund, and what we celebrate with what actually sustains change.

### Burnout Is a Symptom, Not a Personal Failure

Bri named burnout as a structural issue rather than an individual one. *“When burnout shows up everywhere, that’s not a personal resilience problem,” she said. “That’s a system telling us something isn’t working.”*

Burnout came up not as an individual weakness, but as a systemic signal.

When exhaustion is widespread, it points to narratives that reward martyrdom and silence. Several speakers named how nonprofit culture can unintentionally shame people for setting boundaries or naming capacity limits.

Reframing the narrative means telling a different story about commitment. One where sustainability is not a luxury, and where caring for people is understood as a prerequisite for impact, not a distraction from it.

### Language Shapes Power Inside Organizations

Language came up repeatedly as a quiet but powerful force. Julie pointed to how certain phrases can unintentionally shut down honesty. *“When we praise being ‘scrappy’ or ‘doing more with less’ without question,” she said, “we’re often masking inequity and normalizing harm.”*

The conversation also surfaced how language reinforces power dynamics. Words like “lean,” “scrappy,” or “mission-first” are often used with good intentions, but they can obscure inequity and discourage dissent.

Who feels safe to speak honestly about workload? Who gets labeled as “difficult” for asking questions? Whose labor is assumed rather than acknowledged?

Changing the narrative requires paying attention to these everyday moments. It means noticing not just what is said publicly, but what is rewarded internally.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

This conversation was not about replacing one narrative with another or finding the perfect language.

It was about changing what feels normal in everyday nonprofit work. The small, often invisible choices that reinforce inherited stories or quietly begin to shift them.

Here are five grounded ways nonprofit professionals can start reframing the narrative in their own roles, teams, and organizations.

**1\. Treat capacity as shared context, not personal failure.**Instead of absorbing strain quietly, nonprofit professionals can practice naming capacity as neutral information. When limits are spoken aloud, they become shared context for better decision-making rather than private sources of guilt or overextension.

As Bri shared during the conversation, *“Naming limits isn’t a lack of commitment. It’s an act of care. For yourself, for your team, and for the people you serve.”*

**2\. Bring sustainability into everyday success conversations.**Rather than reserving sustainability for strategic plans or annual reviews, weave it into how success is discussed week to week. Ask not only what was accomplished, but what it cost people to get there and whether the pace is repeatable.

**3\. Interrupt scarcity language in real time.**Pay attention to the words that shape expectations. When phrases like “scrappy” or “doing more with less” surface, pause to ask what they are normalizing. Shifting language can open space for more honest conversations about resources, priorities, and care.

**4\. Respond to burnout with curiosity, not correction.**When burnout shows up, treat it as information about systems and structures rather than an individual problem to fix. Reframing the narrative means looking upstream at roles, workloads, and assumptions before asking people to adjust themselves.

**5\. Practice internal honesty before external storytelling.**Narrative change starts inside organizations. Naming what is strained alongside what is working builds trust and alignment. When nonprofits tell fuller truths internally, their external stories become more credible, human, and sustainable.

Reframing the nonprofit narrative is less about saying the right thing and more about changing what we reward, what we normalize, and what we allow ourselves to name.

## Moving the Needle on the Nonprofit Narrative

The most hopeful takeaway from this conversation was that narratives are not fixed. They are shaped, reinforced, and changed by people.

Every time a nonprofit leader chooses transparency over performative optimism, the story shifts.

Alistair captured this shift simply: *“We don’t need better stories. We need truer ones. Ones that reflect how change actually happens and what it really costs.”* Every time an organization invests in its people without apology, the story shifts. Every time impact is defined in human terms instead of transactional ones, the story shifts.

This work is not fast, and it is not flashy. But it is possible.

Reframing the nonprofit narrative is not about lowering ambition. It is about aligning ambition with humanity, so the work can last.
