Rebooting the Party: How AI and Regulation Are Reprogramming Political Organisations

The modern political party is an outdated vessel currently struggling to navigate a digital information environment that rewards speed, decentralization, and "agentic" automation.

Joshua Carrington, Chief Technical Officer of the UK Labour Party, describes the "innovation dilemma" of 100-year-old institutions that remain paralyzed by "three layers of sign-off" while their opponents operate at the speed of viral algorithms. The core problem is that democratic parties have largely failed to use technology for the "good," leaving the field open to fascist and extremist actors who feel no obligation to stick to established rules or norms.

#### **The CTO as insurgent inside the party machine**

﻿

Nowhere is the tension between political culture and technological capacity clearer than in the UK Labour Party where Carrington leads a 30‑person product team, roughly 10 percent of the party’s entire staff. In Westminster’s first‑past‑the‑post system, he argues, this is not a luxury but a weapon: campaign effects can move up to three percentage points of the vote, and in a winner‑takes‑all system “even if you just win by one vote, that’s all that matters.”

Carrington’s team behaves more like a startup than a party back office. They build most of the campaign‑facing stack in‑house, buying infrastructure only when they cannot reasonably own the reliability risk. A flagship example is “Persuasion Pathways,” a system designed to cultivate “hero voters”, undecided citizens who are hard to move but decisive in close races. It began as a paper process: volunteers filled out physical surveys, wrote customised letters and followed up with personal calls. Once the method worked, developers turned it into an AI‑enabled app: volunteers photographed the paper survey, data was extracted automatically, the system generated and queued personalised letters, then prompted follow‑up calls keyed to the issues each voter cared about.

Voters exposed to that repeated, tailored contact were “about 50 percent more likely to vote Labour” than comparable undecideds approached only once at the door, according to Carrington. The point, though, is not the app; it is the experimental culture behind it. A developer was paired with an organiser, tested a low‑tech version, then iterated rapidly based on what actually shifted behaviour. It is precisely this culture that many continental parties lack: as Carrington noted acidly, in a lot of organisations “we’ve not even hit the observe stage” of the OODA loop, parties are using Netflix and AI in their private lives without connecting any of that to their own political work.

#### **German parties: rich in rhetoric, poor in infrastructure**

﻿

If Labour represents the frontier of party‑internal tech, Jochen König, a veteran campaign adviser and founder of Cosmonauts & Kings, offered the counter‑example: Germany’s parties, whose “state is not good” from a technological standpoint. He is unsparing about the democratic camp’s record: “We didn’t do a very good job in the last eight, nine years to be on the democratic side and using technology for the good.” The problem, he insists, is not a lack of talent in party HQs, but “political culture and the way we recruit leaders.”

In his telling, the most dangerous bottleneck is not budget but bureaucracy: even if a German party had Labour‑style resources and a 30‑person tech team, “and you have everything in place for the messaging,” the message still has to be signed off “by three people on paper”, and dies there. Tech, in other words, is colliding with organisational forms optimised for caution, not speed. That friction becomes existential in an environment where hostile actors are moving fast. König warns that while democratic parties talk about AI ethics, “bad actors” on the far right and abroad are already mastering AI bot swarms, coordinated disinformation, and ever more human‑like fake accounts.

He points to Doublespeed, a start‑up backed by Andreessen Horowitz, as emblematic: “AI bot swarms… mimicking humans… an orchestra to interact with each other, change narratives… it’s not even possible for humans anymore to identify.” Against that, he argues, democrats need “something like an old counter task force”, a permanent, well‑funded structure that fights disinformation between elections, not just during them. To build it, parties will have to abandon the comfort of off‑the‑shelf tools and move toward owning infrastructure: “In the future I don’t need ready software products… I need to buy an infrastructure on which I can build my own solutions,” especially as open‑source models make bespoke “agentic platforms” cheaper and faster to construct.

#### **AI: overrated fantasies, underrated leverage**

﻿

Across the session, there was striking convergence on what AI can and cannot do for parties. Overrated, in the view of civic‑tech pioneer Alicia Combaz of Make.org (speaking elsewhere in the summit but squarely aligned with this debate), are proposals to replace citizens with “AI citizens” or artificial personas in democratic processes, “completely overrated” because democracy “is about alterity… about creating dialogue” among real people. Also overrated, argued another speaker, is the fetish of hyper‑granular micro‑targeting: parties are trapped in a loop of repeatedly appealing to the same narrow segments, when the real task is to “broaden the coalition” rather than slicing it ever thinner.

Underrated, by contrast, is AI’s capacity to massively scale content production and meaningful dialogue if, and this is the condition, parties know what they stand for and whom they want to reach. In a media environment where even politically sophisticated people now consume much of their information via YouTube Shorts and similar feeds, parties need tools that can “vastly scale up content creation efforts” while still being grounded in real values and voices. As one investor put it, time and skills are the scarcest campaign resources, and “AI can help with that” long before you move to fully automated messaging.

The deeper consensus is harsh medicine for consultancy‑driven “political tech.” AI can amplify a compelling, authentic political offer, can help volunteers reach more people in more relevant ways, and can protect democratic actors against coordinated manipulation. But it cannot invent purpose, courage or organisational trust. Unless parties can, as Geismeyer put it, “lose that scarcity mindset” and treat ethical, transparent use of AI as part of their brand, they will remain vulnerable to being technologically outplayed by those with far fewer scruples.
