### TEAMS! Newsletter | Sustainability Edition | May 1, 2026

The nonprofit sector has long operated under a "scarcity mindset," in which doing more with less is treated as a badge of honor. As we approach the halfway point of 2026, the tension between our traditional operating models and the accelerating pace of technological change, specifically Generative AI and Agentic AI, has reached a breaking point. We are being asked to adapt faster than our organizations were built to move.

You likely feel this tension every day: the pressure to innovate while maintaining service delivery, the desire to adopt new tools without compromising your mission, and the struggle to keep a weary workforce motivated. Sustainability is no longer just about your endowment or donor list; it is about how effectively you can redesign work to align your team’s unique strengths with the capabilities of machine intelligence.

### Executive Summary:

To achieve mission sustainability, nonprofit leaders must move beyond viewing AI as a mere "efficiency" tool and instead use it as a catalyst for **Work Redesign**. This requires shifting from hierarchical command-and-control to **Strengths-Based Teams**, ensuring that technology supports human motivations for autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

### Key Takeaways

- ***Quality Over Quicker:***AI’s greatest value is not the time saved but the elevation of work quality and human interaction.
- ***The "Midcycle Dip":*** Expect a slump in adoption after the initial excitement; targeted leadership intervention is most critical here.
- ***Experience is the New “Tech-Savvy”:*** Senior employees are often more effective at adopting AI because they possess the "contextual fluency" junior staff lack.
- ***Motivation is Not a Carrot:*** Engagement stems from satisfying psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness, not from external rewards or pressure.
- ***Culture as a Context-Adaptive System:*** Effective cultures aren't static; they are "context-adaptive" and evolve to meet the specific needs of the mission and the organization.

**Elevating the "Human" in the Machine:** In a study of GenAI deployment at Novo Nordisk, a surprising trend emerged: while employees saved over two hours a week, they valued the **quality** of their work, content creation, ideation, and summarization three times more than the time saved.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** Imagine a grant writer using AI to draft a repetitive data report. The sustainability win isn't just finishing the report faster; it's that the writer now has the "cognitive bandwidth" to spend more time in the community, building the high-touch relationships that drive the mission.

**Navigating the Adoption Marathon:** Scaling AI isn't "plug-and-play"; it's a change-management marathon. Most organizations hit a "midcycle dip" when initial wins are exhausted and frustration mounts. Without leadership intervention, this dip can lead to the tool being abandoned altogether.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** After an initial flurry of use of a new donor-management AI tool, your team finds it "hallucinates" or gives generic responses. Instead of letting the project die, a sustainable leader steps in to implement "micro-communications", short, tip-focused newsletters, and identifies "champions" to share role-relevant use cases.

**Leveraging "Contextual Fluency":** There is a myth that "digital natives" will lead the AI charge. In reality, senior employees often use GenAI more effectively because their deep understanding of workflows helps them identify high-impact opportunities and vet AI outputs for accuracy.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** A program director with 20 years of experience knows exactly where project plans usually fail. They can "prompt" the AI with the nuance a junior staffer lacks. Sustainability comes from pairing these "contextual experts" with younger staff to co-create AI playbooks.

**Moving Beyond the Carrot and Stick:** Sustainable leadership requires understanding **Self-Determination Theory**. External rewards (carrots) or monitoring (sticks) often undermine the intrinsic motivation essential to social impact work.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** Instead of "policing" how many hours staff spend in the office, focus on building autonomy (letting them decide how they work), **competence** (nurturing the skills to use new tools), and **relatedness** (supporting collaboration over internal competition). This builds a resilient workforce that stays for the mission, not just the paycheck.

**Culture as Your "North Star":** Culture is not just "how we do things around here"; it plays a role in enabling change. Effective cultures are "context-adaptive," meaning they adapt based on whether the organization needs to reimagine its activities or rethink its direction.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** If your organization is shifting from direct service to advocacy, your culture must evolve from "precision and compliance" to "innovation and creative abrasion." Using **Strengths-Based Teams** ensures that everyone knows why they are in the room, reducing the fear and conflict that often derail transformation.

### 5 Practical Organizational Sustainability Steps:

- ***Host a "Quality Audit":*** Ask your team, "If we could automate 20% of your administrative tasks, what high-value, human-centered work would you do with that time?"
- ***Appoint "Context Champions":*** Identify two senior staff members who are skeptical or curious about AI and ask them to lead a "peer demo" on how they would use AI to solve a specific workflow bottleneck.
- ***Revisit Your Rewards:*** Review your performance metrics. Are you inadvertently rewarding "busy work" (extrinsic) over "mission impact" (intrinsic)?
- ***Socialize Strengths:*** Have your team add their "top 3 strengths" to their email signatures to foster curiosity and collaboration over hierarchy.
- ***Address "AI Shaming":*** Openly discuss the ethical concerns surrounding GenAI. Create a safe space for staff to voice fears about "cheating" or "privacy" to prevent cultural resistance from going underground, and take their concerns seriously.

### The Close

The future of the social sector will be shaped by our ability to lead people through a world of constant change. By redesigning work to leverage human strengths and using technology to amplify, not replace, our mission, we don't just survive; we thrive. This is the path to long-term organizational sustainability.
