### The One Word That Changed How I Understood AI

The shift happened when Claude released Skills.

Before that, all we had were “Prompts.” And I had an association that Prompts had to be these in-depth, master-level mini books that handle complex problems from start to finish. So that’s what I kept trying to build.

I tried to have one big prompt for my entire productivity system—the same window holding context for all of my projects, helping me plan my days, coordinating my weekly reviews, and helping me with time blocking—all things I was doing manually.

And it sucked.

Then I saw people building Skills—and the word itself changed something for me. A Skill isn’t a master prompt that handles a full, complex job. It’s a small, specific cartridge. One input, one set of instructions, one output. You slot it into a bigger system with a handful of other skills, each completing one step.

That’s when it clicked. I had been treating AI as one big assistant with a big job to do. Instead, I needed to create an army of 1000+ AI assistants, each handling one hyperspecific, small job as part of a bigger process.

And here’s the thing—I already think about business this way. I’ve given keynotes on systems thinking & bottleneck analysis. I consider myself good at this kind of work. I just never applied that same lens to AI.

And the moment I did:

- My output became reliably useful
- It became obvious where to use AI
- My prompts & skills became much easier to write
- And I could do a better job consulting my team on how to use it as well

## AI Systems Thinking 101

Let’s start with a high-level crash course in Systems Thinking.

Rather than spending 25+ hours working through the resources I’ll reference below, I want to hit on the most actionable parts of each & show you how they connect. These are the systems thinking “context” documents I’ve been feeding Claude, so it’s worth understanding them before we use them.

And those 3 resources are:

- Thinking in Systems by Donella Meadows
- How to Train Employees by Alex Hormozi
- The Goal by Eliyahu Goldratt

Let’s breeze through each.

### Thinking in Systems by Donella Meadows

[This book](https://amzn.to/4sfg0hX) is a beast of a read: 500+ pages of academic systems theory. And in full transparency, I have not slogged through the entire thing.

But for integrating AI into your workflow, you really only need one concept: Nested Systems.

Here’s how I would explain it to a 5th grader:

Think about making a peanut butter and jelly sandwich. You might think that’s one thing — “make a sandwich.” But it’s actually a bunch of smaller things inside of it:

First you have to get all the stuff out. Then you have to open the bread. Then you spread the peanut butter. Then you spread the jelly. Then you put it together. Then you clean up.

And some of those things have even smaller things inside them. “Spread the peanut butter” is actually: open the jar, grab a knife, scoop some out, spread it on the bread, don’t rip the bread, etc.

And if you really wanted to get specific, you could explain how to open the jar: grab the jar with your right hand, grab the top with your left hand, twist it to the left, etc.

That’s systems thinking.

Every big thing is actually a bunch of medium things, and every medium thing is actually a bunch of small things. You just keep zooming in until you get to something so small you can just… do it.

So the first step to fully integrate AI into your workflows is to go from trying to give it big, vague systems to giving it small, specific subsystems.

Which presents the next question—how small do we need to break things down?

That brings us to the second resource.

### How to Train Employees by Alex Hormozi

[(](https://amzn.to/4sfg0hX)*[Here’s the full video where I pulled ideas from)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aUnorufFIq8)*

If we can break systems down from one big thing to a bunch of smaller things, where do we stop? How small do we need to go?

**It depends on the skill of the person executing the instruction.**

Going back to our peanut butter and jelly example, let’s say we were working with a Master Peanut Butter & Jelly Sandwich maker. To that person, we could give vague instructions — “make me a good PB&J.” We don’t need to explain a single substep. They’ve got it.

Now, if instead we were teaching our 1st grade child to make their peanut butter and jelly for the first time, we might need to slow things way down.

- Open the jar.
- Use the back of the spoon, not the front.
- Wipe it down before you switch from peanut butter to jelly.

And if we were training a ROBOT with zero context on how the world works, how physics works, etc., we might need even more granular instructions for every inch they move along the way.

**Now, this is true for sandwich making, and it’s also true in your business.**

The more skilled the employee, the less granular the instructions can be.

The less skilled the employee, the more granular the instructions need to be

If you have an experienced head of marketing, you can say “go drive more leads.” Vague instructions with tons of subjective decisions, but you can trust their judgement to get it done.

If instead you have a beginner media buyer who has never worked with your business before, you will need to lay out more steps if you want the same outcome.

Now, how does this relate to AI?

Working with AI and working with employees works *exactly the same way*.

Most people treat AI like a senior employee. They give broad, vague instructions and expect it to fill in the gaps. But AI does not shine here. It will pretend it knows how to make decisions, but they are rarely the right ones.

Instead, you should treat AI like a smart but inexperienced new hire. When unsure, err on the side of breaking things down into more steps & giving more specific instructions. The more steps you have, and the more specific your instructions, the better your results will be.

And rather than think you have “one” employee to execute the entire process, you want to build an army of employees that execute each single step of the process, with detailed instructions to execute that step.

This is true both when you’re designing your systems (breaking things down into more subsystems) AND when you’re describing how to execute one of those subsystems (breaking one subsystem down into steps).

So keep this in mind:

**If you’re getting bad outputs from AI, you’re giving instructions that are too vague for its skill level.**

And this brings us to the last resource: which step should I train AI to complete for me?

### The Goal by Eli Goldratt

*([This is my favorite book on the Theory of Constraints](https://amzn.to/4sfg0hX). I highly recommend listening to the audiobook version. And for a full breakdown of how I think about this in a business context, [you can watch this Keynote](https://m.youtube.com/watch?v=tHuJYsBZzcc).)*

Donella Meadows teaches us how to see the structure. Alex Hormozi teaches us how granular to get. And Goldratt teaches us where to focus first.

In The Goal, Goldratt tells the story of a factory manager trying to figure out why his plant can’t hit its targets — even though every individual department looks like it’s working hard.

The answer: it doesn’t matter how fast every station runs if one station is slow. The whole system backs up behind it.

That’s his Theory of Constraints, and it boils down to this:

- Every system is a sequence.
- The output of one step becomes the input of the next.
- Your system is only as fast as its slowest step — the bottleneck.
- All time spent improving anything other than the bottleneck is wasted time, because it just leads to more backup at the bottleneck.

And so to find the best place to integrate AI, you need to two things:

- You need the step to have clear inputs, instructions, and outputs
- Integrating AI into that step needs to save you more time than integrating it elsewhere

In other words, when you’re looking at where to integrate AI, don’t ask “where can I use AI?” Ask “what’s the slowest step in my system that has a clear input and output?”

That’s where AI goes first.

### How These Pieces Connect

To summarize AI Systems Thinking 101:

1. Every big system is made of smaller systems
2. Break the system down to match the level of skill of whoever is executing it—the less skilled the executor, the more granular the instructions
3. Systems are linear, with the output of one system becoming the input to the next
4. Your system is only as fast as its slowest step
5. The best place to use AI is on the most time-intensive step that has a clear input & output

And once you see your workflows through this lens, integrating AI stops being some big, gnarly project and becomes a simple, repeatable process:

1. Identify your system and break it down into subsystems
2. Get granular enough that each step has a clear input, instruction, and output
3. Then, once you’ve mapped this out, find the bottleneck and streamline it with AI

Let’s walk through a live example from within my business to see it in practice.

## The 2 Prompts To Integrate AI Into Your Workflow

This 2-prompt flow follows the exact process we just did manually:

1. Prompt 1 helps you define all of the elements of your system, tuned up or down based on how granular you want to get
2. Prompt 2 takes the decomposed system and helps you identify which step is best suited for AI integration

Let’s walk through them together.

### Prompt 1: Turn Any Process Into A System

A few notes on how to get the most out of this prompt:

- As with any business prompt, this will perform better if your LLM is fully up to date on the context of your business. Stay tuned for next week’s AI Operator where I’ll show you how to create a full suite of business context files.
- This is not a 1-shot prompt. This requires back and forth in an interview format to achieve the clearest system steps and best results
- You can use this both for identifying where both employees AND AI can take over / where you can delegate
```markup
# Systems Decomposition Engine

You are an elite systems thinker and decomposition specialist. Your job is to take any business objective, project, or process and break it down into a clear, executable hierarchy of subsystems and steps — co-built with the user through an interview-style process.

You think using three mental models:

**Donella Meadows (Thinking in Systems)** — Every big thing is actually a bunch of medium things, and every medium thing is actually a bunch of small things. You just keep zooming in until you get to something so small you can just do it. Your first job is always to see the structure: what are the 3-7 medium things inside this big thing? You never jump from one big thing to a 47-step checklist. You map the subsystems first.

**Alex Hormozi (Operationalizing Behavior)** — How far you zoom in depends on how skilled the person executing is. A senior employee can take vague instructions and fill in the gaps. A brand new hire needs every substep spelled out. If you’re getting bad results, you’re giving instructions that are too vague. When in doubt, break it down further. The key test: could someone watching a video of the executor verify whether the step was done correctly? If not, the step isn’t specific enough.

**Eli Goldratt (The Goal)** — Every system is a sequence. The output of one step becomes the input of the next. Your system is only as fast as its slowest step. When you lay out the subsystems, order them by dependency — what must happen before what — because nothing downstream can move until the upstream step is done.

-----

## YOUR PROCESS

### Phase 1: Clarify Before You Build (Interview Style)

When the user gives you an objective, ask clarifying questions **one at a time.** Do not stack multiple questions. Wait for each answer before asking the next.

Your questions should come from this list (skip any the user has already answered):

1. **What is the system?** — What exactly are we breaking down? Get specific enough that you could explain it back in one sentence.
1. **What does done look like?** — What is the end state or output when this system is working (for a process) or complete (for a project)?
1. **What already exists?** — Is any part of this already built, or are we starting from zero? Are there tools, platforms, or constraints already in play?

Move through these quickly. The goal is to gather what you need in as few turns as possible, not to interrogate. If the user gives you a brain dump that answers multiple questions at once, don’t circle back — take it and move on.

### Phase 2: Lock the Subsystems First

Once you have clarity, produce **only the subsystems** — names and one-line purposes. No steps yet. Use 3-7 subsystems and err on the side of fewer to start.

**SYSTEM:** [Name of the system]
**PURPOSE:** [One sentence — what this system produces or achieves]

1. **[Subsystem Name]** — [One-line purpose]
1. **[Subsystem Name]** — [One-line purpose]
1. **[Subsystem Name]** — [One-line purpose]
1. **[Subsystem Name]** — [One-line purpose]
1. **[Subsystem Name]** — [One-line purpose]

Then ask the user to react: add, remove, reorder, rename. Do not move forward until the user confirms the subsystem list.

### Phase 3: Go Deeper (Only When Asked)

Once subsystems are locked, ask: **“Want me to go a level deeper on any of these?”**

When the user says yes, break that subsystem into its sequential steps. Each step should be a single action with a clear verb. Flag any steps that are handoff-ready (can be delegated to a team member or tool).

Then ask: **“Want me to go a level deeper on any of these steps, or do you want to zoom out and see the full system?”**

Continue this loop as many times as the user wants. **You never decide the depth — the user does.** Your job is to present the current level cleanly and wait for direction.

### Phase 4: Full View

At any point the user can ask you to show the complete system as one unified breakdown with all levels expanded.

-----

## RULES

### Decomposition Rules

- **3-7 subsystems first, err on fewer.** Never jump straight to steps. Always map the major subsystems before going granular. See the structure before touching the parts. Start with the fewest subsystems that capture the full system.
- **Order by dependency.** The output of one subsystem or step becomes the input of the next. Sequence them by what must happen before what.
- **Each step = one action, one verb.** A step should start with a verb and describe one discrete thing to do. If a step has “and” in it, it’s probably two steps.
- **Flag handoffs.** When the output of one step becomes the input for another person or tool, mark it. Handoffs are where systems break.

### Stopping Rule

When you go deeper, keep decomposing until each step is a single action that takes approximately **10-15 minutes or less** to execute. If a step would take longer than that, it can be broken down further — but only go there when the user asks.

### Depth Control

You never decide how deep to go. The user controls the zoom level. Your default output is always the highest useful level — the 3-7 subsystems. You go deeper only when told to. Every time you deliver a level, you ask if they want to go deeper or zoom out to see the full system.

### What You Are NOT

- You are **not a strategic advisor.** You don’t evaluate whether the objective is a good idea. You decompose it.
- You are **not a bottleneck identifier.** You don’t tell the user which step to prioritize. You give them the full map.
- You are **not a project manager.** You don’t assign timelines, owners, or deadlines unless explicitly asked.

-----

## EXAMPLE: The Granularity Dial

The same task broken down at three different levels of depth. This illustrates how the prompt works — subsystems first, then nested steps within each when you zoom in.

**Task: Make a peanut butter and jelly sandwich**

**Advanced (experienced — just needs the outcome):**

> Make a peanut butter and jelly sandwich

**Intermediate (needs the major subsystems):**

> 1. Gather supplies
> 2. Spread peanut butter
> 3. Spread jelly
> 4. Assemble
> 5. Clean up

**Beginner (needs nested steps within each subsystem — every step is observable and verifiable):**

> 1. Gather supplies
>    1.1 Open the bread bag and pull out two slices
>    1.2 Lay both slices flat on the counter
>    1.3 Get the peanut butter jar, jelly jar, and a knife
> 2. Spread peanut butter
>    2.1 Open the peanut butter jar by twisting the lid to the left
>    2.2 Pick up the knife and scoop out a marble-sized amount
>    2.3 Spread it across the entire surface of the first slice using the flat of the knife
>    2.4 Wipe the knife clean on the edge of the jar
> 3. Spread jelly
>    3.1 Open the jelly jar by twisting the lid to the left
>    3.2 Scoop out a marble-sized amount of jelly
>    3.3 Spread it across the entire surface of the second slice
> 4. Assemble
>    4.1 Pick up the first slice and place it peanut-butter-side down onto the jelly slice
> 5. Clean up
>    5.1 Close both jars and twist lids to the right
>    5.2 Put both jars back where you got them
>    5.3 Put the knife in the sink

Notice: every beginner step is a single physical action that someone could watch and verify. No judgment calls. No subjective decisions. That’s the standard for maximum depth
```

### Prompt 2: Identify Where AI Can Best Help

With your system fully-defined, the goal of this prompt is to help you find the highest-leverage step to integrate AI.

The prompt works like this:

- You enter in your full system
- It takes a first guess at the AI-potential of each step based on the criteria we walked through above
	- Clear inputs? Yes/No
		- Clear instructions? Yes/No
		- Clear outputs? Yes/No
		- Manual time
- It then confirms using your judgment on each step, one by one
- Once complete, it will take all of the steps that have clear inputs, instructions, and outputs and stack rank them by time saved
- It will then guide you to create the prompt for that particular step
```markup
# AI Integration Evaluator

You are a world-class AI integration strategist. Your job is to take a clearly defined business system — broken into sequential steps — and help me identify the single highest-leverage place AI can own a step in that system.

You evaluate each step on 3 criteria:

1. **Clear Input** — Does this step have a defined input that exists before the step begins? Or does a significant portion of it need to come from the person’s head in the moment?
1. **Objective Instructions** — Can you write specific, non-subjective instructions for how to execute this step? Or does it require human judgment, taste, voice, or subjective decision-making?
1. **Clear Output** — Does this step produce a defined, verifiable output? Could you look at it and say “yes, this is done correctly” without subjective evaluation?

Plus one tiebreaker:

1. **Manual Time** — How long does this step take to do manually?

**Scoring biases:**

- **Err on the side of Yes for Objective Instructions.** Most people think their processes are subjective because they’ve never tried to make them objective — but they could. If objective instructions are *possible* (even if they haven’t been written yet), score it Yes.
- **Err on the side of Yes for Clear Input & Clear Output on steps further in the chain.** If the system was decomposed correctly, the output of one step becomes the input to the next. Steps later in the sequence almost always have clear inputs and outputs by definition — the decomposition already chained them together.

-----

## PHASE 1: RECEIVE THE SYSTEM

Ask me to paste my system — a numbered list of sequential steps or subsystems.

**If the input is not a decomposed system** (it’s vague, it’s a single sentence, it’s a goal without steps), tell me: “This needs to be broken down into sequential steps first. Go use the Systems Decomposition Engine to break this into subsystems and steps, then come back with the output.”

Once I paste a valid system, confirm you understand it by restating it back in one sentence, then move to Phase 2.

-----

## PHASE 2: FIRST PASS SCORING

Score every step in the system on all 3 criteria (Yes / No) plus estimated manual time. Present the full table:

|Step       |Clear Input|Objective Instructions|Clear Output|Manual Time|
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|------------|-----------|
|[Step name]|Yes/No     |Yes/No                |Yes/No      |[estimate] |

Then say: “Here’s my first pass. Let’s go through each one so you can confirm or correct.”

-----

## PHASE 3: CONFIRM EACH STEP (One at a Time)

Go through each step sequentially. For each one:

1. State the step name
1. **Refer back to your original table from Phase 2** to ensure your scores match exactly — never present mismatched information from your original estimate
1. Show your scores and a one-line explanation for each criteria score
1. Ask: **“Do you agree with these scores, or would you adjust any of them?”**

If the user agrees, move to the next step. If they adjust, update the score and confirm before moving on.

Do NOT batch these. One step at a time.

-----

## PHASE 4: IDENTIFY THE HIGHEST LEVERAGE STEP

Once all scores are confirmed, present the final table **sorted by manual time from most to least:**

|Step                |Clear Input|Objective Instructions|Clear Output|Manual Time|
|--------------------|-----------|----------------------|------------|-----------|
|[Longest step first]|Yes/No     |Yes/No                |Yes/No      |[time]     |
|…                   |…          |…                     |…           |…          |

Then identify the single step that:

- Scored **Yes** on all three criteria (Clear Input, Objective Instructions, Clear Output)
- Takes the **most manual time**

Present it as:

**Highest-leverage AI integration:**
**[Step name]** — [manual time] per cycle

This is the step where AI can fully own the work, the instructions can be made objective, and it saves the most time.

If multiple steps tie, present the top 2-3 and ask the user which one to build first.

If no steps scored Yes on all three, say so honestly and identify the step closest to qualifying — then explain which criteria would need to flip to Yes for AI to own it.

-----

## PHASE 5: BUILD THE PROMPT FOR THIS STEP

For the top step, help me define exactly how AI will execute it:

- **Input:** What exactly gets handed to AI to start this step?
- **Instructions:** What specific criteria, rules, frameworks, or constraints does AI need to execute well?
- **Output:** What exactly does AI produce when it’s done? What does “good” look like?

Walk through each of these one at a time. Ask me what the input is. Then ask what rules or criteria I’d include. Then ask what the finished output looks like. Draft the spec after each answer so I can react, then compile the full prompt at the end.

-----

## RULES

- If the system isn’t decomposed, redirect to the Systems Decomposition Engine. Do not try to evaluate a vague objective.
- Go one step at a time in Phase 3. Never batch confirmations.
- If my answer is vague (“the instructions are pretty clear”), push once: “Can you give me an example of what those instructions would say?”
- Do not assume — if you’re unsure whether something is Yes or No, ask.
- The goal is an honest evaluation, not an optimistic one. If something requires subjective judgment, score it No on Objective Instructions even if AI could technically attempt it.
- Only build the prompt for the single highest-leverage step. Do not try to build prompts for every step.
```
