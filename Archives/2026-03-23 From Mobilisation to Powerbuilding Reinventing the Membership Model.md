From Mobilisation to Powerbuilding: Reinventing the Membership Model

Membership isn’t dying - but the old rules no longer work. Across NGOs, campaigns, unions and political parties, organisations are signing people up in moments of urgency… and losing them just as fast. This report argues that the real crisis isn’t recruitment, but relationship.

In a world of distrust, precarity and digital overload, people don’t join to be counted - they join to belong, to matter, and to make a difference they can see. Drawing on interviews and data from organisations representing 300,000 members our new report, The Future of Membership: From Lists of Names to Living Relationships shows what healthy membership looks like now: designed journeys instead of one-off transactions; community as the core product; technology that strengthens human connection rather than replacing it; and models that turn individual commitment into collective power.

\-

Picture this: a protest erupts, 5,000 people flood an NGO's signup page overnight, and leaders celebrate a "membership surge." Weeks later, those names fade into digital dust, unengaged, unremembered, churned. That's not a glitch; it's the standard operating procedure for too many political parties, unions, and advocacy groups, as revealed in the summit session on "The Future of Membership: From Mobilising People to Building Power." Moderated by NationBuilder's Madeline Cazes, with insights from Brand Response founder Sam Lockwood and Better Politics Foundation's Rebekah Ison, the discussion exposed a stark truth: recruitment works fine, but relationships don't. In an era of distrust and digital overload, people don't join to be tallied, they crave belonging, identity, and impact.

The crisis isn't numbers; it's neglect. Surveying 22 membership leaders representing 300,000+ members, the panel's new report found organisations growing or stable on paper, but with tiny fractions actively engaged. "Recruitment isn't the problem, engagement is," Cazes declared, noting how budgets skew heavily to campaigns (over half in most cases) while membership infrastructure starves, only 18% prioritise it equally. Hands shot up from audience campaigners admitting post-surge drop-offs; one group lost those 5,000 protesters as quickly as they gained them. Why? No onboarding, no follow-through, no "high-value response" to a high-value act.

#### **Churn's Human Cost: Friction Points and Missed Moments**

Rebekah Ison shared her raw story: joining a party post-frictionless signup, thrilled by a quick local email, then ghosted until a "weird weekday meeting." She hasn't left yet, but might. "Membership isn't invested in long-term as much as an initial metric," she lamented, echoing the report's call to map "friction points" across journeys. Ison urged auditing pathways from signup to leadership: "What does it look like to go from 'I want to learn more' to 'I want to run for office'?"

Sam Lockwood, architect of Dutch liberal D66's wins, stressed culture over tech. Prepping 18 months ahead with personas and workflows, they captured spikes by *asking* what members wanted, then delivering. D66 ditched fixed fees (from €5 to €10,000), killed passwords for logins (biometrics, one-time emails), and gamified without portals no one used. Result: fluid roles, where donors get actions, volunteers materials instantly, "breaking silos" into loops. "Liquid volunteering," Flo Barre called it elsewhere: sign up for an action, connect to local chapters seamlessly.

But success demands "do now, apologise later" ethos. D66's handsome candidate helped, but culture let teams iterate freely. "I can tell in 15 minutes if a client will succeed," Lockwood said, it's hierarchy-free experimentation.

#### **Community as the Product: Third Spaces in a Lonely World**

Members want "community as the product," per the report, identity, social value over cash or clout. 56% recruit via referrals; top engagement? WhatsApp, Discord. Ison's Political Leadership Entrepreneur Network (PLEN, 140 orgs/43 countries) started as "antidote to loneliness" for solo operatives. Piloting asynchronous LMS playbooks (fundraising, comms), they foster organic subgroups, women's lunches, Latin networks. Post-Berlin gathering: tears, arrests shared, bonds forged.

Zoran Mamdani's NYC campaign embodied "joy": volunteers got coveted swag via service, scavenger hunts drew thousands; people dated, formed soccer clubs. "Your campaign can be a third space," Leah noted, combating loneliness epidemic. Local petitions? Twice the signatures.

Yet demographics loom: ageing bases, cost-of-living barriers. Youth shun €20 fees when identity expresses elsewhere; parties "pay lip service" to diversity but crave loyalty. Ison: Create "new front doors" via issues, they evolve. "They're not young forever." But beware "wreckers", conceptual work needed to vet entrants.

#### **Tech as Enabler, Not Eraser**

Digital amplifies humans, not replaces. Qomon's Euro elections: peer-to-peer recruitment snowballed, measure *organic* links, not clicks. PLEN asked: "We want a WhatsApp group", done. Emails (90% engagement) amplify personal touch.

Lockwood: Design for fluidity, no funnels, loops where donors act, sharers canvass. D66's radical ask-first built trust fueling €10k gifts.

Capacity-building retains: train for habits (cue-behavior-reward). Political parties cue via elections, but ignore realities, kids, jobs block meetings. Supportive environments convert.

#### No Fixed Fees, Fluid Roles, The New Rules

Redefine membership: "Choose-your-adventure," per Ison, no rigid legal silos. "You're a member if you lead for us," Leah reframed. PLEN: No fees, pure participation.

Report: Referrals > ads; community > transactions. Budgets must balance, starve infrastructure, lose movements.

Audience Q: For NGOs sans "cards"? Insights hold, lists are proto-membership if nurtured.

Parties risk death without change: Australia's dominant party trails right-wing upstarts fielding nurses, "random community guys." Relate via local frames.

#### **The Power Pivot: From Lists to Living Ecosystems**

This isn't dying membership; it's obsolete transactionalism. Future: designed journeys, community-core, tech-human hybrids. Invest in relationships, map frictions, honour commitments (one call turns passive to super-donor). Culture eats strategy; joy fuels action.

For Brussels insiders: ALDE's 75 parties, prioritise authentic cores, messengers over tools. Labour's CTO Josh Carrington (elsewhere): 10% staff as devs, competitive edge in FPTP margins.

The call: Shift from lists to ecosystems. Churn kills power; belonging builds it. Parties ignoring this? Obsolete by 2027.
