## The 3 Essential Steps to a Nonprofit Website That Builds Community (and Advances Impact)

  

You know that your nonprofit’s website has the potential to be your most powerful community-building tool. But only if you stop treating it like a business card and start thinking about it as the foundation of a two-way relationship.

It’s the question nonprofits ask me every day: “How do we create an online presence that people *actually* care about?”

Below, you’ll find my response to that question—in three simple steps.

![](https://thenonprofithive.com/wp-content/uploads/The-3-Essential-Steps-to-a-Nonprofit-Website-That-Builds-Community-and-Advances-Impact-1.png)

## But First, What’s at Stake Here?

Before diving in deeper, let’s reflect: *Why does this matter so much?*

Well, consider this:

Someone interested in your cause discovers your nonprofit through a friend’s Facebook post. Excited to learn more, they click through to your website. And within thirty seconds, they’re gone—not because they don’t care about your mission, but because they couldn’t figure out what you actually do, let alone how they could help.

This scenario plays out over and over and over for nonprofits of all sizes, ages, and sectors.

Staff members invest precious time and energy crafting thoughtful pages, tweaking the links, and selecting just the right photos. But instead of the anticipated flood of engaged supporters, they get digital tumbleweeds.

Visitors arrive, glance around, and promptly disappear.

And I empathize with the visitors, because I find myself in their shoes *constantly*. I look at nonprofit websites every day. And more often than not, I ALSO struggle to understand what an organization actually does because the website just doesn’t convey that information clearly.

The unfortunate irony is that many of these visitors genuinely care. They have the time, resources, and passion to become meaningful contributors. But their interest is blocked by a maze of insider language, unclear navigation, and competing calls-to-action.

And for every visitor stuck in that digital maze, a door of real-world opportunity is missed.

## From Business Card to Community Building

The problem isn’t your cause, your team, or even your content.

The problem is that most nonprofits treat their website like a digital brochure when it should function as a ***community-building machine***.

Here’s the difference:

- A brochure dumps information and hopes someone cares enough to figure it out.
- A community-building website takes people on a deliberate journey from light curiosity to engaged commitment.

After analyzing hundreds of nonprofit websites and working with organizations across the sector, I’ve discovered a clear pattern separating what works and what doesn’t.

The most successful ones—those that consistently convert passive visitors into active community members—follow **the same three-step process** that transforms how people experience their mission online:

1. Orientation
2. Trust
3. Momentum

## Step 1: Orient Your Visitors (Don’t Assume They Already Know Your Big Idea)

The biggest mistake nonprofits make is assuming website visitors understand their work.

They jump straight into minute program details, upcoming event flyers, board member bios, and donation appeals without establishing the fundamental context that makes everything else meaningful.

And it’s a huge issue. It’s the most common problem I encounter when reviewing a new nonprofit’s website. I often tell organizations: if I can’t figure out what you do in 10 seconds, your ideal supporter won’t either.

Your organization, your cause, your approach—all of this is crystal clear to you because you live it every day. But to your visitor, it’s like overhearing strangers laughing at inside jokes at a party. It’s confusing and, often, alienating.

Successful nonprofit websites solve this by **treating every visitor as if they’re encountering the organization for the first time**. They answer three fundamental questions before diving into anything else:

1. **What do you do?** Not your mission statement, but a clear, jargon-free explanation. Instead of “We facilitate community-based interventions to address food insecurity,” try “We help local families put food on the table.”
2. **Why does it matter?** Don’t assume people understand the scope or urgency of your cause. Paint a picture of what happens if this problem goes unsolved. Make the stakes clear and personal.
3. **Who is this for?** Help visitors immediately identify whether your organization is relevant to their lives. Are you for families with young children? Small business owners? Recent college grads? The faster someone can answer, “Is this for me?” the more likely they are to keep reading.

This orientation isn’t about being 100% comprehensive—it’s about making them feel like a welcome guest. You’re rolling out the red carpet and helping newcomers get their bearings before asking them to go anywhere else on your site. (And definitely before asking for money!)

## Step 2: Build Trust Through Strategic Transparency

Once visitors have their bearings, they move into evaluation mode.

*“Okay, I understand what this organization does, but can I trust them? Are they the right group to support?”*

This is where most nonprofit websites get tripped up. They either overwhelm visitors with every possible piece of evidence all at once (awards, testimonials, partner logos, staff credentials, financial reports, media features), or they provide so little proof that visitors remain skeptical.

I think this is the trickiest part of the whole equation, because it’s all about striking a balance across all the different groups coming to your website. You need to keep the big picture in mind while also taking into consideration an individual’s experience.

The key to bridging the gap is strategic transparency— **sharing the right information at the right time** to address the specific concerns your audience has. Different visitors will have different trust requirements:

- **Potential donors** want to know their money will be used responsibly and create real impact. They need clear explanations of how donations are used, evidence of past success, and transparency about your approach.
- **Program participants** want to know you understand their situation and can actually help. They hope to see stories from people like them, clear explanations of what to expect, and evidence that you’ll be able to support their needs. They want to feel welcomed, heard, and seen.
- **Community partners** want to know you’re competent and aligned with their values. They need to see your track record, your approach to collaboration, and your standing in the community.

The most effective way to build trust is by anticipating hesitations *before* visitors raise them.

If people frequently call to ask about eligibility requirements, that’s a signal: make that information more prominent. If community members always think your organization does X when you really do Y, make that part of your core messaging.

This isn’t about being defensive or over-explaining. It’s about demonstrating that you understand your community and respect them enough to address questions proactively.

## Step 3: Provide Clear Next Steps (Keep the Momentum Flowing)

The final step is to tell visitors what to do next.

Without a clear next step, it’s like hosting an amazing party and then deleting everyone’s contact info and never seeing them ever again. Why bother?

Once someone understands your work and trusts your organization, they want to know: “What can I do about this?” But instead of clear, compelling next steps, most nonprofits offer a confusing array of options: “Donate! Volunteer! Subscribe! Follow us on social! Read our blog! Contact us! Attend our event!”

This abundance of choice creates what psychologists call “decision paralysis.” When faced with too many options, people often choose none. They leave your website interested but not engaged.

At the other end of the spectrum, some organizations offer next steps that are vague, irrelevant, or totally missing. There’s a program page but no way to sign up. A volunteer page asking for capital campaign donations. An impact page with no next step at all.

I can’t tell you how many nonprofit websites I’ve visited where I have to visit 3+ pages just to do something that should be relatively obvious (join a newsletter, donate, etc). Unless you’ve already built up a huge amount of trust, most visitors won’t go through the extra effort.

In either case (both abundant and lack of choice), the solution is strategic prioritization. For each page of your website, **identify the single most important action you want visitors to take**. Then create a path on that page to guide them toward that action.

This doesn’t mean you can only have one call-to-action on your entire website. Your homepage might have multiple calls-to-action based on your visitors’ interest (donors go here, volunteers go there, program participants go elsewhere). But each individual page should have one primary purpose and one primary next step.

Above all, these [next steps should feel natural and low-risk.](https://thenonprofithive.com/a-case-for-slow-technology-for-nonprofits/) Don’t ask someone to donate $100 on their first visit. Instead, invite them to sign up for your newsletter, [download a helpful resource](https://thenonprofithive.com/time-saving-tech-for-nonprofits/), or attend a free community event.

It’s all about creating stepping stones that build toward deeper involvement.

## An Impactful Website Comes Down to Empathy

*Orientation. Trust. Momentum.*

This three-step approach to nonprofit websites isn’t about manipulation or tricks— **it’s about empathy**.

And if I had to identify one single difference between the best and most confusing nonprofit websites, it’s the depth (or lack) of empathy.

By following these guidelines, you’re revealing a deep understanding of your visitors by making your website easy to understand and navigate. You’re speaking confidently and personally by being transparent about your work. And you’re offering useful guidance by laying out clear paths for engagement.

When you implement this three-step approach consistently across your website, something remarkable happens.

Visitors stop bouncing after two seconds. The site experience feels more like a mapped-out journey and less like a business card. Visitors start exploring multiple pages and taking the actions you’ve pointed them towards.

More importantly, you begin [intentionally attracting people who are aligned with your mission.](https://thenonprofithive.com/nonprofit-community-building-strategies/) Your community becomes more aware, more engaged, more committed, and more likely to advocate for your cause.

The transformation doesn’t happen over night, but the results compound over time.

And unlike many outreach methods that require constant effort, **a thoughtfully designed website continues working in the background**, building relationships and expanding your community even when your team is focused elsewhere.

So here it is again: Orient your visitors, build their trust, and show them exactly how they can be part of the change you’re creating. Do this well, and you won’t have to wonder why people aren’t engaging with your website—the steady growth and momentum will speak for itself.
