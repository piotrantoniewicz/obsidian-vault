### TEAMS! Newsletter | Engagement Edition | April 28, 2026

In the social services sector, we often face a persistent, slippery challenge: keeping our teams truly motivated when resources are scarce and the work is unrelenting. For years, we’ve relied on the "North Star" of our cause to counteract compassion fatigue, which can drain engagement. But mission alone isn't a management strategy, and passion isn't a substitute for a well-designed work environment.

This challenge is the inspiration for today's **TEAMS! Newsletter — Engagement Edition**. We are diving deep into the landmark article *"Employee Motivation: A Powerful New Model,"* published by Harvard Business Review and authored by Nitin Nohria, Boris Groysberg, and Linda-Eling Lee. Their research, grounded in modern science, offers a blueprint for nonprofit leaders to look "under the hood" of human behavior.

If you’ve ever felt the sting of losing a talented program director to a corporate role or watched a high-performing team succumb to "compassion fatigue," you know the struggle is real. We often try to fix motivation with a single lever, such as a modest cost-of-living adjustment or a "culture day," only to find the needle barely moves. Human motivation isn't a single switch; it's a complex engine driven by four distinct, hardwired biological needs.

### Executive Summary

True motivation in the nonprofit sector requires more than a shared vision. According to a Harvard Business Review model, humans are guided by four fundamental emotional drives: **Acquire, Bond, Comprehend, and Defend**. While most organizations over-index on one (usually the drive to bond), performance and retention occur only when all four are addressed in concert. For the nonprofit leader, this means moving beyond "mission-fit" to a holistic approach that balances rewards, culture, job design, and institutional fairness.

**The Four Pillars of the Motivated Nonprofit**

- ***The Drive to Acquire: Beyond the Paycheck:*** Nonprofits often overlook this due to budget constraints, but it includes social status and recognition, not just money.
- ***The Drive to Bond: The Social Fabric:*** This is the "secret sauce" of the sector, the profound need to belong and to share camaraderie with a collective.
- ***The Drive to Comprehend: The Mastery of Craft:*** Our teams stay when they are challenged to learn, grow, and see the tangible impact of their ideas and actions.
- ***The Drive to Defend: The Need for Justice:*** In an unpredictable world, employees crave transparent, fair processes that protect them from arbitrary decisions.

### The Drive to Acquire: Differentiating Excellence

In the social sector, we often default to egalitarianism, yet the **drive to acquire** is relative and insatiable. We want to know how we are doing compared to others. While our salaries may not be the highest, we use our **Reward Systems** to clearly distinguish top performers from average performers.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** Instead of a flat 3% raise for everyone, a local shelter implements a "Special Assignments" program that gives top-performing case managers the lead on high-visibility community partnerships and a "Director’s Award" with public recognition and a modest, merit-based bonus.

### The Drive to Bond: Cultivating "We" Over "Me"

The **drive to bond** turns a group of employees into a movement. It is best satisfied by an organization’s **Culture**. When people feel proud of the institution, their morale skyrockets; when they feel betrayed, it plummets.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** A large multi-service agency breaks down silos between its "Development" and "Program" teams by creating cross-functional "Impact Circles" that meet weekly to address specific client hurdles, fostering mutual reliance and collaboration.

### The Drive to Comprehend: Designing for Meaning

The **drive to comprehend** is the desire to make sense of the world and to make a meaningful contribution. It is satisfied through **Job Design**. Monotonous, dead-end roles are the primary reason talented nonprofit staff leave for new challenges elsewhere.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** A youth mentoring organization realizes its administrative assistants are burned out. They redesign the roles to include "Community Insight" hours, during which staff spend 20% of their time in the field learning about the population they serve, transforming clerical roles into learning journeys.

### The Drive to Defend: The Transparency Mandate

The **drive to defend** is rooted in our "fight-or-flight" response. In nonprofits, this manifests as a quest for **Justice and Transparency** in management. When employees perceive decisions (such as layoffs or budget cuts) as arbitrary, they become resistant to change and vulnerable to outside recruitment.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** During a merger between two community clinics, the leadership holds "Open Ledger" town halls. Even when the news is difficult, transparency around the funding criteria builds trust and fulfills employees' need for security and fairness.

### The Manager’s Influence

While organizational policy is the foundation, the **Direct Manager** has significant influence. Employees are realistic; they know a supervisor can't change the board-approved salary scale, but they expect that manager to use their "wiggle room" to treat people fairly and make the work interesting. A manager who excels in three drives but fails the fourth (e.g., is unfair or capricious) will still see their team's engagement crumble.

### Putting It into Action

- ***Conduct a "Drive Review":*** Review your last team meeting. Did you only talk about the "Bond" (mission/teamwork), or did you acknowledge "Acquisition" (recognition) and "Comprehension" (learning)?
- ***Differentiate a Reward:***Identify one high performer this week and give them a "choice assignment" or public praise that specifically ties their effort to a successful outcome.
- ***Redesign One Task:*** Find a "monotonous" task in your organization. Ask the person doing it: "How could we make this a learning opportunity for you to change how you’re having to do this work?"
- ***Increase Transparency:*** If a decision was recently made that "came from the top," schedule 15 minutes to explain the *rationale* and *criteria* behind it to your team.
- ***Foster a New Connection:*** Introduce two people from different departments who don't typically work together and ask them to share one "best practice" from their respective areas.

### Closing Thoughts

To drive greater team engagement in the social sector, managers need to recognize that leadership is an act of stewardship. When we move beyond the "one-size-fits-all" approach to motivation and intentionally address our teams' hardwired needs, we improve "retention," and we build a team that is engaged, resilient, and ready to face any challenge.
