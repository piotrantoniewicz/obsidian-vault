Power

08.05.2026

The crisis of trust in political leadership will not be solved by another app, dashboard or engagement platform. It will be solved by leaders and institutions that are willing to redesign how ideas gain traction, how marginalised voices are protected, and how technology is constrained to serve authenticity rather than replace it.

The real opportunity is to treat tech as “traction infrastructure” for good leadership, not as a substitute for it.

\-

If you wander through any governance or democracy conference today, the choreography is familiar: a ritual recitation of falling trust indicators, a quick nod to “populism” or “polarisation”, and then the pivot to platforms and AI as the new hope. The 17:30 “Tech for Trust: Reimagining Leadership” session in Berlin used a different starting point. The core claim was stark: politics is not in a trust crisis because it lacks technology; it is in a trust crisis because good ideas and good people cannot survive hostile, polarised environments.

The Better Politics Fund, launched to back “political leadership entrepreneurs”, has the data to prove how acute the appetite for solutions has become. When it opened a small call for €20,000 ideation grants last year, over 500 applications poured in within three weeks, roughly a third of them tech‑centred proposals to “fix” leadership. The question that framed the session cut through the noise: if constraints disappeared, how would you use technology to revolutionise trust in political leaders?

The answers in the room suggested that most people still reach first for tools, fact‑checking layers, blockchain transparency, direct communication channels. But the more interesting argument came from the stage: the priority is not to “digitise politics”, but to redesign the conditions under which political ideas, especially those carried by women and marginalised communities, can actually travel and survive.

#### Trust collapses when leaders are left alone

Trust in leaders rests on three old‑fashioned pillars from organisational psychology: integrity, competence and benevolence. That triad is as relevant to a mayor as it is to a CEO. Yet in the public imagination, political leaders rarely get credit for any of the three, even when many are, as one panellist pointed out, “community‑minded, hard‑working, there for the right reasons.”

Why? One answer offered from the stage: because the system systematically abandons precisely those leaders who might embody those traits.

In regions like Saxony and Thuringia, where far‑right mobilisation has normalised organised hostility, women in local office are not just disagreed with; they are subjected to harassment, procedural sabotage and coordinated narrative attacks. Under those conditions, “political safety becomes an issue of trust in the infrastructure itself”. If stepping forward means exposing yourself and your family to risk, the talent pool shrinks. Many of the people citizens say they want, empathetic, principled, rooted in their communities, simply never run.

That is why the Women’s Political Impact Lab, backed by the Better Politics Fund, is deliberately not another generic “women in politics” mentorship scheme. Instead, it tries to build traction for specific ideas and campaigns in hostile environments. Its underlying theory is simple and subversive: trust does not fail for lack of messaging polish; it fails when leaders with credible ideas are structurally prevented from gaining footholds in public debate.

#### Tech as “traction infrastructure”, not as a new boss

The lab’s design points toward a more intelligent role for technology in politics: not as a communications megaphone, but as “traction infrastructure” for ideas. That means three things.

First, helping leaders identify who they actually need to reach. Not everyone in a territory is equally relevant for every issue. Political tech that treats “the public” as a homogenous mass drives leaders back into safe, vague messaging. Tech that maps concrete influence paths, who listens to whom in a particular district, where trust actually resides, allows local politicians to invest scarce time in places where it can realistically move outcomes.

Second, anticipating where resistance and distortion will surface. In polarised environments, every proposal passes through pre‑existing narratives and networks of opposition. Mapping those in advance, who will label this housing reform as “anti‑family”, who will attack this inclusion programme as “woke overreach”, which Facebook groups and Telegram channels will weaponise it, allows leaders to stress test their narrative and build counter‑moves before the storm hits.

Third, turning “citizen engagement” from a one‑way microphone into a feedback loop between peers. That is where AI‑assisted tools, like the ones used in Dembrane’s deployments across European municipalities, start to matter: not because they allow you to survey more people, but because they can compress hours of qualitative conversation into patterns leaders can act on. When hundreds of residents talk about safety, housing or schools in their own words, natural‑language processing can surface themes, contradictions and unexpected alliances that a traditional town hall would miss.

None of this works if tech is allowed to hollow out authenticity. One of the strongest warnings came from a data strategist who spends his life building political tools: the greatest temptation facing politicians today is to let generative AI talk for them. In the short term, it can produce flawless speeches, Instagram captions and policy explainers. In the medium term, it erodes the only non‑replicable asset a politician has: their own voice.

The paradox is that the more our feeds fill with synthetic content, the more citizens start actively searching for signs of the human. That dynamic may be brutal for overproduced campaigns, but it is a historic opportunity for leaders willing to show up in all their imperfection.

#### The loneliness at the core of political life

If there was one motif that threaded through the session, it was loneliness. Politicians are surrounded by people, staffers, constituents, cameras, and yet report some of the lowest mental‑health scores of any profession, lower even than ambulance workers and police in some studies. In interviews, many describe feeling both constantly watched and profoundly alone.

That loneliness has practical consequences. Leaders who are permanently in fight‑or‑flight mode make worse decisions. They become risk‑averse, cling to party lines, and retreat from the messy relational work that builds real trust. Some of the most revealing anecdotes came from the audience: a civil servant whose sister genuinely believed he was “putting microchips” into COVID vaccines because he worked on digital government tools; activists who have to explain, again and again, that “people in government are you and I, not crazy elites.”

When leaders lose trust in the citizens they serve, as targets of conspiracy theories, harassment or violence, they understandably withdraw. The session made clear that any serious attempt to rebuild trust has to run both ways: citizens need reasons to believe in leaders, and leaders need reasons to trust that their communities will not destroy them for stepping up.

Technology can cushion some of this. AI “agents” that triage incoming messages, monitor abusive content and handle routine casework can give politicians back some cognitive space and reduce the feeling of drowning. So can deliberate design choices like giving leaders social media teams with explicit permission to filter away personalised bile. But tech is a palliative, not a cure. The deeper fix is cultural and institutional: parties that stop treating mental breakdowns as personal failures; media ecosystems that stop rewarding outrage; civic cultures that normalise sending “love letters to politicians” as well as complaints.

#### The illusion of the perfect platform

One of the session’s most useful corrections concerned citizens’ own behaviour. The room contained people building tools for governments, open‑source advocates, and organisers from the global South. Several admitted to a familiar cycle: spend months designing a sophisticated digital participation platform, only to discover that the real constraint is not features; it is the willingness or capacity of institutions to use the input.

The same pattern appears in citizen engagement. As one practitioner working closely with the World Bank on participatory democracy pointed out, technology has made it extraordinarily easy to “give citizens a voice”, to run consultations, launch platforms, aggregate feedback, but has not automatically increased governments’ ability to make sense of what citizens say, or their willingness to act on it. AI can now help by clustering thousands of free‑text responses into themes, flagging contradictions, and surfacing proposals that cut across demographic lines. That is already happening in municipalities that use AI‑assisted tools to process open‑ended feedback from tens of thousands of residents.

But none of it matters if the institution doesn’t have a place to plug those insights in, if there is no budget line, no legislative vehicle, no cross‑party agreement to treat the results as politically binding. Tech without institutional pathways breeds a more corrosive form of distrust: citizens spend time engaging, see nothing change, and conclude that participation is theatre.

The political entrepreneurs on stage argued that the next frontier is not more sophisticated interfaces, but more honest commitments. If governments invite citizens into a co‑creation process, they need to define upfront what is actually on the table, and tie at least some decisions to the outcome. If they don’t, even the best AI summariser becomes complicit in performative democracy.

#### Going offline to save democracy online

Perhaps the most counterintuitive contribution of the evening was a plea to stop worshipping connectivity. One speaker proudly announced that he had gone back to an old‑school radio alarm clock to stop doom‑scrolling last thing at night and first thing in the morning. Another urged politicians to “get off the phone and back into the field,” arguing that the leaders he has seen succeed are those who spend more time on doorsteps and in community centres than on X or TikTok.

That may sound quaint in an age of feed‑driven politics, but it is also a strategic insight. As AI makes it easier and cheaper to flood the infosphere with plausible‑sounding content, the informational value of digital channels declines. Citizens know they are being targeted; they know that what they see may not be real; they adjust by discounting everything. In that context, a short conversation at a market stall or a parent‑teacher meeting, where a politician appears without filters or scripts, becomes disproportionately powerful.

Tech can help those moments land. Tools that allow volunteers to log conversations as audio snippets, then use AI to distil what people actually care about in a neighbourhood, mean that those offline exchanges do not vanish into thin air. Platforms that help local politicians compare notes with peers in other countries, say, women councillors in Lebanon, Germany and Kenya facing similar harassment patterns, can turn isolated acts of courage into shared playbooks.

But the underlying bet is clear: in the coming decade, political trust will be rebuilt less through content and more through contact.

#### The small commitments that add up

The session ended on a deliberately personal note. Instead of another abstract “call to action”, participants were asked to open their phones and write down one concrete thing they would do, after leaving the room, to strengthen trust in politics: send a message of appreciation to a politician they admire; engage positively with leaders they follow online instead of only criticising; explain to sceptical relatives what public‑sector technologists actually do; encourage a friend to run for local office.

Those gestures can feel trivial in the face of structural problems. They are not. The crisis of trust in political leadership is the sum of millions of micro‑interactions where people assume the worst of each other and act accordingly. Reversing that spiral will require institutional reforms, new funding models, better legal protection for frontline leaders, and smarter tech. It will also require more citizens to behave as if politics is something they are entitled to shape, not a distant pathology to complain about.

The most important lesson from the “Tech for Trust” session is therefore not that we need more innovation. It is that we need to be more precise about what we are trying to innovate. Tools are easy to build; traction is hard. If we want to live in democracies where integrity, competence and benevolence are not punchlines but expectations, we will have to stop asking, “Which app will fix this?” and start asking, with more humility and more ambition, “What kinds of leaders, and what kinds of institutions, deserve the power we give them?”
