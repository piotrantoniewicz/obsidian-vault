It seems like there’s a collective exhaustion hanging over the world, and the nonprofit sector is right in the middle of it. For years now, we’ve been jumping from one **“unprecedented event”** to the next—a pandemic, economic uncertainty, social upheaval, and natural disasters. Each one comes with a wave of urgent appeals and a surge of immediate need.

But what happens when the crises don’t stop?

Both fundraisers and donors are feeling a deep sense of **“crisis fatigue.”** The emergency-based fundraising playbook that we’ve all been trained to use is starting to fray. Donors are overwhelmed, and development professionals are burning out trying to make their cause the loudest in a room full of shouting.

So, what do we do now? This month’s questions tackle this shared challenge head-on.

**Your development staff asks:** “After years of one ‘unprecedented event’ after another, our donors are exhausted. We’re exhausted. We keep sending out urgent appeals, but it feels like we’re just shouting into a void. How do we get heard when everyone seems to be asking for help?”

Your exhaustion is real, and it’s a sign that **the old strategy is no longer working**. When every organization defaults to a five-alarm-fire level of urgency, it all just becomes noise. Donors become numb to it, annoyed or even angry, and fundraisers feel demoralized.

The answer isn’t to shout louder. It’s to **whisper to the right people**.

In times of overwhelming noise, the most powerful thing you can do is to get **personal and relevant**. This is the moment to shift from broadcasting to narrowcasting. Instead of another mass email with a red “URGENT” banner, turn your focus to three often overlooked groups:

- your loyal donors
- your loyal volunteers and supporters
- people interested in your cause who’ve been overlooked because they don’t match your standard donor prospect profile

Your first move shouldn’t be an ask. It should be a gift. Send your most steadfast supporters a simple, heartfelt update that does nothing but show them the impact of their past generosity. If they want to donate, make it easy for them to do so, but your messaging should be clearly focused on your gratitude and your update. Remind them that even in a chaotic world, their support is a source of positivity, stability and gratitude. It’s your job to make them feel **seen and valued.**

When you do need to ask, reframe your language. Acknowledge the climate. Instead of, “We need your URGENT help now!” try something that connects with how they’re feeling: “In a world full of noise, we wanted to share something that gives us hope… and show you how you can be a part of it.”

This is the time to double down on stewardship. Pick up the phone and call five loyal donors this week simply to thank them. An authentic, two-minute conversation will do more to secure their long-term support than a hundred generic email blasts.

**Some of your board members aren’t so sure about this approach, and ask: “** This feels so low-key, I’m afraid we won’t bring in the money we need. What else can we do in this climate to respect our donors but also bring in the funds we need to run our organization?”

This is the critical second-half of the equation. Our role as non-profit professionals should not be focused on separating our donors from the world’s stress, but our role *can be* to help them find meaning and purpose in an anxiety-producing world. Inviting them to be a trusted partner in helping us address our mission can help them calm the noise while also meeting your revenue goals.

The key is to show your donors how their support of your organization can give them not only a distraction, but also a sense of control and peace. And the single most effective tool for this is **monthly giving.**

Recurring monthly gifts are the ultimate antidote to crisis fatigue for a donor. A monthly donation is a calm, intentional, and sustainable act. It allows a donor to say, “This is a cause I believe in, and I am going to support it consistently, regardless of the crisis of the day.” It turns their giving from a reactive, emotional decision into a proactive, deeply held value.

Frame your monthly giving program this way. Position it not only as a convenience for the organization, but also as an additional path for the donor to achieve a more balanced, meaningful and impactful philanthropic life.

You can also care for them by offering ways to engage that don’t involve their wallet. Are they burned out on giving? Fine. Invite them to a 20-minute virtual briefing on a program they care about. Ask them to share a social media post that made them proud. Send them a one-question survey: What will give you confidence that your support in us was well placed? *(Thanks TJ McGovern for this survey question!)*

Give them a way to connect that reinforces your shared values, not just their capacity to give.

The path to being heard and the path to caring for your donors are the same. Both are paved with authentic, human-to-human relationships. In times of constant uncertainty, people crave connection, stability, and the knowledge that they can still make a tangible difference. Trust, not urgency, is your most valuable and sustainable asset.
