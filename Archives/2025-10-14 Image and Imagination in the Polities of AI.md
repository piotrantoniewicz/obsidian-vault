[Image and Imagination in the Polities of AI](https://substack.com/home/post/p-176166086)

With our ability to alter, manipulate, or create images entirely anew, we face a crisis not simply of authenticity but of assumption.

---

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!W95B!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46dabc4f-5e3e-4e74-865d-c48a01f0f841_600x600.png)

This is the most true image of the problems in Portland right now. It is also generated with AI. Only this image represents what is really going on fully. At least for me, it is the truest image about what is actually happening.

The MAGA side claims Portland right now is fires on the street, riots and a “hellscape.” Our side says it’s [singing grandmas and people in silly animal costumes.](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/BSkDJ7eCpb8)

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BSkDJ7eCpb8)

Both have used selected video and photos to prove their point. (And some, at least on the Right, were from another continent and another time - not Portland at all).

As one protester, known locally as the Portland Frog, put it:

> “I obviously started a movement of people showing up looking ridiculous, which is the exact point. It’s to show how the narrative that is being pushed with how we are violent extremists is completely ridiculous. Nothing about this screams extremist and violent. So it’s just a ridiculous narrative that the Trump administration wants to put out so they can continue their fascist dictatorship.” — Seth Todd, Portland Frog 🐸✊

Perhaps both “truths” exist on some level in how people experience and understand the moment. The ‘truth’ behind the matter has little to do with pixels representing colors in digital images we see, and much more to do with the meaning behind what is happening.

There’s an old anecdote about Picasso (who BTW was ‘ [antifa](https://thedemlabs.org/2025/10/13/picasso-was-an-anti-fascist-antifa-compare-his-art-with-current-headlines/) ’ himself) that goes something like this: in his later years, after being widely acknowledged as the best painter of his time, a wealthy businessman approached him to commission a portrait of his wife. He offered a large sum but only if Picasso would “paint her as she actually looks.” Picasso responded, “All of my paintings look like the people I paint.” The man protested, “No, I mean as she *really* appears.” They went back and forth like this until finally, the man pulled from his wallet a small photograph of his wife and said, exasperated, “This! This is how she really looks!” Picasso studied the image for a moment and replied, “I never would have expected your wife to be so flat and tiny.”

Politics is like that too. It’s the way societies make decisions — and will always be, to some degree, a fight over meaning, over the consensus of what, or even who, exists and how. At one point in history there was no word to describe my queerness as an identity — there was only behavior, no category, no community. In such a world, fighting for my equal rights to be guaranteed by law and governance would be impossible. There are people today who still deny the real gender of trans people by denying fundamentally that they exist. That is a fight happening today in the public political sphere and one I am willing to fight for.

Democracy is the process that best facilitates this evolution of meaning — a system that is supposed to guarantee, at least in principle, equal access to participation in those decisions. But when the *culture* of democracy decays, even if the *institutions* remain, it is something we no longer do as such. Democracy erodes not when votes stop being counted, but when meaning stops being shared and synthesized through connection and constructive conflict - when our capacity to see one another as real collapses. The decay of equal participation in representing our realities and shared meaning isn’t an accident; it’s the outcome of systems engineered to privatize meaning, to hollow democracy into performance for market player benefit and the result of inadequate linear systems: market logic incentives.

The issue here, then, is largely about where our information — and thus our shared sense of reality — is sourced. We’ve assumed for too long that an image can be neutral, a proof of accuracy and ‘reality’ - that it can be taken at face value, but that was never true.

Now, with our ability to alter, manipulate, or create images entirely anew, we face a crisis not simply of authenticity but of assumption. The phenomenon of fake news — and now its AI descendant, the deep fake — is not primarily about *accuracy*. In fact, most fake news is absurdly unbelievable. The power is a contextual one and lies not in being convincing but in filling the void left by the collapse of democratic culture itself. The gap between the destroyed culture of democracy and the unreformed institutions of democracy has grown so vast that there is now a class of people desperate to believe in underlying conspiracies — desperate to confirm, through the outrageous, their own suspicion that something unseen and corrupt must be controlling the world that no longer makes sense to them. This desperation is the symptom of civic disconnection. When people lose real participation in democracy, they start seeking belonging in fictions that mimic participation.

Creating with AI and then claiming a photo is unaltered certainly poses problems, but perhaps part of the problem is that we ever placed so much faith in the supposed “unaltered” images we saw on the nightly news. Having lived on multiple continents, I know how deeply cultural the presentation of “truth” is. Every angle, every word, every silence is a perspective. Every image is already a politics. And AI as a new entrant into this space, is itself a polity.

These AI systems themselves already represent the overrepresented, unless those whose realities are marginalized reclaim them intentionally. The data sets and the design of the large AI systems by Big Tech overwhelmingly reflects the white, the global north, the male, the abled and neurotypical, the corporate, the cis and heterosexual, the Judeo-Christian, the hegemonic, etc etc.. In effect, what the constant pull of hegemonic gaze does is systemically erase the existence of visibility, voice, and experiential reality of marganilized communities: BIPoC, Queer, disabled, immigrant, female, etc. etc. The forced attempts to represent these communities through AI ends in a caricature of our existences - the view of our lives as other through that which is white, male, cis, abled, American/Global North and heterosexual. It is a world where women are oversexualized, Arabs are first seen as suspected terrorists, queer people are only white gay men ready to party, and on and on.

But AI, if used with awareness, can also allow creation at higher levels. It can let us express our realities, our experiences, our meanings, even our existence.

We must understand these tools for what they are. The realities we see in images — whether captured or generated — are never the truth themselves, but they can point toward truth as experience and meaning when used with intention. Yet not all images bear the same weight in how they point. A photograph carries within it a trace of the world — an index of what once *was* — that grounds it in a shared reality. Generated images, in contrast, are born untethered; they reach for truth not through record but through imagination. Both can reveal or deceive, but they do so through different relationships to the real. To collapse that difference entirely would be to lose the unique power of either — and to risk a world where meaning dissolves into pure projection, where every image belongs only to the beholder. In an age where the existence of many of us is still under question, showing our truths is not indulgence; it is the battle we fight. We must use AI and its potential to collaborate in creation — not to mislead, but to elevate our realities, to move our collective understanding forward, to transform us for the better.

Back to the Frog: I created this image in AI using prompt instructions and two “real” photographs — though neither of them are of a moment I personally witnessed. One of those images, in fact, cannot even be found online in China; the government has used its power to erase its pixelated representation. That erasure is not technological — it is political. For many Chinese today neither this digital representation or the event exists as a result of the repression of reality when unjust power is confronted with democracy. And the question we face now as a society is how we will use these new tools — as we once used the camera, film, or Photoshop — either to serve the authoritarians who deny meaning and control what is seen, or to resist them by reclaiming the act of seeing itself.

The truth was never in the image. It lives in what we make of it — together. In its best case, in a practice we call Democracy.

This is where the future must go: toward reclaiming the power to create, and creating so that the people hold the power. Toward using intelligence — artificial or otherwise — to expand what is possible for collective meaning, agency, and imagination.
