### TEAMS! Newsletter | Stewarship Edition | April 10, 2026

Most nonprofit leaders wake up thinking about the gap: the gap between rising demand for services and tightening constraints on funding, talent, and time. We treat these as external problems to be managed through better fundraising or leaner operations. But there is a quieter, more systemic leak in our organizations, the "soft" stuff we push to the bottom of the board agenda because it feels less urgent than a budget shortfall. We talk about culture in hushed tones of "well-being" and "morale," yet we rarely treat it as the hardwired engine that determines whether our mission succeeds.

I recently revisited the work of **James Heskett**, the **UPS Foundation Professor Emeritus at Harvard Business School**, and his seminal book, *Win from Within: Build Organizational Culture for Competitive Advantage*. Heskett is a pioneer who helped link employee engagement to organizational results decades ago. His research confirms what many of us feel but struggle to prove: culture isn't a "soft" administrative concern; it is a quantifiable, primary engine of performance that can account for up to 50% of the difference in an organization’s operating performancecompared with competitors. In the social services sector, where our people *are* our product, culture is our competitive advantage and our most sustainable superpower in delivering our mission.

### Executive Summary: The "Win from Within" Philosophy

The central thesis of Heskett’s work is that organizations do not fail because of bad strategy; they fail because their culture is too rigid to execute necessary strategic pivots. For non-profits, this means moving away from a "crisis mentality" born of scarcity and toward a "Culture Cycle" that treats internal health as a leading indicator of social impact. By quantifying culture through the **"Four Rs"—Referrals, Retention, Returns to Labor, and Relationships** —leaders can move from "passionate lip service" to a structured, data-backed transformation that yields results in 6 to 12 months.

### Key Takeaways

- ***Culture is Strategy’s Foundation:***The old cliché "culture eats strategy for lunch" is a myth; instead, they exist in a symbiotic relationship in which culture provides the agility to survive market shifts.
- ***The "Four Rs" are Your Leading Indicators:*** Financial statements are "rearview mirrors," while metrics such as employee referrals and retention serve as "windshields" that predict your organization's future health.
- ***Transformation Moves at the Speed of Trust:*** Cultural change doesn't take decades; determined leadership can hardwire new behaviors and drive substantial shifts within a single year.
- ***The Middle Management Revival:*** In our new world of hybrid and remote work, middle managers are vital conduits who ensure mission and culture sharing and training doesn't "fall off the grid".
- ***Toxic Performers are Culture Killers:***A leader’s true test is the courage to remove "producers" who meet their numbers but undermine and damage the organization's core values.

### Applying the Framework to Social Services

***Moving Beyond the "Lunch" Myth:*** We often hear that culture is more important than strategy. Heskett argues that they are two sides of the same coin. In a non-profit context, consider a food bank shifting from a "distribution model" to a "root-cause advocacy model." That is a massive strategic pivot. If the internal culture is "smartest person in the room" (rigid), staff will resist the change. If the culture is one of "idea sharing" (agile the strategy succeeds because people are ready to adapt.

***Quantifying the "Soft" Stuff: The Four Rs:***Heskett’s "Four Rs" framework is a gift to non-profit boards who want data.

- ***Referrals:*** Do your volunteers and donors advocate for you? This reflects community trust.
- ***Retention:*** High turnover is a "resilience proxy." Losing a veteran social worker isn't just a hiring cost; it’s a loss of institutional knowledge and client trust.
- ***Returns to Labor:*** In the social sector, this is our "Efficiency Proxy." It asks: Are we maximizing the value of our limited resources by reducing administrative friction?
- ***Relationships:*** This is the depth of your donor and community partnerships—the "Trust Proxy" that sustains you through funding droughts.

***The Myth of the Long Transformation:***Many CEOs and Executive Directors avoid cultural work because they believe it takes many years. Heskett’s 14-point action plan shows that basic changes can occur in six months. This is critical for non-profits facing urgent societal shifts. By clearly defining "From/To" behaviors, for example, "From honoring the staff member who works the most overtime to honoring the team that collaborates most effectively", leaders can quickly reset the organization’s "operating system."

***Leadership as the Primary Task:***Culture cannot be delegated to HR. It is the primary responsibility of the Executive Director or CEO and the entire leadership team. This involves "Modeling Owner Thinking," for example, Bill Marriott of Marriott Hotels picking up trash, and maintaining "Visibility" to stay out of the ivory tower. In the social sector, this also means bridging the gap between internal culture and "Cultural Competence." If your internal culture is one of blame and suspicion, you will inevitably fail the diverse populations you serve externally.

***Managing in the Hybrid Era:*** Remote work is a "real challenge" for value-sharing and teaching. We are seeing a rise in "screen-to-screen" employment, where workers feel no emotional connection to the mission. Heskett suggests that middle managers are the "key to remote work success." They must shift from "management" to "engagement," ensuring that even remote volunteers feel "connected, valued, and safe."

### Putting It into Action

- ***Quantify Your "Four Rs":*** Review your data from the past twelve months. Is your staff referral rate up or down? What about volunteer retention? Treat these metrics as seriously as your P&L.
- ***Conduct a "From/To" Discussion:***Sit with your leadership team to define one specific cultural shift needed. "From \[Old Behavior\] to \[New Behavior\].”
- ***Identify the "Believers":*** Ensure the right people are on the "bus." If you have managers who are meeting their goals but poisoning the well, it’s time for a difficult conversation.
- ***Practice "Management by Walking Around":*** Even if your "walking" is virtual, increase your visibility. Ask frontline staff: "What is the biggest friction point in your day?"
- ***Hardwire one value:*** Pick a core value and integrate it into your performance reviews. If "Transparency" is a value, make "Information Sharing" a measurable goal.

### Closing Thoughts

Our strategies are always influenced by the economy, political shifts, and global crises. But an adaptive, agile culture is an enduring foundation that cannot be easily hacked or copied. When we "Win from Within," we aren't just making better places to work; we are building the resilience needed to solve the complex social challenges of the future. Let’s start treating it as the primary engine of our mission and impact.
