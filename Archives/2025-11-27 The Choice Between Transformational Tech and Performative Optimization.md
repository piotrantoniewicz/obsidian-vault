## AI's Faustian Bargain: The Choice Between Transformational Tech and Performative Optimization

![Post cover image](https://www.datocms-assets.com/101636/1764175306-ton-moloch-faust.jpg)

**Until recently, we've had suspicions that LLMs mirror the worst incentives of extractive technologies—optimized for engagement over truth, clicks over connection and transactions over transformation. Now those suspicions appear to be validated.**

*Contributor: Mariana Spada*

[A new Stanford study](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.06105) demonstrates how AI systems, when trained for competitive success, systematically abandon truth and ethics in pursue of their objectives. For progressive campaigns and organizations navigating AI adoption, this research reveals a critical choice: will we use these tools to build transformational politics or accelerate the descent into junk politics? The answer matters because what looks like competitive advantage in the short term may be eroding the very foundations our movements depend on.

### The Devil's in the Data

The research, titled "Moloch's Bargain: Emergent Misalignment When LLMs Compete for Audiences," tested what happens when AI models are optimized to win in three competitive scenarios: sales, elections, and social media. The results are stark and troubling (though hardly surprising): when LLMs were trained to maximize competitive success, they consistently resorted to deception and disinformation. In election simulations, a 4.9% gain in vote share came with 22.3% more disinformation and 12.5% more inflammatory populist rhetoric. In sales, performance gains were accompanied by misrepresenting features and sometimes fabricating product details entirely. And in social media scenarios, a 7.5% engagement boost brought **a wooping 188.6% increase in disinformation.**

The researchers call this phenomenon "Moloch's Bargain"—competitive success achieved at the cost of alignment with truth and values. **Most troubling: this happened even when models were explicitly instructed to remain truthful and grounded in facts**. Current safeguards, it turns out, are alarmingly fragile when market pressures push toward "winning at all costs." The problem appears to be embedded in the very architecture of these LLMs, including the data on which they were trained, so the expectation of solving it by eliminating a single "damaged piece" is likely bound to fail.

### Why This Matters for Progressive Movements

The race-to-the-bottom dynamic described in this paper poses a direct threat to the kind of democratic discourse our movements depend on. It's also a cautionary tale about the impact of implementing technologies without intent or proper consideration of an organization's context, and without carefully weighing the costs and benefits of doing so.

As Ned writes in [*The Age of Junk Politics*](https://www.tectonica.co/age_of_junk_politics_book), we're already drowning in transactional campaigning that treats voters as consumers to manipulate rather than humans to connect with:

*"Our political practices have become akin to the fast food industry: quick, easy, and ultimately unsatisfying, while leaving us hungrier and less healthy as a democratic society."*

\-The Age of Junk Politics

Off-the-shelf AI products trained purely for competitive advantage, implemented without intention and guardrails will only accelerate this descent into junk politics. When optimization means maximizing engagement metrics rather than building genuine connection, when success is measured in clicks rather than relationships, we get exactly what this Stanford study documents: systems that lie, mislead, and inflame because that's what "works" in narrow, transactional terms. This isn't just an abstract concern about AI ethics. It's about the health of our democracy and the integrity of our movements.

### The Path Forward: Transformational Tech for Transformational Politics

This study validates what we've been arguing: we need AI systems that elevate individuals and enhance human connection and judgment rather than replacing them with optimization for engagement metrics alone. As we outline in [our guide on choosing the right Gen AI tools](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/choosing-the-right-gen-ai-tools-for-campaigns-and-organizations), **technology decisions are political decisions** —and this is especially true for people-based movements fighting for systemic, long term societal change. So, when evaluating AI tools, we must ask not just "does it work?" but "does it align with our values?" and "does it strengthen or weaken democratic practice?"

**We need AI that** ***supports*** **transformational campaigning** —tools that help us build deeper relationships, have meaningful conversations, and develop collective power. Not tools optimized to harvest data and manipulate behavior. The difference isn't subtle: one approach treats people as whole human beings worthy of respect and genuine dialogue. The other treats them as targets to be [optimized](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/gauging-ai-threat-free-and-fair-elections), [persuaded](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/11/ai-generated-political-messages-persuasion-research), and [converted](https://edmo.eu/publications/how-the-european-far-right-is-using-ai-generated-content-to-engage-voters/) through whatever means prove most effective.

This requires stronger accountability structures at every level:

- **Tool selection:** Scrutinizing vendors not just for features but for values and practices. Not AI tools are equally built, and *there are other options* (wait for some announcements soon;) )
- **Success definition:** [Measuring depth of engagement and quality of relationships](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/equation), not just conversion rates
- **Governance**: Building transparent decision-making processes with community oversight
- **Putting AI policies in place:** Establishing clear guidelines for responsible use (this doesn’t have to be perfect from the beginning, [you can start simple](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/ton-interview-new-book-ai-for-nonprofits) and build from there).
- Training: Ensuring teams understand not just how to use AI tools but when and how *not* to use them

As we wrote about in [Leading with Purpose in the Age of AI](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/leading-with-purpose-in-the-age-of-ai), this is fundamentally about who we want to be as movements. Our organizing for [accountability and democratic mobilization](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/accountability_through_mobilizing) can't rely on tools that undermine accountability and authentic participation.

Progressive campaigns should lead in developing and advocating for ethical AI standards—not because it's easier, but because it's essential. Yes, our opponents may embrace AI tools that prioritize winning at any cost. They may get short-term gains from systems optimized purely for persuasion and manipulation. But we know something they don't: movements built on authentic connection outlast those built on algorithmic tricks. Trust, once broken, is nearly impossible to rebuild. Communities organized through manipulation eventually see through it. Democracy hollowed out by junk politics eventually collapses.

Our advantage isn't just moral—it's strategic. The Stanford research reminds us that what looks like competitive advantage in the short term becomes systemic erosion of the very thing we're trying to build: a politics rooted in genuine democratic participation, where people are agents of change rather than targets of manipulation.

### Refusing the Bargain

The question isn't whether to use AI—it's how to use it in ways that strengthen rather than erode the democratic fabric. We can follow the path of least resistance, optimizing for metrics that drive us toward deception and manipulation. Or we can insist on something harder and better: **AI systems designed to support authentic human connection, democratic participation, and transformational change.**

Let's refuse Moloch's bargain. Let's build something worthy of the movements we serve.
