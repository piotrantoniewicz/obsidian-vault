A few weeks ago, I was chatting with someone about their team’s early wins with AI. They’d started using it for drafting donor communications, summarising meeting notes, pulling together board reports. The usual starting points. And it was working. People were saving real time, sometimes hours a week.

So I asked the obvious next question: what are they doing with that time?

The answer was a long pause, followed by something like, “I mean, they’re just getting through more of their to-do list.”

This is the trap. And almost every organisation I talk to falls into it.

Someone saves two hours on a task that used to take an afternoon. Great. So now they spend those two hours on the next pile of admin. The workload stays the same. The pace stays the same. The only thing that changed is the tool. And three months later, leadership wonders why morale hasn’t shifted and nobody seems particularly excited about AI anymore.

**The reward for doing more work in less time should not be more work.**

I know that sounds obvious when you read it on a screen. But in practice, it’s the default in almost every organisation I’ve worked with. Not because anyone decided it should be that way. Because nobody decided it shouldn’t be.

**What happens with saved time is a leadership decision**

This is one of those areas where the absence of a decision is itself a decision. If leadership doesn’t explicitly define what saved time gets used for, it gets absorbed. It fills back up with whatever was next on the list. Emails. Reporting. Data entry. The kind of work that expands to fill whatever time is available to it.

I think there are roughly three categories of reinvestment worth considering, and the best organisations I’ve seen do all three in some proportion.

The first is experimentation. This is probably the most important, and the hardest to protect. Once people have some fluency with AI tools, they start noticing opportunities that nobody planned for. A better way to onboard volunteers. A dashboard that could tell a story about program impact. A process that’s been manual for years but doesn’t need to be. Those ideas only surface when people have the time and permission to explore. Without protected time, they stay in someone’s head, filed under “when things slow down.” Things never slow down.

The second is human connection work. This is the one I think gets overlooked most often in the AI conversation. Consider a fundraiser who’s been spending four hours a week drafting donor emails. AI brings that down to one. The question is whether those three hours go back into the admin pile or into picking up the phone and actually talking to donors. Building relationships. Having the conversations that no AI tool can replicate. In a sector built on trust and connection, the ability to reinvest efficiency gains into more human work is genuinely transformative. Not in the buzzy, marketing sense of that word. In the practical sense that donors who get phone calls give differently to donors who get emails.

The third is the work that was never a priority. Every nonprofit I’ve ever worked with has a backlog. Projects that matter but aren’t urgent. The onboarding guide that’s been on someone’s list for three years. The impact report that could unlock new funding if someone had the time to do it properly. The volunteer training materials that everyone knows are outdated but nobody has capacity to fix. That backlog is a graveyard of good intentions, and AI-driven efficiency is the first real opportunity most organisations have had to actually work through it.

**The backlog reframe**

I keep hearing a version of this concern in conversations: if AI makes us more efficient, does that mean we need fewer people?

It’s a fair question, and I don’t think dismissing it helps anyone. But I also think it’s the wrong framing for most nonprofits.

Think about it this way. What nonprofit organisation is saying, “I don’t have enough things for people to do”? They’re all saying the opposite. We need more people. We need more resources. We need more hours in the day. The backlog isn’t a nice-to-have list. It’s full of work that would genuinely advance the mission if anyone had the capacity to do it.

AI doesn’t remove the need for people. It removes the excuse for not getting to the work that matters most. But only if someone makes the decision to direct that capacity toward it.

Will AI impact hiring and how many people organisations need over time? Almost certainly. But that’s true across every sector, not just ours. And for most nonprofits, the more immediate reality is that they have more work than they can handle and a team that’s stretched thin. The efficiency gains from AI aren’t a threat to those teams. They’re a release valve, if leadership treats them that way.

**The start-up thought experiment**

One exercise I’ve found useful with organisations is deceptively simple. Ask yourself: if we were founding this organisation today, from scratch, with the tools that exist right now, how would we do things differently?

I was talking to someone about a European nonprofit that did something close to this. They had a team of around 30 people, and they pulled four of them out and said, see if you can rebuild our whole organisation just using AI. Not as a threat. As a genuine experiment. What would we look like if we started fresh?

I love that, because it forces you out of the incremental mindset. Most AI adoption conversations are about taking existing processes and making them slightly faster. This is a different question entirely. It’s asking whether the processes themselves still make sense.

We’ve all been doing things the same way because that’s how they’ve always been done. And I think there’s an opportunity, with the time that AI creates, to step back and ask whether “how we’ve always done it” is still the right answer. Usually, it’s not. But you never get to that question if every saved hour just gets swallowed by the inbox.

**This is a leadership conversation, not a technology one**

I want to be direct about something. None of what I’ve described here requires any technical knowledge. It doesn’t require a new tool or a bigger budget. It requires one decision from leadership: we are going to be intentional about what happens with the time AI gives us back.

That decision is the leverage point. Without it, your team saves time and nothing changes. The work just compresses. People might even burn out faster, because now there’s an expectation that everything should take less time, but nobody reduced the volume.

With it, you create space for the work that actually moves your mission forward. The relationships that need building. The ideas that need exploring. The projects that have been waiting years for someone to have the capacity to take them on.

Your team is going to get faster with AI. That’s almost inevitable at this point. The question that matters is what your organisation does with the difference. And that question belongs to leadership, not to the tools.
