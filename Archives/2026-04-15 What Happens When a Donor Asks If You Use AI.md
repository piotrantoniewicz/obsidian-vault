Most people cringe when they hear the word “policy.” It conjures images of documents written once, filed somewhere in SharePoint, and never opened again. I get it. That reputation is earned.

But here’s what I keep running into when I talk to nonprofit leaders about AI: they tell me they know their staff are using it. They just don’t know how.

That sentence should be a red flag, and in most organisations, it isn’t being treated as one.

In the last [article](https://kylebehrend.substack.com/p/what-three-years-of-conversations?utm_source=profile&utm_medium=reader2), I laid out the zoomed-out map — the full picture of how AI adoption fits together for nonprofits. I said the first step isn’t signing up for ChatGPT. It’s having the conversation. This piece is about the vehicle for that conversation, and why the humble AI policy is the most underestimated tool in the entire process.

## The scaffolding, not the cage

When I talk about AI policy with organisations, I’m not talking about a compliance document that tells people what they can’t do. I’m talking about scaffolding — something that gives people a framework to operate within so they can actually grow.

A good AI policy provides clarity. It tells your team when and how they can use AI, when they shouldn’t, what data they can feed into these tools, and what stays out. It creates shared language across the organisation so that everyone, from the CEO to the newest caseworker, is operating from the same understanding.

That clarity matters more than most leaders realise, and not just for efficiency reasons. It matters for trust.

Consider this scenario. Your organisation uses predictive AI to show different donors different suggested donation amounts on a page, based on their previous giving history. A donor calls and asks whether you use AI. One staff member says no. Another says yes. You’ve just created a trust problem — not because of how you used the technology, but because your people weren’t on the same page about it.

Donors are going to start asking these questions. Funders already are. And in a sector built on trust, particularly in philanthropy and fundraising, having inconsistent answers isn’t a minor issue. It’s a reputational risk you can prevent with a conversation and a document.

## The living document

Here’s where the framing matters. An AI policy isn’t something you write once and shelve. The technology moves too fast for that. Features that didn’t exist when you drafted your policy will emerge within weeks. New integrations will change what’s possible — and what’s risky.

I use a specific example when I talk about this. Early on, many organisations adopted the sensible-sounding rule: don’t put any private data into AI tools. Reasonable enough. Then the major platforms rolled out connectors — the ability to plug in your Google Drive, your Gmail, your SharePoint — and suddenly people were linking their entire organisational filing systems to AI tools without anyone stopping to ask: does this go against our policy?

Nobody reviewed it. Nobody flagged it. Because the policy was treated as a finished thing rather than a living one.

I recommend reviewing your AI policy at least every couple of months. Not because you need to rewrite it each time, but because the landscape shifts that quickly, and your policy needs to reflect how your organisation is actually using these tools, not how it was using them six months ago.

## What goes in it

I’ve developed a template that’s freely available on my [website](https://aiimpacthub.com/free-resources/), and I walk organisations through a version of it regularly. The skeleton of a good nonprofit AI policy covers several key areas.

**Definition and scope.** What are you actually talking about when you say “AI”? This policy might focus on generative AI specifically, but you may also need to address predictive AI separately. Getting clear on what’s in scope prevents confusion later.

**A curiosity mindset within guardrails.** The policy should encourage exploration, not shut it down. The goal is responsible experimentation — creating the conditions for people to try things while managing risk.

**Data privacy and protection.** This is the big one. What data can be used with AI tools? What absolutely cannot? Are your team members on paid plans that opt out of using your data to train models? If they’re on free tiers, do they understand the difference?

**Human oversight.** Every output needs a human in the loop. AI drafts; people decide. Quality control, fact-checking, and final judgement stay with your team. This needs to be explicit, not assumed.

**Transparency and accountability.** Will you disclose when AI has been used in your content? Under what circumstances? And critically — who is accountable when something goes wrong?

**Training and review.** Who is responsible for making sure people actually know how to work within this policy? How often will the policy itself be reviewed? And who is the go-to person for questions about compliance or concerns?

None of these sections need to be lengthy. Some of the most effective AI policies I’ve seen are a few pages at most. **What matters is that they’re clear, shared, and actually used.**

## The real value is the conversation

Here’s what I’ve found organisations don’t expect when they start building their AI policy: the process itself is where the magic happens.

When you say “we’re developing our AI policy,” it opens the door to questions that most organisations have been quietly avoiding. Where are people already using AI? What tools are they using? What’s working? What’s not? Where do they think the organisation should be using it? What makes them uncomfortable?

Those conversations surface things no leader could discover through a survey or an all-staff email. They reveal the shadow use that’s already happening, the fears people haven’t voiced, the ideas people have been sitting on because they didn’t know if they were allowed to explore them.

The policy gives people permission — not just to use AI, but to talk about it openly. And that visibility is the foundation everything else gets built on. You can’t train people effectively if you don’t know where they are. You can’t manage risk if you don’t know what’s happening. You can’t build a culture of experimentation if people feel like they need to hide what they’re doing.

## The leverage point

In systems thinking terms, this is an intervention that changes the information flows in your organisation. AI goes from something happening invisibly across dozens of desks to something the organisation understands, guides, and learns from collectively. That shift — from invisible to visible, from individual to shared — is where the disproportionate impact lives.

It’s not expensive. It doesn’t require a consultant, though having someone walk you through it can help. It doesn’t require technical expertise. It requires a decision to start, and a commitment to keep the conversation going.

If you read the last article and thought “right, but what do I actually do on Monday morning,” this is it. Start the policy conversation. Not because the document itself will transform your organisation, but because it creates the conditions for everything that comes after.

The scaffolding goes up first. Then people can build.
