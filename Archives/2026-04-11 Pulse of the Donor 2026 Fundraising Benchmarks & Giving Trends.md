### Executive Summary: smaller gifts masked record monthly giving growth

###### The paradox

Average gift sizes were down in 2025. On the surface, that looked like a warning. But conversion rates were rising. Mobile was accelerating. And social — the channel most organizations judged by the first gift — was quietly building recurring pipelines at twice the rate of email within 60 days.

###### The reality

Donors weren’t pulling back. They were moving faster, committing smaller amounts upfront, and converting to monthly giving at rates that reward patience over immediacy. In every market, mobile conversion grew faster than desktop. And social donors, written off as low-value at acquisition, were among the strongest recurring converters in the data.

###### The opportunity

The donors are there. The signals are consistent. The organizations that grow in 2026 will be the ones whose revenue architecture is built around how donors actually give today — mobile-first, social-driven, and commitment-led.

## 

**How we built** this year’s Pulse of the Donor

We analyzed millions of transactions across our global nonprofit network, standardizing currencies to USD, and applying statistical controls to ensure the data was accurate and useful.

### Data sources

Anonymized, non-PII transaction data from 1,000+ nonprofit organizations across four countries (United States, Canada, United Kingdom, Australia) that processed donations through Fundraise Up in 2025.

### Geographic coverage

Our primary focus was on the United States (largest sample, clearest behavioral patterns), with comparative context from Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia. Where patterns were US-specific vs. universal, this was explicitly noted.

### Organization inclusion criteria

Organizations were active on the platform for a minimum of 12 months (24 months for YoY metrics), had at least 500 successful donations in the analysis period, had at least 6 months with 5+ donations per month, and had statistical outliers removed.

### YoY calculation methodology

For each metric, we calculate organization-level YoY change. Median of these changes reported (more robust than comparing aggregate averages). High-value donations (>$10,000) were excluded to prevent outlier distortion.

CONVERSION

### 

Conversion **grew unevenly** across markets

More donors converted this year than in 2024 across nearly every market, except for Canada. The UK led with 12% growth, nearly double Australia’s 6.8% gain. The US grew modestly at 1.6%.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/bf628d78df/conversions-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/bf628d78df/conversions-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Canada held flat at zero growth while the UK posted 12% — a gap that can’t be explained by donor intent alone. The range of outcomes within each market tells the real story: in the US, some organizations saw double-digit declines while others posted double-digit gains. The same wide spread appears in every market. Overall conversion growth is real, but it is concentrated — a meaningful share of organizations declined even in markets where the median moved up.

Social media was the primary driver of conversion growth across markets, with social traffic posting the strongest YoY gains of any channel in all four countries. Email also grew consistently across all markets, reflecting its role in reaching already-engaged audiences. Search, by contrast, declined in the US and Canada.

### 

Why this **matters**

Conversion growth is unevenly distributed, which means the return on optimization efforts isn’t equal across markets either. The UK’s 12% gain signals an audience that’s increasingly ready to complete transactions — improvements to checkout, mobile experience, or campaign messaging build on top of that momentum. Understanding where your market sits in this distribution is the input that makes prioritization decisions defensible rather than intuitive.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/66be8e0e48/conversion-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/66be8e0e48/conversion-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Benchmark your conversion rate against your country, not a global average.
2. If you’re below benchmark, audit your checkout for friction: unnecessary fields, slow load times, unclear asks.
3. Since social traffic skews heavily toward mobile, consider express checkout options like Apple Pay or Google Pay to reduce completion time.

CHANNELS

### 

**Social wins on conversion**, search on value

In the US, social traffic drove 10% conversion growth. Social first gift value was $14.40 versus $71 for search. The 60-day recurring conversion data shows why that gap doesn’t tell the whole story.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/d510ada5a7/channels-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/d510ada5a7/channels-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Search first give value leads in every market — $71 in the US, $71.40 in Canada, $63.80 in Australia, $40 in the UK — but search conversion declined in the US (-0.7%) and Canada (-1.5%). Social shows the opposite pattern: high conversion momentum and low first-transaction value.

Social donors consistently converted to recurring giving within 60 days at higher rates than email donors across all four markets. In the US, social’s 60-day recurring rate was 5.8% versus 2.9% for email. The UK showed a similar gap at 6.7% versus 3%. Australia showed the starkest difference: social converted at 9.1% within 60 days compared to 2.1% for email — more than four times the rate.

### 

Why this **matters**

First-transaction value measures the entry point, not the relationship. If you measure social only on the value of the first gift, you may be under-investing in your highest-LTV acquisition source. Social donors convert to recurring giving within 60 days at substantially higher rates than email donors in every market — which means the leadership decision is not which channel drives the biggest first gift, but which channel builds the most durable donor relationships.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/6440d7e33a/channels-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/6440d7e33a/channels-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Track social by 60-day recurring conversion rate alongside first gift value to get an accurate picture of channel value.
2. Run cohort analysis comparing social versus search donors at the 6-month mark.
3. Use channel-specific post-gift upsells — emotional, story-driven messaging on social and impact-based content for search.
4. Rely on AI-powered suggested amounts to optimize presets by source, keeping social presets lower to match first-gift behavior.

SOCIAL MEDIA

### 

**Facebook = donations**, TikTok = commitment

Facebook drove strong conversion growth everywhere — up 9.6% in the US, 40% in Canada, 17.5% in the UK, and 49% in Australia — while Instagram, LinkedIn, X, and YouTube declined.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/2ffe2ebcf5/social-media-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/2ffe2ebcf5/social-media-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

The "social" channel is not a single audience. Facebook is growing as a conversion driver while others contract. TikTok tells a different story: it has the highest recurring enrollment rate of any social media in the US at 18.7% and Australia at 22.2%, despite not appearing as a conversion growth leader. Facebook brings donors in; TikTok appears to bring donors who commit.

### 

Why this **matters**

Treating social as a single channel produces averages that obscure two meaningfully different donor behaviors. Platform-level data changes both where you invest and how you structure the ask — Facebook donors and TikTok donors are not the same audience arriving through the same door.

And if you're measuring those channels only on immediate Return On Ad Spend, you’re making it worse. A TikTok donor who converts at a lower rate today but recurs at 2x the rate over 18 months doesn’t look like your best acquisition source, until you stop measuring the door and start measuring where it leads.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/497x476/0169ca5aa2/social-media-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/497x476/0169ca5aa2/social-media-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Consider allocating social spend toward Facebook if it's your primary conversion driver.
2. Structure TikTok content and asks around recurring giving, given its disproportionate recurring enrollment rate.
3. Monitor LinkedIn, Instagram, and YouTube performance in your specific market before maintaining significant investment in those platforms.

GIFT AMOUNTS

### 

Smaller gifts, **more donors**

One-time gift amounts declined in the US (-2%), Canada (-2.5%), and Australia (-1%). Monthly gift amounts fell further: US -5%, Canada -9%, Australia -14%. The UK was the exception, with both growing.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/84abc6e003/gift-amount-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/84abc6e003/gift-amount-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Declining gift amounts coincided with growing conversion rates across most markets — more donors giving smaller amounts. The recurring enrollment picture adds another layer: the UK sits in the growth leader position with both monthly gift amounts and enrollment rates rising. The US shows a sustainable commitment pattern — enrollment up, amounts down. Canada and Australia show both metrics declining, which is the more urgent market signal.

### 

Why this **matters**

Two things can be true at once: average gift amounts are falling and the active donor base is growing. Organizations optimizing solely for average gift size are reading 2025’s data backwards. The more important signal is whether enrollment is growing, because that’s what determines the long-term revenue base, not the size of any single transaction.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/952x994/75e09d0a98/gift-amounts-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/952x994/75e09d0a98/gift-amounts-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Track recurring enrollment rate alongside average gift amount rather than treating gift size as the primary health metric.
2. In markets where both metrics are declining — Canada and Australia — prioritize enrollment recovery before gift size optimization.
3. Use AI-suggested amounts to calibrate presets by market, device, and channel rather than applying a single default.

DIGITAL WALLETS

### 

Digital wallets displace cards, **but at a cost**

Digital wallets are the fastest-growing payment category across all four markets. The speed they add to checkout comes with a consistent tradeoff: smaller one-time gifts in most markets.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/4d1ee5a782/wallets-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/4d1ee5a782/wallets-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

The gift size gap between credit cards and digital wallets is significant in most markets. In the US, credit card one-time gifts averaged $239.40 versus $163.50 for Apple Pay — a 32% difference. Canada shows a 26% gap, the UK 42%. Australia is the exception: Apple Pay donors gave more on average ($144.20) than credit card donors ($135.80). At the same time Fundraise Up followed up this trend and released features to support it: we’ve added Express Checkout to the mobile web flow. It delivered a 1.5% increase in one-time donation conversions, confirming that removing friction improves completion rates.

### 

Why this **matters**

Donors are choosing speed, and the checkout experience shapes what they give. The gift size gap isn’t inevitable — Australia’s data shows market context and checkout design both play a role. The strategic question isn’t whether to support digital wallets, but how to configure the experience around them.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/2a92173efa/wallets-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/2a92173efa/wallets-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Use AI-suggested amounts calibrated by payment method to present appropriate presets for digital wallet users.
2. Add a post-gift upsell for wallet completions — wallet donors are strong candidates for recurring upgrade prompts.
3. Track wallet donor behavior at 60 and 90 days to capture the full value picture.

PAYMENT TYPES

### 

Payment types **vary widely** across markets

Canada shows 80% credit card dominance. The UK leads on Apple Pay at 24%. Australia leads on PayPal at 27%. The US has the most fragmented payment landscape of any market we looked at.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/cbe9429f6e/payment-types-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/cbe9429f6e/payment-types-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Credit cards remain dominant across all four markets but are declining — down 4.8% in the US, 3.3% in Canada, 6.3% in UK, and 5.8% in Australia. Apple Pay is the only method growing in every market: 38% in the US, 9% in Canada, 38% in the UK, and 13% in Australia. In the US, Venmo dropped 71% and now represents just 0.5% of donations. Payment preferences are not converging across markets, they are diverging.

### 

Why this **matters**

Checkout optimization is market specific. Canada’s 80% credit card share means optimizing deeply for one method. The UK’s 24% Apple Pay share aligns directly with its position as the highest mobile giving market. Australia’s 26% PayPal share makes it a primary giving channel there, not a secondary option. A payment hierarchy built for one market will underserve another.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/62c473f293/payment-types-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/62c473f293/payment-types-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. In Canada, prioritize frictionless credit card payments — fast load times and saved card support.
2. In the UK and the US, treat Apple Pay as a primary checkout option alongside credit cards.
3. In Australia, position PayPal as a primary giving channel, not an alternative.
4. In the US, monitor Venmo as a possible signal of shifting preferences — its 71% decline and 0.5% share reflect broader consolidation toward digital wallets.

MOBILE

### 

Mobile now **dominates giving** in three markets

The UK leads at 64.4% mobile giving share. Australia reached 52.1% YoY after a 10% increase YoY. The US crossed the majority threshold at 51.1%. Canada trails at 39.6% but gained 8 points YoY.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/3e61446382/mobile-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/3e61446382/mobile-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Mobile conversion rates are growing faster than desktop in every market. Mobile completion time dropped faster than desktop YoY — the UK showed the sharpest decrease in completion time at -11% on mobile versus -4% on desktop. Desktop one-time gifts remain 1.5 to 2 times larger than mobile gifts across all markets, and monthly gifts follow a similar pattern. The exception is Australia, where the desktop-to-mobile gap for monthly gifts is more than twice, with desktop averaging $52 versus $24 on mobile. 

### 

Why this **matters**

Mobile is the primary entry point for giving in three markets and is closing fast in the fourth. Smaller mobile gifts and larger desktop gifts are both expected behaviors — donors on mobile are giving in a different context, not a different mindset. When 50%+ of your donation revenue is already coming from mobile, a desktop-first design focus is a structural misalignment. Mobile-first UX is a revenue architecture decision; your budget, design priorities, and checkout configuration should reflect device revenue share. Organizations that build around mobile volume rather than trying to replicate desktop gift sizes are better positioned for where the distribution is heading.

### 

**What you can do** about it

CANCELLATIONS

### 

Self-service **decreases cancellations**

Orgs with self-service donor infrastructure saw cancellation completion rates drop across most markets in 2025. Portal logins rose 26% in the UK, 25% in Canada, 18% in the US, and 6% in Australia.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/cad3b6d172/cancellation-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/cad3b6d172/cancellation-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Donor retention held in line with industry norms: US 20%, UK 19%, Canada 26%, and Australia 26%. Canada and Australia lead retention, likely reflecting less fragmented donor attention markets. On cancellations, the data shows that when donors attempt to cancel, redirecting them to a payment method update rather than completing the cancellation is working — Canada and Australia each saved 12% of cancellation attempts this way.

### 

Why this **matters**

Retention improvements in 2025 came primarily from operational infrastructure — payment retry logic, save flows, and donor control tools — not stewardship alone. Donors who can manage their giving relationship through self-service tools are less likely to cancel. The portal login growth in Canada and the UK suggests donors are willing to engage when the tools are useful.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/e65adb2dcd/cancellations-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/e65adb2dcd/cancellations-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Enable AI-optimized payment retry timing to reduce involuntary churn.
2. Deploy proactive credit card expiration alerts 30 days to 60 days before expiry.
3. Invest in donor portal self-service with pause, reduce, and payment update options.
4. Use save flows that offer payment method alternatives before completing a cancellation.

GIVINGTUESDAY

### 

GivingTuesday delivers **exceptional conversion**

US conversion on GivingTuesday reached 40.2% — 50% above the annual average of 26.7%. Canada reached 51.7%, nearly 70% above its annual average of 30.5%.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/f33b29e807/giving-tuesday-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/f33b29e807/giving-tuesday-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Processing volume grew 25.4% YoY in the US and 34% in Canada on GivingTuesday. Conversion is high because intent is high — donors arrive on this day already committed. Recurring enrollment drops during GivingTuesday as donors shift into one-time completion psychology. Evening giving dominates in the US, with the peak at 8pm to 9pm becoming the primary spike of the day. Canada, normally a morning-peak market with no evening activity, transforms into an evening-peak market on GivingTuesday.

### 

Why this **matters**

GivingTuesday is a volume day for committed donors, not a persuasion day for undecided ones. The evening dominance in both markets means campaign momentum builds throughout the day and peaks after work hours — which has direct implications for email send timing and social campaign scheduling.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/25398c1e31/giving-tuesday-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/25398c1e31/giving-tuesday-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Segment donors who gave on GivingTuesday in prior years and target them with countdown and match campaigns.
2. Show higher presets to match giving season donor mindsets.
3. Schedule your heaviest campaign sends for late afternoon and early evening.
4. Use social proof elements to build momentum throughout the day.
5. Automate instant tax receipts for year-end tax planning.

GIVING SEASON

### 

**Year-end gifts are up** but recurring declined

One-time gift amounts spike 39% in the US and 25% in Canada during November and December. Monthly gift amounts rose modestly at 8% to 10%. Recurring enrollment dropped in both markets.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/c42d618513/giving-season-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/c42d618513/giving-season-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

During giving season, US recurring enrollment dropped from 9% to 7% of all donations. Canada dropped from 5.1% to 3%. Donors shift into one-time completion psychology — they are willing to give significantly more as a single gift but less likely to commit to an ongoing plan. The one-time gift spike is substantial: US average one-time gifts reached $291.40 during giving season versus a $209.90 baseline. Canada moved from $152.60 to $190.40.

### 

Why this **matters**

Giving season and recurring acquisition are working against each other in the same window. Donors who give in November and December are primed for larger one-time gifts — pushing recurring enrollment in that period works against the grain of demonstrated donor behavior. The more effective approach is to use giving season to maximize one-time gift size and reserve recurring acquisition efforts for the rest of the year.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/ce4d8d89a1/giving-season-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/ce4d8d89a1/giving-season-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Show elevated presets during giving season to match donor willingness to give larger one-time amounts.
2. Replace recurring upgrade messaging with year-end and tax-planning framing during November and December.
3. Target GivingTuesday and giving season donors for recurring conversion in January and February, when one-time completion psychology has passed.
4. Automate instant tax receipts for all giving season donations.

AI

### 

AI features generated **major revenue lift**

They accounted for 48% of the 70 A/B tests ran last year and drove the largest individual gains.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/1f7ff8124f/ai-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/1f7ff8124f/ai-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

When donors see AI-suggested gift amounts, organizations raise 10% to 15% more revenue on average — and acquire twice as many donors. The AI model that sets default donation frequency based on who’s visiting a campaign generated annual uplift across three tested variants. When the platform automatically determines whether to ask a donor to cover transaction fees, 87% say yes — producing a 3% revenue lift.

Smaller features add up, too. One-tap checkout via Apple Pay, Google Pay, or PayPal raised first gift value by 0.21% and lifted one-time conversion by 1.5%. Timed email reminders to donors who started but didn’t finish a donation converted 10% of those donors into completed gifts. AI-based retries for failed recurring charges reduced cancellations by 6%. Fraud detection flagged 1.3% of transactions and blocked 98.8% of them before they reached payment processing.

### 

Why this **matters**

AI features don’t operate in isolation — the largest gains come from combining suggested amounts, default frequency determination, and checkout optimization into a connected experience rather than enabling features individually.

AI optimization powers small decisions in real time for every donor: what amount to show, whether to suggest monthly giving, whether to ask about fees. Individually, each decision moves a small number. Together, across every transaction, they build. The 87% adoption rate of adaptive cost coverage signals that transparency about fees strengthens donor trust rather than weakening it.

**What you can do** about it

2. Set up email recovery for abandoned donations with AI-optimized timing.
3. Donors are increasingly aware that digital experiences are tailored to them. Organizations that are upfront about how it works build more durable trust. Make sure your donor communications are transparent about personalization.

### 

**Data governance & AI**: what nonprofit leaders need to know

Donors trust organizations with their personal and financial information. That trust extends to how organizations use AI. A data breach or improper data handling is both a compliance and a donor relationship problem. As AI becomes more embedded in fundraising infrastructure, the organizations maintaining donor confidence are those that have governance policies in place before something goes wrong, not after. Your guiding principles:

1. Audit which AI tools your team is currently using and whether they’re consumer or enterprise tier. If teams are using free tools for fundraising work, upgrade or restrict access.
2. Create a clear internal policy: no identifiable donor data in public AI tools. Use anonymized datasets, aggregated performance exports, or synthetic examples instead.
3. Ask your fundraising platform provider directly how your data is stored, whether it’s used to train shared models, and what protections are in place.
4. Document your AI data practices and make them available to your board.

TRIBUTES

### 

Tribute rates vary by **sector and culture**

The US and Canada see 13% tribute donation rates — more than twice the UK’s 6% and Australia’s 5%. Healthcare leads all sectors at 24% to 26% in North America.

![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/8806927cc5/tribute-big.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/2666x1500/8806927cc5/tribute-big.png/m/1540x1392/filters:format(png):quality(60))

### 

What the **data shows**

Tribute giving — donations made in honor or memory of a person — makes up 9% of all donations on average when the option is available, but varies significantly by market and sector. Healthcare shows the highest rates across all markets: 26% in the US, 24% in Canada, 12% in the UK, and 13% in Australia. International affairs is consistently lowest across all markets. Religion sits at 9% in the US and Canada despite high overall donor engagement — religious giving is more often about belonging than memorialization.

### 

Why this **matters**

Tribute rate is only actionable when benchmarked against your sector and market, not as a universal average. A North American healthcare organization at 13% is underperforming relative to its sector norm of 24% to 26%. An international affairs organization at 8% is performing above its sector average. The cultural gap between North America, the UK, and Australia also means that checkout prominence for tribute should reflect local giving motivations — memorial-driven in North America, more mission-driven elsewhere.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 497 476'%3E%3C/svg%3E)![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/a1152ba61a/tribute-small.png/m/150x150/filters:quality(60):blur(24))![](https://a.storyblok.com/f/285823045102956/994x952/a1152ba61a/tribute-small.png/m/994x952/filters:format(png):quality(85))

### 

**What you can do** about it

1. Benchmark your tribute rate against your sector and market before drawing conclusions about performance.
2. For healthcare organizations, make tribute prominent at checkout with automated honoree notifications and memorial pages.
3. For international affairs organizations, keep the tribute option available but unobtrusive.
4. For all sectors, consider anniversary-based memorial giving prompts for existing tribute donors at the one-year mark.
