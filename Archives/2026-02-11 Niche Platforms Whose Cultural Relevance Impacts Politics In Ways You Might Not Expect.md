## 1 big thing: Not every platform matters the same way 🎯

These aren’t the platforms that will move elections. They won’t dominate campaign budgets or drive news cycles.

But Snap [creates products](https://mashable.com/article/kevin-systrom-instagram-stories-snapchat) like stories before they hit Instagram. Pinterest reveals design moods that campaigns should understand. LinkedIn hosts the professional networks where policy gets shaped. Reddit is where [15% of people get news](https://www.theargumentmag.com/p/twitter-is-not-real-life). Discord is where Gen Z organizes when you’re not looking. Microsoft secures the infrastructure campaigns run on.

**Why it matters:** Campaigns that only focus on Meta, Google, and TikTok miss tactical opportunities and cultural signals. The sophisticated ones pay attention to where niche audiences congregate and what those platforms reveal about broader trends.

## 2\. Snap: Still here, still Gen Z 👻

**The stats:** 25% of US adults [use Snapchat](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/11/20/americans-social-media-use-2025), biggest demographic 18-29.

**What campaigns do:**

- **Custom Geofilters:** Geo-fence specific events (debates, rallies, conventions) with campaign branding overlays
- **Political ads allowed** (but it’s not a top platform for spending)

**Why Snap matters:**

- **Peter Hamby’s “ [Good Luck America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_Luck_America) “** has run for 10 years on Snap—one of the earliest examples of vertical video political content and a precursor to today’s multi-media independent journalists
- **[Wearables coming in 2026](https://investor.snap.com/news/news-details/2025/Snap-to-Launch-New-Lightweight-Immersive-Specs-in-2026/default.aspx):** Snap first released glasses in 2016, new version expected this year—everyone’s watching how they compare to Meta’s Ray-Bans

**My take:** Snap is unsexy but persistent. If your campaign needs to reach Gen Z and you’re not experimenting here, you’re missing a channel.

## 3\. Pinterest: The trend forecaster 📌

**The policy:** [Banned all paid political advertising in 2018](https://www.cnbc.com/2020/09/03/pinterest-says-it-will-no-longer-allow-ads-on-election-content.html). In 2020, stopped showing ads when users search election-related terms like candidate names or “polling place.”

**How campaigns adapt:** Organic strategies only—infographics, campaign merchandise, informational pins that align with user interests without direct solicitation.

**Why Pinterest actually matters:**

It’s not about reach. It’s about **[trend reports](https://business.pinterest.com/pinterest-predicts/) that identify design aesthetics and cultural moods** before they hit Instagram or TikTok.

**What campaigns should do:** Pay attention to Pinterest trend reports. They attach trends to overall public moods—understanding what resonates aesthetically and emotionally helps campaigns tap into those feelings rather than being oblivious to them.

**My take:** Campaigns don’t use Pinterest much right now, but the smart ones mine it for cultural intelligence.

## 4\. LinkedIn: Deeply unsexy, potentially important 💼

**The stats:** [1 billion](https://about.linkedin.com/) registered members globally.

**The policy:** [No political ads allowed](https://www.linkedin.com/legal/ads-policy).

**Who’s there:** Professional political operatives, policy wonks, corporate leaders, OpenAI and Anthropic sharing company updates.

**Why it matters:**

LinkedIn isn’t where campaigns happen—it’s where **indirect political influence** happens. Policy gets shaped. Professional networks form. Corporate political positioning gets announced.

## 5\. Microsoft: The invisible infrastructure 🔒

**CEO Satya Nadella:** Didn’t attend Trump’s inauguration but Microsoft [donated $1 million](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/01/09/microsoft-contributes-1-million-to-trumps-inauguration-fund.html) to the inaugural fund.

**Why Microsoft [matters for elections](https://news.microsoft.com/en-cee/2024/09/26/strengthening-democracy-in-the-digital-age):**

**Cybersecurity & Campaign Infrastructure:**

- **Defending Democracy Program** offers AccountGuard (free enhanced security for federal, state, local campaigns)
- Threat intelligence notifications about foreign, state-sponsored cyberattacks
- Highly discounted Microsoft 365 for Campaigns—secure email and file-sharing setup in ~5 minutes
- Training on cybersecurity and AI for campaign stakeholders
- They regularly do an annual [Global Online Safety Survey](https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2026/02/09/building-a-safer-digital-future-together/) and their newest released this werk showed that confidence in identifying deepfakes **fell 21% YOY**, with users more aware of how realistic AI generated images have become. Young adults (18–24) were most confident in their ability to detect deepfakes and were the lone age group to score just over 50% correctly.

**The platforms people forget:**

- **Bing:** 4-8% global market share with Copilot integrated—same AI summary issues as Google but less scrutiny
- Cloud services campaigns rely on
- Office products used across political operations

**[The 2024 AI Accord](https://securityconference.org/en/aielectionsaccord/):** Microsoft wrangled the **Tech Accord to Combat Deceptive Use of AI in 2024 Elections** at Munich Security Conference—signatories pledged to deploy technology countering harmful AI-generated content, track origin of deceptive content, and raise public awareness.

**The geopolitical tightrope:** Huge business in Europe means Microsoft has to be careful with geopolitical positioning—European customers [fear Trump dependencies](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/05/business/europe-economic-independence-united-states-intl). Microsoft is doing bare minimum to keep Trump happy without drawing Democratic ire.

**My take:** Microsoft does more for election security than almost anyone talks about. They’re essential infrastructure trying very hard not to become the story.

*The niche platforms aren’t where campaigns win. They’re where campaigns miss things. And right now, most are missing a lot.*
