## Nonprofit Startup Challenges: What It Really Takes to Build Something That Lasts

  

*\*this article was co-authored with ChatGPT to best synthesize the many nuggets from a 45 minute conversation concisely\**

Starting something new in the nonprofit world sounds inspiring on paper.

A bold idea. A clear mission. A desire to make a difference.

But anyone who has actually tried to build something from the ground up knows the truth is far more complex.

Nonprofit startup challenges are not just about funding or strategy. They are about navigating uncertainty, building trust without a track record, and holding onto your purpose while everything around you feels unstable.

In a recent Nonprofit Hive LinkedIn Live, we brought together founders and leaders who are actively building in this space: Matt Fogarty, Founder at Cherry Willow Apparel; Ssekitto Emmanuel, CEO at Faces Up Uganda; and Anouk Bertner, Executive Director at Future of Good. Each of them is navigating what it means to start, grow, and sustain something in the social impact sector—across different contexts, communities, and challenges.

The conversation was facilitated by Robyn Greaves, Nonprofit Hive Advisory Board Member and Co-Founder at Pravi, and made possible with the support of our session sponsor, [Common Cause Grants and Consulting](https://commoncausegrants.com/), whose commitment to strengthening early-stage nonprofit leaders helps make conversations like this accessible to the sector.

So what does it actually take to build something that lasts in this space—and how do you do it without losing yourself in the process?

## Biggest Lessons for Nonprofit Startups

1. Clarity matters more than scale in the early stages.
2. Trust is your first form of capital.
3. You will need to unlearn as much as you learn.
4. Progress is often slow, and that is not failure.
5. Community is not a byproduct. It is infrastructure.

Across the sector, research continues to reinforce these realities. Studies from organizations like Stanford Social Innovation Review and Bridgespan highlight that early-stage nonprofits often fail not because of weak ideas, but because of unclear strategy, leadership strain, and lack of relational infrastructure.

What this panel reminded us is that behind every “startup success story” is a long period of uncertainty, iteration, and quiet persistence.

## 4 Hard Truths About Building a Nonprofit Startup

These are not the kind of insights that usually make it into pitch decks or polished case studies. But they are the realities that shape whether something lasts or quietly fades out.

Across this conversation, what emerged was not a list of tactics, but a set of deeper truths. The kind you only really understand once you are in it. The kind that challenge how we think about growth, success, and what it means to build something that actually holds.

### 1\. You Are Building Trust Before You Build Anything Else

Before programs scale or funding stabilizes, there is a quieter phase where everything depends on relationships. This stage can feel invisible, but it is where the real foundation is set.

People are not just investing in your idea. They are investing in you, your consistency, and your ability to listen and respond with care over time.

This means showing up consistently, listening deeply, and being willing to adapt based on what you hear, even when it slows things down.

**Pro Tip from Ssekitto Emmanuel:** *“Start by asking better questions instead of trying to prove you have all the answers.”*

---

### 2\. Capacity Will Always Feel Tighter Than Your Ambition

One of the most common nonprofit startup challenges is the tension between what you want to build and what you realistically have the [capacity to sustain](https://thenonprofithive.com/trust-based-nonprofit-funding-models/). That gap can feel frustrating, especially when the need feels urgent.

Early-stage founders often try to do too much, too quickly, thinking momentum comes from volume. But more often, it leads to fragmentation and exhaustion.

Sustainable growth comes from making intentional decisions about what not to do, and being honest about what your current capacity can truly hold.

**Pro Tip from Matt Fogarty:** *“If everything feels urgent, nothing is strategic. Choose what actually moves the work forward.”*

---

### 3\. You Will Need to Unlearn Traditional Success Metrics

Many founders enter the sector with ideas shaped by corporate or traditional nonprofit models, where [success is tied to scale, speed, and visible outputs](https://thenonprofithive.com/depth-nonprofit-impact-reporting/).

But success in early-stage work often looks different. It is slower, more relational, and sometimes harder to quantify in clean, reportable ways.

It might look like a small but deeply engaged community instead of rapid growth. Or one strong, aligned partnership instead of multiple surface-level ones that do not hold.

**Pro Tip from Anouk Bertner:** *“Define success in ways that reflect your mission, not just external expectations.”*

---

### 4\. You Cannot Build in Isolation

There is a myth that founders need to have everything figured out before they begin. That clarity comes first, and connection comes later.

In reality, the strongest nonprofit startups are built in conversation with others from the very beginning. Insight, feedback, and shared experience shape what gets built in ways no single person could do alone.

[Peers, mentors, community members, and collaborators are not extras.](https://thenonprofithive.com/nonprofit-community-building-belonging/) They are part of the infrastructure that allows the work to grow and adapt.

**Pro Tip from Ssekitto Emmanuel:** *“Find your people early. It will save you time, energy, and unnecessary mistakes.”*

## Old Approach vs Sustainable Startup Approach

There is often an unspoken pressure for nonprofit startups to follow a familiar path. Move quickly. Show traction. Prove impact early.

But what this conversation revealed is that the organizations that last tend to build differently. More intentionally. More relationally. And often, more slowly than expected.

This shift is not about lowering ambition. It is about aligning how we build with what we actually want to sustain.

| **Traditional Approach** | **Sustainable Startup Approach** |
| --- | --- |
| Scale quickly | Build intentionally |
| Focus on outputs | Focus on relationships |
| Say yes to everything | Prioritize strategically |
| Work in isolation | Build in community |
| Measure success by growth | Measure success by impact |

## Common Nonprofit Startup Challenges

**Trying to do too much too soon.  
**Early momentum can feel like it comes from saying yes to everything. But as Matt Fogarty noted in the conversation, learning to narrow focus was critical: *“If everything feels urgent, nothing is strategic.”* Many founders realize this only after stretching themselves too thin.

**Confusing visibility with impact.  
**It is easy to equate attention with progress. Anouk Bertner reflected on how this shows up across the sector: “We can get pulled toward what’s visible instead of what’s actually meaningful.” Real impact often happens in quieter, less visible ways.

**Building without community input.  
**Solutions built in isolation rarely hold. Ssekitto Emmanuel shared how his approach evolved: *“Start by asking better questions…* ” That shift toward listening first often comes after early missteps in assuming what communities need.

**Over-relying on short-term funding.**Chasing quick funding can shape decisions in ways that are hard to sustain. Several speakers reflected on learning to align funding with mission, not the other way around, even when it meant moving more slowly.

**Burning out before momentum builds.  
**The pressure to prove progress quickly can lead to exhaustion early on. As Robyn Greaves grounded in the session, *“There’s a more honest way to build in this sector.”* Many founders only recognize the need for pacing after hitting this wall.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

In practice, building a nonprofit startup looks less like a straight line and more like a series of small, intentional decisions that compound over time. It is rarely a single breakthrough moment. More often, it is a pattern of choices that slowly shape something stronger, more grounded, and more sustainable.

It looks like testing ideas before scaling them, even when there is pressure to move faster. It means piloting, listening, adjusting, and sometimes starting over. That process can feel slow, but it builds something far more resilient than rushing to expand before the foundation is ready.

It looks like choosing depth over visibility. Prioritizing meaningful relationships, community trust, and alignment over quick wins or external validation. As Anouk Bertner highlighted, what is most impactful is not always what is most visible, and learning to sit with that tension is part of the work.

It looks like asking for help earlier than feels comfortable. Reaching out to peers, leaning on community, and being willing to admit when something is unclear or not working. Ssekitto Emmanuel’s emphasis on asking better questions reflects this shift from proving to learning.

It also looks like being honest about what is not working. Letting go of ideas, partnerships, or approaches that do not hold, even if time and energy have already been invested. That kind of honesty is not failure. It is what allows the work to evolve.

And perhaps most importantly, it looks like building systems that support people, not just programs. Creating space for reflection, pacing, and shared responsibility. Because without that, even the strongest ideas struggle to last—and the people behind them burn out before they ever have the chance to see their impact fully take shape.

### Frequently Asked Questions about Nonprofit Social Media

******What is the biggest challenge for nonprofit startups?******

The biggest challenge is balancing ambition with capacity while building trust and securing sustainable funding at the same time. In the early stages, founders are often holding multiple roles while also trying to prove credibility, which can stretch both time and decision-making. The tension is not just about doing more, but about choosing what matters most and letting the rest wait.

******How do nonprofit startups get funding?******

Most rely on a mix of grants, individual donors, partnerships, and early-stage fundraising strategies while building credibility. Early funding often comes through relationships rather than formal processes, which means trust-building and storytelling are just as important as proposals. Over time, diversifying revenue becomes critical to avoid over-dependence on any one source.

******How long does it take for a nonprofit startup to stabilize?******

It varies, but many organizations take several years to build consistent funding, systems, and community trust. Stabilization is not just financial—it also includes clarity in strategy, defined roles, and repeatable processes. Many founders underestimate how long this phase takes because much of the progress is invisible.

******What skills are most important for nonprofit founders?******

Relationship-building, adaptability, strategic thinking, and the ability to prioritize are essential. Founders also need emotional resilience and the ability to navigate uncertainty without clear roadmaps. Much of the work involves listening, learning in real time, and adjusting direction based on what is working and what is not.

****Can nonprofit startups avoid burnout?****

Yes, but it requires intentional pacing, clear boundaries, and building support systems early. Burnout often happens when urgency overrides sustainability, so founders need to actively create space for rest, reflection, and shared responsibility. Building in community and support from the beginning makes a significant difference.
