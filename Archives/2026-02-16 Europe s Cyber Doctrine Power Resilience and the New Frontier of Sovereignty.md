Europe’s Cyber Doctrine: Power, Resilience and the New Frontier of Sovereignty

Europe has slipped into a permanent “grey zone” where the first strike in any crisis is digital, not kinetic. Cyber operations precede tanks in Ukraine, nudge elections from Berlin to Chișinău, and can plunge parts of a capital into darkness without a single shot being fired.

﻿

What used to be a niche concern of defence committees and technologists is now the front line of European sovereignty, yet institutions built for analogue warfare and paper-based bureaucracy are scrambling to catch up.

\-

Europe’s Cyber Doctrine

﻿

The emerging European doctrine is clear: cyber security is no longer a technical afterthought but a question of power, who can keep their systems standing when adversaries weaponise software, infrastructure dependencies and even allied platforms. The uncomfortable message for voters is that resilience will cost money, administrative disruption and cultural change. The more hopeful message is that, if Europe moves fast, cyber defence can become not just a shield but a growth engine and a test of democratic resolve.

﻿

#### **From “nerd issue” to permanent hybrid war**

﻿

For years, cyber security was treated as a subculture of “political tech nerds,” politely parked in specialised committees and specialist beats. That era ended when Russian aggression against Ukraine made visible what military planners already knew: before missiles fly, servers fall. The war in Ukraine did not invent hybrid warfare, but it exposed to European publics that cyber operations are part of war, not an optional prelude.

European defence leadership now describes the continent as already in a form of conflict, even if many national politicians still cling to the vocabulary of peace. That semantic hesitation matters. Calling the situation “not quite war” has been a way to postpone investments, to underplay the risks tied to digital dependencies and to reassure citizens that nothing fundamental must change in their daily lives. Yet the reality is that cyber-attacks on administrations, critical infrastructure and information ecosystems are continuous and transnational; in cyberspace, there are no internal borders and no neutral bystanders.

Public perception is catching up. Phishing scams, fake online shops and social media fraud have made the abstract tangible for everyday citizens, while the avalanche of deepfaked messages, AI-authored scams and synthetic personas has eroded the intuitive ability to distinguish the real from the fabricated. The battlefield has moved from sophisticated zero-day exploits in obscure systems to the phones, inboxes and feeds of everyone.

﻿

#### **Europe discovers that defence is a single market problem**

﻿

Europe’s internal market has long been an economic project; its defence posture remained largely intergovernmental. Cyber has blown up that division. No member state can secure its networks alone when hostile actors hop across borders in seconds, and common vulnerabilities propagate through shared suppliers, cloud services and standards.

The European Union has quietly begun to build a defence architecture it never planned for. A fully-fledged committee on security and defence in the European Parliament, a new Commissioner tasked with common procurement and space, and a Vice-President with explicit responsibility for cyber attacks are early institutional attempts to turn scattered national responses into something like a continental strategy. A European defence industry programme that includes cyber capabilities and common technical procurement is perhaps even more significant: it acknowledges that sovereignty requires shared tooling as much as shared doctrine.

Still, the pace is painfully slow compared with the tempo of attacks. Russian incursions into Ukraine started in 2014; the dramatic weaponisation of drones in conflicts like Armenia–Azerbaijan has been visible for years. Yet only in the last year have EU-level efforts taken a form that resembles operational cooperation. The gap between the speed of geopolitics and the speed of European institutional design remains one of the continent’s greatest vulnerabilities.

﻿

#### **Regulation is shifting from compliance to resilience**

﻿

In Brussels, the first instinct in the digital realm was regulation: the Cyber Resilience Act, NIS2, the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) and a forest of related frameworks. For a while, cyber security was treated as another compliance box, a checklist of obligations for software vendors, banks or cloud providers. That mindset is evolving.

Security leaders in major firms now say Europe is finally moving from “regulation and compliance” to “operational resilience.” The key year will be 2026, when many of the legal milestones and implementation deadlines hit. The question is whether public administrations and critical sectors can translate dense legal texts into working capabilities, monitoring, detection, incident response, in time.

AI sits at the centre of this pivot. For defenders, AI is the first technology that allows security operations to scale in a way that can keep pace with automated attackers. Instead of hunting for “needles in haystacks,” machine learning systems can “burn the haystack” and surface anomalies across billions of events, improving detection capacity by orders of magnitude. Yet the same tools that aid defenders empower opponents: hostile actors now use generative models to craft unique malware, hyper-personalised phishing messages and social engineering campaigns that mimic local idioms and plausible context.

This “defender’s dilemma” remains unresolved. Defenders must be right almost all the time; attackers only need one successful exploit. But Europe’s current regulatory debate often risks hamstringing defensive AI capabilities in the name of risk aversion, even as adversaries show no such restraint.

﻿

#### **The geopolitics of code: allies, black boxes and cloud sovereignty**

﻿

The uncomfortable truth for Europe is that its cyber defences are entangled with non-European platforms. The GPS constellation underpinning logistics, finance, aviation, power grids and emergency services is controlled by the United States; it has already been disrupted in the early phase of Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, leaving European navigation systems effectively “lost” for a period. European efforts like Galileo exist but lack full coverage and investment, emblematic of a broader pattern: doing just enough to claim a capability, but not enough to lead.

The same pattern appears in defence procurement. Germany’s acquisition of American F-35s for nuclear roles comes with “black boxes”, components and software that remain opaque to the buyer, with the supplier retaining visibility into operations. That asymmetry of insight is a strategic liability. Buying the best systems from allies makes sense; surrendering visibility into how they function does not.

Large US cloud providers present a similar paradox. On one hand, they offered life-saving resilience to Ukraine: virtualised infrastructure protected under initiatives like CyberShield kept key government websites online under sustained attack, and 50,000 cloud productivity licences allowed Ukrainian administration to continue functioning from the relative safety of distributed data centres. On the other, European governments fear legal regimes like the US CLOUD Act, which could compel providers to hand over data or even shut down services.

Technologists have started to engineer around that political risk. “Data sovereignty” architectures encrypt data in European clouds with keys held by local partners, so that even if US authorities request access, what they receive is unreadable without European cooperation. More ambitious models go further: “dedicated” cloud regions in Berlin or Paris, run by European operators with separate IP space and encryption, and even “cloud in a box” air‑gapped infrastructures adopted by armed forces such as the Bundeswehr to ensure that critical systems can run without reliance on external networks.

These designs do not reject American technology; they re-balance the control over it. They tacitly acknowledge that in some domains, independence is neither realistic nor desirable, but that sovereignty can be achieved by combining external innovation with internal control over keys, governance and operations.

﻿

#### **Talent, mentality and the politics of failure**

﻿

Hardware has long been Europe’s comfort zone: precision machinery, industrial engineering, automotive excellence. Software, and especially high-growth, risk-tolerant software entrepreneurship, has not. For decades, ambitious European technologists decamped to Silicon Valley, drawn by funding, tolerance for failure and the promise that a failed startup was a badge of honour, not a career stain.

Reversing that brain drain is as much about culture as budgets. European leaders increasingly argue that cyber security and AI should be framed not merely as costs or threats, but as “job motors”, sectors that can provide high-quality employment and economic competitiveness. That requires universities, research agencies and pension systems to feed not only world-class research (which Europe already has) but also the capital and risk appetite needed to scale companies, especially beyond the seed stage.

Rigid federal structures, 16 German Länder with separate bureaucracies, for example, complicate the picture, making it harder to harmonise digital standards and procure shared solutions. Yet the larger obstacle may be psychological: a political culture that remembers failure for decades and instinctively prefers to regulate before it builds. In cyber, that bias is becoming a strategic liability.

﻿

#### **Citizens as the weakest link, and the first line of defence**

﻿

For all the talk of satellites and sovereign clouds, Europe’s cyber posture will ultimately be decided in ordinary offices and homes. Most local administrations will never have a dedicated cyber expert; they will live and die by basic hygiene: whether officials use password managers and two‑factor authentication, whether updates are applied, whether suspicious messages are recognised.

Simple tools can radically reduce attack surfaces. Security agencies point out that universal adoption of strong authentication and password managers would dramatically cut successful account compromises. Yet adoption remains patchy, even among political and media elites. That is why tech companies have invested in safety engineering centres in European cities like Munich and Málaga, coupling product development with public education campaigns around practices such as two‑factor authentication, password management and safer internet days.

At the strategic level, cyber security leaders insist that “security is a team sport.” Major cloud and platform providers share threat intelligence even as they compete, because no single actor sees the full picture of attacks across Europe, North America and the Indo-Pacific. But for that team sport to work, citizens must accept that they, too, have a position on the field, and that a careless click on a hyper-personalised phishing message can undo millions of euros of investment elsewhere.

﻿

#### **The politics of honesty: resilience will hurt, and that’s the point**

﻿

The hardest truth for elected leaders to admit is that genuine resilience will change everyday life. Voters who have tuned out bad news, stopped reading papers or avoid online information out of anxiety are precisely those who must be re-engaged. Security cannot be delivered as a background service with no visible trade‑offs; it will mean more stringent digital identities, more demanding expectations on how citizens manage their devices and data, and higher public spending in areas that rarely yield ribbon‑cutting ceremonies.

Yet there is an opportunity embedded in that discomfort. Treating cyber capacity as core infrastructure, as real as roads or railways, allows Europe to align its security ambitions with industrial policy. Investments in secure software, sovereign clouds and safety engineering can generate exportable expertise, not just sunk costs. Framing cyber employment as public service, making it “cool to work in city halls,” as one European politician provocatively put it, can bring a new generation into public administration.

If Europe wants to be more than a passive market for American hardware and Chinese code, it must embrace cyber security as both a strategic necessity and an economic strategy. That means fewer speeches about “not quite war,” more candid conversations about risk, and an acceptance that in the 2020s, defending peace and freedom begins with defending packets.
