**Key Takeaways**

- **Authenticity beats perfection—every time.** In a sea of polished, AI-generated content, what actually stands out is what feels real. Typos, candid photos, unscripted moments—these aren’t flaws, they’re signals of humanity. And that’s what people connect to.
- **Real people > polished templates.** Photos and videos of your team, volunteers, and community will consistently outperform designed graphics. Even imperfect or “faceless” event shots create more connection than overly curated content.
- **Small, consistent updates build deeper trust.** You don’t need big announcements to show up. Behind-the-scenes moments, quick updates, and day-to-day glimpses help your audience feel like insiders—not just observers.
- **Social media is about community, not broadcasting.** The strongest nonprofit content invites people in. Asking questions, sharing stories, responding to comments—this is what turns followers into relationships.

---

In a world saturated with AI-generated content, the prevailing advice is that to stand out is to be “authentic” on social media. But what does that even mean?

AI’s role in the current social media landscape is growing at breakneck speed and has impacted messaging across the board.

As AI becomes more and more mainstream and is incorporated into many nonprofit teams’ workflows, we’re seeing a shift in content. Some organizations are using the technology to create images. Some use it to repurpose content from one format to another. Some, like me, use it for easy emoji population into captions. And unfortunately, some use it to develop their copy entirely from scratch.

This is where things get sticky. No matter how good you are with AI and how well developed your prompts may be, readers can often tell that a human did not write what they’re reading. Even if they do not consciously recognize it as AI, they tend to connect with it less.

As we see more and more “perfect” content coming from AI and less and less humanity in the messaging we encounter, the one thing that can help your organization [stand out in the crowd is authenticity.](https://thenonprofithive.com/nonprofit-social-media-strategy/)

This looks different for every organization, but the good news is that it is often easier than the unrelatable perfection so frequently produced by AI. Here are a few ways your nonprofit can be authentic.

## Photos and Videos

Templates have a place, and I’ve worked with organizations before that primarily use them due to a real lack of content. However, they will always underperform when compared with photos and videos of real people.

Sharing unpolished, even candid, photos and videos of your team, volunteers, and/or program participants helps your audience feel a human connection. That connection is critical to creating community, and *community is the primary goal of nonprofit social media*.

And if your team is camera-shy, don’t give up on photos entirely. I have a small nonprofit client in the same boat, and some of our best-performing posts have been conference or event photos without a single face in them. They still outperformed our polished templates. Spoiler alert: almost any photo is better than no photo.

## Keep Them Informed (in an interesting way)

Help your community understand your work by sharing both big and small updates. Videos of team members or volunteers sharing results from a recent event or program illustrate not just the work you’re doing, but who’s doing it. If your work is not sensitive in nature, share videos or photos of event attendees or program participants describing their experience.

Facebook and Instagram stories can be a great way to connect. Jump on a quick story and share what the organization is up to today. Share behind-the-scenes photos of small things like an all-staff meeting, volunteer training, or event prep. Small updates allow your audience into the daily happenings of your organization, helping them feel like insiders.

## Give up Perfection.

Coming from a stewardship background, I understand the pressure to get every tiny detail right. But here’s what I’ve learned: perfection doesn’t build connection.

I can’t tell you how many times I’ve spotted a typo in nonprofit posts. But here’s the thing: my reaction was never judgment. It was relief. Because a typo tells me a real human wrote that post, not ChatGPT.

When creating content, whether drafting a caption, taking a video, or creating a graphic, remember that people relate to people. This means sharing images that aren’t perfectly lit or perfectly centered, or videos that feel natural rather than scripted. Those small imperfections often make content feel more real and approachable.

## Make Content Human-Centered

Understanding your audience is the heart of any successful social media strategy. If you understand your audience, you can create content that resonates with them and empowers them to engage in meaningful ways.

For instance, if you’re a library nonprofit, you know that your followers likely love to read. Share photos or videos of your team with the books they’re reading this month and ask your audience to share what they’re reading.  
  
If you’re an animal welfare nonprofit, allow your followers to connect with individual animals. Tell stories about them. Introduce your audience to their back stories, daily routines, and preferences, and share updates about them. Encourage your audience to ask questions about the animal or their habitat and respond in the comments.

A human-centered approach extends beyond just knowing your audience. It means inviting them in. One way I’ve done this for a client was by simply asking their community for input. We shared a [post](https://www.instagram.com/p/C2uvcjiLE5I/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MzRlODBiNWFlZA==) with a call for tips, and the response was incredible. From there, we created additional [content](https://www.instagram.com/p/C56DW0IBIJl/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MzRlODBiNWFlZA==), tagging the peer organizations we referenced and thanking them for their input. It was a simple ask that built real connection.

## Take Breaks From the Corporate Voice

Your organization should have a distinct tone and voice, but that should not be the only voice your community hears. Invite staff members, volunteers, or program participants to lend their perspective through a guest post or for a story takeover. This illustrates the reality of your work and allows your organization to feel more human and less institutional.

## Engage Regularly

It is not possible to build a digital community for your nonprofit without regular, thoughtful engagement. Respond to comments in a timely manner and do so conversationally. Welcome new followers and sign welcome messages with the name of a staffer rather than your organization’s name. Over time, you will begin to recognize your key followers and build authentic relationships with them. Want to dig deeper? I’ve written more specifically about engagement strategies [here](https://www.instagram.com/p/DV8xb77gVU2/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MzRlODBiNWFlZA==).  
  
In reality, authenticity can be hard to convey. It’s something I still wrestle with on my own accounts. The question is: how do we maintain professionalism while still sharing enough personality for people to actually feel connected? What I’ve found, for myself and my clients, is that there’s no single right answer. It’s an ever-evolving process, and perfection isn’t the goal.

Focus on creating content that aligns with your mission and connects with your community. And remember, your audience is human. Their emotions are what build the strongest connections, and AI cannot relate to them the way the people behind your mission can.
