Agentic AI will bring about a major shift in the breadth of services non-profits can offer without needing to expand their existing workforce, believes Jared Chung, founder of CareerVillage.

The technology, which he expects to become the “new way that knowledge workers will work”, is [described by chipmaker NVIDIA as using](https://blogs.nvidia.com/blog/what-is-agentic-ai/):

> Sophisticated reasoning and iterative planning to autonomously solve complex, multi-step problems…Agentic AI systems ingest vast amounts of data from multiple data sources and third-party applications to independently analyze challenges, develop strategies and execute tasks.

Chung acknowledges it is early days for the technology, which he sees as being in a similar place to where ChatGPT was a few years ago. But he expects the situation will change quite rapidly:

> This will be the new work model – not today, but soon. So, organizations need to start thinking about how to take advantage of the opportunities agentic AI offers in supporting beneficiaries more effectively. I think by 2026 or 2027, it’ll really take off among non-profits as it’ll be built into supplier’s applications, but it’s already being adopted by tech-oriented NGOs like ours now.

Career Village, which was set up in 2011, was supported in boosting its AI capabilities here by tech for social good NGO Fast Forward’s Accelerator program. The organization’s aim is to democratize access to career advice and guidance for members of underrepresented communities. Chung explains:

> I had mentors that were very instrumental in me going to college and getting my first job and career. So, I started mentoring myself when I became a young professional as so much good can come from having that kind of support. The challenge is that it’s very difficult to scale.
> 
> So, we started with the question of ‘what are the key elements of career navigation and coaching that can scale using technology and the power of generosity, so everyone can have access to the basics of career information and advice?’

## Overcoming past limitations

The NGOs first product was its career Q&A website. This operates in 190 countries with the help of 190,000 expert professionals, who volunteer to answer users’ questions from a local perspective within an hour. But as Chung points out, there were limits to the service such an offering could provide:

> You get an answer within an hour, but you still have to wait. You also can’t put your resume in there or connect to a specific job post or scholarship. So, in January 2023, we started to experiment with generative AI. Even with all the technology’s issues and hallucinations, we wanted to see if there was potential here for us to offer services we hadn’t been able to before so we could expand how we support learners.

This led to the development of Coach with the help of 20 other NGOs and educational institutions. The chatbot supports over 13-year-olds in discovering and navigating the right career path for them on a free-of-charge basis.

Input comes from the biggest Large Language Model providers, such as Google, and output is mediated by experts to ensure accuracy. The platform is monitored and managed using technology that CareerVillage developed in-house, and its work is financed by grants from Foundations, such as Google.org and Bill and Melinda Gates. Chung says:

> It’s a cost-effective approach. The way traditional non-profits work is to spend a lot of money on each interaction with their beneficiaries, and their costs are variable. But with AI, you may have to invest a lot in the system upfront but each interaction with a chatbot only costs between $0.03-$0.10. So, it’s a crazy cheap way to offer personalized career coaching that provides a social impact return on investment.

## Field testing agentic AI

The next step will be to add support to Coach for agentic AI, which is currently being field-tested with expert involvement. Chung explains:

> Nothing will go to a learner until a staff member has approved it and sent it. But as reliability goes up, the hope is to allow agent-based services to go directly to learners without human intervention.
> 
> It’s early days for agentic AI and things are at a similar point here as they were when ChatGPT was first released. But we’d expect that to happen at some point later this year.

Chung expects to deploy the new functionality in a variety of ways. If a learner asks Coach to help them build a professional network, for example, the generative AI element would be there to offer suggestions and talk them through how to do it.

The agentic AI functionality, on the other hand, would take the process a step further by finding a local conference and asking the learner if it should get them a pass – which it will do, if required.

But there are also other potential internal uses for the technology too. This includes automating low value technical processes, such as exporting log data to.cfc files.

## How tech is helping lift farmers out of poverty

Another nonprofit that has just deployed agentic AI in its Farmer.Chat tool is Digital Green. The international development NGO operates in India, Ethiopia, Kenya and Nigeria.

Its goal is to help smallholder farmers who work less than an acre of land and earn less than $1-2 per day lift themselves out of poverty. A huge 85% of such farmers are women as men commonly leave their homes to go and work in the cities.

A key challenge they face, says Rikin Gandhi, the organization’s Founder and Chief Executive, is that:

> Their ability to negotiate with the market is low as their farms are so small, so it’s difficult to get good deals on things like seeds, financing, and transporting produce to market. Therefore, we saw an opportunity to leverage digital technology to help improve the efficiency of how farmers work together and how they work with the market.

In 2006, Gandhi joined Microsoft Research’s Technology for Emerging Markets team in India to explore just this issue. The upshot was Digital Green, which has since been spun out to become an independent entity.

The NGO trained local cooperatives, government workers and other NGOs to create videos of farmers sharing information and practices for use by their peers. These were shown to members by cooperatives and women’s groups. But during the COVID-19 pandemic, because farming communities were no longer able to meet in person, people increasingly moved online. As Gandhi explains:

> Generative AI got better with [GPT 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GPT-3) \[launched in 2020\], so we saw an opportunity to pull things together in the form of Farmer.Chat \[a ChatGPT-based AI-enabled assistant\]. On the knowledge side, farmers want local information, but they also prefer multi-modal functionality, such as local voices and images, to obtain more tailored insights and recommendations.

As an example of how Farmer.Chat works, a farmer might be keen to know how much fertilizer to use on a given crop for optimum results, and how to apply it. Once asked the question, the assistant analyzes Digital Green’s knowledge bank of internal video content along with external sources, such as local weather pattern data. It then provides the farmer with a tailored recommendation, which might include not to spray before the rains come in a couple of days’ time.

## The possibilities of agentic AI

Since OpenAI launched its [agentic AI tool Operator at the start of this year](https://openai.com/index/introducing-operator/) though, Farmer.Chat’s functionality has been enhanced. The tool could now display comparative pricing from local fertilizer suppliers, for instance.

Over time however, the aim is to enable farmers to use agentic functionality as a means of connecting with other service providers. This includes cooperatives that sell seeds or fertilizer, or coops wanting to buy from them. Another aim is to provide farmers with more contextualized information. Gandhi explains:

> The idea is to enable farmers to have greater agency and choice over the information they want. We’ve had 1.4 million queries so far, which means we’re starting to see ways we could improve the system. For example, farmers aren’t looking for generic information. They want it contextualized to fit their specific soil, climate etc. So, we’re looking at how to do that and how to expand coverage to provide information over wider geographies and languages.

The benefits to farmers of having used AI to date have been significant, with an average rise in income of between 30-40%. But the technology also promises to cut costs for Digital Green and its partners too, many of which have tight resources. For instance, to provide in-person training on a particular farming practice costs on average $35 per person. But the figure falls to $3.5 with videos, and to $0.35 with AI.

To ensure other organizations operating in the same space do not need to reinvent the wheel, Digital Green has open sourced its system and published it on [GitHub’s Hugging Face AI community](https://github.com/huggingface).

## My take

By upskilling tech nonprofits in emerging technologies, such as agentic AI, Fast Forward’s Accelerator program has the potential to make a real difference to the lives not just of people working in such organizations but to their beneficiaries too.
