Last week, I heard about six development directors who left their jobs. Resigned. Quit suddenly. Just... gone.

That's not normal. Something is happening in the nonprofit world right now, and it's costing organizations millions.

Here's what I know from decades in this space: development directors don't leave because the work is hard. They leave because the work is *impossible*. And by the time you figure out that something's wrong, it's too late.

I've been in your shoes. I've hired development directors. I've fired them. And I didn't know they weren't doing their jobs until eighteen months had passed and the revenue had stopped coming in. That's not their failure. That was mine.

Here's what I missed: I didn't have a system. And without a system, you can't see the truth until the damage is already done.

### The Problem Has Two Faces

When I talk to executive directors, I'm hearing the same story from two different angles.

**The first story sounds like this:** "I hired a development director. For about a year, everything seemed fine. Then I realized the pipeline was empty. We had no major gift prospects. No cultivation plan. No visibility into where the money was actually coming from. By the time I noticed, we'd lost eighteen months of relationship-building."

That's the *silent* failure. The one where your development director is busy—maybe very busy—but doing activities that don't move the needle. Writing grant proposals instead of cultivating major donors. Attending events instead of having one-on-one conversations. Moving fast but in the wrong direction.

**The second story sounds like this:** "My development director was crushing it. But I kept asking for more. More events. More proposals. More outreach. More board engagement. Until one day, she just couldn't anymore. She burned out and left."

That's the *visible* crisis. The one where you see the person struggling but don't realize that what you're asking is actually impossible without the right infrastructure.

Both scenarios end the same way: your dev director leaves, your revenue takes a hit, and you're left scrambling.

### Why You Don't See It Coming

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **without a system, you can't measure whether your development director is actually doing their job.**

You can see activity. You can see them busy. You can see proposals going out and events happening.

But are they *building major gift relationships*? Are they moving donors through a predictable pipeline? Are they cultivating the right people at the right time? Are they positioned to ask for money when the time is right?

You have no idea.

This is why I went eighteen months without realizing we had a problem. The person looked busy. They were in the office. They were doing *something*. But we had no scorecard. No visibility into the pipeline. No way to know if the work actually mattered.

"If it's not on the scorecard, it didn't happen." That's the rule I learned the hard way.

When you don't have a scorecard—a clear system that tracks prospects, stages, and next steps—you're flying blind. And your development director knows it. They're scrambling. They don't know if what they're doing is right. They're operating on instinct instead of strategy.

That's burnout. That's the setup for them to leave.

### The Cost of Losing a Development Director

Let's talk money for a second.

When a development director leaves, here's what happens:

You lose eighteen months of institutional knowledge about your donors.

You lose relationships. The donors that person built trust with don't automatically trust the next person.

You lose pipeline momentum. Prospects that were being cultivated go cold.

You have a three-to-six-month hiring gap while you recruit and onboard someone new.

For a $3M organization, a development director might be responsible for $500K to $1M in annual revenue. Lose them, and you're looking at a significant revenue dip.

But here's what costs even more: the *invisible* cost. The opportunity cost. The major gifts that didn't happen because there was no system to make them happen.

If your organization should be bringing in $1.2M in major gifts but you're only bringing in $600K, the difference isn't always because your dev director is lazy. It's because there's no system to *make* major gifts happen consistently.

### Two Reasons Development Directors Leave

I'm seeing two distinct types of departures right now.

**Involuntary exits (the silent failure):** The development director wasn't actually doing the work. Or more honestly, they didn't know *how* to do the work because there was no system, no process, no roadmap. After a year or two of floundering, you finally realize the pipeline is empty. You have to let them go. And then you have to explain to the board why you didn't catch this sooner.

**Voluntary exits (the burnout):** The development director was trying hard. They were showing up. But you kept asking for more without building infrastructure to support it. More events. More donors. More proposals. No staff support. No database. No cultivation plan. Just... more. Eventually, they burn out and leave.

The irony? Both situations are *preventable*.

### What a Real System Looks Like

Here's what I've learned: when you have a system, you can see *everything*.

You know which donors are in which stage of the relationship.

You know what the next step is for each major gift prospect.

You know how many people should be in cultivation, how many should be ready to ask, how many should be in stewardship.

You know whether your development director is actually moving people through the pipeline.

You have a *scorecard*. And if something's broken, you see it within weeks. Not eighteen months.

A real system also prevents burnout because your development director isn't doing everything. They're not writing grants *and* cultivating donors *and* managing events *and* doing stewardship. They have a clear role. They have support. They have a process.

They know exactly what they need to do each week to move donors forward.

That's the difference between a development director who burns out and one who thrives.

### The Red Flags You're Missing

If you don't have a system in place, watch for these signs:

**You can't answer these questions:**

- How many major gift prospects do we have?
- What stage are they in?
- When is the next touchpoint?
- Who is responsible for each relationship?
- How many asks are scheduled for the next quarter?

**Your development director is:**

- Doing the same activities week after week with no clear progression
- Juggling too many things at once without priority
- Spending more time on grant writing than donor cultivation
- Working nights and weekends consistently
- Expressing frustration about not knowing what to focus on next

**Your board is:**

- Asking "Where are the major gifts?" without understanding the cultivation timeline
- Confused about roles and who should be doing what
- Not engaged in the major gift strategy

If any of these sound familiar, your development director is either struggling or about to be.

### What to Do Right Now

Step one: Stop asking "Is my development director doing their job?" and start asking "Do I have a system that makes their job possible?"

Because here's the truth: a great person in a broken system will eventually fail. And a mediocre person in a great system can thrive.

You need both. You need the right person *and* the right infrastructure.

A real system does this:

- **Maps every donor to a stage.** You know who's in awareness, cultivation, solicitation, and stewardship.
- **Defines clear next steps.** There's no guessing. The person knows exactly what needs to happen next.
- **Creates accountability.** Everyone on the team—board and staff—knows their role.
- **Tracks progress.** You see problems early, not eighteen months later.

This isn't complicated. But it requires intentionality. It requires you to say, "We're going to build a system, and we're going to commit to it."

### The Window Is Closing

The exodus is happening now. Great development directors are leaving organizations that don't have systems. Organizations that ask for too much with too little infrastructure.

If you want to keep your development director—the good one or the one you're about to hire—you need to build this now.

Not next year. Not after you find the right person. Now.

Because the next development director you hire will also burn out or underperform without a system. And the one after that. And the one after that.

The problem isn't your development director. The problem is the infrastructure.
