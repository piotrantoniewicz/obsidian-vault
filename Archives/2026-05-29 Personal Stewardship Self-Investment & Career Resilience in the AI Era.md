### TEAMS! Newsletter | Stewardship Edition | May 30, 2026

### You Are the Asset That Matters Most

Most of what’s written about AI right now is from 30,000 feet, focusing on labor market projections, displacement curves, restructuring charts, and sector-by-sector forecasts. It’s useful in the same way weather maps are. But none of it tells you, personally, what to do on Monday morning.

This edition of TEAMS! lands closer to the ground. The most important conversation about AI isn’t happening in McKinsey decks or Senate hearings. It’s happening between you and the version of yourself you’ll be in three years.

I call this **Personal Stewardship,** and I’d argue it’s the most consequential form of stewardship any of us practice. You can be a brilliant steward of a team, a budget, a brand, or a board. But if you are not stewarding *yourself*, your talent, your judgment, and your capacity to adapt, every other form of stewardship eventually erodes. You are the asset that matters most. And that asset has to be managed, nurtured, and expanded with the same discipline you’d apply to any portfolio you’re responsible for.

What follows brings together a few frameworks I keep returning to: economist Tyler Cowen’s tactical playbook, Don Miguel Ruiz’s *The Four Agreements*, Kevin Kelly’s writing on technological symbiosis, and Luis Garicano’s work on knowledge hierarchies. Different lenses, same conclusion: in a moment of exponential change, self-investment isn’t a soft skill or a personal-growth indulgence. It’s the operating system.

### The Tactical Playbook: Cowen’s Seven Ways

Tyler Cowen has laid out seven practical guidelines for protecting and growing our individual value as machine capabilities accelerate. These aren’t theoretical. They’re the closest thing we have to a field manual:

• **Lean into “messy jobs.”** Roles that are non-linear, fluid, multi-component, and dependent on real-time judgment are structurally harder to automate. The more your work looks like a flowchart, the more vulnerable it is. The more it looks like a jazz set, improvising against a known structure, the safer it is, and the more interesting it gets.

- **Show up in person.** Remote work has its place, and I’ve benefited from it. But the data is clear: physical presence builds social capital, organizational trust, and the kind of face-to-face evaluation that no Slack thread can replicate. Proximity still matters.
- **Master the tools.** This one is non-negotiable. Refusing to learn AI tools right now is the 2026 equivalent of refusing to learn email in 1998; it doesn’t make you principled, it makes you obsolete. Deep proficiency in prompting, agent integration, and workflow design isn’t a side hustle. It’s the work that will help you navigate into the future.
- **Move toward high-verification fields.** Sectors that require human accountability, regulatory checking, physical execution, and field judgment, such as biomedicine, energy, healthcare, skilled trades, and infrastructure, are expanding precisely because digital networks can’t replace the verification layer.
- **Become an experimentalist and data harvester.** AI generates hypotheses at an industrial scale and at dizzying speed. What it lacks is contextual data from the physical and historical world. The professionals who thrive will be the ones who test, observe, capture the unmodeled signal, and bring it back into the loop.

> ***As machine capabilities expand, the marginal returns on uniquely human inputs — spot creativity, physical stewardship, ethical accountability — escalate exponentially.”***

That sentence is worth reading twice. The frame isn’t human-versus-machine. It’s human inputs that the machine cannot generate, sold into a market that is increasingly desperate for them.

### Three Lenses That Sharpen the Picture

***The Four Agreements:*** In The Four Agreements, Don Miguel Ruiz offers a deceptively simple operating principle: Always do your best. Most people read that as a motivational poster. I read it as a discipline. Your “best” isn’t static. It shifts with your health, your context, your environment, and, critically, the tools available to you. Under the conditions we’re living in, “doing your best” without engaging the new capabilities is no longer doing your best. It’s an outdated definition of the same words.

Refusing to understand generative AI, or pretending the shift isn’t structural, is a quiet violation of this agreement. Conversely, dedicating real cognitive energy to mastery is how you keep your integrity intact while the ground moves underneath you.

***Kevin Kelly’s Symbiosis:*** Kevin Kelly has argued for years that the future of work is not a zero-sum contest between humans and machines but a state of deep symbiosis. You will not be judged on whether you do a task better than an AI. You’ll be judged on how well you work with it.

This aligns with Cowen’s emphasis on tool proficiency, which can produce 3x to 10x gains in output per person. The point isn’t to compete with the model. The point is to treat it as a collaborative cognitive layer that frees you to focus your finite human attention on the things that compound, such as empathy, curiosity, ethical judgment, and original synthesis.

***Garicano’s Knowledge Hierarchy:*** Luis Garicano’s research describes how organizations are built as problem-solving hierarchies. Routine tasks are automated or pushed down. Exceptions: the ambiguous, the unprecedented, the genuinely hard escalate up. Personal stewardship means deliberately climbing this hierarchy. Seek out the ambiguous and cross-functional work. Volunteer for the problems no one has a playbook for. Position yourself as the person who handles what the algorithms can’t.

> ***Careers compound. Not in the routine. In the exceptions.***

### Stop Depreciating Yourself

For most of the last century, careers were treated as depreciating assets. You earn a degree, ride the curve, and retire on a cushion. That model is broken and a dead-end path for your future.

In an environment with a rapidly accelerating technological multiplier, holding your skills constant is the same as falling behind. The math is unforgiving: if AI capability scales while your competency stays flat, your relative value drops, your wages soften, and your optionality narrows. The graph only goes one way, down.

**Personal stewardship flips the model.** Human capital has to be reinvested continuously, with a deliberate allocation of your time, focus, and energy to skill development. That’s how you neutralize obsolescence and, more importantly, capture the upside of the productivity gains everyone keeps talking about.

### Why This Matters Beyond You

Personal stewardship begins as an individual commitment, but its dividends are systemic. When enough people take ownership of their development, a few things happen collectively:

- ***Talent finds its highest use.***A self-directed workforce shifts away from automated, low-leverage roles and toward expanding fields. That reallocation reduces structural unemployment and increases the likelihood of new opportunities.
- ***Ethical guardrails hold.***AI generates outputs at scale, but it has no conscience, no legal standing, yet, and no contextual judgment. Accountable humans remain the essential layer for checking, verification, ethics, and risk management. Stewards keep the system honest.
- ***Experience stays in the room.***When experienced professionals achieve real tool fluency, they combine institutional memory with high-velocity execution. Leadership stays grounded in wisdom and context, rather than being displaced by raw processing speed. This may be the single biggest payoff of personal stewardship for those of us with miles on the odometer.

### The Path Forward

The rise of AI doesn’t marginalize human labor. It clarifies what’s essential about it.

Resilience won’t be delivered to you by your employer, your industry, or our government. It will be built by you through deliberate practice of personal stewardship. Cowen’s tactics. Ruiz’s standard. Kelly’s symbiosis. Garicano’s climb. Different vocabularies, same instruction: lean into complexity, master the tools, and treat self-investment as a non-negotiable personal obligation.

> *This is the work. And it’s the most interesting work any of us will ever get to do, even for those of us in the back half of our careers.*
