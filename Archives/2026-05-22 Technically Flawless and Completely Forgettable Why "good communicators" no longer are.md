I've been watching a lot of political and advocacy leaders communicate lately, and I think we have a problem that goes deeper than skills training.

Most senior ministers are widely regarded, by every traditional measure, as good communicators. They don't make mistakes. They stay on message. They bridge back to talking points. They speak in complete sentences. If you were (maybe you are?) their media advisor, you’re pretty happy. And yet, I don't think anything they say sticks with the audience for two minutes after the clip ends. There's no realness behind it. Competent operators hitting their marks, absolutely. But resonance, persuasion? Not really.

To illustrate my point, go watch Greens Senator Steph Hodgins-May's recent Instagram reel. She filmed herself, in the back of a car at night, trying to crash a Labor corporate fundraiser at Hotel Realm in Canberra the day after the federal budget. It’s vertical video, slightly shaky, captioned in the visual language people use on short form video platforms. When she approaches she gets turned away at the door. Which is totally fair because you can’t just crash your opponents events!

But the thing I noticed was that the whole thing is the content. The argument she trying to make is this: Labor is too close to corporate donors and look what happens when you try to walk through the door. The post currently has upwards of five and a half thousand likes, a thousand shares, and a very positive ratio.

> The point here isn't to single out Hodgins-May (or the Greens, calm down). The same comparison can be made across and within every political party and most NGOs.

By contrast look at Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez doing an Instagram live cooking dumplings while explaining how a bill moves through Congress. Or think back to Jacinda Ardern's Facebook lives during COVID. She literally sat in a hoodie at home talking to people directly. Very different to Scott Morrision's equivalent attempts at the time. Jacinda wasn't doing communications in the traditional sense: she was being a person on camera while also being the prime minister.

### Trained for a pre-algorithmic world

There's a generation of leaders, in politics, business and NGO’s, who learned communication in a pre-algorithmic world. That training was built around risk management. Don't make a mistake. Don't say anything that can be clipped and used against you. Always bridge back to your message grid. It’s like the goal is to leave every encounter with the same approval rating you arrived with: don’t worry about upside or downside. This is fine if your audience is the parliamentary press gallery (or a shrinking generation still present in the gallery), but it’s kind of a disaster if your audience is the actual public.

If, as a leader, you genuinely don't understand how people *communicate* in 2026, you probably don't understand *people* in 2026. The channel is not a layer you can add on top of your leadership (“let’s get the boss on TikTok!”). It is where most people live their lives minute to minute. It's how millennials and younger generations talk to each other, how we get our news, decide what we think, and figure out who we trust. It’s how people interpret and shape their world. If your comms style is incomprehensible to that world, your understanding of community concerns probably is too.

### New platforms have changed audience expectations on every platform

The shifts in how people communicate aren't just changing expectations on TikTok or Instagram or on podcasts. They're changing expectations everywhere. The language of authenticity that audiences have learned from short-form video, creator content, and direct-to-audience newsletters has moved to the mainstream (or it has supplanted mainstream?). It now shapes what people expect from a TV interview, a stand up, a press conference, or a corporate keynote

This is why a politician's evasive answer on 7.30 doesn't even look good on 7.30 anymore. There was a time where that sort of sparring was accepted as the language of the medium. But the viewer is no longer comparing it to other evasive answers on 7.30. They're comparing it to what they are seeing in vertical video and on podcasts. The average viewer is are watching a politician on the news after having just watched a creator on TikTok speaking with vulnerability and uncertainty to camera, or reading newsletter breaking down an issue without all the spin and hedging. So in that informational context, when the politician then says "look, what I'd say to that is, what we've been very clear about from day one is,” there is a really powerful dissonance. The medium is the same as it was twenty years ago, but the audience has wildly different expectations.

### Be willing to look imperfect, get caught trying

So what do modern communicators have that traditional communicators don’t? I think it’s the willingness to look imperfect. To be caught trying. To let people see their process.

Senator Hodgins-May let herself be filmed getting turned away from a Labor fundraiser. AOC's dumplings didn't look particularly good And Jacinda’s COVID era lighting wasn’t great. Old-school political and NGO comms would never allow any of this. What those people don’t understand is that being perfect on camera comes across as really inauthentic in the algorithmic age. A leader appearing polished comes across as cagey. Intense message discipline just looks scripted.

> Generations of leaders have been put through a professional communications culture that has produced communicators who are technically flawless and completely incapable of being believed and trusted.

This is not a problem media training can fix, because media training is mostly what got us to this point. What's needed is something more uncomfortable: leaders who actually engage with how people live, talk, fight, and laugh in 2026, and who let that shape how they communicate rather than running it through a filter designed for a press conference in 1997.

The leaders who'll be effective in the next decade will be the ones who can be a recognizable human being. The ones who can only repeat lines and dodge questions will remain technically flawless and completely forgettable.
