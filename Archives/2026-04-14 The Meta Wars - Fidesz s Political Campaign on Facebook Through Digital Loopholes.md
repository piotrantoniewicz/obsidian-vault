The Meta Wars - Fidesz’s Political Campaign on Facebook Through Digital Loopholes

In the arena of modern political warfare, control is no longer just about who owns the television stations or the billboards. It is just as well about owning the digital space. In Hungary, Viktor Orbán’s Fidesz government has long been a master of traditional media dominance, but as the world shifts toward stricter tech regulations, the Hungarian ruling elite has found a new frontier: controlling the narrative also on Meta which has become especially challenging following the platform’s recent transparency restrictions and the outright ban of pollitical ads in the EU. But fear not: Orban and his team deploy various tactics through the strategic use of online “Fight Clubs” and “Digital Circles”, overstepping classification rules and even the use of hundreds, if not thousands of fake profiles and pages, through proxy organizations.

\-

By Attila Kerekes (Randstad Enterprise)

There is nothing new about a political entity wanting to minimize friction between its message and the voter. However, when the European Union and tech giants like Meta (the parent company of Facebook and Instagram) introduce hurdles, such as labeling and banning political ads or requiring transparency in funding, the Fomer Hungarian ruling party hasn’t just reacted; it doubles down.

#### The Meta Barrier and Fidesz’s creative solutions

Even before last year, Meta had introduced various restrictions globally to curb election interference, including the "Ad Library" which shows exactly how much a page spends and who is being targeted. There were times in 2024 before the EP elections, when Fidesz and their allied organizations [spent more on political ads than any other party in Europe](https://www.politico.eu/article/europe-election-political-ads-spend-online-meta-google-fidesz/). For a government that relies on a constant, overwhelming stream of narrative-setting content, these transparency requirements are a nuisance.

Nonetheless, even with these restrictions, we saw the rise of the "proxy" ecosystem. Organizations like *Megafon Centre* or *Civil Union Forum (CÖF)* acted as the vanguard. While not officially part of the state or the party, they spent millions of euros on social media advertising. Because they often categorize their content as "social issues" or "public awareness" rather than direct party political campaigning, they can sometimes navigate the gray areas of platform policies. This allows for a massive influx of pro-government messaging that feels grassroots but is fueled by industrial-scale resources. All of that, while claiming that they officially have nothing to do with the government, nor are they funded by them, but they are only financed by “generous conservatives” - who strangely one-by-one convey the exact same messages that the ruling party does. [This has been challenged even in court](https://telex.hu/belfold/2022/01/28/megafon-per-telex-itelet-kozpenz), and they were unable to prove that they don’t have public funds backing them.

But then came last October, and [the complete ban on political advertising in the EU](https://about.fb.com/news/2025/07/ending-political-electoral-and-social-issue-advertising-in-the-eu/). Nevertheless, these came as no surprise to anyone, and Fidesz had taken many steps to prepare for this “new world of digital political campaigns”. And its approach since has become threefold.

#### Misclassification

The easiest of these steps is simply ignoring the ad ban regulation of Meta, and since the ads are ‘self-classified’ when launched, Fidesz and its candidates don’t select the right category and categorize their messages as of public interest, or anything that’s unrelated to the banned political ads category. This just shows how much this regulation is flawed - and to be honest has been abused not only in Hungary, but [in other countries as well](https://www.dutchnews.nl/2026/03/metas-own-ban-on-political-advertising-is-not-working/). Although these ads can be reported, and most of the time they are taken down once flagged, the process is lengthy and manual, therefore by the time actions are taken, the messages are delivered to most of their target audience. Setting up a manual approval process of each ad, of course, probably is an impossible task to ask from Meta, but maybe AI-powered classification engines could be proven useful here, especially as Meta claims to be developing Superintelligence; they could start putting that to good use in this instance.

#### Digital Fight Groups And Circles

Another way that Fidesz had decided to prepare for the looming and later enacted ban on political ads, was setting up its own ‘digital army’ called “Harcosok Klubja” i.e. Fight Club (taken from the 1999 classic). [Orban proudly claiming that this was his own idea](https://444.hu/2025/09/11/orban-hatrebb-huzza-rogant-a-sajat-kezebe-veszi-a-kampanyt-es-kiteriti-a-lapjait), it originally started on WhatsApp and was later moved to Facebook, as a private group. Its goal is simple: keeping fans and followers engaged through daily tasks and missions, and setting a course for them on which facebook posts to engage on whether positively (posts of Orban and government members, or MP candidates) or negatively: to comment on the posts of opposition candidates or of Magyar Peter (the leader of the biggest opposition party, TISZA). Originally it aimed at reaching 100k members within months, [it currently sits at ca. 61k.](https://www.facebook.com/groups/729605139802429/)

Additionally, another group was brought to life - the Digitális Polgári Kör (Digital Citizen Circle), which aims to recreate the very successful grassroots organizations that kept Fidesz alive in spite of its multiple failed attempts at winning the elections in the early 2000s (2002 and 2006). As Orbán put it, Fight Club is the active, more aggressive part of their digital home taking strategy, while the Digital Circles sit as the coglemeration of the more silent but nonetheless supporting group of Fidesz allied voters. The Digital Circles are also organized based on [topics of interest](https://dpkor.hu/#korok) (from “For Families” and “Science and AI” to “Farmers” and even “Gamers”), thereby giving a more smaller community feeling to it - even if in the digital sphere. Most of these Circles are still basically Facebook Groups, though, with more or less activity in them, and also being used as mouthpieces to echo and widen the reach of Fidesz’s campaign messages. Also, one of Orbán’s latest tours around the country was labeled as Digital Circle Meetups, where people (after registration and approval) had the chance to see the PM in person, and even ask questions from him on a couple of occasions.

#### The shadow army

And the last piece of Fidesz’s online campaign puzzle was probably the most shady one - a digital bot army, that is most likely controlled through proxy organizations. Based on the investigative journalism of Hungarian (still) independent news outlets, [these are hundreds of mostly Facebook Pages](https://telex.hu/techtud/2026/01/08/a-milliardos-a-felesege-meg-a-filmrendezo-haverja-oriasi-fideszesek-kar-hogy-egyikuk-sem-valodi), most of the with AI generated profile pictures, kept dormant for some time, but lately activated with seemingly one purpose only: to engage in online discussions in the Facebook comments, thereby boosting the reach of Fidesz representatives and main figures. Strangely though, the reason why this bot army was discovered was down to Meta’s restrictions and complexity of creating actual user profiles - due to these, Pages had to be created, which are more easily done, and can be easily multiplied, nonetheless, they remain trackable, in terms of creation date, as well as owner.

The traces of ownership [actually lead back to the organization](https://444.hu/2026/01/30/megtalaltuk-a-trollfarmot-ahonnan-alprofilok-szazaval-ontjak-a-fideszes-kommenteket-a-facebookon) that holds the previously mentioned Circles together - the Digitális Demokráciafejlesztési Ügynökség (Digital Democracy Development Agency). Fidesz and groups supporting them do have this tendency to almost ironically name organizations as heroic groups fighting for democracy, while appearing to be doing the exact opposite of that.

#### Data as the Ultimate Political Asset

The true engine behind this digital dominance, however, is personal data. Information as the most valuable currency in 21st-century politics, and the Hungarian government has been accused by various NGOs and international observers of leveraging even state databases for these purposes.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, many Hungarians registered for vaccines via a government portal. Later, those same email addresses were used to send out political newsletters and "government information" that mirrored Fidesz campaign slogans (including in this election cycle now in 2026). By using state-collected data to build direct communication channels, the government found another channel to bypass traditional advertising altogether. When you have the email address and phone number of the voter, you don't need to worry about Meta’s algorithm or ad restrictions, you are already in their pocket. Nonetheless this has far reaching consequences in a digital world, where citizens will be even more wary of using digital government services, or downloading and using government apps, when they see that their data is constantly being misused for political purposes.

#### What lessons can be drawn from the Hungarian case?

The challenge for the Hungarian opposition or any political force in similar hybrid regimes: how do you fight a narrative that is everywhere at once? When the government uses public funds to collect data, and then uses that data to fuel a private political machine, the playing field is no longer level. It is a "hybrid" approach where the lines between the state, the party, and the individual are blurred until they disappear.

If you are an opposition party or a reformer in a hybrid regime, the Hungarian example offers a stark lesson: **Digital sovereignty is just as important as on-the-ground presence.**

1\. **Build Your Own Data:** You cannot rely on third-party platforms that can change their rules overnight. Parties must invest in their own GDPR-compliant databases to ensure they have a direct line to their supporters. A line, that their supporters willingly and openly allowed them to use, and not abusing data sets otherwised provided for different uses.

2\. **Agility Over Bureaucracy:** The government’s use of "influencer" proxies works because it feels personal and rapid. Opposition movements often get bogged down in committee-approved messaging, while the government’s digital army has already posted ten videos.

3\. **Transparency as a Weapon:** While the government hides behind proxies, the opposition can gain credibility by being radically transparent. In an era of "fake news" and shadow spending, authenticity is a rare and powerful commodity. And at the end of it all - nothing EVER will beat a personal handshake, a speech that was heard on the town square of your own village or city. Magyar Péter is the prime example of this that Yes, you CAN fight digital shadow proxies very effectively with on-the-ground presence.

### Conclusion: The Marathon Continues

The Former Hungarian government’s attempts and successes to "jump" Meta’s restrictions are a testament to their adaptability. They recognize that in a hybrid regime, the goal is not just to win the argument, but to ensure that the other side’s argument is never even heard. By merging state data with private-style digital marketing, they have created a feedback loop that is incredibly difficult to break.

However, as I have mentioned before, success is not final. These digital loopholes exist because the government is, in some way, afraid of a fair fight on a level playing field. For those seeking change, the path forward is clear: you cannot just react to the government’s digital maneuvers. You must build your own infrastructure, protect your own data, and speak to the public **in person,** somethingthat no algorithm can suppress.
