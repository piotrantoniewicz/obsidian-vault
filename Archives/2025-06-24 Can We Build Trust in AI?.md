### And why it matters more than ever.

As Kendrick Lamar once said, “All we ask is trust. All we got is us.”

Trust is the foundation of our everyday lives and interactions. It’s trust in our currency to get paid and make purchases, our relationships to build friendships and families, and our laws to protect us.

Trust, as defined by the [National Institutes of Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10083508/#ref73) “ *is an individual's calculated exposure to the risk of harm from the actions of an influential other* ”, and put more simply “a directional transaction between two parties.”

But when it comes to AI, trust is in short supply. Artificial intelligence will reshape many aspects of our lives - from how we advertise, govern, communicate, diagnose illness, and make decisions. While it still seems very early in the development, usage, and adoption of this new technology, Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, [wrote in a recent blog post](https://blog.samaltman.com/the-gentle-singularity) that “humanity is close to building digital superintelligence.”

So, what is the biggest barrier to fully embracing AI adoption? A lack of trust in the technology (hallucinations, misinformation), the developers and the application. Fortunately, it’s not too late to earn and build it, but it must be done now.

**Americans Are Less Likely To Trust AI And Why That Matters**

According to [KPMG’s 2025 Trust, Attitude and Use of Artificial Intelligence](https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/ai-and-technology/trust-attitudes-and-use-of-ai.html) report, the U.S. falls far behind many of its peers and competitors when it comes to safe and ethical use and perceived trustworthiness of AI. Just over 40% of U.S. respondents said AI systems are safe and ethical to use, compared to over 75% in China and close to 80% in India.

> While the U.S. has the largest companies building and deploying these AI models - OpenAI ($300 Billion) and Anthropic ($61 Billion), along with Google and Meta it is the sustained usage and implementation that is critical. Many other countries including China could race ahead with faster adoption given the much higher level of trust. It’s therefore, not the lack of tech, but the lack of trust that could be the biggest risk to winning the AI race.

**We Must Address The Trust Deficit**

Trust cannot be manufactured. It’s not a set of training data that can be fed into the models. It’s a human centered construct and it’s essential. As the research from KPMG underscores, “trust is important because it underpins the acceptance and sustained adoption of AI.” We must not only trust the systems but the people building those systems.

It is therefore up to us. Those building, adopting, investing, governing and reporting on this technology to lead and earn trust and develop technology that can be trusted.

As the historian Yuval Noah Harari said eloquently on a [recent podcast with Reid Hoffman](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uuBLxWowDqI&list=PLV6sFuGk6xd8VmLNy0SAEnY3MRQ0AGZx2&index=1),

“ **The AI observes us. It sees us - if we are developers who behave in untrustworthy ways we can’t produce tools that can be trusted.**”

**Can Legislation Help?**

Trust in government is at a record low. According to [Pew Research, only 22% of Americans](https://www.pew.org/en/trend/archive/fall-2024/americans-deepening-mistrust-of-institutions) say they trust the federal government. The [EU passed AI legislation](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/topics/en/article/20230601STO93804/eu-ai-act-first-regulation-on-artificial-intelligence) in 2023, but still some of the largest counties in Europe still lack trust in AI. Finland, for example, ranks last on KPMG’s measurement on safe and ethnical use of AI. It seems highly unlikely that simply regulation will equate to trust in AI. There have now been 22 ‘Deepfake’ bills passed in state legislatures so far, with many more pending in legislatures. While it’s unlikely that we’ll see any federal legislation, we are left with a patchwork of state legislation that creates a legal and operational mess. Particularly if you are working across multiple states. While legislation is needed, this approach is potentially hindering meaningful adoption from good actors. We must work to understand and monitor the legislation and pro-actively work with local elected officials to have meaningful progress on guardrails, upskilling and future proofing.

**4 Ways To Help Build Trust in AI Through Leadership Not Just Legislation**

Where do we go from here? What can we do to build trust? It is up to all of us, as leaders, to build and contribute to the future of AI and we can start with the following strategies:

1. **Leadership:** From the public to the private sector, leadership matters. Decision-makers must lead proactively, set clear standards, and prioritize long-term trust over short-term metrics.
1. **Education & Access:** Industry specific guidance, fine-tuned to use cases and with clear, accessible education will help more people engage critically–not fearfully—with the technology..
1. **Inclusivity & Transparency:** AI is not a one-size-fits all tool.Not every model or platform will or should work for your use case. We need to be clear on how it’s applied and where it’s used, and humans are core to that decision-making
1. **Thoughtful Implementation:** This is a marathon, not a sprint. Balance speed with intentionality. We can no longer afford to just ‘move fast and break things' anymore. Development, execution and understanding of the tools is critical and guardrails that help not hinder adoption are key.
