*This morning I opened my inbox to find Google announcing they'll start analyzing all our Gmail communications for "data protection insights" in four weeks. Enabled by default, naturally. After using AI tools for over five years and helping nonprofit consultants navigate ethical AI adoption through AIxImpact, this felt familiar: corporate surveillance positioned as an organizational benefit.*

### What Actually Happened Today

Let me tell you what that email really said, because the corporate speak is doing a lot of work here.

Google already scans your emails if you have [Data Loss Prevention rules](https://support.google.com/a/answer/9646351?hl=en), which are policies that block emails containing sensitive information like social security numbers or credit card data. What's new is they're now building systematic intelligence reports from that scanning. They know which departments share what sensitive information, who your external partners are, and where your "risk areas" are located.

The technical capability isn't new. The systematic analysis and organizational profiling is.

**Corporate AI analytics for organizational communications isn't new, but the scale and systematic nature of Google's approach represents an escalation.** Microsoft has documented how their [Viva Insights analyzes communication patterns](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-viva/insights) to provide behavioral insights to organizations, showing how email and collaboration data can generate valuable organizational intelligence.

### Why This Matters for Nonprofit Consultants

[As someone who's spent 35 years in nonprofit management and helped raise over $200 million for Bay Area causes](https://www.gayleroberts.com/), I see how these policies affect the entire sector differently than individual organizations might. Through my work with dozens of nonprofits and co-founding [AIxImpact](https://www.aiximpact.com/) (a peer-led learning community for consultants navigating AI), I've observed how corporate AI policies and software impact organizations that often lack the resources to evaluate these trade-offs themselves.

When Google systematically analyzes organizational communications, they're not just gaining intelligence about individual nonprofits: **they're mapping the entire ecosystem of social change work.** Which advocacy organizations are coordinating on policy campaigns. Which foundations are shifting their funding priorities. Which community groups are facing internal challenges. This represents competitive intelligence about the sector itself.

### The Security Case and the Governance Gap

**Let's not kid ourselves: cybersecurity threats organizations face are real and escalating rapidly.** At RSAC25 earlier this year, I listened to CISOs and security engineers who reported that [AI is expanding attack surfaces faster than defense systems can adapt](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/ai-risk-responsibility-notes-from-rsac25-gayle-roberts-cfrm--hvufc/?trackingId=Z1clf9EWRpiKWgYDb6EuKg%3D%3D). Legacy security tools struggle with AI-native risks like prompt injection and agent chaining. SOC and SIEM platforms designed for traditional threats are struggling to keep pace with foundation models, APIs, and autonomous agents, which introduce entirely new attack vectors.

Google's analytics can identify concerning patterns, such as sensitive financial data being shared externally at unusual rates or executive communications being forwarded outside the organization more frequently. These insights have legitimate security value.

The critical issue is that Google's solution is disproportionate to the security threats it claims to address. Organizations need targeted security tools that detect email-based attacks without comprehensive organizational intelligence gathering. T **he systematic analysis of communication patterns, strategic discussions, and internal priorities goes far beyond what's necessary for cybersecurity.**

The governance problem becomes clear when this rollout happened through procurement, not policy. No public input, no stakeholder consultation, no democratic deliberation about embedding systematic communication analysis into organizational infrastructure. This suggests the primary motivation isn't security, it's competitive intelligence gathering positioned as a security service.

### The Real Trade-offs

The individual privacy concerns are real, but they're not the biggest problem. When Google systematically analyzes organizational communications, they're gaining competitive intelligence that has serious market value:

- Which organizations are expanding or struggling based on email volume and urgency patterns
- Internal strategic priorities based on what leadership discusses most frequently
- Vendor relationships and their stability based on external communication analysis
- Financial health indicators based on which "risk areas" get flagged most often

Even with enterprise contract safeguards, this represents a massive intelligence gathering operation that benefits Google's business development far beyond the security value provided to organizations.

### My SAFE Framework Applied

A good place to start is with my [SAFE Framework](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/safe-20-centering-power-justice-community-nonprofit-gayle-lkztc/), designed to help organizations evaluate AI systems by examining power dynamics, not just technical capabilities.

Organizations should **Scan** for whose power this system actually serves and whose interests are being served. While there are legitimate security benefits, Google gains far more organizational intelligence than they provide back in security insights.

We need to **Affirm** values by demanding genuine consent processes rather than opt-out defaults. Security tools should require informed consent, especially when they involve new forms of organizational analysis.

We must **Field-Test** for systemic harm to organizational autonomy and democratic governance. When private companies systematically analyze how organizations communicate, this concentrates power over institutional knowledge.

And we need to **Elevate** these concerns while building alternatives that provide security benefits without surveillance capitalism.

### What Organizations Can Do Right Now

First, understand that opting out doesn't make you anti-security. You can block emails containing credit card numbers without feeding Google's intelligence operation. The security function and the analytics function are separate, even if they use the same scanning capability.

**To opt out:** In your Google Admin console, go to Security > Access and data control > Data protection. Click the "Data insights scanning and report" card, then turn off the "Enable Gmail Insights" button.

Second, if you decide to opt in, understand the limitations of corporate accountability. Tech companies don't provide individual responses about how your data gets used. That's not how their business models work. Your organizational data becomes part of their broader intelligence systems, with transparency limited to high-level privacy policies and terms of service.

Third, consider your risk profile carefully. Organizations serving vulnerable populations face different calculations than tech companies when it comes to communication pattern analysis.

**High-risk organizations should seriously consider alternatives:** Immigration rights groups, labor organizers, reproductive rights advocates, domestic violence shelters, legal aid providers, and civil liberties organizations face heightened surveillance risks. Government data requests often target these sectors, and communication pattern analysis could reveal sensitive strategy, funding sources, client information, or community connections.

**Most other nonprofits can reasonably stay with proper governance:** Food banks, educational nonprofits, environmental groups, and arts organizations typically don't handle information that poses significant risks if analyzed for patterns. The key is implementing strong data governance, security training, and using alternative platforms for your most sensitive discussions while keeping Google for routine operations.

Fourth, understand the broader surveillance context. Major tech companies have [dramatically increased data sharing with government agencies](https://proton.me/blog/big-tech-data-requests-surge), with **Google, Meta, and Apple collectively turning over details of 3.16 million accounts to authorities from 2014 to 2024.** When organizations interact with agencies that could potentially use Google's AI tools AND have their own communications analyzed by Google's systems, this could create comprehensive intelligence about policy implementation and organizational responses. Given the current federal administration, I think we can all anticipate that this type of data sharing will increase.

### Building Democratic Alternatives.

But the good news is that there are alternatives. [Barcelona's DECODE platform](https://ajuntament.barcelona.cat/digital/en/technology-accessible-everyone/accessible-and-participatory/decode) demonstrates how communities can control digital infrastructure. The city uses decentralized technology to ensure [residents participate in decisions about algorithmic systems](https://decodeproject.eu/blog/pilots-which-will-help-citizens-regain-control-over-their-personal-data.html) rather than just receiving their outputs.

While email infrastructure requires different technical complexity, the governance principles apply. The [Platform Cooperativism Consortium](https://platform.coop/) explores how democratic ownership can reshape digital systems, with their [2025 conference on Cooperatives and AI](https://platform.coop/events/cooperativeai/) addressing exactly these questions.

Closer to home, AIxImpact members are building tools that serve community needs rather than corporate intelligence gathering. [Change Agent](https://thechange.ai/), for example, focuses on data extraction capabilities that organizations control directly, rather than feeding into larger surveillance systems.

If you got that email this morning, you have four weeks to decide whether the security benefits justify feeding Google's intelligence operation. Make that decision based on your actual security needs and risk tolerance, not ideology.

But also ask harder questions: Could these security insights be provided through systems with better governance? What would email infrastructure look like if it were designed to serve organizational security without extracting competitive intelligence?

Because **this email isn't really about Gmail analytics. It's about whether our digital infrastructure serves democratic values or corporate intelligence gathering.**

The organizations that demand better governance now will be ready when communities start expecting technology that actually serves them.
