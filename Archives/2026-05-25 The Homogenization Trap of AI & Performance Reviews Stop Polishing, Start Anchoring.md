### TEAMS! Newsletter | Talent Edition | May 25, 2026

The nonprofit sector has long wrestled with a painful irony: we are a sector powered by deep human capability, yet our tools for measuring that capability are fundamentally flawed. Every year, every manager or supervisor spends countless hours agonizing over performance review narratives, trying to capture the elusive essence of what makes an employee truly exceptional or in need of more coaching. We rely on subjective memories, personal relationships, and storytelling ability, often producing evaluations that reflect a reviewer's writing skill more than an employee’s actual impact.

To ease this important yet burdensome ritual for most people, organizations are rapidly deploying AI assistants to draft and polish performance review narratives. On the surface, this seems like a major win for overextended managers. But a greater danger is emerging. By using AI to produce weak observations that sound polished and professional, we aren't fixing or even improving the performance management review process. We’re potentially trapping our organizations in a cycle of beautiful, homogenized prose that completely sanitizes the unique, messy, and brilliant contributions of our people.

### Executive Summary

Most organizations are misdirecting Generative AI in talent management. Instead of using AI as a glorified copyeditor to write smoother performance narratives, we need to redirect it toward a far higher purpose: surfacing direct, verifiable evidence of work in action and uncovering areas for future growth and development. By shifting the evaluation process away from subjective trait labels and toward specific, meaningful contributions, or even potential shortfalls, leaders can build a more transparent, equitable review process that recognizes and rewards the drivers of mission success and ensures a focus on future development.

### Key Takeaways

- ***Polished is Not the Same as Reliable:*** AI-drafted reviews create a false sense of consistency, flattening distinct employee contributions into a standardized, convincing voice that masks underlying manager biases.
- ***Surface Evidence, Don't Smooth Narratives:***The true power of AI lies in its ability to scan historical work to identify the specific moments when judgment and impact actually occurred.
- ***Measure What Drives Outcomes, Not Just Outputs:*** Traditional metrics capture easily quantifiable outputs, but AI can often reveal overlooked, higher-order capabilities such as mentorship, foresight, and cross-functional influence.
- ***Reframe the Conversation Around Moments:*** Shifting from abstract trait ratings to evidence-based discussions compels both managers and employees to assess real-world impact.
- ***Build Guardrails for AI Review Use:*** Transitioning to evidence-based AI review preparation requires strong governance over how information is gathered to ensure employees retain control over their work details and that casual communications remain strictly off-limits.

### The False Promise of Polished Prose

When a major enterprise reports that AI cuts review-writing time by 40%, resource-constrained nonprofit and human resources leaders naturally sit up and take notice. In a sector where burnout is rampant, reclaiming hours spent on documentation feels like a win for everyone.

However, when AI is used merely to compile and “beautify” traditional narrative reviews, it creates an optical illusion of quality. Reviews produced this way tend to converge on a single, fluent tone. The critical distinction that once set a carefully considered evaluation apart from generic, superficial praise begins to erode.

- ***The Reality Check:*** If every performance review sounds equally persuasive, confident, and professional, poor information becomes nearly impossible to detect. A manager working from incomplete observations or subtle biases can now craft a highly credible narrative that masks these blind spots. In the social services sector, where equity and objectivity are so important, this homogenization doesn't solve our talent review and development problems; it exacerbates them.

### Shifting from Adjectives to Artifacts

To unlock the potential of Generative AI in the annual review process, we need to start by altering the prompt. Instead of asking an AI tool to *"help me write a performance review for Jane,"* we should direct it to *"find examples where Jane changed the direction of a project or resolved a complex funding challenge"*.

Consider a typical nonprofit scenario: a program director is labeled in their annual review as "demonstrating exceptional strategic leadership under pressure." While that sounds excellent, it is a subjective assessment. By shifting to an evidence-based approach using AI, vague praise can be replaced by program impact details:

- The precise decision memo in which the director identified a flawed assumption in a food distribution program and corrected it before launch.
- The project retrospective detailing how they successfully restructured a failing community outreach initiative.
- The cross-functional emails and meeting transcripts showing how they aligned disparate stakeholders around a difficult trade-off.

Suddenly, the performance review is no longer an exercise in creative writing or a debate over adjectives. It becomes an objective record of the director’s work where they exercised judgment and influence, with clearly visible outcomes.

### Capturing Invisible Contributions

Nonprofit success rarely stems from easily quantifiable, linear outputs alone. While a for-profit sales team can track quotas on a CRM dashboard, social impact work relies heavily on systemic, collaborative capabilities.

When organizations try to force objectivity, they almost always lean toward what is easiest to measure, such as the number of grant applications submitted, events hosted, or clients served. What they consistently overlook are the higher-order contributions that prevent failure and accelerate long-term growth:

- The quiet mentorship given to a struggling case manager that accelerates their professional development.
- The subtle conflict resolution that keeps a fractured community coalition moving forward harmoniously.
- The strategic foresight that spots a shift in donor behavior and quietly redirects an organization’s fundraising strategy.

Because these behaviors leave no footprint on “dashboards”, they remain invisible. However, AI systems can analyze broader communication networks and interaction patterns to spot these behaviors. They can map influence flows across email threads and project channels to identify the "unsung heroes", the employees who constantly help others solve problems and keep critical initiatives from derailing.

### Putting It into Action

Transitioning your teams from narrative-based evaluations to evidence-based AI curation requires intentional changes in how your organization manages talent. Here are five practical steps you can implement in the coming weeks:

- ***Change the Questions in Your One-on-Ones:*** Reframe them to focus on specific, important episodes: *"What specific moment this month best revealed your strategic thinking in action?"*
- ***Double-Check Your AI Prompts:*** Review how managers may be using embedded tools such as Microsoft Copilot or Google Workspace AI. Instruct them to stop using AI to draft narrative paragraphs and instead use it to scan historical project records to surface key inflection points.
- ***Define Clear Boundaries for Data Scoping:*** Establish an organizational policy that specifies which systems are fair game for performance evidence and which are off-limits. Make it explicit: evaluations may draw on formal work documents, such as project proposals, grant drafts, and meeting notes, but never on casual Slack DMs, personal emails, or private messages.
- ***Implement Employee-Controlled Impact “Folders”:*** Build trust by giving your staff absolute control over their own AI-curated impact “folder”. Let the AI system surface relevant events or accomplishments to the *employee first*, allowing them to choose which verified impact to include in their final self-assessment.
- ***Enforce the Human Guardrail:*** Formally dictate that AI should never, under any circumstances, generate performance ratings, automated scorecards, or talent decisions. AI must act strictly as an objective curator of facts, leaving all judgment, context, and empathy entirely in human hands.

### The Close

In the social sector, our people are the engine that drives our mission. Perpetuating a broken, narrative-based performance system that rewards storytelling over actual contributions does a profound disservice to our work, employees, and communities.

We can use Generative AI as a superficial shield to make a flawed, subjective process look beautiful on paper, or we can use it to democratize talent development. By using technology to surface evidence of impact, we can foster workplaces that more accurately recognize, celebrate, and reward the contributions team members make to our success.

> ***"Talent management means valuing verifiable moments of impact, which AI can help uncover, supporting a culture that recognizes, elevates, and sustains excellence."***
