### One year of AI Impact Hub, and the barrier I still can't remove.

Last week, on my birthday, I made a decision that felt equal parts obvious and terrifying.

I took the majority of AI Impact Hub’s foundational courses — prompting, custom GPTs, AI policy templates — and made them completely free for anyone working in the nonprofit sector.

I’d love to tell you it was a strategic masterstroke. The truth? It was driven by something closer to desperation.

Not personal desperation — global desperation.

**The numbers that broke me**

Seventy percent of nonprofits don’t have a budget for AI training. More than half cite cost as the primary barrier to adoption. And only 14% have even published an AI policy — which is really just the bare minimum signal that leadership has acknowledged this technology exists.

I kept staring at those numbers and thinking: these are the organisations working on the world’s biggest problems. Injustice. Inequality. Poverty. Climate change. Factory farming. Modern slavery. Mental health crises growing faster than the systems built to address them.

The single most transformative technology most of these organisations will encounter in their lifetimes is sitting right there — and cost is locking them out of even understanding what it can do.

So I removed the cost.

---

**The uncomfortable truth I didn’t want to admit**

Here’s the part I wrestled with for a long time.

If you’d offered me these same free resources during my worst years of burnout in the sector — the years that nearly broke me — I probably wouldn’t have used them.

I was too busy being busy. Too exhausted to learn something new. Too deep in the cycle of urgent tasks to step back and think about what might actually make things better.

And that’s the trap. The people who need this most are often the least able to engage with it. They’re buried under soul-crushing manual tasks, drowning in admin, running on fumes — and the idea of adding *one more thing*, even a free thing, even a potentially transformative thing, feels impossible.

Making training free removes a real barrier. But it doesn’t remove the burnout. It doesn’t remove the culture that normalises overwork. And it doesn’t remove the leadership gap that leaves individual staff to figure this out alone.

---

**Where leadership is failing**

Fourteen percent. That number haunts me.

An AI policy isn’t magic. But it’s the bare minimum signal that someone at the top has sat down and said: *this matters, we need to think about it.* And 86% of organisations haven’t even done that.

The real barriers I keep seeing aren’t about technology or cost. They’re about visibility. Staff normalise inefficient tasks because they don’t know alternatives exist. They don’t know that the thing eating three hours of their week is a problem that’s already been solved. No one has shown them what’s possible — and no one at the top is creating the conditions for them to find out.

Then there’s the fear. Some staff worry AI will replace them, which keeps them from engaging with the very tools that could make their work more meaningful and less grinding.

I’ve worked with leadership teams where we met every single week — training them, upskilling them, developing strategy, building an implementation timeline with tool guidance. That top-down investment changes everything. But it’s the exception, not the norm.

And here’s where I have to be honest: by making the training free for individuals, there’s a risk I’m letting leadership off the hook. Reinforcing the pattern of leaving it up to staff to figure it out on their own time, with their own energy, on top of everything else they’re already carrying.

This needs to be both. Top-down *and* bottom-up. Leadership needs strategy, investment, and guided support. Individuals need accessible resources and permission to experiment. Neither alone is enough.

---

**So where’s the leverage point?**

If cost isn’t the barrier, and leadership buy-in is slow, and burnout makes learning feel impossible — where does the actual shift happen?

I keep coming back to the same answer, and it’s almost embarrassingly simple.

Five minutes.

Just open the tool. Pick one task — one thing you’re already doing that feels tedious or repetitive. Open ChatGPT or Claude. Create a project. Drop some relevant files in. Start a conversation. And go back to that same project tomorrow.

That’s it. That’s the intervention.

Slow and steady consistency compounds and wins every time versus trying to do everything all at once. I’ve seen this pattern over and over. The people who break through aren’t the ones who take a weekend course or attend a conference or read every article about AI strategy. They’re the ones who build a small daily habit and let it compound.

The flywheel effect with AI is real. I see it in my own work building agentic systems — agents with skills, memory, and schedules. You can’t do everything at once. But five minutes today becomes ten tomorrow, becomes a fundamentally different way of working within weeks.

> The leverage point isn’t access to AI training. It’s the daily habit of actually using the tool.

---

**What an aha moment actually looks like**

At a recent conference, someone came up to me buzzing with excitement. She’d discovered Google’s NotebookLM and was recording every presentation she attended, uploading them to a notebook, and using it to continue her learning afterwards — creating infographics, pulling out key insights, turning conference notes into something she could actually integrate into her work.

She hadn’t taken a course. She hadn’t been mandated by leadership. She’d found a tool that solved *her* specific problem, and suddenly the whole landscape opened up.

That’s what the aha moment looks like. It’s not abstract. It’s deeply personal. It’s: *I had no idea this was possible.*

You can have more than one. You can have them with different tools, different use cases. But the first one requires you to show up and try — and that’s the five minutes I’m talking about.

---

**What’s at stake if nothing changes**

I’ll be direct: by 2028, nonprofits that aren’t prioritising AI are in for a rude awakening.

The exponential curve is steepening. Funder expectations are shifting — they’ll start asking how organisations are leveraging AI, and *“we haven’t started yet”* will stop being an acceptable answer. Staff will demand access to tools. NVIDIA is now proposing providing engineers with half their salary’s worth in AI tokens each year. Meanwhile, most nonprofits won’t even give their people a $20/month paid licence.

Completely different spectrums.

I believe we’ll start seeing organisations actually shutting down because of this gap. And something will flip that defies the traditional logic of the sector: smaller, more agile organisations will make a bigger impact than larger ones — with fewer resources — because they found the leverage point faster.

There’s an irony I can’t shake. Some nonprofits celebrate being around for fifty years. But shouldn’t the goal be to solve the problem as fast as possible? Shouldn’t success actually mean putting yourself out of business? AI doesn’t guarantee that — but it’s the most powerful accelerant these organisations have ever had access to. And right now, it feels like a profound lost opportunity.

The pain of staying the same has to eventually outweigh the pain of changing. I just hope that awakening doesn’t come too late.

---

**What I’m asking**

The free courses are [there](https://aiimpacthub.com/). Prompting fundamentals. Custom GPTs. AI policy templates. The building blocks. If you work in the nonprofit sector, I genuinely hope you’ll use them and share them with someone who could benefit.

But I also want to hear from you.

If cost isn’t the barrier — what is? What are the constraints you’re actually facing? What would it take for you to open an AI tool for five minutes tomorrow?

Because I’ve removed one obstacle. I want to know what the others are, so I can work on those too.
