**What Europe Might Learn from the Strategic Fractures of the Harris Campaign, Zohran Mamdani - and the Alternatives Emerging.**

The story of Kamala Harris’s 2024 campaign has already sparked widespread analysis, with [*Süddeutsche Zeitung via The Atlantic*](https://www.sueddeutsche.de/projekte/artikel/politik/inside-the-democratic-rupture-that-undermined-kamala-harris-s-presidential-hopes-e605715/?utm_source=Twitter&utm_medium=twitterbot&utm_campaign=e605715&reduced=true) offering a particularly detailed look into the strategic and structural fractures that defined its final stretch.

But the dynamics it surfaced - competition between campaign entities, tension between data and narrative, the limits of targeted persuasion - aren’t just relevant to American politics. They raise deeper, long-standing questions about how democratic campaigns are built, and what kinds of power they’re really trying to generate.

From a European perspective it can feel like a mirror. Our own political systems face many of the same tactical dilemmas: how to communicate at scale without losing human connection, how to use technology without flattening complexity or becoming disconnected from lived experience.

And alongside these, a more urgent challenge: how to respond to a far right that has grown more electorally competitive, culturally confident, and strategically coherent. Figures like Marine Le Pen, Nigel Farage and Viktor Orbán are no longer just riding waves of discontent. Like Trump, they are actively shaping them by offering movements that channel grievance into a sense of political and cultural belonging.

Their success highlights the limits of technocratic or transactional approaches. And it underscores the need for the political centre, and progressives in particular, to build campaigns that are not only persuasive, but emotionally resonant and structurally grounded in everyday life.

### Fast Politics, Shallow Ground

Future Forward - the super PAC supporting Harris - built one of the most technologically advanced campaign operations ever seen. It raised more than $900 million, accounting for 69% of all Democratic presidential super PAC funding, and ran over 3 billion digital video ad impressions. Its testing regime was just as ambitious: thousands of unique ads, over 14 million voter surveys in the final 10 months.

> It reached far - but not necessarily deep.

The official Harris campaign focused on democratic threat and Trump’s instability; Future Forward prioritised economic persuasion based on data modelling. Both had merit. But with no shared framework, fragmentation prevailed, and arguably created space for Trump’s return. Whether that was the decisive cause is up for debate. In my opinion it underlines a deeper crisis: both camps argued over renting attention while the electorate was drifting further away from that whole way of doing politics.

I’ve seen similar dynamics in Europe - not between campaigns and PACs, but between party leadership and civil society, or between national teams and local organisers. Disagreements over how to interpret data, how much to trust it, or what counts as signal versus noise often unfold in spaces where no meaningful groundwork is being done.

Campaigns can become so absorbed in internal metrics - engagement rates, favourability shifts, sentiment tracking - that they lose sight of the fundamentals of organising: building trust, developing leaders, and sustaining participation.

### A Warning and an Opportunity

Contemporary campaigns are designed for speed and scale. Message discipline and digital targeting are core strengths. But when they aren’t connected to real relationships and narrative depth, they turn politics into a delivery system: content pushed toward tired or disengaged audiences, rather than an invitation into a shared political project.

In many structurally excluded communities - among young people, racialised groups, and the working poor - this dynamic is especially sharp. Politics arrives in the final weeks of a campaign, then vanishes. The problem isn’t the message. It’s the lack of consistent presence and care. Building genuine relationships with these communities is ever more vital, [as recent social network research shows](https://campaignlab.uk/project/how-social-networks-shape-political-victories-lessons-from-trumps-2016-campaign/) (from the brilliant Campaign Lab) small groups of engaged people can be powerful in turning campaigns.

In Europe, we’re seeing the same drift. Parties have become strategically capable at the centre but often disconnected. Field operations, membership structures, and embedded organisers have withered especially in excluded communities. The far right understands this and exploits the vacuum.

### Technology Will Push This Further

Political technology is only becoming more powerful and more accessible. Artificial intelligence is already reshaping how campaigns test messages, produce content, and allocate resources. Used well, it can save time for campaign staff and improve responsiveness. But without grounding in real-world relationships, it risks replicating the very patterns that weakened the Harris operation: narrow targeting, rigid strategy, strategic overconfidence.

AI isn’t inherently political, it's a tool. But if its only use is optimising ad placement or micro-targeting, it may further displace the harder work of relationship-building and make campaigns blind sighted. And the public is not naïve. As these tactics proliferate, voters will become even more resistant to them.

Better scripts and slicker ads won’t re-earn trust.

### Another Way Is Possible

There are other models emerging not just in theory, but in practice. Take the recent example of [Zohran Mamdani’s campaign in New York](https://www.theguardian.com/news/ng-interactive/2025/jun/29/does-zohran-mamdani-new-york-primary-victory-offer-roadmap-for-democrats). While Harris’s operation relied on billion-dollar broadcast machinery, Mamdani a democratic socialist backed by grassroots energy ran a campaign rooted in affordability, cultural fluency, and face-to-face organising. His team knocked doors ([1,621,326 of them](https://mmilesgoodrich.substack.com/p/itsthe-politics-of-no-translation)), spoke multiple languages, held club nights and community events. They met people, listened, and built something participatory. His victory was less about perfect message targeting more about real connection and simple direct language.

Whether his approach is transferable to all battlegrounds is open to question. But it serves as a sharp reminder: voters aren’t disengaged because they need more targeted content. They’re disengaged because they don’t feel included in the political process at all.

### Reconnecting to What Already Exists

It’s possible to design campaigns that are both strategic and grounded. But doing so requires a shift in starting point from persuasion to participation.

In Europe, that might mean reconnecting with the networks already embedded in our civic life but too often sidelined in electoral strategy: cooperatives, unions, neighbourhood groups, cultural clubs, diasporic and faith communities, businesses, schools, civil society organisations.

> Many of these types of organisation were born in Europe and remain strong. We don’t need to reinvent them but we do need to bring them back to the centre of political work.

And it means getting out from behind the dashboards. Actually meeting people. Holding space to listen. Giving voters something more than messaging: giving them authorship. And for those working in political tech it's about building tools that facilitate, not undermine, that work.

That’s not easy. But neither is losing to movements built on resentment.
