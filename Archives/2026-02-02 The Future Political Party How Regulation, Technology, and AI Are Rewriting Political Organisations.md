The Future Political Party: How Regulation, Technology, and AI Are Rewriting Political Organisations

The core issue of this session is the **"existential inertia"** of mainstream political parties, which are currently being outpaced by **high-velocity populist communication loops**. **Joshua Carrington (Labour Party CTO)** and **Jochen Koenig (Cosmonauts and Kings)** identify a crisis of political culture where bureaucratic layers and a fear of failure prevent democratic parties from adopting the **agile, in-house technical capabilities** necessary to fight propaganda. The discussion concludes that parties must transform into **modular, technical hubs** that prioritize **sovereign, open-source AI infrastructure** to reclaim their role as authentic messengers.

\-

Session Summary

The modern political party is an outdated vessel currently struggling to navigate a digital information environment that rewards speed, decentralization, and **"agentic" automation**. **Joshua Carrington, Chief Technical Officer of the UK Labour Party**, describes the "innovation dilemma" of 100-year-old institutions that remain paralyzed by "three layers of sign-off" while their opponents operate at the speed of viral algorithms. The core problem is that democratic parties have largely failed to use technology for the "good," leaving the field open to **fascist and extremist actors** who feel no obligation to stick to established rules or norms.

﻿

The main points of the discussion focused on the shift from "Build or Buy" to **"Buy to Build."** **Jochen Koenig, founder of Cosmonauts and Kings**, argued that parties no longer need to buy ready-made SaaS products but should instead invest in **sovereign infrastructure** built on open-source models to ensure their strategies are not "fed" into the LLMs of foreign tech giants. Carrington highlighted the **Labour Party’s 30-person in-house team**, which allows for "iterative campaigning"—for example, automating personalized follow-ups with "hero voters" that increased support by 50%. This specialized "horsepower" is essential because campaign effects now account for up to **3% of the overall vote share**, often the margin of victory in modern elections.

﻿

The conclusions reached by the panel suggest that the party of the future will be a **fragmented network** where small teams use agentic AI to simulate the presence of a massive staff. **Fanny Geismeyer, head of the political unit of the ALDE Party**, emphasized that tech remains a "vehicle" rather than a solution; without an **authentic messenger and a true core**, even the most expensive tools will fail to solve the "trust crisis". There was a consensus that "scarcity makes you creative," and smaller, underdog parties may actually be better positioned to leapfrog the established "elephants" by utilizing AI for rapid, **out-of-the-box thinking**.

﻿

The flow of the argument shifted from internal organizational decay to the **"OODA loop"** (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) as a model for survival. Carrington and Koenig noted that many parties have not even reached the **"observe" stage**, remaining disconnected from how technology is currently being weaponized against them by "AI bot swarms" that mimic human interaction. This led to a discussion on **"digital sovereignty,"** with a call for a European "third way" that avoids the extractive models of U.S. and Chinese tech.

﻿

Diplomatically, the session highlighted a rift between the **sophisticated creative destruction** of U.S. politics and the "slow decline" of European party structures. Koenig was particularly critical of the progressive side's reliance on "Obama-era references," noting that they have "collapsed" in their messaging over the last 12 months. However, a point of consensus emerged regarding the need for **"allies in the party"**—decision-makers who understand technology but are not yet constrained by the "logics and process" of the aging system.

﻿

The **bottom-line** of this hour-long discussion is that **democracy will not survive if its institutions remain slow** in an environment where propaganda is getting faster. The "Future Political Party" must be an **ecosystem of champions** who view tech as an instrument for "public impact" rather than a risk to be managed by lawyers. To remain relevant, parties must "earn the political system over and over again" by mastering the very technology that currently threatens to make them obsolete.
