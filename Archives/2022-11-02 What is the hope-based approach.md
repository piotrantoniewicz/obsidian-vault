### How to use hope as a smart strategy for social change

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SbkU!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff7b79013-2855-40bb-bed5-c08babaa28c8_1101x267.png)

***Hope-based is a strategy for anyone working for social change to make their activism more strategic and effective. It's a practical, open-source five-shift approach anyone can use for social change under a creative commons license.***

## We all have a choice between hope and fear

We human beings have a built-in, instinctive negativity bias. This leads us to focus on problems, threats and the things we are against. But we also have an incredible capacity for creativity, compassion and change. When activated, it allows us to think of solutions, opportunities and show what we are for. We just have to train our ability to respond to the world with hope, not fear.

If we approach issues with our problem-focused, fear-based mindset we talk about what is going wrong, but forget to also think about what could be instead.

The hope-based approach is focused on articulating alternatives so that we have a chance to make them happen.

When we talk about hope, what we mean is that **hope is the idea that tomorrow can be better, if we act.**

It is a worldview that believes we human beings can change. That we can train shared humanity: hope, caring, kindness, compassion. We are on a mission to organize hope: to **use hope as a smart strategy for social change**, so that we make compassion common sense in our politics and societies.

#### How do we respond to populism?

Hope-based started as a means to counter authoritarian populism. Because we in social change have policies, and populists have stories.

But where are we going? What is our dream? What is the destination?

We need a vision, and that has to come from somewhere, and someone. We believe the answer to this question comes from inside us. This is a tool for finding the hope in our story.

**It is not about finding a hopeful story, it is about finding the hope in every story.**

#### How Hope-based Works

Social change depends on people working together globally to change attitudes, behavior and culture. Hope-based communication uses brain science, human psychology and marketing to multiply the vision values and voices of the social change movement.

The hope-based approach was originally developed as a tool for narrative strategy at Amnesty International in 2017-2018. This approach involves showing, not telling, people how to change. Activists can use hope-based to tell stories that drive new attitudes, behavior and narratives.

We help activists to with the following tools based around the five shifts:

- Hope-based communication: how to reframe your activism in five shifts.
- Hope-based strategy: create your own strategy in five steps.
- Hope-based mindset: tools for inner work and mindful activism drawn from psychology and sports science.

#### What are the five hope-based shifts?

Hope-based is a practical five-shift approach any changemaker can use to *base* their daily work on the change they *hope* to see.

The five hope-based shifts are:

1. **From Fear to Hope:** Changing emphasis from what we fear to what we hope for.
2. **From Problem to Solution:** Focusing on solutions rather than problems.
3. **From Against to For:** Advocating for our positive, intrinsic values instead of the negative, extrinsic ones we oppose.
4. **From Threat to Opportunity:** Reframing threats as shared opportunities to grow and improve our lives and societies.
5. **From Victim to Human:** Emphasising human dignity, agency and collaboration over victimhood.

#### A theory of change based on brain science

Hope-based is an approach designed specifically for changemakers struggling to adapt to the challenges of the 21st century such as populism and new technologies. This approach is a game-changing application of brain science to social change.

Brain science tells us that human beings learn behavior from what they see. **This means social change must focus on showing the change we want to see.**

Our values will only grow if someone sets out to cultivate them. This means changemakers need to constantly repeat what they want to see more of, rather than fighting what they oppose.

**Our approach is also marketing-based:** We can spread change if we integrate compelling storytelling and the science of empathy with principles of marketing and a deep understanding of the algorithms that power content engagement. Doing this, we can inspire better human behavior, foster compassion, and build human connection.

#### Why we need hope, not fear

Fear is a highly organised force in today’s world, with populist authoritarians using digital technology to spread division and hate at unprecedented levels. Meanwhile, social change organisations are often stuck in reactive mode, inadvertently reinforcing negative narratives by constantly responding to them, leaving activists feeling ineffective, demotivated, and burned out.

Society and politics is pervaded by a sense of fear that seeds apathy, division and self-interest. Stories of decline and cruelty get far more attention than stories of change and humanity. In this climate, it is hard to share hopes for a better future, even though hope is what we need right now. We are missing big hopeful ideas - the visions, values and voices that inspire us to be better and provide shared goals to unite around.

Humans learn action from the stories we tell: so telling more hopeful stories about humanity and drive better action - we can bring a radically better world into being, if we can imagine it.

Anyone can use the hope-based approach to rediscover their hope (or that of their community) and turn it into action. Our movement elevates and amplifies hope-based people and ideas to make them more likely to become reality.

#### The role of hope in social change

There are three strategic reasons to pursue hope:

1. Hope is a necessary emotion for social change because people who feel hope are more likely to have empathy, whereas fear makes us focus on self-interest.
2. People are more likely to act if they believe change is possible.
3. Seeing evidence for hope changes narratives about what is possible and desirable that influence actual political decisions as well as our own attitudes and behavior.

When engaging in hope-based communication we are doing two things:

1. Trying to activate hope in the audience in order for them to be open to what we have to say, to care about others and to take action.
2. Shifting our own work to focus on what we hope to achieve rather than what we want to avoid.

#### Our Vision: Making Compassion Common Sense

What if we invested as much in nurturing the change we hope to see as we do in fighting what we fear?

We want a world where compassion is common sense. We believe that hope is a smart strategy for promoting the attitudes, behavior and culture that underpin democracy, human rights, freedom to move, gender equality, racial justice, climate justice and civic space - worldwide.

We can reclaim the initiative by building a new global ecosystem for stories and narratives that nurture the trust, mutual care and agency essential for democracy, human rights and climate justice.

**Because the stories we tell today are the actions we take tomorrow.**

For new social norms, or new social change tactics, to get picked up by our mirror neurons, they need to be seen: we need to show, not tell, the change we want to see happen.

We need to get organised to promote the values, vision and voices of our movement. With a global, concerted effort to promote [intrinsic values](https://www.commoncause.com.au/values-map-and-definitions) and real alternatives, we can develop innovative responses to crises and find renewed purpose and resilience.
