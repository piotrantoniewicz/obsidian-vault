*Contributor: Ned Howey*  
  
Let me begin where I need to: with absolute clarity. I abhor political violence. I condemn Kirk’s murder without hesitation. Political violence is disgusting and dangerous, always. It corrodes our humanity and undermines democracy itself. Even when it comes for someone whose politics I fought with every fiber of my being, I cannot waver: violence is never the answer.

That is why I am writing this piece. Not gloating, nor celebration, nor any degree of joy in what has come to pass. Nor would I argue that Kirk in some way deserved this attack. It is because I abhor violence this moment demands closer examination than just rallying around Kirk’s ideology and legacy.

I cannot accept the rewriting of Kirk’s legacy as it's happening right now - into some model of “doing politics right.” Ezra Klein, in his [reflection,](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/09/11/opinion/charlie-kirk-assassination-fear-politics.html) suggested that Kirk was practicing politics in exactly the right way, because he showed up on campuses, debated with people he disagreed with, and “used his words.” But “use your words” is the admonition of a kindergarten teacher trying to stop a playground fight, not the standard for a democracy. If this is our bar — that at least Kirk wasn’t firing guns at people himself — then our democracy is already in ruins.

Doing politics right means something much deeper. It means being open to connection. It means allowing others’ experiences to move you. And, most of all, it means being willing to grow. To be changed. To see yourself as wrong, and admit it.

To be doing politics right would have required three simple words from his mouth: “I was wrong.” Ever. On any topic.

But Charlie Kirk never uttered those words, “I was wrong”. Not once among all his recorded years of debates, podcasts, Turning Point content, endless Twitter/X posts, etc. and there is no instance of him admitting error. Instead, his model was to create viral soundbites, staging debates where unsuspecting students were lured into “Prove Me Wrong” exchanges, only for Kirk to splice the footage into videos of him looking triumphant. This wasn’t politics as connection; it was politics as performance. Mere spectacle.

His “Prove Me Wrong” debates were exactly that: Not opportunities to learn, nor opportunities to listen or authentic attempts to learn and examine one’s own preconceived beliefs and biases. They were a contest by a self-proclaimed king. The onus of responsibility was always to be on the other to prove - for his assumed righteousness, as he sat from a position of power (one he himself refused to recognize). Kirk’s ‘show’ was always in a courtroom of his own setting, under his complete control, with himself as judge and jury, and a predetermined ruling prepared. The expectation for people from or arguing on behalf of marginalized identities to ‘win’ in this context itself is blind to the realities of power that exist in that and every other setting. Which is at its core - what Kirk set out to erase and replace with the presumption of privilege.

And who was it for? Mostly those young men from some kind of predominant power identity who feared condemnation if they voiced their biases amidst the liberal culture of college campuses today (more about that later). Kirk gave them relief. He gave them the feeling of being right. The intoxicating relief of watching someone “say what they were thinking,” without shame, without apology. Sounds a lot like another big political figure today, does it not? It was not dialogue — it was affirmation. A factory of content meant to prove the certainty of his own righteousness.

Consider the most viral example: the TikTok clip in the final week before Trump’s 2024 victory. A student said they believed Trump would be an “anti-war president.” Kirk beamed and amplified the claim: “Even young liberals see Trump as the peace president.” That video racked up millions of views — 8 to 10 million on TikTok alone, plus millions more across Instagram and YouTube. Analysts later credited Kirk with helping shift young voters toward Trump at a crucial moment.

But Kirk was wrong. Catastrophically wrong. Trump has been no peace president. Wars escalated, not diminished. Russia’s aggression in Ukraine continued, with Trump oddly seeming to side with Putin at times. The U.S. enabled escalation in the genocide in Gaza. Around the world, conflicts burned hotter. Kirk could have said, “I was wrong.” He could have admitted that the student’s hope had been misplaced. Instead, he doubled down, shifting his outrage onto trans people and scapegoating the marginalized on his come back tour. Again, always right. Never wrong.

This refusal to grow was not incidental — it was his brand. He proudly said things like, “I think it’s worth it to have … some gun deaths every single year so that we can have the Second Amendment. That is a prudent deal.” He called the Civil Rights Act of the 1960s a mistake. He literally mocked empathy itself as weakness. And he never retracted, never reflected, never admitted error.

As I wrote in my book [*The Age of Junk Politics*:](https://www.tectonica.co/age_of_junk_politics_book) “Politics today has been hollowed into spectacle. A culture of cheap victories, hot takes, and moments engineered to go viral — all at the expense of genuine connection and growth.” On the surface, Kirk’s campus debates looked like authentic engagement. But in reality, they were a kind of reprocessed, staged “authenticity” designed for ideological gain. He turned debate into content, certainty into currency, and politics into a marketplace of affirmation rather than a practice of transformation.

And yet, as I also wrote, “Trump voters are not our enemies.” The rise of right-wing populism, including Kirk’s own following, is not simply about individual hatred. It grows out of a profound disconnection from civic participation and a gnawing sense that institutions no longer represent people. The right has long flirted with the language of civil war; in the wake of Kirk’s death, they now declare it openly.

They are not wrong to say we live in polarized times. But what remains unspoken is that this polarization has roots across the political spectrum in how we practice our politics. Transactional politics — emptied of meaning beyond clicks, polls, and short-term wins — has become the default culture. Fueled by social media platforms that reward outrage, commercial incentives that monetize division, and political machines chasing tactical victories, this culture has hollowed out democracy itself. These systemic causes, which I explore in detail in *The Age of Junk Politics*, form the backdrop against which our current crisis must be understood. In this vacuum, anti-establishment movements have surged — not without cause — because people no longer feel their agency matters.

That is why I argue conversations are the centerpiece of democratic life. Doing politics right is not about “winning” an argument. It is about a politics where the very process of making decisions together puts us in contact with one another and becomes the site of transformation. Where people are not props, targets, or consumers, but participants. Where dialogue is not performance but a practice of seeing one another more deeply. Transformational politics is about how we show up to one another, how we build trust, how we grow through difference.”

Kirk’s entire persona was the antithesis of that.

But we on the left cannot escape indictment here either. No, Charlie Kirk wasn’t doing politics right. Nor are we. Too often, we too have shut down others who did not understand, demanded comprehension from those who never had the chance to learn, canceled without offering redemption, denounced apology and growth. I admit it myself: I have been wrong. I have closed off when I should have leaned in. We often do this out of fear, out of the defensiveness of our own marginalization. (To be clear, there has to be reciprocity to listen on all sides - ensuring we don’t call people out of our movements is different than assuming the burden of explaining oppression to those who are being oppressed.) But fear is not strength. And if we too refuse growth, then we too are failing politics.

This is why the shooting of Charlie Kirk is such a double bind. We are asked to give empathy for a man who scorned empathy. We are asked to condemn violence against a man who said children’s deaths were an acceptable price for unregulated guns. We are asked to be surprised — and worse, to retreat from calls for gun regulation — when a man is shot in the middle of an argument, at a “Prove Me Wrong” rally. Think that through.

And all this within months of Democratic lawmakers themselves being shot for their politics, while Republicans mocked them or stayed silent.

Some could argue in fact that Kirk was not responsible for the words of these GOP leaders. But then let’s take Kirk’s own words at the death of George Floyd, another crisis of murder that sparked a political moment. In the days after, Kirk dismissed Floyd with cruelty — calling him “a scumbag,” insisting he “shouldn’t be celebrated,” and saying he didn’t care that Floyd was dead. He celebrated that death as proof of his worldview, stripping away empathy in favor of scorn.

We will not use those same words for Kirk. Nor will we celebrate his killing. To do so would be to mirror the very ideology we stand against.

We must be clear: most of us on the left abhor violence, always. No one deserves to be killed for their beliefs or identity. That is why ours is the side of anti-war marches, of calls for gun regulation, of opposition to the death penalty. But we cannot accept hypocrisy. We cannot allow selective anger. Kirk’s defenders want us to treat his murder as proof that his words must be accepted. But his words were far more aligned with justifying violence than with building peace. And the facts are clear: the overwhelming majority of [political violence in the U.S. comes from the right.](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2023/02/28/extremism-right-wing-deaths/)

We cannot be seduced into believing that mourning Kirk’s assassination requires endorsing his legacy. We cannot let this moment become, in the hands of those pushing toward dictatorship, their Reichstag fire — the excuse to strip away rights and escalate authoritarianism.

So yes, let us condemn his killing. But let us also defend the human rights he opposed and stand up for the voices that are marginalized that he attacked. These are not contradictions, unless maybe your brain works within the twisted logics of Charlie Kirk.

And let us not abandon the one thing Kirk was right about: that politics must involve conversation. But not as a game of one-upmanship. Not as a performance of being right. The goal must be to connect, to be changed, to grow. To be wrong. That is the true promise of democracy and its hope that we can evolve together towards better societies, making better decisions together.

In accordance with my values, I condemn all killing and cruelty — including Kirk’s. As a human and a widower, I feel deeply for those who loved him, and I mourn with them. Yet as an American, I fear for our country and our democracy should we simplify the meaning behind this tragedy.
