Confessions from Political Tech Leaders: Real Talk, Urgent Lessons, and Predictions for 2026

The core issue identified in this "confessional" is the **fetishization of "scale for the sake of scale,"** which has led to **shallow user engagement** and the dehumanization of political messaging. **Leah Bae (Higher Ground Labs)** and **Flo Barre (Qomon)** confess that the movement has over-optimized for mass contact while neglecting the **change management and training** required to use tech effectively.

The discussion concludes that 2026 will be defined by a **"resurgence of humanization"** and the rise of **agentic AI for hyper-local organizing**, turning supporters back into the primary messengers of political vision.

\-

Session Summary

For too long, the political tech sector has operated on the assumption that more is always better, but leaders are now admitting that **"scale for the sake of scale"** was the wrong move. **Leah Bae, Principal at Higher Ground Labs**, identifies the core problem as a "shallow use" of technology wherereach is treated as the end goal rather than a means to sustained participation. This has created a "BANI" world—**brittle, anxious, non-linear, and incomprehensible**—where progressive organizations struggle with low contact rates and a total lack of personalization.

﻿

The session’s main points revolved around **"real talk" regarding failed implementations** and the necessity of technology having a "seat at the table" during strategy planning. **Flo Barre, CEO of Qomon**, noted that tech fails most often when it is treated as a "magic system" added at the last minute rather than the **"operational backbone"** of a campaign. **Oliver Wells of NationBuilder** highlighted a "crisis of skepticism," where voters are fatigued by digital ads and are increasingly yearning for **verification and authentic human relationships** in their online interactions.

﻿

The conclusions drawn for 2026 are provocative: **Flo Barre predicts that several European elections could be invalidated or cancelled** by authorities due to technological disruption. The panel agreed that the future lies in **"humanization" facilitated by tech**, such as "agentic AI for organizing" that allows small, resource-strapped campaigns to recruit and persuade voters with human-like intelligence. The consensus is that the era of the "top-down" centralized campaign is over, making way for **"liquid volunteering"** where supporters can join and share actions instantly without the friction of app downloads.

﻿

The flow of the argument touched on the **"Mamdani effect,"** where campaigns that create "culture and joy" outperform those that rely on dry policy and AI-generated slop. The panel debated whether **innovation during a campaign is a "edge" or a "risk,"** with Leah Bae arguing that "innovation is power" while Flo Barre warned that buggy bots can create catastrophic "noise" in the heat of an election. This led to a consensus that **"tactical innovation"**—the clever deployment of existing tools—is often more effective than building something entirely new.

﻿

Diplomatically, the session positioned **GDPR as a "European superpower"** that has unexpectedly benefited campaigners by preventing the "deliverability crises" common in the unregulated U.S. list-building market. There was a shared skepticism regarding **"artificial personas"** for market research, with **Alicia Combaz (Make.org)** arguing that democracy requires "alterity" and real people in the room to reach a common ground. The speakers also addressed the misconception that they are "just Cambridge Analytica," emphasizing that their mission is to **teach people to use technology to empower themselves**.

﻿

The **bottom-line** for 2026 is that campaigns must **"struggle before they scale,"** learning to organize in an analog way before automating their efforts. As the "eyeballs move into different places," the only thing that won't change is the necessity of **owning your data** and maintaining direct contact details for your supporters. Ultimately, political tech leaders believe the next era will be won by those who use **agentic AI to remove "shit work,"** freeing up human volunteers to do the one thing machines cannot: tell a compelling, authentic story.
