The Fundraising Fallacy

What anthropology understands about donors better than marketing does, and how we can use it in nonprofits and political fundraising? We too often rely in the nonprofit sector on a simplistic assumption: that giving works like a transaction. Anthropology suggests the opposite. Giving is relational, not transactional, and this has structural implications for how we design fundraising programs that actually work.

\-

Originally published on https://gwflow.substack.com/

We too often rely in the nonprofit sector on a simplistic assumption: that giving works like a transaction. Anthropology suggests the opposite. Giving is relational, not transactional, and this has structural implications for how we design fundraising programs that actually work.

Over the past decade, fundraising has quietly absorbed the mechanics of e-commerce. 

Conversion funnels, A/B testing, video page, super-sophisticated landing pages, frictionless checkouts. One-click donations via Apple Pay or autofill. From a purely technical standpoint, the reasoning is straightforward: market the donation, rely on donation online, make people dream on the fundraising page, reduce effort, remove obstacles, increase completion.

On this narrow point, the data is clear: reducing payment friction works. But the data also shows that the more complex a donation form becomes, the more conversion rates drop.

﻿

#### Simplification works.

An experimental study by [NextAfter](https://www.nextafter.com/experiments/how-video-donation-page-affects-donor-conversion/) compared several versions of an online donation page and found that removing an embedded video and **simplifying the form structure increased conversion rates by up to +52%.** Even emotionally engaging content, when introduced at the wrong moment, creates cognitive friction and slows action. ([Link to the study](https://www.nextafter.com/experiments/how-video-donation-page-affects-donor-conversion/))

This is not a trivial finding. It confirms what many practitioners already suspect: donation forms should be simple, almost invisible. The fewer distractions, the better.

But this insight is often taken one step too far, or rather, in the wrong direction. If simpler forms convert better, it is not because the form is where the decision happens. It is because, by the time someone reaches the form, the decision has already been made.

The real issue, then, lies in how we define what a donation is and where we choose to focus our efforts to increase donations.

﻿

#### The mistaken assumption at the heart of modern fundraising

﻿

Much of contemporary fundraising rests on an assumption that rarely gets articulated, let alone challenged: the idea that donations behave like ordinary financial transactions.

Put schematically, the logic is simple:

I give money => You deliver impact => Our business here is done.

The model feels intuitive because it mirrors how monetary exchanges usually work.

Transactions exist to settle accounts. They balance obligations. They allow relationships to end cleanly. That is precisely why money is such an efficient coordination tool among strangers.

But donations do not operate according to that logic.

﻿

#### Why transactions close and gifts don’t.

﻿

In *The Gift,* Marcel Mauss described giving not as an act of generosity, but as a social mechanism structured by obligation: the obligation to give, to receive, and to return. Refusing any part of this cycle is not neutral. It signals a refusal of alliance.

What matters here is not the moral quality of the gesture, but the imbalance it creates. A gift introduces a situation in which something has been given and something remains outstanding. That unresolved state is precisely what binds people together.

*David Graeber, in Debt: The First 5,000 Years,* pushed this reasoning further. Societies, he argued, do not hold together through balanced exchanges. They rely on obligations that are deliberately left open, vague, and impossible to fully quantify. Social bonds endure not because accounts are settled, but because they are not

Money, by contrast, is a technology of closure. It allows interaction without ongoing obligation. It makes it possible to walk ay:

*“All human interactions are not forms of exchange. Only some are. Exchange encourages a particular way of conceiving human relations. This is because exchange implies equality, but it also implies separation. It’s precisely when the money changes hands, when the debt is cancelled, that equality is restored and both parties can walk away and have nothing further to do with each other.*

*Debt is what happens in between: when the two parties cannot yet walk away from each other, because they are not yet equal. But it is carried out in the shadow of eventual equality. Because achieving that equality, however, destroys the very reason for having a relationship, just about everything.”*

***David Graeber, Debt, the First 5 000 years Chapter 5: A brief treatise on the moral grounds of economic relations***

Seen through this lens, the problem with transactional fundraising becomes easier to name. When donation systems are designed to help donors feel that they have “done their part,” they are doing exactly what money is meant to do: they are closing the relationship.

﻿

#### How optimization closes the moral loop.

﻿

This is where the current emphasis on frictionless design becomes more ambiguous.

Yes, removing unnecessary fields and distractions increases completion. The “NextAfter findings” leave little doubt on that point. But they also imply something else: the donation form is not the place where meaning should be constructed.

When organizations overload the payment moment with storytelling, explanation, and emotional cues, they misunderstand its role. The form should not persuade. It should execute a decision that has already taken shape elsewhere.

More importantly, when fundraising treats the donation itself as the end of the journey, it creates a form of moral closure. Donors give once, feel cleared, and move on. Churn is not primarily a messaging failure. It is the structural consequence of treating giving as a closed exchange.

﻿

#### Practical Implications:

**Designing donation as infrastructure, not a checkout.** If donations open relationships rather than close them, several consequences follow.

- The first donation should be understood as an entry point, not an objective. It marks the beginning of a relationship that needs to be structured over time. Every donation should therefore be paired with a role ( even a minimal or symbolic one) that signals inclusion in a collective.

- From Amounts to Personas, segmentation follows the same logic. Donors should not primarily be grouped by amount, but by relationship to meaning, to history, to action. The ask may remain the same; what changes is what that ask represents to the person receiving it. The future of fundraising is not about increasing the average donation size; it is about upgrading donor roles.

- The Transactional Donor: They give once, often in response to a surge of emotion or a tax incentive. Their action is driven by context rather than relationship. Churn is high because the donation is treated as a completed gesture. The relationship remains shallow by design.

- The Identity-Based Donor: They give because donating aligns with how they see themselves. Giving is not an exception, but a confirmation of coherence. Repetition comes from the consistency between their values and their actions. Here, the donation is less about solving a problem and more about expressing belonging.

- The Donor-Ambassador: They give, and then they mobilize. Peer-to-peer drives and matching gifts are not just tactics for them; they are expressions of a social role. Their value lies less in their individual gift and more in the social function they perform within the collective.

- Rhythm matters as well. In Europe especially, nonprofits often worry about asking too often. But fatigue does not come from frequency. It comes from extraction without reciprocity. Organizations can ask frequently, provided they are not always asking for money. Narrative, recognition, and inclusion must outweigh financial requests.

- Legitimate Urgency. A state of permanent emergency erodes trust. Urgency is effective only when it is:

- Collective rather than individual.

- Time-bound rather than continuous.

- Anchored in moments rather than pressure.

When viewed through this lens, fundraising stops looking like a communication problem and starts looking like a systems design problem. It ceases to be a department of persuasion and becomes a department of architecture, designing the spaces where belonging is built, one contribution at a time.

﻿

#### 🇺🇸 🇪🇺 Debt, Giving, and the US–EU Divide.

﻿

The contrast between the United States and Europe makes these dynamics easier to observe.

In the United States, moral debt is culturally explicit. Donations are often public and performative. Giving signals affiliation. Micro-donations and peer-to-peer campaigns thrive because obligation is understood as a form of commitment.

In much of Europe, debt carries a different moral weight. There is a strong tendency to neutralize obligation quickly, whether through taxation, state redistribution, or one-off donations. Tax relief becomes a legitimizing mechanism, framing giving as a settled exchange rather than an open alliance.

This difference is not superficial. It shapes how fundraising systems are designed and helps explain why many European nonprofits remain structurally dependent on large donors and fiscal incentives.

Europe did not build systems to grow relationships. It built systems to settle accounts. The challenge is not a lack of generosity, but a model of giving designed to close obligations rather than extend relationships.

﻿

#### You should keep the relationship open.

﻿

Of course, my point is not to argue against technology. Digital tools, automation, and optimization are not the problem. They can even be powerful tools for creating relationships and helping donors move into more engaged roles. They are part of the environment we operate in, and often necessary to function at scale or with low ressources. What matters is what we ask those tools to do.

A donation can be treated as a way to settle something: a moral obligation, a sense of guilt, a moment of attention. In that case, the system does exactly what it is designed to do. It allows the relationship to end quickly and cleanly.

But a donation can also be treated differently. As the first visible sign of a relationship that is not meant to be closed right away. Something that introduces continuity rather than resolution.

Most fundraising systems are quietly built around the first interpretation. They optimize for closure, even when they claim to focus on engagement.

Fundraisers should focus on the relationship, not lose time obsessing over funnel tweaks or form optimization. The work is relational, before and after the gift. It requires remembering what actually puts people in a position to give: the social mechanism of giving, the imbalance created by the gift itself, and the fact that giving belongs to something larger, structured by a societal obligation to give, to receive, and to return.

The donation is not the end of the story. It is the beginning. Social bonds endure not because accounts are settled, but because they are not. 

Giving opens a relationship that refuses to be closed.
