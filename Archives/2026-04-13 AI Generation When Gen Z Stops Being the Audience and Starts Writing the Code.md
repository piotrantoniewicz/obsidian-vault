AI Generation: When Gen Z Stops Being the Audience and Starts Writing the Code

For Gen Z, artificial intelligence is not a novelty, it is infrastructure. From personalised feeds to homework helpers and chatbots that double as late‑night confidants, AI is woven into their daily routines so tightly that many barely register it as a distinct technology. That casual intimacy is precisely the problem. As next‑generation systems like SORA‑style generative video blur the line between real and synthetic politics, AI literacy is becoming a democratic survival skill rather than a niche technical competence.

\-

The “Agents of Change: Gen Z and AI” session asked a blunt question: what happens to democracy when the first generation to grow up inside AI‑mediated environments is simultaneously the least responsible for building them and the most exposed to their failures?

#### A Generation Under Continuous Computation

By one regulator’s estimate, adolescents now spend about three hours a day on platforms like TikTok, YouTube or Instagram. Those hours are not neutral entertainment; they are the cutting edge of the attention economy, where algorithmic systems predict and shape what users see, feel and believe.

Where earlier generations at least shared a handful of TV channels or newspapers, Gen Z’s informational diet is hyper‑personalised. Each swipe is a data point, each pause a signal. Recommendation engines learn not just tastes but vulnerabilities, loneliness, anger, insecurity, and feed content accordingly.

This raises two intertwined concerns:

- Democratic agency: If political information is filtered through opaque optimisation processes, young citizens may never encounter dissenting views or credible institutional voices.

- Mental health: AI‑driven feeds exacerbate anxiety, body image issues and doom‑scrolling, particularly among young users already navigating economic precarity and climate dread.

Yet despite this, many in Gen Z remain indifferent to AI as a political issue. They notice its convenience, not its power.

#### Indifference on the Surface, Anxiety Underneath

The practitioners working directly with young people brought a more nuanced picture. In projects like Make.org’s Gen‑Z‑focused deliberation initiatives in Canada and Europe, participants already display a surprisingly high level of critical awareness: they question how chatbots shape learning, worry about losing critical thinking, and demand clear labelling of AI‑generated content.

Concerns cluster around:

- The integrity of information, fears that deepfakes and synthetic videos will make it impossible to trust what one sees, especially in elections.

- The erosion of skills, using AI for homework or creative tasks without understanding underlying concepts.

- The impact on mental health, AI‑boosted engagement systems keeping them in loops of comparison, outrage or despair.

What is missing is not sensitivity, but structured opportunities to turn that unease into civic agency.

#### AI Literacy as Civic Education

Viewed through this lens, “AI literacy” cannot be reduced to coding classes or technical explainers. It must become part of civic education: understanding when one is interacting with an AI system, how it impacts opportunities and rights, and what recourse exists when things go wrong.

The session’s policy experts argued for a baseline of capabilities every citizen should have:

- Recognising AI‑generated content through labels, watermarks or provenance metadata.

- Knowing how algorithmic decision‑making affects access to education, housing, jobs or public benefits, and how to contest unfair outcomes.

- Being able to report harmful or malfunctioning systems, in the same way one would call an emergency number.

European regulation is starting to nudge in this direction. Emerging rules around watermarking AI‑generated media could, if enforced, make it easier to distinguish synthetic campaign videos from real ones. Incident reporting mechanisms and risk assessments for “high‑risk” AI systems may eventually create the equivalent of “fire services” for algorithmic disasters.

But laws alone will not teach a teenager when to question a feed, or how to push back against a chatbot that oversteps. That requires institutions, schools, youth organisations, parties, willing to treat AI literacy as seriously as reading and voting.

#### Who Gets to Use the Machines?

One of the panel’s most provocative claims came from the practitioner side: in real campaigns, it is not Gen Z candidates who are making the most effective use of AI. Data from the 2025 German federal election showed that among more than 50 candidates supported by an organisation working to diversify Parliament, the heaviest and most sophisticated AI users were often older, well‑resourced politicians.

That reveals a paradox. AI is often hailed as a great equaliser, tools that allow three or four people to do the work of a 50‑person team, from content production to data analysis. In theory, that should empower under‑represented voices: young activists, candidates of colour, community campaigns on shoestring budgets.

In practice, effective use of AI in politics still requires:

- Time to experiment and learn workflows.

- Money for subscriptions, compute and training.

- Organisational support to integrate tools into campaign operations.

Those are forms of privilege. Unless guarded against, AI could paradoxically deepen misrepresentation, making already dominant groups even more efficient at getting elected, shaping narratives and occupying public attention.

#### Agents, Cambridge Analytica and the Next Phase

To understand where this might go, one speaker reached back to an earlier hype cycle: Cambridge Analytica. In the mid‑2010s, political consultants weaponised apps that scraped Facebook friend networks, built psychographic profiles and microtargeted voters. The scandal eventually forced a reckoning over data consent and platform responsibility, but only after the tools had been deployed in major elections.

Today’s “agentic” systems are more powerful. Instead of static targeting, they promise autonomous bots that can:

- Crawl the open web for personal traces.

- Construct multimodal psych profiles from videos, posts and interactions.

- Engage in one‑to‑one conversation at scale, adapting tone and content in real time.

Technically, a small team could already build a system that imitates a political campaign worker, tailors messages to an individual’s fears and hopes, and does so invisibly. The line between persuasion and manipulation would blur to vanishing.

That is why some on the panel argued that regulation must move faster this time: banning certain uses outright, imposing transparency obligations for political bots and restricting the most intrusive forms of behavioural targeting.

#### Building Pro‑Democratic AI, Not Waiting for Brussels

Despite these risks, the session refused to succumb to fatalism. Several speakers work directly on tools that use AI not to hack voters but to strengthen democratic practice:

- Deliberative platforms that scale citizen assemblies and public consultations, using AI to cluster proposals, surface common ground and overcome polarisation.

- Assistive systems that help citizens understand complex policy questions, rather than nudging them toward predefined answers.

The message to the room, stacked with political tech professionals, was direct: do not wait for regulation or school curricula to fix AI literacy and democratic resilience. If you are building tools, ask who they empower. Design workflows so that under‑represented groups can benefit: activists with little money, candidates without party machines, communities in rural areas whose voices are rarely heard.

The same applies to user‑generated campaigns. When energy providers or public institutions invite citizens to co‑create AI‑generated images or messages, participation spikes; one campaign saw more than 100,000 people join. Done well, such exercises can turn passive users into co‑authors of political narratives rather than targets.

#### From Subjects to Architects

The most urgent challenge is to stop treating Gen Z as a problem to be managed, too online, too fragile, too misinformed, and start recognising them as the generation with the most to gain and lose from AI’s trajectory.

That implies a different kind of invitation:

- Bring young people into the rooms where AI policy is made, not just as token consultees but as co‑designers.

- Fund youth‑led experiments in AI‑supported organising, from climate campaigns to local democracy projects.

- Challenge political parties to open their data and tools to younger members, so they can learn, misuse, correct and improve them from within.

Gen Z did not choose to grow up inside algorithmic systems any more than previous generations chose to grow up under broadcast TV. But unlike their parents, they are facing this shift at a moment when democratic norms are already under strain, from climate crises, wars, economic shocks and resurgent authoritarianism.

The question is whether they will be left as subjects of opaque systems built elsewhere, or enabled to become architects of an AI‑literate democratic culture that can survive whatever SORA‑style technology comes next.
