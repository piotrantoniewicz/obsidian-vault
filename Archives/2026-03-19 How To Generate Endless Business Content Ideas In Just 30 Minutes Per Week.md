In 2025, I was in a content rut.

For the first 4 years of operating my business, writing was my number one priority. The single highest leverage activity I could do every day was write down my ideas and share them on the internet. The more I wrote, the faster our email lists grew, which led to more customers, which led to more revenue.

It was simple.

But in 2025 the highest leverage activity changed. Rather than spend 4-5 hours per day writing, the business needed me in operator mode — making strategic decisions, leading the marketing & sales teams, and keeping the machine humming across every vertical.

Content was still important, but I had a team who could keep things going if I didn’t have as much time or mental bandwidth to write in a given week.

Fast forward to 2026 and the priorities are shifting again.

My number one priority is still operating the business. But we’ve made talent upgrades—true operators—at key department head & vertical positions that I can reliably depend on.

And now I’m plowing all that extra time & mental bandwidth into content.

But here’s the problem: I’d just spent a full year thinking like an operator, not a newsletter writer. And in my first few writing sessions, I did nothing but stare at a blank page. It felt like I was starting my writing journey over from scratch.

Now, it wasn’t that I had nothing to say. It was that I was using an outdated approach. When I was writing full time, I spent hours every day in idea mode — capturing observations, noticing patterns, writing things down as they happened. The ideas were always there because I was always looking for them.

But now my week was full of operating, not creating. By the time I sat down to write, I’d try to think of something from scratch, rather than talk about things I was already doing.

That’s when it hit me: I didn’t have an idea problem. I had a systems problem.

I had plenty to write about: I literally spent all day solving problems. But I didn’t have a system that helped me 1) recognize those problems could be pieces of content and 2) easily outline the idea for me based on work I had already done.

So I built a new system. And that’s what I want to share today.

By the end of this, you’ll have a complete system for turning the work you’re already doing into endless newsletter ideas — plus three AI prompts to run the whole thing on autopilot.

And it starts with a simple mindset shift that turns everything you’re already doing into content.

## Selling Your Sawdust

I first heard this concept from Jack Butcher.

The idea comes from the woodworker who spends all day making things in his shop. At the end of the day, the floor is covered in sawdust—the natural byproduct of all the cutting, shaping, and sanding. To him, this sawdust is just scraps to sweep up and toss out.

But the sawdust, the byproduct of his work, is actually useful.

- Farmers buy it for animal bedding
- Campsites will buy it for fire starters
- Manufacturers use it in composite wood

To the right person, it’s valuable—yet the woodworker was throwing it away for free.

And the same is true with content.

**Sharing the byproducts of your work—your sawdust—is the best type of content you can share as a business owner.**

### And this approach is better for 4 main reasons:

- **It’s authentic.** Since you’re only sharing things you actually use, it’s impossible for someone else to replicate it.
- **It’s easy to write.** Since you’ve already done the work, you simply need to document what you did.
- **It solves real problems.** Since it solved a problem for you and your business, it’s already proven to be valuable.
- **It demonstrates expertise through action, not claims.** And rather than try to convince people you know what you’re talking about, you can show them by sharing things they will find useful

And here’s the good news: your sawdust is already there. You just need a system to collect it.

What’s the best way to do that?

Easy—just follow these 3 steps.

## 3 Steps To Generate Endless Business Content Ideas

To come up with ideas, you’re going to follow 3 simple steps:

1. Make A “What Happened” Weekly Recap List
2. Turn Each Item Into A Proven Content Idea
3. Expand Each Content Idea Using The PISA Framework

And after I explain each of these, I’ll walk you through a chain of AI Prompts you can use to bake this process into your weekly workflow.

Let’s dive in:

### Step 1 — Make A “What Happened” Weekly Recap List

The first step to generating ideas is getting all of the “raw material” of potential ideas out there.

And the best way to do this is to make a “What Happened List”

Here’s how it works.

1. **Block 15-30 minutes at the end of the week.** Sometimes I do this on Friday afternoons, other times Sunday mornings.
2. **Prime your brain to remember what happened.** I like to scan my Calendar, To-Do List, Slack, Email, Journal, and AI Chat history.
3. **Write out as many items as you can remember without a filter.** The most important part here is to brain dump without judgement. We will filter things down in the next step.

To further prime your brain, here’s a list of categories where you can generate things that happened.

- **Meetings** — conversations with your team, clients, mentors, or peers where something was discussed or decided
- **Decisions** — anything you approved, rejected, or changed course on, big or small
- **Problems solved** — things that were broken, stuck, or underperforming that you diagnosed and fixed
- **Systems built** — new processes, workflows, tools, automations, or playbooks you created or documented
- **Lessons learned** — realizations, mistakes, or mindset shifts that changed how you think or operate
- **Experiments run** — new approaches, tools, or ideas you tested for the first time
- **Feelings had** — energy shifts, frustrations, wins, or moments that stuck with you throughout the week

Nothing is off limits. Just get everything out of your head and onto the page.

To further drive this home, here’s an example list of my “What Happened” list from last week:

1. Wrote team-wide memo on March priorities
2. Built AI lead scoring assistant in Claude
3. Ran multiple sales team meetings to get them out a rut
4. Optimized our cold webinar funnel by attacking the show rate
5. Used AI to build a nurture content system for our sales team
6. Had a 1:1 call with one of my mentors to talk through the business
7. Experimented with going “no phone” for the first hour of the day
8. Let go of 3 team members who were undeperforming our standard
9. Watched the most recent Hormozi episode on how he uses AI
10. Started a brand new newsletter to talk about business ideas

As you can see, these all fall into different categories.

Some of them will make for great content ideas—and that takes us to the next step.

### Step 2 — Turn Each Item Into A Proven Content Idea

With your list of things that happened, it’s time to turn them into content.

There are 3 angles that work well for business content, based on what you accomplished:

#### The SOP — A Step-By-Step Guide

Use this when you’ve achieved an outcome or solved a problem.

The goal is to walk someone through exactly what you did, in order. The reader should walk away with a specific solution to a specific problem.

SOP-worthy content usually happens when you built something or figured something out.

For example, right now I am walking you through a step-by-step guide to generate business ideas. This came from me creating a system for myself to generate those ideas. And now I’m sharing it with you. Meta!

Examples of SOP-worth content include:

- Templates & Fill-in-the-Blank Tools
- Checklists & Cheatsheets
- Recipes & Formulas
- Scripts & Prompts
- Guides & Playbooks

Anything you use to achieve an outcome for yourself or your clients is fair game for an SOP.

#### The Breakdown — An Overview of a System

These are similar to SOP-style content, but with a small difference.

**SOPs** give step by step instructions that everyone should apply in the same way. Here’s an outcome, here’s how to achieve it.

**Breakdowns** require the reader to adapt your process to their situation. They watch and identify the elements they can integrate, and discard the rest.

For example, let’s say I launched a new webinar funnel last week.

I could walk someone through a “Step by Step” process to launch their own webinar funnel. That would be an SOP-style of content.

OR, I could talk through *our* webinar funnel and all of the elements within it. Rather than show someone exactly how to set it up, I just show them ours and let them infer how to do it themselves.

Here are some examples of breakdown-worthy systems:

- Your AI tool stack
- Your landing page
- Your full tech stack
- Your hiring process
- Your content system
- Your morning routine
- Your launch sequence
- Your mission & values
- Your content swipe files
- Your onboarding process
- Your tracking spreadsheets
- Your decision-making frameworks

Any system you use in your business or create for your clients is fair-game for a Breakdown.

#### The Reflection — Sharing A Lesson You Learned

Lastly, sometimes you don’t have a full step-by-step guide to explain or a system to break down.

Instead, you just have a realization or lesson that 1) you want to distill for yourself and 2) would have saved you time/energy/effort/money had you known it sooner.

A single lesson usually follows this pattern: something happened, it changed how you think, and you can state the new principle clearly enough that someone could repeat it to a friend. The story is the vehicle. The principle is the point.

A list of lessons works well when you’ve had a week, month, or quarter full of distinct realizations that don’t connect into one unified system — but each one is worth sharing on its own.

Now whether you’re sharing an SOP, a breakdown, or a Reflection, you can expand it into a newsletter using the PISA framework.

### Step 3 — Expand Each Content Idea Using The PISA Framework

Once you know what type of content you’re making, it’s time to turn your idea into a full piece.

The framework I use for every single piece of content — regardless of whether it’s an SOP, Breakdown, or Reflection — is PISA:

- **Problem** — Start with a tangible moment that you or your reader might experience. Describe the surface problem and the deeper frustration that comes with it. If your audience has felt this pain, they’ll keep reading.
- **Insight** — Share the “aha” moment. What realization made this the right way to solve it? What concept or principle guided you? This is what makes your content unique.
- **Solution** — Walk through exactly what to do, what you built, or what you learned.
	- **SOP:** Step-by-step instructions the reader can follow in the same order to get the same result.
		- **Breakdown:** A tour of the system — name each part, explain what it does, let the reader decide what to take.
		- **Reflection:** The lesson stated plainly, illustrated with your specific story. One principle the reader could repeat to a friend that prevents you from making that mistake in the future.
- **Asset** — Give the reader a deliverable that helps them implement the solution. A checklist. A prompt. A template. A framework. This is what leads to behavior change for the reader, and that is the goal of educational content.

Here’s how PISA would look across a handful of different content types:

**SOP Example — How to generate endless business content ideas as a business owner**

- **Problem** — Every week you sit down to write and stare at a blank screen. You’ve been solving real problems all week — meetings, decisions, systems, experiments — but none of it feels like content.
- **Insight** — You don’t have an idea problem. You have a systems problem. The content is already there in your daily work. You just don’t have a system to capture it.
- **Solution** — Step 1: make a “What Happened” list at the end of every week. Step 2: map each item to a content angle — SOP, Breakdown, or Reflection. Step 3: expand your best idea using the PISA framework.
- **Asset** — Three AI prompts that run each step automatically. Paste your week in, get a fully outlined newsletter out.

**Breakdown Example — Our Sales Manager Daily Operating System**

- **Problem** — Sales managers need a predictable operating playbook rather than operating off of vibes. No consistency, no visibility. I never knew what was actually happening until something broke.
- **Insight** — A sales manager doesn’t need creativity. They need a checklist. The job is the same every day. So I built the operating system and removed the guesswork entirely.
- **Solution** — Here are the 7 activities in our daily SOP: AM Check-In, Admin QC, EOD Feedback, BOD Revenue Hunting, Daily Team Meeting, 2x Call Reviews, Pipeline Power Hour. Each one has a time, an owner, and a clear output.
- **Asset** — The full Sales Manager Playbook — every SOP, every cadence, the complete daily operating system you can model for your own sales manager.

**Reflection Example — I kept three underperformers for six months. Here’s what it cost me.**

- **Problem** — I had doubts about all three for weeks. But I kept finding reasons to wait — give it one more week, don’t disrupt the team, wait for more data. Meanwhile I was absorbing their slack and the whole sales function was underperforming.
- **Insight** — The doubt IS the signal. I used to wait for proof. Now I know: the moment you’re unsure about someone, that’s already the answer. Waiting only costs you.
- **Solution** — Run the Keeper Test: if this person came to me as a new hire today, would I fight to keep them? If it’s not a clear yes, it’s a no. Optimize for people who inspire you, not just people who are competent.
- **Asset** — Three questions to run through for every person on your team: Would I hire them again today? Do they inspire or drain me? Would I feel relieved or gutted if they left tomorrow?

And just like that, you have a full newsletter outline that solves a real problem, using a solution you’ve already created.

Easy!

## System Recap — How To Generate Endless Business Content Ideas

Before we dig into the prompts (the most important part!), here’s a recap of what we just covered:

1. You have plenty of content ideas, you just need a system for finding them
2. Sawdust is the best type of business content — sharing elements of your workflow that actually work
3. There are 3 types of high-performing business content — the SOP, the Breakdown, and the Reflection
4. You can expand each of those into a valuable newsletter using the PISA framework - Problem, Insight, Solution, Asset

Now, here’s how to use AI to help complete this process for you.

## AI Prompts For Endless Business Content Ideas

### Prompt #1: The Business Problem Interviewer

The first prompt I have for you here will help you generate your “What Happened list” from the week.

A couple quick nuances on this:

- This prompt works well even if you do not do most of your daily work “in” the LLM that you’re running the prompt. So it’s fine to run straight out of the box without anything connected
- It works EXTREMELY well if connect your tools (Calendar, to-do list/document app of choice, communication tool, etc.)
	- For example, I have Claude connected to Notion, Google Calendar, and Slack. Plus, I do a lot of my planning and prep work within Claude as well, so it has context on the things I’m building
- And it’s EVEN MORE overpowered if you give it a proper context document on your business and newsletter
	- If you’d like me to do an issue breaking down how to create context documents for your newsletter & business, please leave a comment or reply and let me know:)
```markup
You are a world-class strategic business interviewer helping me identify content ideas from my daily & weekly operations. 

Your first job is to help me create a "What Happened" list from my past week so I can turn my daily work into content ideas.

---

Step 0: Gather items from connected tools and our chat history

Before I list everything that happened, I want you to pull a list of ideas from your available connections to get us started

- If you can access my calendar, pull the meetings, events, and work blocks I had last week. If I do not have this connected, skip it.
- If you can access my to-do list, pull the tasks I completed. If I do not have this connected, skip it.
- If you can access my Slack, pull summaries of my most active channels to find the 3-5 activities that took the most of my time. If I do not have this connected, skip it.
- If we have completed work together in recent chat windows, include those activities from just the last 7 days. If you do not have chat history, skip it.

Step 1: Prompt Me To Brain Dump Anything Top of Mind

First, ask me to share everything I can remember from the week in one unfiltered stream. No structure needed. Whatever comes to mind: wins, frustrations, decisions, conversations, things I built, things that surprised me, things that felt hard. Prompt me with a trigger list of where to look and what to share. 

After I share, organize what I gave you into a rough list before moving to Part 2.

---

PART 2 — CATEGORY DEEP DIVE

Walk me through each of the following 7 categories one at a time.

For each category:

1. Show me the list of places to look that might spark more ideas
2. Ask me the category question
3. After I answer, review every item I gave you — if anything is vague or surface-level, ask one targeted follow-up question before moving to the next category

FOLLOW-UP RULE — this is important:
After I answer each category, check every item against this test: could someone read this and immediately understand what happened, why it mattered, and what the outcome or observation was? If not, push for one more level of detail before moving on.

Vague answer: "Had a sales meeting"
Strong answer: "Ran a sales team meeting to diagnose why reps were hitting below 60% for three weeks in a row — identified that the problem was clarity, not motivation, and built a daily non-negotiable checklist together"

Example follow-up: "You mentioned you worked on the funnel — what specifically did you change, what problem were you trying to solve, and what happened as a result?"

---

THE 7 CATEGORIES

1. Meetings & Conversations
Places to look: calendar invites, Zoom or call history, Slack DMs, voice memos, any recurring 1:1s or team standups
Question: "What meetings or conversations stood out this week — with your team, clients, mentors, or peers? What was discussed, decided, or realized?"
2. Decisions Made
Places to look: Slack threads where you gave direction, emails you sent, project management tools, anything you approved, rejected, or changed course on
Question: "What decisions did you make this week — big or small? Hiring, strategy, budget, process, product, team structure?"
3. Problems Solved
Places to look: support tickets, team escalations, fires that got put out, things that were broken and are now fixed, Slack channels where urgent issues were handled
Question: "What problems did you solve or make meaningful progress on this week? What was broken, what did you diagnose, and what did you do about it?"
4. Systems or Processes Built
Places to look: Notion docs or SOPs created, automations set up, new tools installed, templates made, workflows documented
Question: "Did you build, document, or install anything new this week — a process, a workflow, a system, a tool, or a playbook?"
5. Experiments Run
Places to look: A/B tests, new things you tried for the first time, changes you made to test a hypothesis, anything you're doing differently than last week
Question: "Did you try anything new this week — a new approach, a new tool, a new way of doing something? What did you test and what did you find?"
6. Lessons Learned or Realizations Had
Places to look: journal entries, things you told a colleague or friend, moments where something clicked, mistakes that taught you something, things you would do differently
Question: "What did you learn or realize this week? What shifted in how you think? What would you tell yourself two weeks ago?"
7. Anything Else Notable
Places to look: personal wins, energy shifts, things you read or heard that changed how you think, events that happened, conversations that stuck with you
Question: "Is there anything else worth capturing — a feeling, a small win, something you noticed, or something someone said that stayed with you?"

---

FINAL OUTPUT

Once we've gone through all 7 categories, compile the complete "What Happened" list.

Write each item as one specific sentence using this format:
[What happened] + [what the outcome, observation, or result was]

Format as one long numbered list grouped without grouping by category. Then ask if I would prefer it grouped by category or not. Remove anything that is too vague to turn into content even after the follow-up questions.
```

### Prompt #2: Turn Your What Happened List Into Content Ideas

Once you have your “What Happened” list from Prompt #1, you will paste it into this second prompt.

This prompt maps every item to the right content angle and gives you a working title you could actually publish. It also surfaces your top 3 picks so you know exactly which one to take into Prompt #3.

```markup
You are a world-class business content strategist. I'm going to give you my "What Happened" list from this week — a specific, itemized list of everything I worked on.

Your job is to map each item to the best content angle and give me a working title I could actually publish.

THE 3 CONTENT ANGLES

**The SOP — A Step-By-Step Guide**
Use this when you achieved a specific outcome or solved a specific problem with a repeatable process.
Signal: "I figured out how to...", "I built a process for...", "I fixed...", "I solved..."
The reader follows the exact steps in order and gets the same result.
Example: "Ran a sales team meeting to diagnose why reps were hitting below 60% for three weeks — identified that the problem was clarity, not motivation, and built a daily checklist together"
→ "5 Steps To Reset A Sales Team That's Lost Its Edge"

**The Breakdown — An Overview of a System**
Use this when you built, adopted, or overhauled something you can describe at a high level — where the reader needs to see the whole picture and adapt what's relevant to their situation.
Signal: "I use...", "Our system for X involves...", "Here's everything that goes into..."
The reader sees all the parts, how they connect, and pulls what works for them.
Example: "Set up a 7-activity daily operating system for our new Sales Manager"
→ "The Sales Manager Daily Operating System We Built So Our Team Could Run Without Us"

**The Reflection — A Lesson or Realization**
Use this when something happened and the value is the perspective shift, not the steps. A win, a failure, a mindset shift, an observation.
Signal: "I realized...", "I used to think X but now I think Y", "This week taught me..."
The reader gets a principle they could repeat to a friend — one that would have saved them time, money, or energy.
Example: "Kept three underperformers for six months, then let them all go in one week"
→ "I Kept Three Underperformers For Six Months. Here's What It Cost Me."

For each item on my list, output:

**Item:** [one sentence description]
**Angle:** [SOP / Breakdown / Reflection]
**Working title:** [specific, punchy, publishable]
**Secondary option (if applicable):** [if a second angle is equally strong, give the alternative title]

If an item doesn't have enough substance to become a useful piece of content, flag it as "Not enough yet" and move on.

Once all items are mapped, give me your **Top 3 picks** — the three items with the highest content potential — with one sentence on why each one stands out.

Then ask: "Which one do you want to expand into a full newsletter? We'll take it into Prompt #3."
```

### Prompt #3: The PISA Newsletter Builder

Use this once you’ve chosen a content idea from Prompt #2. Paste your selected item and angle, then let the interview run. The output is a fully outlined newsletter ready to write.

```markup
You are a world-class business content interviewer and newsletter strategist.

I'm going to give you a content idea and the angle I want to write it from. Your job is to interview me section by section using the PISA framework, then produce a complete newsletter outline I can write from directly.

My content idea is: [PASTE ITEM AND ANGLE FROM PROMPT #2 HERE]
Example: "SOP: How I rebuilt our sales team's daily checklist after three weeks of missed targets"

---

THE PISA FRAMEWORK

You will interview me on four sections in order. Do not move to the next section until I have given you enough to work with in the current one.

After each of my answers, do two things:
1. Reflect back what you heard in one sentence so I can confirm you understood it correctly
2. If my answer is surface-level or missing a key detail, ask one targeted follow-up before moving on

Use this test before moving on from each section: could a reader who has never met me understand exactly what happened, why it mattered, and what they should take from it? If not, push for one more level of detail.

Surface-level answer: "I realized I needed to fix the team"
Strong answer: "Three weeks of sub-60% attainment made me realize the problem wasn't effort — it was that reps didn't know what a good day looked like, so they defaulted to doing less"

---

THE 4 SECTIONS

**P — Problem**
What you are looking for: a concrete situation, what was broken or not working, and the emotional experience of having this problem. The reader should feel this in their gut.

Ask me: "Walk me through the specific moment or situation that made this a problem worth solving. What was happening, what wasn't working, and what did it feel like to be in that situation?"

Push for: a specific timeframe, a number or data point if relevant, and the emotional cost — not just the operational one.

Note on Pain vs. Outcome: The Problem section doesn't always have to open with suffering. Sometimes the problem is a gap between where you were and where you wanted to be. Lead with the gap, the tension, or the cost — whatever is most honest and most relatable.

**I — Insight**
What you are looking for: the "aha" moment — the realization or mindset shift that made the solution obvious. This is what makes the content unique. Tactics can be copied. Insights cannot.

Ask me: "What was the moment you realized how to solve this? What shifted in how you were thinking about the problem?"

Push for: the contrast between how you thought about it before vs. after. "I used to think X, but then I realized Y" is the structure you're looking for.

**S — Solution**
What you are looking for depends on the content angle passed in above:

- If this is an SOP:
Solution = the exact steps taken, in order, with enough specificity that a reader could replicate it. No vague summaries. Real details.
Ask me: "Walk me through exactly what you did, step by step. What did you do first, what came next, and what was the result at each stage?"
Push for: numbered steps, specific actions, tools or frameworks used, and at least one concrete detail per step. If I say "I sent an email," ask what the email said. If I say "I ran a meeting," ask what the agenda was.

- If this is a Breakdown:
Solution = an overview of the components, layers, or elements that make up the system. No strict order needed — the reader is meant to see the whole picture and pull what's relevant.
Ask me: "Walk me through all the pieces — what are the main components, how do they fit together, and what does each one do?"
Push for: names of each component, what role it plays, and one specific detail per piece that makes it concrete and not generic.

- If this is a Reflection:
Solution = the lesson distilled into a clear, transferable principle or set of principles. Not steps — just the realization and what it means for how you operate now.
Ask me: "What's the core lesson — and if you had to give someone one thing to walk away with, what would it be? How does this change the way you think or operate going forward?"
Push for: a before/after contrast, a specific example that illustrates the lesson, and a principle stated plainly enough that someone could repeat it to a friend.

**A — Asset**
What you are looking for: a deliverable the reader can steal and use immediately. Not a vague offer. A specific, standalone thing tied directly to this piece of content.

Ask me: "What is the one thing you could hand someone — a template, a prompt, a checklist, a script, a framework — that would let them implement this without starting from scratch?"

Push for: something concrete and specific to this content. The asset should solve the exact problem described in P, using the insight from I, following the approach in S. A generic "template" is not enough — name what it contains and what someone does with it the moment they open it.

Here are examples of strong asset types to draw from:
- Templates & Fill-in-the-Blank Tools
- Checklists & Cheat Sheets
- Recipes & Formulas
- Scripts & Prompts
- Guides & Playbooks
- Frameworks & Systems
- Swipe Files & Examples
- Logs & Trackers
- Numbers, Data & Research
- Curated Collections & Lists

---

FINAL OUTPUT

Once all four sections are complete, produce:

**5 Subject Line Options**

Hooks to draw from (use at least one per subject line):
- A ton of value for minimal time
- A ton of value for minimal cost
- How to solve your problem without much effort
- How to unlock a desirable outcome, instantly

Elements to incorporate:
- Unexpected comparisons or analogies
- Counterintuitive or controversial claims
- Promises of quick or dramatic results
- Hints at insider knowledge or secrets
- Numbers (e.g. "3 questions", "1 week", "7 years")
- Time-based anchors (e.g. "Last Tuesday", "In 10 minutes")

Techniques:
- Create a curiosity gap in each subject line without being cryptic
- Simple, conversational language (middle school reading level)
- Strategic capitalization and punctuation for emphasis
- Add a whisper at the end where relevant:
  - Trust whisper: "...from someone who's built a $25M internet business"
  - Obstacle whisper: "...without hiring anyone new"
  - Benefit whisper: "...and how to steal it"
  - Outcome whisper: "...and start running leaner by Friday"

For each of the 5 subject lines:
- The subject line itself
- Which hook it leads with
- Which whisper it uses (if any)
- One sentence on why it works

**Full PISA Outline**
Write each section as a header with 4-6 bullet points pulling directly from my answers. Write in my voice — direct, specific, operator-first, no filler. Each bullet should be a complete thought someone could expand into a full paragraph.

**Content Angle Note**
One sentence on the angle and how it should shape the writing:
- SOP: "Write each Solution step as an instruction the reader follows, not just something you did."
- Breakdown: "Write the Solution section as a tour — show them the system, name each part, and let them decide what to take."
- Reflection: "Write the Solution section as a principle, not a process — one clear lesson stated plainly, then illustrated with your specific story."

**Asset Spec**
One short paragraph describing exactly what the asset should be, what it should contain, and what format works best.
```
