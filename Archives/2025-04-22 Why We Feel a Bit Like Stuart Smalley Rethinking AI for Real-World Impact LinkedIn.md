By:

[Jonathan Petts](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jonathanpetts/), Co-Founder and CEO, [Upsolve](https://www.linkedin.com/company/upsolve/)

[Steven Lee](https://www.linkedin.com/in/steven-msk-lee/), CEO, [SkillUp Coalition](https://www.linkedin.com/company/skillupcoalition/)

When we think of AI, we immediately think of “ *Stuart Smalley*.” You remember ” *Stuart Smalley* ” from SNL fame, right? The self-proclaimed: " *I'm Good Enough, I'm Smart Enough, and Doggone It, People Like Me!*” (if you haven’t seen the genius of Al Franken, [take a look](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HMRX-Wj2WOk)). But here’s the thing. We’d imagine that if “ *Stuart Smalley* ” transposed from Al Franken’s imagination into the real world, the affirmation would mask a strong dose of insecurity (Stuart *Small* -ey). The insecurity, perhaps, that we all feel, in some manner or form.

As leaders of technology-first nonprofits building AI tools, insecurity seems always at the forefront. Are we doing enough? Are we doing the right thing? Are we taking the right approach? Are others doing it better? And most pointed: do funders like it, where every win brings affirmation; every loss - much more prevalent than the wins - travail. Insecurity.

Our initial rollout of AI didn’t necessarily quell the insecurity. To support our users, both organizations began deploying AI tools over the past few months. Like many, we started with AI chatbots. The premise was simple: answer user questions with trusted, curated information to reduce confusion and avoid misinformation.

### What We’ve Learned

What we’ve learned: It’s more complicated than that.

At Upsolve, we deployed a chatbot on our informational articles, designed to quickly answer user questions and help them move forward. At SkillUp, [along with our partners](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/skillup-coalition-launches-skillup-ai-a-human-centered-ai-agent-to-guide-non-degree-workers-toward-high-opportunity-careers-302379655.html?tc=eml_cleartime), we utilized a chatbot to guide users through our curated content of career, training programs, and/or jobs. The results? Low usage. Lower engagement and follow-through. Minimal improvement in conversion. At SkillUp, our AI chatbot was one-quarter to one-third less effective for users compared to those who went to our core experience, based on key metrics. At Upsolve, only 3,000 users engaged with our chatbot, despite our significant site traffic.

We’re still unpacking why, but two early hypotheses stand out:

Overly Restrictive Design: Both our chatbots relied on a RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) approach that only pulled from Upsolve-authored or SkillUp-curated content. That meant it couldn’t answer critical wraparound questions—even when those questions were central to a user’s decision. Or said differently, by pulling only from Upsolve or SkillUp content—for legitimate reasons, to minimize misleading information—we limited the creative power of generative AI that makes it so powerful. We weighted safety over creativity, which limited our impact.

The Cognitive Burden of Prompting: We assumed users would know what to ask. But prompt engineering is a skill—and it’s not reasonable to expect our users, moderate to low-income Americans—to have it. Typing a precise question into a chatbot isn’t intuitive for everyone, especially when you’re not even sure what to ask. We underestimated how hard it is to formulate the right question under pressure.

While chatbots help with basic inquiries, they struggle with the deeper challenges our users face in the often long, complicated journey to reach economic mobility. Many users don’t trust or engage with chatbots at all (per a recent [Gallup study](https://news.gallup.com/poll/654905/americans-everyday-products-without-realizing.aspx?utm_source=chatgpt.com), 72% of Americans believe AI will make misinformation worse). Others receive generic or unempathetic responses that don’t reflect their real needs. We’ve seen drop-off rates we’d rather not see. The tools weren’t meeting people where they were—and in many cases, they weren’t helping people move forward.

And so, we’ve been asking ourselves harder questions. Not just “Does the chatbot work?” but “Does it work better than doing nothing at all?” The answers were mixed. Some users found real value, especially when offered specific, recommended next steps. But overall, chatbot users were less likely to create profiles, less likely to follow through, and less likely to take action than those using the platform without the AI layer.

### What’s Next

This doesn’t mean chatbots are a failure. There’s value in chatbots, particularly for basic questions that form the basis for many of the prompts. But when navigating complex, high-stakes, nuanced journeys, like career navigation (SkillUp) or debt/credit repair (Upsolve), they’re often not enough. It means they need to evolve. It means that we, Upsolve and SkillUp, needed to evolve our AI strategy.

That’s what we’re working on now. Instead of building AI just to answer questions, we’re building AI that helps users *act*. Tools that surface at the right moment in the product experience to help people take the next step—and the one after that. We’re identifying the key points/use cases where users drop off, and building real-time support that helps them push through. At Upsolve, we call it *in-app AI support*. At SkillUp, we think of it as “horizontal integration”—AI guidance that functions more like Google’s AI Overviews than a typical chatbot. In short, to act as a real-time companion; a tool that helps users move through the product flow, surfaces relevant content before the user asks for it, and adapts dynamically to what we know about the user. As we write this, the language sounds like high-falutin nonsense. That’s not what matters. What matters is the goal: helping users make progress, not just get information.

In short: we’re learning. We’re adapting. And we’re trying to be honest about what it takes to build AI tools that are genuinely helpful for low-income users navigating complex systems.

We’re also investing in learning. Measuring outcomes. Asking: Does this reduce drop-off? Does it lead to more jobs, higher wages, lower debt, better credit?

Because offering static responses isn’t enough. It helps, but it leaves too many people behind.

### Looking Ahead: A Call for Collaboration

There’s a lot of noise in the AI space right now. We contribute to this noise. We don’t have all the answers. But we wanted to share our early learnings in case they’re helpful. We’re sharing our honest assessments to open a conversation. We’re uplifting our insecurities because it’s how we feel. To be honest, we don’t know if our evolution will work. Judgment calls are subject to, well, judgment; subject to human fallacy. Our intuition says yes, based on what our users are telling us and where we think AI is going. But we don’t really know. If you’re wrestling with similar challenges or doubt, or if you’ve had major wins—or if you’re interested in exploring how we might learn together—reach out. Maybe we’ve missed something. Maybe there’s a better way. We’d love to learn from you.

Thank you!
