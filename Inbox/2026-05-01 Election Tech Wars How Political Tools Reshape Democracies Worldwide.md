Power

01.05.2026

Few sectors are as haunted by their own buzzwords as modern political campaigns. For a decade, “data‑driven”, “AI‑powered” and “micro‑targeted” have doubled as signals of competence. The more complex the tech stack, the more impressive the operation, at least on paper.

But as a growing number of campaigners quietly admit, many of the most heavily tooled‑up campaigns of recent years have lost not because they lacked technology, but because they mistook it for strategy. The most dangerous trend in political tech is not under‑investment. It is over‑reliance.

\-

The robots will tell us

Inside one recent high‑profile campaign, a telling phrase took hold: “I’ll let the robots decide.” It began as a joke, a way to describe the battery of polling, message‑testing, and optimisation tools that now sit between strategists and voters. But over time it hardened into a culture.

Faced with contentious decisions, whether to respond to a line of attack, whether to invest in persuading a hostile demographic, whether to risk a bold, values‑heavy message, senior staff increasingly deferred to what the models said. If an ad defending transgender rights underperformed in isolated A/B tests, the conclusion was clear: do not touch the issue. Never mind that, in the real campaign, voters were already hearing about it constantly from the other side, framed in hostile terms.

What looked, from the inside, like scientific caution was, in fact, a form of political risk‑aversion dressed up as objectivity. Saying “the data shows we shouldn’t respond” is an attractive shield when the real fear is being blamed if the response fails.

The result is a campaign frozen by its own instrumentation, mistaking the absence of statistically clean evidence for a mandate to do nothing. Politics, however, does not pause for the perfect sample. As one strategist bluntly put it, “older‑school” decision‑making, informed by qualitative research, experience, and a sense of narrative, would have recognised the need to answer loud attacks even if the robots had not yet given their blessing.

#### The engagement ladder and the WhatsApp test

If over‑reliance on testing is one problem, over‑engineering is another. Many parties still design technology as if every supporter were a full‑time activist, eager to download apps, learn workflows, and input detailed data.

The reality is that political engagement is a ladder, not a binary. At the bottom are citizens barely paying attention; above them, sympathisers willing to sign a petition or respond to a WhatsApp message; then donors, then occasional volunteers, then the core organisers for whom politics is a second job. Each rung tolerates a different level of friction.

Campaigns that insist on complex tools at the bottom of that ladder set themselves up to fail. If a casual supporter is asked to navigate a party‑built suite to RSVP to an event, they will drop off. If a volunteer is expected to master multilayered canvassing software to knock on a neighbour’s door, the door will likely remain unknocked.

The best practitioners apply a brutal rule: the simplest effective tool wins. In many contexts, that means starting with WhatsApp and email, not with bespoke apps; using channels people already inhabit rather than trying to drag them into a closed ecosystem. For higher‑engagement roles, a dedicated party app or organising platform makes sense, but it must feel like an upgrade, not an obligation.

Simplicity is not anti‑tech. It is an acknowledgement that every additional click filters out a chunk of potential supporters, and that no machine learning model can persuade someone who never makes it through the onboarding screen.

#### Building for the next campaign, not just this one

Even when tools are well designed, they often die on election night.

One quiet scandal of political tech is how often parties behave as if history resets after each loss or win. Volunteers’ contact logs, canvass notes, and supporter lists are locked inside proprietary systems with no easy export function. New leadership teams arrive, declare existing tools “broken”, and start again from zero. Five years later, the cycle repeats.

The antidote is unglamorous but transformative: think like an archivist. Before signing any contract or building any system, campaign leaders should ask a single question: when I am exhausted and stepping aside, what will I be able to hand my successor? If the answer is not “at least a clean CSV they can upload into whatever comes next”, something has gone wrong.

It is an “evergreen CSV” mindset, building datasets, not just dashboards. In practice, that means insisting on interoperable formats, clear data governance, and simple documentation. It means recognising that the most valuable asset a campaign can produce may not be a single victory, but an infrastructure of knowledge that compound over cycles.

This is particularly critical for parties outside the US, where institutional memory is often thinner and turnover higher. Too many European parties, in particular, treat digital innovation as a series of one‑off experiments rather than as the slow, cumulative work of building capacity.

#### The human conversation is still the product

There is another trend quietly reshaping modern campaigning: the rediscovery of unscripted conversation.

For decades, field programmes trained volunteers to stick to scripts, tightly worded, centrally approved, designed to minimise risk. Tablets replaced clipboards, but the interaction remained stiff. In practice, voters quickly recognised these exchanges for what they were: transactions, not conversations.

The most ambitious organisers are now trying the opposite. They train activists to leave the script behind, to listen first, to ask open questions, and to treat each interaction as an opportunity to learn rather than to recite. The technology comes after: once the conversation is over, volunteers record a short summary of what they heard and upload it to a platform where AI tools can extract patterns, concerns, and themes at scale.

This inversion matters. Instead of forcing the voter to fit the template, the template adapts to the voter. Instead of asking “Did they agree with message X?”, the campaign asks “What did they actually talk about?”

AI has a productive role here. Large‑language models are exceptionally good at processing messy qualitative notes and turning them into structured insights. They can surface unexpected issues, regional differences, and language that resonates, giving strategists a living map of public sentiment far richer than any survey topline.

But this only works if the front end remains profoundly human. A campaign that uses AI to listen better may deepen trust; one that uses it to automate candidate speeches risks eroding the very authenticity voters still crave.

#### Keep it simple, and be honest about culture

Ask practitioners from India to Europe for a single piece of advice to a campaign about to head into an election, and the answers converge.

“Keep it simple,” says one operative who works across large, complex party structures with thousands of volunteers. Begin with the technologies people already know, above all, WhatsApp, and resist the urge to “over‑train” unpaid activists on tools they will resent. Recognise that for most supporters, politics is something they fit around jobs, families, and stress; tools must respect that reality.

“Think about who comes after you,” urges another. Design systems with continuity in mind. Make it possible for the next team to build on your work rather than starting from scratch.

“Teach people how to be human again,” adds a third. Scrap rigid scripts; instead, invest in training activists to hold decent, empathetic conversations, then give them lightweight tools to capture what they hear.

Threaded through all of this is a more uncomfortable truth: technology does not fix organisational culture. It exposes it.

In parties where leaders hoard information, fear blame, and punish failure, sophisticated tools become new battlegrounds, ways to avoid responsibility, or to marginalise dissenting voices under the guise of “what the data says”. In parties where experimentation is encouraged and failure treated as learning, even simple tools can unlock significant progress.

As one leadership coach put it, “Culture eats strategy, and tech, for breakfast.” A campaign that treats its volunteers as data points will use technology one way; a campaign that treats them as co‑creators of a shared project will use it another.

The real question is not whether technology belongs in politics. It already does, and it will not leave. The question is whether those who wield it are willing to accept that there is no software substitute for courage: the courage to take a stand even when the tests are inconclusive, to listen rather than broadcast, and to build for the long term in a profession obsessed with the next news cycle.

In an age of dashboards and decision‑trees, that may be the most radical innovation of all.
