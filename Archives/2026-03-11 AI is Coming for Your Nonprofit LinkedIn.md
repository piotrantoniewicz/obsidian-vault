### That's Not a Threat — But It Does Require a Decision.

Most nonprofit leaders I talk to are somewhere between curious and overwhelmed when the subject of AI comes up.

The hype is deafening. The tools are multiplying faster than anyone can evaluate them. And the organizations best positioned to fund, staff, and experiment with new technology are not typically the ones running lean on mission budgets with teams already stretched thin.

So it's easy to understand why many nonprofit leaders have adopted a wait-and-see posture. Let the tech sector sort it out. Let the larger organizations run the experiments. We'll adopt what works when the dust settles.

I understand that instinct. I also think it's a mistake.

---

Here's what's actually happening: AI is compressing the advantage that well-resourced organizations have always held over smaller, mission-driven ones.

For the first time, a two-person nonprofit communications team can produce the volume and quality of content that used to require a full department. A program director can analyze data, draft reports, and prepare grant narratives in a fraction of the time. A small development team can research funders, personalize outreach, and track relationships with tools that were unimaginable five years ago.

The opportunity for nonprofits isn't to match what large institutions do. It's to do more of what only they can do — direct community relationships, authentic mission delivery, trust built over years — while AI handles more of the operational weight.

That is a genuine opportunity. But only for leaders who decide to engage with it rather than wait it out.

---

The challenge is real too, and it deserves honest treatment.

AI raises legitimate questions about authenticity. When a donor receives a communication, when a community member reads your newsletter, when a funder reviews your narrative — does it matter whether a human wrote it? For many mission-driven organizations, voice and relationship are the product. Using AI carelessly can hollow out exactly what makes the organization worth supporting.

There are also real concerns about data privacy, about staff anxiety, about the risk of adopting tools faster than your organization's culture can absorb them.

These are not reasons to avoid AI. They are reasons to be intentional about how you engage with it.

---

The Stoic framework I keep returning to here is simple: distinguish between what you can control and what you can't.

You cannot control the pace of AI development. You cannot control whether your peer organizations adopt it, whether funders start expecting AI-assisted reporting, or whether the tools available today look anything like the ones available in two years.

What you can control is your organization's relationship to the technology. Whether you approach it from a place of clarity about your mission and values, or from a place of anxiety and reaction. Whether you use it in ways that amplify your humanity or substitute for it.

The leaders I most respect in this space aren't the ones chasing every new tool. They're the ones who have asked — quietly, deliberately — *what does this need to serve in order to serve our mission?* And they're building from that answer.

---

There's a practical place to start, and it isn't a technology audit.

It's a conversation with your team about what you stand for and what you're trying to protect. What is the irreducibly human part of your work — the part that AI should support, not replace? What is the operational burden that drains your people and serves no one — the part that AI can and should absorb?

That clarity is what makes every subsequent technology decision easier. Without it, you're just reacting to whatever's loudest.

The change is coming regardless of whether you're ready for it. The question — as it has always been for mission-driven leaders — is whether you're going to face it on your terms or someone else's.
