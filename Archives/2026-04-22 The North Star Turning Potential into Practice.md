### TEAMS! Newsletter | The AI Edition | April 22, 2026

For many, Artificial Intelligence and transformative technology can feel like just another wave threatening to swamp our core missions. But as discussed at the recent ***Stanford Social Innovation Review*** webinar, *"Transformative Technology That Unleashes Nonprofit Scale,"* the goal is to do more than just to stay afloat; it's to harness the wind and move forward!

The tension we all feel is real: how do we modernize without losing our soul? How do we adopt AI without creating "digital silos" that distance us from the people we serve? This edition of TEAMS! and it isn't about AI hype; it's about the accountability and discipline required to make sure AI works for your mission.

### Executive Summary

To achieve scale, nonprofits need to move from manual, siloed work to an end state where inclusion and care are informed by data and driven by innovation and collaboration. Scale isn't found in a single "flashy" tool but in building a foundation that governs professionalized frontline teams, unifies fragmented data, and uses AI to lower barriers to access.

### Key Takeaways

- ***Solve for the Bottleneck*****:** Identify the specific constraint, whether it is language, data silos, or navigation, that limits your ability to scale.
- ***Unify Before You Replace:*** Don't disrupt what works. Instead, build "connective tissue" (such as a modern data lakehouse) to link existing systems.
- ***Infrastructure Over One-Offs:***Treat technology projects as durable systems with versioning and auditability, not as temporary fixes.
- ***Mission as the North Star:*** Every technological "experiment" must align with a measurable mission outcome.
- ***Phased Modernization:*** Break grand visions into "bite-sized" phases to avoid disrupting core operations during the transition.

### Start with the Constraint That Limits Scale

As Kelli Rasmussen of Project GENIE (American Association for Cancer Research) noted, their biggest barrier wasn't a lack of data but a language barrier. By using AI-powered translation (with human guardrails), they transformed a manual, inconsistent process into a scalable global infrastructure.

- ***Real-World Scenario:*** A local social services agency might find that its "bottleneck" is the 20 hours per week staff spend manually reconciling spreadsheets across finance and donor databases.

### The Power of a Unified Data Platform

Wendell Thomas of Smile Train highlighted that their challenge was "fragmented data" across multiple systems. They didn't replace their CRM or finance systems; instead, they built an AWS-based "lakehouse" to unify them. This creates a "single source of truth" essential for accurate AI insights.

- ***Real-World Scenario:*** Instead of a donor receiving three separate emails because they exist in three separate databases, a unified platform enables a single, cohesive, and personal relationship.

### Experimenting with "Multi-Agent" AI

Kathy Miu of the American Red Cross discussed "Clara AI," a multi-agent system that uses domain-specific "sub-agents" for disaster response, volunteer services, and fundraising. This approach enables intent recognition and orchestration, ensuring the right information reaches the right person instantly.

- ***Real-World Scenario:*** A disaster victim uses a web chat to request help; a "Disaster Sub-Agent" identifies the need, routes it to the local chapter, and triggers a "Volunteer Agent" to locate available responders, all within seconds.

### Right-Sizing Your Technology Maturity Level

Irene Blanchard-Wu from AWS emphasized that not every organization needs to start with agentic AI. Whether you are in the "Momentum to Modernize" phase (migration) or the "Pathfinder" phase (generative AI), your project must be right-sized to your current technical capacity.

- ***Real-World Scenario:***The "Agentic AI" Trap. The Executive Director hears about "Agentic AI" and attempts to build an AI-driven voice bot to interview youth, assess their needs, and automatically assign them to programs. The Result: Failure.

### Cross-Functional Buy-In

Success requires a "Total Team" approach, with teams that include programs, IT, and development. Technology is no longer an "add-on"; it is the primary enabler of the mission.

- ***Real-World Scenario:*** **Before** launching a new AI intake tool, the organization convenes the IT head, the frontline social worker, and the donor relations officer to ensure the tool serves all three stakeholders.

### Putting It Into Action:

- ***Identify Your "Language":*** What is the single manual task or data silo that is your organization’s biggest barrier to speed?
- ***Audit Your Foundation:*** Do you have a "single source of truth" for your donor and program data, or are you still living in "Spreadsheet City"?
- ***Draft a "Press Release" for the Future:*** Follow Wendell Thomas's advice, write the press release about your success two years from now, to keep your team focused on the ultimate goal.
- ***Assemble Your Stakeholders:*** Schedule a 30-minute coffee meeting with your IT lead and a program director to discuss a small process that could be automated.
- ***Explore the "Imagine Grant":*** Visit the AWS Imagine Grant website to determine whether your 2026-2027 project fits the "Pathfinder" or "Momentum" categories.

### Closing Thoughts

Our path forward is clear: we use new technologies with focused intent to improve our outcomes. When we treat technology as a steward of our mission rather than a "new toy", we scale our organizations and our impact.
