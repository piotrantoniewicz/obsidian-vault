Hey, [Dr. Astrid Scholz](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ajscholz/) here. I want to expand on something I posted in an update after my recent travels in the UK and Europe

> 🌐 Everywhere I went, people were reeling from the harm that the US tech broligarchy is doing to the world. And from the realization that civil society, philanthropy, humanitarian aid and other sectors that are so important for bringing about the new world we want urgently need their own, non toxic technology infrastructure. The kind that [Armillaria](https://www.linkedin.com/company/armillaria/) and [NeedsList](https://www.linkedin.com/company/needslist/) have been building, as well as other [techforpurpose](https://www.linkedin.com/search/results/all/?keywords=%23techforpurpose&origin=HASH_TAG_FROM_FEED) companies--all of whom are looking for growth funding.

We are now seeing play out what happens when technology is built on a set of design principles that value building fast and breaking things, revenue models predicated on mining users' attention and engagement, and growing companies from valuation to valuation. All this with little regard for the impacts that the choices of business model, company culture, and community norms have on our children's minds, the natural environment, and now our democracies.

### If it's free to use, you are not the customer: you are the product

I distinctly remember, 15 years or so ago, when we were contemplating the use of the then new cloud technologies for the nonprofit where I was working at the time, [Ecotrust](https://www.linkedin.com/company/ecotrust/), cautioning against the use of Google products over Microsoft. The latter, at least, was in the business of selling software, whereas the former's free products triggered my economist's spidey sense. **If it's free to use, you are not the customer: you are the product.** Never in my wildest dreams would I have guessed to what extremes the tech industry would take the monetization of users, I was just applying Microeconomics 101 and common sense.

Fast forward to today, and here we are, in the middle of, as [Carole Cadwalladr](https://www.linkedin.com/in/carole-cadwalladr-88b6a3335/) put it at the TED conference earlier this week, a digital coup:

Not surprisingly, the titans of the resulting surveillance capitalism have fallen in line with the Trump regime, and the havoc wreaked by DOGE and the executive branch on the humanitarian and social impact sector is only the beginning of what might happen next given the pervasive use of digital technologies built by a small oligopoly of tech companies--virtually all market segments, from search to enterprise software, to AI are dominated by a small number of very large companies--notably Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, and Nvidia.

And: much of the social sector runs on these very tools and platforms. This will increasingly be a problem, not least since, to quote Audre Lorde, **"the master's tools will never dismantle the master's house."**

### There are three major issues:

1. Between the known disregard for privacy and things like copyright laws (like Carole's, mine and so many other's digital content has been used to train the AI models without our consent) and the complicity of the tech bros, **can the social sector really trust that the data and sensitive information they are storing on these services are safe**? What's to prevent any of these providers from colluding with DOGE to mine your data for information on immigrants, trans people, and other vulnerable segments of the population, or just plain critics of the regime? Not the privacy protections that are the norm here in the US.
2. Much of Silicon Valley style technology is not built for the purposes that animate the social impact industry. This plays out in a number of fronts, but is most striking when it comes to trying to collaborate effectively. When multiple organizations come together in a multi-stakeholder initiative, whose project management, contact management and other business technology do we use? Try it: it's really hard to get the business accounts of various enterprise software apps to talk to each other. This **lack of interoperability** creates friction in how data can move across platforms used by multiple partners, nor is it easily possible to track who has done what to the data while in their stewardship.
3. It's even worse when you consider how social change organizations treat the data belonging to the people they are serving. We have in the past critiqued this as a business model problem, with **organizations treating data they have collected about the social enterprises and NGOs in their networks as their data when in reality the only source of truth about a social enterprise is the enterprise.** There is, however, also a design problem with most Silicon Valley style software in that the prevailing per-user pricing makes it cost-prohibitive to turn the data over to the original owners for updating. Instead, many network organizations fall back on having a curator who is tasked with reaching out to the member organizations to update their profiles. The average social enterprise is a member of 10+ networks across the alphabet, from [Ashoka](https://www.linkedin.com/company/ashoka/) to [Zebras Unite](https://www.linkedin.com/company/zebras-unite/), so this **lack of data sovereignty** ends up putting the onus on the people with the least amount of time and resources to keep information about what they are working on fresh if they want to derive value from the networks they join.

These issues have only become more pronounced in the tech broligarchy, and it is high time that the social change industry re-examine its relationship with Silicon Valley tech. **There is now urgency to figuring out what safe alternatives look like that ladder up to an impact tech stack and infrastructure that mobilizes people, data and capital to tackle the urgent problems of our time.** Including, now, the real and present danger of needing to offshore data, assets and operations as the United States become less safe for nonprofit and social impact organizations large and small that are working with vulnerable communities, or indeed pursue any agenda (like climate change research) that does not align with the current political ideology.

Luckily, the cost of building tech has come down dramatically, and the proposition for knitting together a number of platforms that have been building pieces of the tech we need---including messaging apps like Signal, secure office tools like [Proton](https://www.linkedin.com/company/protonprivacy/), social networking tools like [Hylo](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/we-need-dissident-tech-now-dr-astrid-scholz-prhoc/#), investment diligence like [Artha Networks Inc. (ANI)](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/we-need-dissident-tech-now-dr-astrid-scholz-prhoc/#), solutions curation and coordination like [Armillaria](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/we-need-dissident-tech-now-dr-astrid-scholz-prhoc/#), disaster reponse and recovery planning like [NeedsList](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/we-need-dissident-tech-now-dr-astrid-scholz-prhoc/#) and so many more--has a considerably smaller pricetag than it did 10 years or so ago when I first reinvented myself as a tech entrepreneur.

### Will we break the impact infrastructure impasse?

As I elaborated in my recent post, however, there has been woeful underinvestment in purpose built digital technology that serves the social impact sector. This is largely due to misaligned incentive, and the lack of curiosity on the part of funders to invest in existing solutions versus building something *de novo.*

The responses, especially the private ones, have been instructive.

On the side of the impact technologists, there is widespread enthusiasm for a collective approach to building the digital infrastructure we need to coordinate and mobilize people, capital and innovations at the speed and volume required for addressing the Sustainable Development Goals in time. On the side of people representing the money that could make a different here, it's been confusion and hurt feelings--after all, everyone has the best intentions. Alas, these best intentions have (so far, at least) to the successive reinventing of the idea that we need better infrastructure, but seemingly we can't get to the next step, of actually building it.

What are we waiting for?
