### TEAMS! Newsletter | AI Edition | April 8, 2026

Many of you, especially in the nonprofit sector, have been on the front lines of every major societal shift, often stepping in when markets fail and policies fall behind. Today, we stand at the edge of a once-in-a-lifetime technological upgrade as Sam Altman and OpenAI described the race toward superintelligence. For leaders of today’s organizations or board members overseeing their companies, the "Intelligence Age" may seem like a distant, shiny goal of endless promise or a looming threat to the human-centered work you and your teams do. For those of us in the social services sector, this is the paradox of the future we confront every day, asking ourselves, Is AI a friend or a foe?

The tension we're all experiencing is real. We're being asked to navigate a future where AI could generate results in minutes that would currently take your staff months. Yet, we're rooted in a reality of shrinking budgets, a fragile "care economy," and a digital divide that risks leaving many of our staff, customers, clients, and communities even further behind. We can't afford to be passive observers of this industrial policy. If we don't help shape the "democratic process" OpenAI invites us into, the benefits of this new era will be concentrated in the hands of a few, while the social services sector is left to cope with the "disruption and dislocation" of many.

### Executive Summary: The TL;DR

My understanding of the core idea Sam Altman presents in his "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" essay is that the move to superintelligence is inevitable and will reshape work, knowledge, and prosperity. To ensure these benefits everyone, we must go beyond small steps and adopt a bold new social contract. For the social impact sector, this means advocating for a "Right to AI," helping to establish "Public Wealth Funds," and ensuring the "care economy" is professionalized and protected as a space for human-centered work.

### Key Takeaways:

- ***Democratic Agency Is the New Standard:***Nonprofit leaders must shift from just using AI to actively overseeing its implementation to ensure it enhances, rather than diminishes, individual agency.
- ***The Care Economy as an Economic Anchor:*** As routine tasks are automated, human connection, such as childcare, eldercare, and social services, will become the premium "human-centered" work of the future.
- ***A Shift from Labor-Based to Capital-Based Support:***We need to prepare for a future in which traditional payroll taxes, a main source of funding for social safety nets, may be replaced by revenues from capital and public wealth funds.
- ***The "Right to AI" is a Civil Right:*** Access to foundational models should be considered a public utility, like electricity or the internet, to avoid creating a permanent "intelligence underclass".
- ***Proactive Resilience Over Reactive*** ***Safety***: Building a resilient society needs real-time monitoring of AI’s effects on local wages and displacement, instead of waiting for a crisis to increase support.

### Claiming the "Right to AI" for Our Communities

Altman argues that access to AI should be a fundamental right, likening it to global literacy. In the social services sector, we recognize that "access" is rarely equal. If the building blocks of modern AI remain costly or restricted, small community clinics or local food banks will fall behind in efficiency.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** Imagine a refugee resettlement agency using a "Right to AI" voucher to access a high-tier multilingual model. This enables them to deliver real-time, nuanced legal and medical translations that would otherwise cost tens of thousands of dollars, effectively leveling the playing field with well-funded private firms. We must champion these "low-cost access points" to ensure the productivity boom doesn't end at the gates of the Fortune 500.

### Elevating the Care and Connection Economy

The document highlights a crucial shift: as AI takes over "projects that take months," the demand for human-centered work, such as education, healthcare, and community services, will increase. This remains our main focus. However, this shift will only succeed if these roles are supported with better wages and improved job quality.

- ***Nonprofit Example:*** A youth mentorship organization uses AI to manage all administrative scheduling, reporting, and grant tracking. Instead of "saving money" by cutting staff, the leadership uses the "efficiency dividend" to raise mentor salaries and provide more one-on-one time with at-risk youth. We must ensure that "human-centered" doesn't become a euphemism for "low-paid labor" in the AI era.

### Navigating the New Social Safety Net

OpenAI's proposal introduces "Adaptive Safety Nets" that trigger automatically based on real-time economic data. For social impact leaders, this represents a significant shift from the slow, bureaucratic grant cycles we currently face. It indicates a move toward "portable benefits" that follow a worker from a gig job to a nonprofit to a startup.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** A regional workforce development board partners with a "Public Wealth Fund" to provide "wage insurance" for workers whose industries were disrupted by AI. This allows the nonprofit to focus on retraining the worker for the "care economy" without the worker facing immediate housing instability.

### Worker Voice: Leading from the Middle

The policy proposes providing workers a "formal way to collaborate with management" on AI deployments. In many nonprofits, there is concern that AI is used as a tool for "doing more with less," leading to staff burnout. We must use AI to remove the "administrative or exhausting tasks," not just to increase workload.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** A social work supervisor hosts a "Redesign Lab" where they identify tasks like data entry or case notes as "soul-crushing." They introduce AI tools to handle these specific tasks, but the team imposes a "hard limit" on using AI to monitor employee productivity or automate sensitive client interactions.

### Trust, Accountability, and the "AI Trust Stack"

Finally, the document discusses an "AI Trust Stack," a set of tools designed to verify authenticity and assign responsibility when an AI makes a mistake. For nonprofits, whose primary currency is trust, this is crucial. We cannot afford to rely on "misaligned systems" that might display "hidden loyalties" or biases against the populations we serve.

- ***Nonprofit Scenario:*** A housing advocacy group employs an AI-powered auditing tool to examine local government records, ensuring that AI-assisted housing voucher allocations do not discriminate against minority neighborhoods. They are not just using AI; they are using it to hold those in power accountable.

### Practical Steps for This Week:

- ***Start the "Democratic Conversation":*** Host a brown-bag lunch with your staff and board. Ask: "If a Public Wealth Fund existed, how would we want its returns distributed to our clients?"
- ***Review Your Vendor's Governance Practices:*** Ask your software providers if they have "mission-aligned product governance" or if they are "hardened against insider capture.”
- ***Join a Regional AI Hub:*** Find local "CAISI" (“Center for AI Standards and Innovation”) chapters or university research hubs. Ensure the nonprofit perspective is included in their "representative input processes."
- ***Draft an AI Ethics Charter:*** Create a straightforward, one-page document for your organization that outlines "clear rules for how we will and will not use AI," emphasizing the importance of protecting client privacy and maintaining human agency.

### Final Thoughts: The Unvarnished Assessment

Sam Altman’s essay is a masterpiece of "optimistic realism." He recognizes the risks of wealth concentration and job disruption while depicting a world where "living standards should rise" for everyone. However, as citizens and leaders in the social sector, we must be honest: **OpenAI is an incumbent.** While they advocate for "common-sense regulation," their scale makes them a leading driver of the risks they aim to mitigate.

Our focus as citizens should also include **power dynamics**. We must insist that the "Public Wealth Fund" is not just a philanthropic gesture, but a fundamental right. We need to make sure that "superintelligence" doesn't become a reason to bypass democratic principles in the pursuit of "speed to discovery." The social services sector is society's conscience; if we don't bring that conscience to the AI table, we're simply managing the decline of the old world instead of building the new one.

Let us be the architects of an Intelligence Age that prioritizes people.
