The Future of Membership: From Mobilising People to Building Power.

Membership isn’t dying - but the old rules no longer work. Across NGOs, campaigns, unions and political parties, organisations are signing people up in moments of urgency… and losing them just as fast. This report argues that the real crisis isn’t recruitment, but relationship. In a world of distrust, precarity and digital overload, people don’t join to be counted - they join to belong, to matter, and to make a difference they can see.

Drawing on interviews and data from organisations representing 300,000 members our new report, The Future of Membership: From Lists of Names to Living Relationships shows what healthy membership looks like now: designed journeys instead of one-off transactions; community as the core product; technology that strengthens human connection rather than replacing it; and models that turn individual commitment into collective power.

Session Summary

The modern political organization is suffering from a fatal disconnect between **recruitment success** and **long-term power**, resulting in what experts call an "engagement crisis". **Madeleine Cazes, Events & Partnerships Manager at NationBuilder**, argues that while organizations are growing their databases, they are effectively building "lists of names" rather than active, self-sustaining movements. The problem is rooted in a systemic under-investment in the infrastructure of **belonging**—the onboarding, relationship building, and member experience design—which is often starved of resources in favor of short-term campaign goals.

﻿

The panel highlighted that **56% of recruitment** is driven by human trust and referrals from existing members rather than digital advertising, yet organizations continue to prioritize financial sustainability and political leverage over the **social value** that members actually crave. **Sam Lockwood, founder of Brand Response**, pointed to the success of the **D66 campaign**, which succeeded by treating "culture as strategy," allowing teams to iterate freely and asking members what they actually wanted rather than dictating it. Furthermore, the panel identified that the "fixed fee myth" of membership must be shattered to allow for a fluid range of contributions, from micro-donations to significant annual commitments, reflecting a move toward **asynchronous and accessible** participation.

﻿

The conclusion of the dialogue is that the **"product" of modern membership is community**, and technology must serve to strengthen human connection rather than replace it. **Rebekah Ison of the Better Politics Foundation** characterized effective networks as an "antidote to loneliness," suggesting that for members to stay, they must feel their identity is represented and their values are being "lived out" through the organization. Successful groups like those using **WhatsApp and Discord** are thriving because they provide "nurtured" spaces that deliver information directly relevant to the user’s daily life, bypassing the friction of outdated member portals.

﻿

The flow of the argument moved from the failures of **"vanity metrics"** to the necessity of **"high-value responses"** for high-value engagement. Cazes and Lockwood debated the demographic shifts in membership, noting that while the donor base is aging, the solution for reaching the youth is not a separate strategy but a **radical rethinking of the "front door"** to the organization. A notable point of tension was whether political parties can survive without legal reform, as their rigid democratic structures often act as impediments to the **fluidity** required by modern volunteers.

﻿

Diplomatically, the session underscored that European organizations have a "superpower" in **GDPR**, which forces a more sustainable and ethical approach to list-building compared to the "burn the list" culture of U.S. politics. There was a strong consensus that "transactional benefits" like physical swag are no longer enough to retain members; instead, leaders must be **authentic and transparent**, even about their mistakes. **Tornike Abuladze (My Voice Counts)** added a commercial perspective, arguing that political tech must move from one-off projects to real, for-profit products to remain independent of shifting political interests.

﻿

The **bottom-line** is that the future of power lies in **resilient membership ecosystems** where "liquid volunteering" allows people to move seamlessly between online and offline spaces. Organizations that fail to invest in the **"supportive environment"** required to move a member from a viral "cue" to a sustained "habit" will continue to see their influence evaporate once the election dust settles. Ultimately, to build power in 2026, organizations must stop counting heads and start **honoring the human motivation** behind every sign-up.
