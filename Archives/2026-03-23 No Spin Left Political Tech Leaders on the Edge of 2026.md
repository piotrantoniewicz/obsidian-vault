No Spin Left: Political Tech Leaders on the Edge of 2026

Campaigns have been using powerful tools in shallow, performative ways while neglecting the human relationships and institutional reforms that make those tools meaningful. The panel’s “confessions” amount to a roadmap for 2026: stop chasing vanity metrics, treat members and supporters as high‑value partners, give tech a real seat at the table, and rebuild parties so they can recruit and retain the next generation without being hollowed out from within.

\-

Why Political Tech is Going Small and Human

If you want to know how badly politics has been coping with technology, ask the people who build the tools. In an unusually candid panel titled “Confessions from Political Tech Leaders: Real Talk, Urgent Lessons, and Predictions for 2026,” three insiders, Flo Barre of Qomon, Leah (Higher Ground Labs), and Oliver Wells of NationBuilder, sketched a movement that has often used tech shallowly, chased scale for its own sake, and ignored the humans who actually join. Their diagnoses of recent campaigns, and their predictions for 2026, sounded less like TED‑ready optimism and more like a plea: stop wasting the next decade repeating the last one.

The panel’s starting point was emotional, not technical. Asked to describe their 2025 in a single word, the answers were telling: “scepticism,” “BANI” (brittle, anxious, non‑linear, incomprehensible), and “outsiders.” The field, in other words, has moved beyond “VUCA” buzzwords into a sense that institutions are fracturing faster than they can be rebuilt, that anxiety is a permanent operating condition, and that the most effective campaigns are behaving like insurgents even when they sit at the centre. Against that backdrop, political tech is no longer a niche; it is the nervous system through which parties feel, misread, or ignore the world.

#### **When scale becomes a liability**

The first confession was simple: campaigns have misused the tools they already have. Wells, who leads business development at NationBuilder, admitted that his own company’s products can be “actually quite damaging” when used only for “reach and scale and broadcast” rather than for “deeper engagement building” and “sustained engagement.” It is a quiet indictment of the sector’s favourite metric: the big list.

NationBuilder’s software makes it trivial to blast petitions, emails and fundraising asks to thousands of contacts; done well, this can be a gateway into community‑building. But when campaigns treat those broadcast tools as “the end goal” rather than “a means to get to the end goal” of durable relationships, they burn through attention, trust and time. The result: impressive dashboards, anaemic movements.

Leah, who sits on the investment side of progressive political tech, coined the phrase that haunted the rest of the conversation: “shallow use.” The problem, she argued, is not a shortage of tools but a shortage of strategic clarity and “change management support” to help organisations integrate them. There are “a lot of tools out there,” she noted, but “more training, more adoption, and more change management support is required to truly adopt those tools well.” Tech is “power” only if organisations understand it as infrastructure, not as a shortcut.

Flo put it more bluntly: the biggest failure is when technology does not “have a seat at the table, alongside the comms director.” When digital remains an afterthought, or “the unit at the end of the corridor,” the temptation to treat it as a magic system is overwhelming, and almost always disappointing.

#### **2026: invalidated elections, authoritarian drift, and a hunger for humanisation**

If the recent past has been defined by shallow use, the near future may be defined by structural shocks. Asked to “tease something juicy” about 2026, the panel did not reach for incremental predictions. Flo warned that “several elections in Europe could be invalidated or cancelled by authorities,” hinting at both legal and legitimacy crises as campaigns cross new lines in their use of data, content and AI. Leah predicted “the rise of authoritarianism around the world and in the United States,” and said she was “very happy to be here” in Europe because “there’s so much that the Americans can learn… in terms of managing, responding to, and dealing with authoritarianism.”

Oliver took a different tack, forecasting a “resurgence of humanisation”, a renewed “people’s need for that, generally in day‑to‑day life, but \[also\] for that to be facilitated by tech.” Whether that fully materialises in 2026 or later, he suggested, depends on whether tools are designed to foreground human connection or to automate it away.

Taken together, their predictions paint a stark picture: a continent where electoral law, authoritarian pressure and civic expectations are all shifting at once, and where campaigns will be judged not just on what they win, but on how they win it.

#### **When campaigns miss the “moment”, and the human**

Nothing illustrated the gap between tech promise and lived reality more vividly than the audience member who described joining a party, signing up as a member and a volunteer, and “still not heard anything” months later. The panel was unsurprised; their own research had already found that “membership isn’t invested in long term as much as just driving it as an initial metric.”

Rebecca, from the Better Politics Foundation, added a personal example. She joined a party and was impressed by the frictionless online signup and swift local email; then the process hit an analogue wall. To participate meaningfully, she was told she needed to attend a physical meeting “at a weird time on a weekday.” The tech had been optimised, but the pathway, the human journey, had not.

Her recommendation was deceptively simple: parties should “map all of the kind of friction points that new members are coming across, maybe not even new, throughout a member’s entire journey.” When two politically engaged people can describe similar experiences of being ignored, one through silence, one through inaccessible participation, the issue is not recruitment but retention.

Oliver’s answer was even more concrete. Membership, he argued, is a “very high‑value thing to have done,” an act of identity that demands a “high‑value response.” The minimum viable response is a one‑to‑one human conversation that asks, “what is your motivation for joining?”, not via a survey, but in “a way that is not just scripted.” In the European elections he worked on, personally calling a 60‑year‑old woman who had just joined turned a passive supporter into a super‑donor and recruiter; she attended every meeting, donated €250 every time an email went out, and called others herself. At one point he rang her to say, “you don’t need to give money every time I send you an email,” because her peer‑to‑peer work was already invaluable.

It is a small story with large implications: the most effective “technology” in campaigns remains a phone call, but only if the organisation knows how to route, empower and follow up on it.

#### **Membership, without the fetish**

The conversation repeatedly revolved around “membership,” but the panel resisted the temptation to treat it as a sacred category. One NGO representative asked whether the insights apply when there is no formal membership, only lists of supporters, donors and volunteers. For Oliver, the answer was yes: the label matters less than the ability to let people “move between different roles and what they want to do in terms of leadership in the organisation.” Traditional parties with rigid legal definitions of membership often trap themselves in “siloed tracks,” unable to accommodate people who want to contribute in unconventional ways.

In the Better Politics Foundation’s Political Leadership Entrepreneurial Network, by contrast, “you’re a member if you want to be.” There are no fees; the currency is participation. Leah suggested a broader reframing: in modern movements, “anyone who chooses to be a leader within your movement and act on your behalf is this revamped version of what would have been previously called a member.”

The bigger challenge, however, lies ahead. Parties across Europe have ageing memberships, donor bases and volunteer corps; as one participant put it, demographic change is “going to steamroll a lot of organisations if they don’t really change what they do over the next 10, 20 years.” Rebecca observed that parties are “paying lip service” to the need for younger, more diverse leaders, but often balk when presented with real outsiders: they want new faces, “but we also want them to be loyal.”

Her warning was stark: parties that cannot resolve this contradiction “will have no choice but to change, or they will die out.” She pointed to her native Australia, where one of the two dominant parties of the last 80 years is now polling behind a party to its right that plans to run candidates in every seat, many of them not long‑term party loyalists but nurses, factory workers, or “some random guy who just is well‑known in the community.” Voters, she argued, will increasingly pick people who “know what the issues are in their community, and they’re going to frame them with their worldview,” regardless of whether they fit old membership archetypes.

#### **Young people, new fronts doors, and wreckers**

What, then, should parties actually *do* about younger people? The answers were pragmatic, not utopian. Rebecca argued that parties should obsess less over whether young supporters “love the organisation” and more over whether they “love one issue” enough to get involved. Parties can create “new front doors”, issue‑based campaigns, local projects, digital communities, that allow people to enter without having to embrace the entire brand on day one. Over time, as their lives and values change, the organisation’s “offer” must evolve with them.

At the same time, she cautioned against naive openness. Allowing “people to come into political parties and completely ruin them,” she noted, is not a hypothetical risk but a recent memory in several countries. The hard conceptual work lies in helping parties distinguish between those who are “not totally in love with everything of the organisation but are an ultimate good” and those with the potential to “come in and be a wrecker.” That is not a question technology can answer; it goes to culture, candidate selection and internal democracy.

#### **AI, vanity metrics and post‑literate politics**

Underpinning many of these anxieties is the fear of a “post‑literate politics world” where short‑form video and AI‑generated content displace the hard work of thinking. One audience question worried about staff who “throw garbage” into AI tools and then present the output as finished work; the leader has now ordered team members to write longhand on paper or whiteboard before touching generative tools. The danger, he suggested, is that AI becomes a “silver bullet” that accelerates the drift away from “critical thinking, systems thinking, deep thinking, first principles.”

The panel did not deny the risk. Leah urged movements to “stop confusing output with outcomes,” warning that impressive content does not automatically translate into power. That is particularly true, she noted, for “vanity metrics”, petition signatures, clicks, viral posts, that are easy to track and easy to use in grant reports, but have “very, very minimal” impact on hard outcomes like turnout.

Flo’s answer, and her confession, pointed in a different direction. The campaigns that impressed her most, she said, were those that started by asking members what they actually wanted, rather than imposing a tech platform from above. In one network, people simply asked for “a WhatsApp group,” which became a space for political, personal and even religious greetings, and did more to sustain connection than any learning management system. Elsewhere, email, that oldest of tools, remains “how folks are engaged with by over 90%,” even as organisations experiment with more exotic channels.

If AI is to avoid becoming another layer of noise, it will have to be inserted into these *existing* human structures, not imposed against them.

#### **Confession as a roadmap**

For all its apparent pessimism, the panel ended on a practical note. Asked what they would recommend to organisations that have already “missed the moment” with new members, that have left supporters waiting months without contact, the answers were iterative, not heroic. Start with an apology. Map the journey. Make one phone call. Give one person ownership of the relationship. Invest in a peer‑to‑peer culture where “people know us by name” and treat lunch with staff as a core part of the offer, not a fringe benefit.

The core lesson of these confessions is that the most urgent political tech problem of 2026 is not a lack of tools, but a lack of courage to redesign institutions around them. If campaigns continue to treat tech as a way to “look modern” while clinging to 20th‑century internal cultures, they will keep burning through members, staff and voters in equal measure. But if they absorb the hard‑won lessons from these leaders, about shallow use, humanisation, and the real cost of ignoring the people who sign up, they might just be able to turn scepticism into something more ambitious: a politics whose infrastructure finally matches its rhetoric.
