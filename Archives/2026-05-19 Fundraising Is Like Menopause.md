**We're Experimenting on Ourselves Because No One Built the Science**

I'm on a group text with my high school friends right now and it's basically a clinical trial.

One friend's on bioidentical hormones. Another's doing testosterone patches. Someone's swearing by magnesium glycinate before bed. A few are gutting it out raw because their doctor said "you're fine" while they couldn't sleep for six weeks straight.

Every few days another screenshot drops in the chat — a TikTok doctor, a new study, a supplement someone's cousin tried. We're 46-year-old women experimenting on ourselves and trading notes like 18th-century alchemists, because the medical system never bothered to study us.

Half of the population goes through this. *Half.* And the best science we have is patchy, contradictory, and mostly came out in the last ten years because someone finally said hey, maybe we should ask the women.

So we DIY it. We crowdsource. We try things. We compare notes at brunch. We get one shot at our 40s and 50s and we're just… winging it.

And every time I read those texts I think: this is exactly what fundraising looks like.

### The parallel is uncomfortable

Fundraisers are out here experimenting on themselves and swapping advice in DMs.

One ED is testing a new donor portal. Another is trying the "raise your hand" email someone posted on LinkedIn. Someone's copying a year-end campaign template a peer sent over. A DoD heard about a webinar that promised "the framework" and signed up at midnight. A board member forwarded an article. A consultant said do this. A funder said do that. The Chronicle dropped a new report. Someone tried ChatGPT.

We're all running our own experiments on our own donor files with no control group, no peer-reviewed protocol, and no one ever published the results.

And then we wonder why the field hasn't moved.

### Same root cause

Women's health got under-studied because the people designing the studies didn't think it mattered enough. Fundraising hasn't been studied properly because nonprofits are seen as the soft science, the warm-fuzzy sector, the place where you make do.

There are exceptions. [Russell James, J.D., Ph.D., CFP®](https://www.linkedin.com/in/encouragegenerosity/) has spent decades doing actual neuroscience on donor behavior. [Gregory Warner](https://www.linkedin.com/in/gregmarketsmart/) is running real experiments on what works. A handful of researchers have built the beginnings of a real evidence base.

But most of what passes for fundraising "best practice" is folklore. Someone's anecdote becomes a conference keynote becomes a $2,000 course becomes the thing your board chair sends you at 10pm on a Sunday.

The math maths the same way every time: we're guessing. With other people's money. While the work that funds cancer research and food pantries and after-school programs sits underfunded because we never built the infrastructure to fund it properly.

### What I want instead

I want fundraising to get the same upgrade women's health is finally getting. Not perfect — just serious.

I want studies. I want real data on what donor cultivation moves the needle. I want to know which signals predict a major gift and which ones are noise. I want to stop pretending that the woman who raised $50M for one cause in one city in one economy has The Answer for every shop in every sector.

I want a scorecard. I want a system. I want to stop flying blind.

Until that exists at scale, I'm doing what my friends and I do in the group chat: comparing notes with people I trust, testing things on my own file, writing down what worked and what didn't, and refusing to take "that's just how it is" as an answer.

We are not un-sophisticated. We're un-infrastructured.

Same as our perimenopause. Same as our sector.

Let's fix it.
