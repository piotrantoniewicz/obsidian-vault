In 1936, the American political scientist Harold Lasswell wrote that politics is “who gets what, when, and how.” This distributional understanding of politics broadens our field of view away from just political parties, elections, and the theatre of Parliament. What politics actually is, is a more fundamental question: how are we going to divide resources, power and opportunity in our society - and who’s making those decisions?

**By this definition, every advocacy organisation, and arguably every service delivery non-profit, is ‘doing politics’ all the time, whether they acknowledge it or not.**

Being real for a minute: NGO and advocacy world has, to a large extent, de-politicised itself over the last 20 years. Routinely I see leaders deliberately softening their public language around the structural causes of the problems they are trying to solve. As if problems like homelessness, food insecurity, or the climate crisis were just magically put on earth as some sort of divine trial to test the ingenuity of the third sector. These challenges reflect choices, made by people with power, that served particular interests. Pretending otherwise, or refusing to say so clearly, doesn't make you more effective. It makes it less honest, and eventually, less trusted by your community.

When you avoid commentary on tax, revenue, and all the trade-offs public policy involves, you are accepting the decision makers frame that the structures we have are unable to be questioned. Even if those systems produce the problems you’re working to undo. This works to keep governments, particularly (but not exclusively) conservative ones on board.

I get the instinct. By framing everything in the most palatable terms possible, you won’t make any enemies right? Besides, funding relationships are delicate. Boards are cautious. Being seen as partisan can cost you access. And so the slow retreat from politics begins, presented internally as ‘careful strategy’ and externally as ‘balance’.

I think this is a massive mistake, and we are just beginning to see the consequences play out in the sector.

In "In Defence of Politics" Bernard Crick argued that the conflict between interests is inevitable in any industrialised society.

> Politics isn't a dirty word or a partisan activity. It's the mechanism by which free societies manage that internal conflict without resorting to coercion or force. Opting out of politics doesn't make the conflict go away. It just means someone else manages it, on terms you had no part in shaping.

For NGO and advocacy leaders, the decision to depoliticise is itself a political act. Staying quiet about structural causes while trying to address their symptoms is taking a position. Refusing to comment on the revenue settings that constrain public investment in the things you care about is taking a position. Avoiding any language that might make a government minister uncomfortable is taking a position. These choices have consequences for the communities we serve.

The gas export tax debate playing out right now in Australia is a good example. Australia is one of the world's largest gas exporters. For years, the taxation arrangements on those exports have meant the public receives a fraction of what comparable resource-rich countries capture from similar industries. That revenue could have funded hospitals, housing, climate investment, social services. The connection between resource taxation and public capacity to invest in the things the that change-makers say they care about is clear.

More than fifty community organizations, including ACOSS and Oxfam Australia, made that argument explicitly to a Senate inquiry this year. They named the system, the trade-offs, and said plainly who bears the cost of the status quo. This is leadership from the sector and it builds trust with communities.

> The organizations that stayed quiet on this debate didn't protect themselves from politics. They just opted out in hopes of a pat on the head from power.

I am not suggesting that all non-profits need to start getting involved in elections (in fact, that’s almost always the worst time to engage in politics). What I am saying is develop a damn worldview. Be clear about it internally. And make deliberate decisions about when and how to express it publicly.

The leaders I've most respected in this sector over the years have all shared one quality. They had a genuine political worldview, grounded in their experience and their values, and they knew how to use it strategically.
