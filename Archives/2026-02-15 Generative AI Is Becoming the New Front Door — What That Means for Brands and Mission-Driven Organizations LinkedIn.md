Last week I attended a conference sponsored by [Meltwater](https://www.linkedin.com/company/meltwater/) focused on how generative AI and large language models (LLMs) are reshaping brand reputation, visibility, and trust. I walked away convinced that we’re at a meaningful inflection point — not just for SEO or marketing tactics, but for how organizations like [Save the Children US](https://www.linkedin.com/company/save-the-children-us/) show up in the world.

The biggest takeaway for me was simple but profound: **Generative AI is becoming the new front door.**

For a growing number of people, AI‑generated summaries are now the first — and sometimes only — exposure they have to a brand, organization, or cause. That shift has wide‑ranging implications, especially for organizations whose work depends on trust, credibility, and public understanding.

### From Search Rankings to AI Narratives

For years, digital strategy has largely been oriented around search rankings and traffic. But LLMs change the dynamic. Instead of presenting a list of links, AI presents *answers* — synthesized narratives that blend information from many sources and deliver it with confidence.

That means two things:

1. **Visibility is no longer guaranteed by ranking alone.**
2. **How your organization is described — tone, framing, and context — matters as much as whether you’re mentioned at all.**

AI doesn’t just surface facts. It interprets them. And people tend to trust those interpretations.

### Why This Matters Even More for Mission‑Driven Organizations

For nonprofits and humanitarian organizations, this shift is especially important.

When someone asks an AI about a crisis, a charity, or how donations are used, the response they receive may shape:

- Whether they trust an organization
- Whether they decide to give
- How they understand complex or sensitive issues

If that answer is outdated, incomplete, or subtly misframed, it can influence perception at scale — even if no one ever clicks through to a website.

This puts a premium on **accuracy, clarity, and consistency across all the places AI systems draw from**.

### The Rise of Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)

One concept that resonated with me is the idea of **Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)** augmenting traditional **Search Engine Optimization (SEO)** — organizations and brands need to optimize not just for search engines, but for how AI systems understand and describe you.

At a high level, this comes down to three ideas:

### 1\. Be the Source

Organizations need strong, authoritative owned content that clearly explains who they are, what they do, and why it matters — written to answer real questions people ask, not just to rank.

### 2\. Be in the Source

Earned media, partnerships, and third‑party mentions matter more than ever. These aren’t just awareness drivers — they become source material for AI.

### 3\. Shape the Narrative

Community spaces, forums, research, and public‑facing explanations all influence the broader information environment AI draws from. In some cases, these spaces can matter just as much as traditional media.

What stood out to me is that **this work doesn’t belong to one team**. Brand, SEO, communications, and PR are all shaping the same inputs — whether intentionally or not.

### Monitoring AI as a Reputation Channel

Another major shift: AI itself is becoming a reputation channel.

Even though organizations can’t “respond” inside an LLM the way they can on social media, AI systems are constantly summarizing brands and causes on behalf of millions of users. That makes it increasingly important to understand:

- How an organization is being described
- Which sources AI is relying on
- Whether sentiment and framing align with reality

The goal isn’t perfection — it’s awareness and trend‑spotting, so inaccuracies or gaps don’t persist unnoticed.

Meltwater showed off their new [GenAI Lens](https://www.meltwater.com/en/products/genai-lens) product -- it looked like a really indispensable new tool that brands should have in their toolbox to adapt and stay ahead of this new reality.

### What I’m Taking Back With Me

For me, the conference reinforced that this isn’t about chasing the latest tech trend. It’s about recognizing that **the information environment has changed**.

AI is now an interpreter between organizations and the public. And like any interpreter, it’s only as good as the source material it’s given.

That means:

- Clear, factual, well‑maintained content matters more than ever
- Earned credibility compounds over time
- Narrative gaps don’t stay empty — something else fills them

For organizations that care deeply about trust and impact, adapting to this shift isn’t optional. It’s part of stewardship.

I’m still processing what this means tactically, but strategically the direction feels clear: **if we don’t actively shape how our work is understood by AI, we’re leaving that understanding to chance.**
