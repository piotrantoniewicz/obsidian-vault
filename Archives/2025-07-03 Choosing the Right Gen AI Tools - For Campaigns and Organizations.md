*Contributor: Ned Howey*

As Generative AI tools continue to flood the market, evaluating which tools are truly fit for purpose is becoming a critical task—especially in campaigning and advocacy, where technology shapes not only internal workflows but also the integrity of democratic participation. At Tectonica, we’ve been hearing a lot of questions about how to pick the right tools. We drafted this to hopefully help our sector get started.

> An important note. We strongly believe that adopting Generative AI is less about picking the perfect tool—and more about *learning the right ways to use the tools*. Even more so than with past technological adoption. (Much more coming soon on the former in future writing and new services - [get it touch if you are curious to know more](https://www.tectonica.co/hire_us)).

There are certainly no shortage of new tools to pick from (for a list of ‘top’ 125 general AI tools [check this out from Superhuman AI](https://airtable.com/apppaprA2GXXxBSjs/shrjsPiubnIHIqTwH/tblCm6yevfjitvoCh/viwKzKHKKqd5d5bk8)).

Of course, to even get started you need to be using *some tool.* And there are so many things to choose, it can feel overwhelming for people just diving in. All these terms, all these choices—it can feel like a labyrinth of decisions: You’re choosing between general Gen AI tools ([ChatGPT](https://chatgpt.com/), [Claude](https://claude.ai/), etc), and domain specific tools (like presentation creation, ad creation, or fundraising writing tools). While we tend to overuse the term, [not everything Gen AI is an LLM](https://medium.com/mr-plan-publication/not-everything-is-an-llm-8-ai-model-types-you-need-to-know-in-2025-6fb026bcdc82). Even within the big LLMS, you have to choose function type for your workflow: choosing between [general chat LLMs](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/which-ai-to-use-now-an-updated-opinionated), [research](https://www.zdnet.com/article/i-tested-chatgpts-deep-research-against-gemini-perplexity-and-grok-ai-to-see-which-is-best/) tools, [different models](https://platform.openai.com/docs/models) within platforms, [GPTs](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/8554397-creating-a-gpt) and [agents](https://generativeai.pub/i-tried-12-ai-agent-frameworks-in-2025-heres-the-brutally-honest-guide-you-actually-need-d68dbf6ed2ad), multimodal (like those combining text and image) versus task-specific generators, RAGs, etc. etc. It's a lot.

To make things even more confusing, AI is so hyped right now that a lot of tools claiming to be AI solutions are really just older software platforms that have incorporated AI into them (often poorly). A lot of domain specific ones are not that great, but were developed riding the boom of AI excitement driven by investors and have yet to see if they are fit for purpose once the seed cash runs out. (That said, [there are a number emerging in the progressive space that have key differentiators for political use](https://highergroundlabs.com/ai-landscape-report/)). Most domain specific written content tools are just pulling from the APIs of larger LLMs (makes sense as LLMs themselves are wildly expensive to develop well) and you can get the same output just by knowing how to work a general LLM without a recurring fee.

(It’s strongly recommended not to consider building your own GenAI tool for now - except in very specific cases. [It’s almost certainly not needed and not worth it.](https://ask.krista.ai/hubfs/Website%20Papers/white%20papers/Illusion%20of%20Simplicity%20of%20Building%20Your%20Own%20GenAI%20Systems%20-%20Krista%20Software.pdf))

If you are really lost and overwhelmed, take a deep breath. I’ve got good news: **Generative AI is for you, no matter your current knowledge level**. And it's easier to get started than you think: **I’d suggest just signing up for a pro account on Claude or ChatGPT or similar, and starting to play around to learn the basics of the logics and possibilities of Generative AI.** (I use both for different purposes and they each have their advantages). Still unsure which general system to start with? Ethan Mollick put out a great overall guide on [picking a general purpose AI.](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/which-ai-to-use-now-an-updated-opinionated) (It was published in January though, so even a lot has changed since then.)

> Many in our progressive space are worried about using AI at all - which is an entirely valid question. This article doesn’t dive deep into that and its *very valid* concerns about systemic impacts on the environment, wealth inequality, etc. The reality is, even if your campaign, party or org doesn’t have an official adoption plan, likely members of your team are using these tools - whether or not it's being discussed or following an intentional approach. Just ignoring AI isn’t going to stop it no more than the deer will stop the car by staring at it. And while the sector of those working in politics and advocacy will have a very large systemic impact on our democracies in *the way they apply* AI technologies, they are less likely to have much of an impact on these other systemic harms by relegating their use (considering the size of the sector compared with commercial and private use). For all of this, we recommend intentional adoption, and an organization-specific theory of change.

The potential application of Gen AI is so broad ([beyond just content creation - which is no longer the top use](https://hbr.org/2025/04/how-people-are-really-using-gen-ai-in-2025)), and the systems are developing and changing so rapidly, it’s difficult to make specific recommendations in a general article without knowing your organization’s context. Rather I’m offering here some general ideas and questions to consider when selecting any tool (or a number of them).

There is already some good advice out there to get us started. All the way back in November 2023 (an eternity in AI age years), [Forbes put out a good set of questions for businesses to ask themselves](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbestechcouncil/2023/11/08/five-questions-to-consider-when-evaluating-an-ai-solution/) when evaluating an AI solution - and these still are deeply relevant, and relevant to organizations and political parties as well:

1\. How does the solution manage privacy and security?  
2\. Has the solution been evaluated for bias?  
3\. Is the solution equipped to address regulatory compliance?  
4\. Is the solution designed to improve over time?  
5\. What level of implementation support will be required?

While not specifically named - a lot of those have even more relevance to concern and consideration in political work. Below I share three additional questions I believe should be asked for those organizations working in campaigns and advocacy, political parties, unions, etc - anyone whose work directly impact civics and democracy.

---

**6\. Does the AI tool solve the problem you are trying to solve?**

At Tectonica, we often remind clients: “ **tech follows methods**.” It’s easy to get dazzled by flashy features—especially when AI tools are often built more for marketing appeal than for practical use. But the real question isn’t *can* a tool solve *a* problem—it’s whether it solves the **right** problem, and whether that problem is a **priority** in achieving a larger goal.

At the **macro level**, AI tools should align with an organization’s [**theory of change**](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/theory_of_change). Are we trying to deepen participation? Build leadership? Shift public narratives? Tools should be judged on how well they support those strategic aims. Don’t get too excited by the shiny new thing. Look for the thing that will make your work easier and impact more powerful.

At the **micro level**, we evaluate AI tools through our **Tech Stack Assessment service**, where we identify **technical and strategic requirements** —things like integration capacity, data privacy, and functional needs tied to actual workflows. We use practices like user stories to define what success looks like before we assess tools.

There’s a growing market of AI products, but most are **not fit for purpose** —they’re rushed to market, driven by hype, and full of **fluff**. Even promising tools, like Fireflies for note-taking, we find often fail because they lack the **control and specificity** required for campaign work context. We know from [HGL’s AI Landscape Report](https://highergroundlabs.com/ai-landscape-report/), that many campaigns and organizations are using AI reactively, driven more by external pressure than internal strategy, often without clear goals, policies, or training frameworks in place. On the flip side, experimentation is a good place to start and get buy-in. But more structure is needed to effectively select and utilize the tools.

---

**7\. How does it ‘play’ with other required tools?**

AI tools don’t exist in a vacuum—they need to **integrate into broader ecosystems** of technology. Especially in campaigning, workflows rarely live within a single platform. Tasks often span across tools for outreach, data, messaging, and compliance, all of which must communicate effectively with one another.

At Tectonica, we’ve seen this play out repeatedly in our Tech Stack Assessment work. Even when an AI tool looks strong on its own, if it **can’t connect** with core systems like [CRMs](https://www.tectonica.co/guide/crms-for-people-based-change), [CMS platforms](https://www.tectonica.co/guide/websites-for-people-based-change), or political data tools, its value drops sharply. This is especially critical in political work, where **specialized tools** —voter file databases, targeting platforms, engagement software—are often central to how campaigns function. Many of the AI systems won’t consider our essential technologies and the need to integrate with tools that function in the core of our unique operations.

Product ecosystems matter here. Tools designed with open integration in mind tend to perform better over time, while platforms that push closed, proprietary systems—like Microsoft often does—can end up isolating the campaign’s data and functionality - forcing a choice between limiting the needed flexibility by staying within the closed tools or decentralizing and disconnecting workflows. An AI tool might be impressive on paper, but if it creates silos, it creates friction—and that undermines its strategic value.

---

**8\. Is it ethical to use in a civic / political context?**

For political and advocacy organizations, evaluating AI tools can’t stop at performance—we also have to ask whether their use is **ethically justifiable in context**. While norms are beginning to form (most now agree that AI-generated deepfakes should be off-limits), we’re still operating in a vast gray zone. That’s dangerous terrain for campaigns that depend on public trust.

At Tectonica, we’ve argued that the ethical bar in politics needs to be **higher** than in other sectors, because campaign work is fundamentally about democracy. That means we don’t just ask whether a human is in the loop—we ask **where** that human is, and what role they’re playing.  
  
While the competition argument will always exist (“the other side will win if we don’t do the same”), progressives lose when democracy loses so guarding against the systemic impact of our use as a community is necessary if we want to protect against not just losing the campaign, but losing our cause. Harmful systemic impact is not about drawing red lines for back actions that clearly go too far, but recognizing the gradient externality of harm in different degrees of harmful use.

AI might be able to generate messaging, but if it replaces **authentic direction, vision and voice** —of a candidate, supporter, or constituent—it can erode the civic function and relationships we’re trying to build. As outlined in our article, [*The Democratic Dilemma of the AI Age*](https://www.tectonica.co/posts/ai_ethics), there are parts of democratic participation that **should not be outsourced to predictive algorithms**, even if the tech allows it.  
  
A good litmus test for this is to question if the tool preserves and amplifies human voice or replaces and removes it. Does it reflect the real human experience, voice, and values of the individuals or teams using the tool? Does AI expand human connection - or does it flatten it into generic emotionally and strategically devoid content? This wave of new technology is different from the previous. The past was about automation and replacement. The real value of the AI revolution is augmentation and collaboration - yet many are still working under the old paradigm and causing harm by doing so. And this is particularly dangerous in the context of use in democratic processes such as campaign work. While this is much about how we use these tools, if the tool you are selecting is designed to limit this, reject it out the door, no matter how much ‘efficiency’ it claims.

Ethics in this context isn’t abstract. It’s about protecting the legitimacy of campaigns, safeguarding representation, and ensuring that tools empower rather than replace people. Democracy is something we do, not something we have. And practice - where the people participate in its process - is important to safeguard.

Evaluating AI tools isn’t just about exploring innovation—it’s about protecting the integrity of strategy, ecosystems, and democratic values. As we deepen our assessments at Tectonica, we’re committed to making sure that any technology we recommend not only works, but works **in service of human goals for democracy**, not at their expense.
