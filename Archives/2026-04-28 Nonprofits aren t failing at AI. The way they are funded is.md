The AI funding gap in the nonprofit sector is not what most people think it is.

Over two-thirds of nonprofits experienced funding cuts in 2025, [while demand for their services continued to rise.](https://cep.org/report-backpacks/a-sector-in-crisis-how-u-s-nonprofits-and-foundations-are-responding-to-threats/?section=intro#intro) In that context, where the remaining funding goes is not a detail. It determines whether artificial intelligence becomes a real operational advantage for under-funded and under-resourced teams or another stalled experiment.  
The problem is not access to technology alone but the supporting ecosystem around it.

Nonprofits can already experiment with a chatbot or build a pilot or a proof of concept on their own. What is harder is building the organizational infrastructure that determines whether any of that actually works at scale. This includes data governance. Staff capacity during implementation. Governance frameworks built before the first model trains rather than after the first mistake. And these require investments that separate a funded AI project from a working AI tool. And they rarely appear in anyone’s grant budget.We know it because we made versions of this mistake and learned from it ourselves at Tech To The Rescue, which connects software agencies with NGOs to build custom tech solutions. Early on, we optimized for matching speed. We introduced organizations to tech partners quickly, assuming that access to tools and tech talent would work almost instantly. What we underestimated was how much groundwork an organization needs to do before a tech partnership actually works. Optimizing for that took time.

## The value of organizational readiness for AI adoption

When organizations described what stalled their pilots, the answer was rarely the technology. It was organizational and technical groundwork that nobody had budgeted for, dropped onto understaffed and underfunded teams. The technology had arrived. The organizational readiness had not.

So we redesigned the approach entirely to be ecosystem-driven, taking organizations through intensive AI literacy bootcamps, strategy development, mentorship, and peer learning. All of it leads to implementation through a match with a vetted pro bono tech company to build something together. And the building is what actually drives responsible AI adoption.

What stalls these projects is not the technology. It is the last mile: where technical capability has to meet organizational design, data has to be clean enough to use, and staff have to absorb a new way of working while still running their programs. Large enterprises with dedicated AI teams and infrastructure budgets struggle with this. Nonprofits navigate the same challenges with a fraction of the resources and almost no funding specifically for it.

## The development sector is working hard for AI adoption

Even with those obstacles, the sector is further ahead than most funders realize. Sixty-seven percent of the organizations we work with are applying a Responsible AI Framework to their solutions.

Many of them had responsible practices long before they touched AI — because when you work with refugees, patients, or victims of domestic violence, a bad decision does not just affect a user metric, it affects a life. NGOs bring that understanding into how they build out their AI usage.

Nearly all of the organizations that submitted baseline data ahead of being matched rated their understanding of AI at near zero. Two years on, 76% had direct leadership involvement in their AI journey, with the CEO or director in the room, and over 60% are already planning to expand AI into other areas of their work. That is not a sector experimenting at the margins. That is organizational transformation in progress, with a sector working seriously on AI adoption, largely without the funding infrastructure to match.

[Fast Forward](https://www.devex.com/organizations/fast-forward-31241) ’s “The 2025 AI for Humanity Report” found that [84%](https://www.ffwd.org/2025-ai-for-humanity-report) of AI-powered nonprofits say additional funding is their critical need for scaling. Our data points in the same direction, but with more specificity about where the gap sits. The current funding logic covers tool acquisition: software grants, cloud credits, and tech partnerships. What it seldom covers is the organizational groundwork that makes those tools usable.Part of the reason is structural. A 2025 report by the Center for Effective Philanthropy found that [84% to 87%](https://cep.org/report-backpacks/ai-with-purpose-how-foundations-and-nonprofits-are-thinking-about-and-using-artificial-intelligence/?section=intro) of foundation staff have limited to no understanding of their grantees’ AI needs or technical capacity, and fewer than 20% of nonprofits report their funders have ever engaged them in conversations about AI at all. Foundations are not withholding support out of indifference. Many simply do not know what their grantees need — because nobody has asked.

## Changing funding models for efficient AI adoption

Nonprofits are also sitting on something the sector is only now beginning to value properly: decades of field notes, case records, community relationships, and hard-won knowledge of what actually works and for whom. The problem is that most of it is still not in a form you can build on. Getting it there is unglamorous work, and it rarely makes it into grant proposals.

Consider [Justdiggit](https://justdiggit.org/), which has spent over a decade supporting farmers in Tanzania with land restoration techniques aimed at increasing resilience to climate change. When they came to us to build an AI tutor inside their app, Kijani, the technology was not the only hard part. What they needed was time and space to figure out what actually works before committing to a direction. Most grant structures do not budget for the months of trial, adjustment, and organizational learning that sit between a funded project and a working solution. As Justdiggit’s Carl Lens put it, “we have tried some things that we might otherwise not have done,” and around 1,500 farmers in Tanzania are already interacting with their AI-powered tools, before full integration is even complete.The clearer data will come when more solutions reach deployment. But what we can already see is that the sector builds too little compared to its ambitions and potential. What is already clear is that the organizations making genuine progress have funders who understand that the distance between strategy and deployment is where the investment actually matters.

The fix is not complicated. Add a data-readiness line to existing AI grants so organizations can prepare before the tech partner arrives. Extend project timelines by at least one cycle, because the distance between planning and deployment is longer than most grant windows expect. Budget for staff time during implementation.

The funding infrastructure has not caught up with what the sector is doing. That is the gap worth closing.
