## Summary

Most nonprofit leaders complaining about "terrible" AI outputs aren't facing an AI problem, they're facing a garbage-in, garbage-out problem. While 60% of nonprofits sit on the AI sidelines and 20% resist entirely, early adopters are discovering something revolutionary: context engineering. This approach moves beyond crafting better prompts to creating AI systems that already know your mission, programs, and voice before you even start typing. Using the SCOPE Framework (Storage, Cleaning, Organization, Preparation, Engagement), nonprofits can transform AI from a demanding intern requiring constant supervision into a seasoned staff member who anticipates your needs. The result? Grant proposals that once took 20 hours now require 20 minutes, donor communications that maintain an authentic voice automatically, and strategic time freed up for relationship-building and innovation. Here's how to build AI systems that understand your nonprofit as deeply as your most experienced team member.

### How Context Engineering Could Cut Your Grant Writing Time from 20 Hours to 20 Minutes

Last Saturday's dinner party followed a predictable script. Between the appetizers and main course, someone inevitably launched into their latest AI rant: "I asked ChatGPT to write a fundraising email, and it was terrible! AI is so overhyped."

I've heard similar complaints, or ones just like it, dozens of times over the years. But here’s the truth. Nine times out of ten, the problem isn't AI. It's garbage in, garbage out. Most people craft prompts like they're texting a friend: vague, context-free, and wondering why the results disappoint.

Unfortunately, this scenario plays out daily in hundreds of thousands of nonprofits across the nation. In my previous organization, we had the classic 20/20/60 split: twenty percent of us (like myself) were early adopters seeing remarkable results, while twenty percent dug their heels in firmly because AI felt like an identity threat. The remaining 60 percent sat on the sidelines waiting to see which way the wind would blow.

For the most part, the difference didn’t have anything to do with talent or tech-savviness. The resisters saw AI's outputs and worried about replacement. The adopters understood that AI can amplify your work, *making you more valuable* *and not redundant*. Master the fundamentals of prompting, and you unlock transformational efficiency. Continue with poorly crafted requests, and you'll join the dinner party complainers.

But here's what's exciting: we're moving beyond even good prompting engineering toward something far more powerful—context engineering.

## The Prompting Foundation Still Matters

Before diving into defining and describing context engineering, let’s first address prompting best practices, before taking it to the next level. Fortunately, there is an exceptional new resource from a recent [**OpenAI**](https://www.linkedin.com/company/openai/) hackathon: the [**Nonprofit AI Cookbook**](https://jakeporway.github.io/prompt-cookbook/). This free library offers 365 nonprofit-focused prompt templates designed to help any and every organization generate better content faster.

Even seasoned AI users will find value here. I sure did. There are three levels of prompts—low, medium, and high degrees of complexity—covering nearly every imaginable need, including:

- **Create and Communicate**: Content creation, communications, and storytelling
- **Automate the Admin**: Repetitive task automation and administrative efficiency
- **Answer and Assist**: Question answering, support, and guidance systems
- **Sort and Scan**: Information categorization, evaluation, and prioritization
- **Learn and Decide**: Data analysis, research, and strategic decision-making

While I thought I was pretty skilled at prompting, I'm already seeing noticeable improvement in my outputs after just starting to explore these templates.

## From Prompting to Context-Aware Systems

While these prompts are powerful, they're still just requests. Every interaction begins with a limited memory window, forcing you to rebuild your organizational understanding within nearly every AI conversation. While ChatGPT has some advanced memory management tools that provide generalized persistent context, it is still quite limited. But what if your AI already knew your mission inside and out, understood your programs fully, and spoke in your authentic voice from the very first word until the very last?

My evolution toward exploring context engineering began with OpenAI's Custom GPTs this past year (followed by [Custom Instructions](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/unlocking-power-custom-instructions-generative-ai-how-gayle-k4x7c/) more recently). These tools allow you to upload up to twenty files that are persistently available within the chat, along with GPT-specific custom instructions that define your project's voice and approach. Although they have recently been upgraded to provide access to more powerful models, they still don't yet offer the full context integration I describe later in this essay. Still, Custom GPTs represent a crucial stepping stone; they've shown me how transformational even basic context awareness can be, setting the stage for exploring more comprehensive systems.

Think of traditional prompting like hiring a new college intern to work on your team. They are talented but need a lot of supervision and feedback. While leveraging Custom GPTs is like hiring individual talented freelancers for each project. They're highly skilled, but you must still brief them on your organization's background, voice, and goals every single time. However, context engineering is like having a seasoned staff member who's worked with your organization for years, understands your mission deeply, and anticipates your needs before you voice them. It's simply a game-changer.

This transformation is compelling for nonprofits because our mission-driven work requires a nuanced understanding of impact, authentic storytelling, and deep alignment with organizational values. These are precisely the elements that are often lost in most generic AI interactions.

## Why Nonprofits Need Context Engineering Now

The nonprofit sector faces unique AI challenges that make ultimately mastering context engineering essential rather than optional. Unlike businesses focused on products and profits, nonprofits must communicate complex social impact, navigate diverse stakeholder expectations, and maintain authentic alignment with their mission in every piece of content they create.

Consider grant writing. A successful foundation proposal requires understanding the funder's priorities, your organization's specific programs, past impact metrics, current challenges, and future goals. Without context engineering, you'll spend hours explaining these elements to AI for each proposal. With it, your AI instantly accesses your complete organizational knowledge and crafts compelling, accurate narratives that align with both your mission and the funder's interests.

The same principle applies to donor communications, program reports, volunteer recruitment, social media content, and board presentations. Each requires a deep organizational context that traditional prompting cannot efficiently provide.

## SCOPE Framework: Building on Proven Methods

My recommended context engineering best practices build upon my previous recommendations for nonprofit AI adoption: [**SMART.AI**](http://smart.ai/) for organizational readiness and [**SAFE 2.0**](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/safe-20-centering-power-justice-community-nonprofit-gayle-lkztc/) for centering power and justice.

I'm implementing this approach within my own personal workflow, but the real transformation happens at the organizational level. My **SCOPE** Framework represents the next evolution: a comprehensive system that transforms any organization's AI workflow from reactive prompting to proactive collaboration.

### S — Storage: Building Your Unified Knowledge Base

The foundation of context engineering is centralizing your organizational knowledge in a single, accessible location. This means moving beyond scattered files across email attachments, local hard drives, SharePoint folders, and the chaos of Google Drive.

Create a master repository that mirrors and unifies all essential organizational documents. Label each folder clearly with its tier level.

- **Tier 1: Authoritative Sources**: Current strategic plan, official program descriptions, latest impact data
- **Tier 2: Reference Materials**: Past grant proposals, board presentations, communications templates
- **Tier 3: Historical Context**: Archived strategies, old branding, superseded materials.

The key is accessibility across platforms. Your unified storage must integrate seamlessly with multiple AI tools: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and emerging platforms. This ensures consistent context regardless of which AI you're using for specific tasks. Google Workspace offers particularly smooth integration pathways, though each is slightly different, for each of these models.

### C — Cleaning: Eliminating Information Chaos

Raw organizational files contain years of accumulated clutter that confuses AI systems and dilutes your authentic organizational voice. The cleaning phase requires surgical precision in removing outdated information while preserving institutional knowledge.

Archive all outdated document versions, eliminate duplicate files, and remove draft materials that no longer represent your current programs or positions. Pay special attention to old grant proposals with outdated program descriptions, superseded strategic plans, and communications that reflect past branding or messaging.

This process often reveals gaps in organizational knowledge. You might discover that your most successful program lacks current documentation, or that your impact metrics exist only in scattered email updates. Cleaning isn't just about removing files; it's about identifying and filling critical knowledge holes that leave AI without essential context. This foundational stage can feel tedious, but you’ll thank me later when you see the improved AI outputs.

**When adding files to your AI dataset**, remove unnecessary text that does not contribute to your context quality. This includes title pages, forewords, tables of contents, indexes, glossaries, footnotes, page numbers, boilerplate disclaimers, and any other filler. These elements add noise and can reduce AI performance by distracting from the core content. Keep only material that will help AI understand your organization’s programs, metrics, voice, and mission.

### O — Organization: Creating Intuitive Structure

Effective organization goes beyond neat folder hierarchies. Your structure must enable both human users and AI systems to quickly locate relevant information and understand relationships between different organizational elements.

Implement consistent naming conventions that include dates, document types, and content themes to ensure clarity and organization. Create brief index files for each primary folder that explain contents and relationships to other organizational materials. Include a master relationships.md file that maps connections: "Program A serves the same population as Program B but focuses on prevention vs. intervention," or "Foundation X prefers our youth programs while Foundation Y funds our policy work." These explicit relationships help AI make intelligent connections between different organizational elements. These index files serve as roadmaps for AI systems, helping them understand how different documents connect to create your complete organizational story.

Consider organizing by function (programs, development, communications), time (annual folders with clear date ranges), and audience (board materials, funder communications, public content). The goal is intuitive navigation that enables AI to access relevant context for any request quickly and efficiently.

### P — Preparation: Optimizing for AI Understanding

AI systems process different file formats with varying degrees of success. The preparation phase involves converting your organized content into formats that maximize AI comprehension and minimize processing errors.

Convert PDFs to Word documents or plain text formats whenever possible. PDFs, especially those created from scanned documents, often contain formatting artifacts that confuse AI systems. Break large documents into smaller, focused files that address specific topics rather than comprehensive reports that cover multiple programs or periods.

Add descriptive headers, clear section breaks, and consistent formatting that help AI systems understand the document's structure and content hierarchy. Consider creating summary documents that distill complex reports into key points, metrics, and outcomes.

When adding files to your AI dataset, remove unnecessary text that does not contribute to your context quality. This includes title pages, forewords, tables of contents, indexes, glossaries, footnotes, page numbers, boilerplate disclaimers, and any other filler. These elements add noise and can reduce AI performance by distracting from the core content. Keep only material that will help AI understand your organization's programs, metrics, voice, and mission.

This phase also involves creating template documents that capture your organization's voice, style, and standard approaches to common communications. These templates serve as style guides, helping AI maintain consistency across all generated content.

Create voice calibration examples at different formality levels. Include samples of how your organization communicates in board presentations (formal, metrics-heavy), donor newsletters (warm, story-driven), social media (casual, engaging), and grant proposals (professional, impact-focused). Save these as voice-samples-formal.doc, voice-samples-casual.doc, etc.

Crucially, address data security and privacy concerns during the preparation process. Ensure sensitive donor information, client data, and confidential strategic materials are properly secured with appropriate access controls. Most AI platforms now offer enterprise-grade security features specifically designed for organizations handling sensitive data.

**To further optimize your system, turning it into a full-fledged** [**RAG**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrieval-augmented_generation) **(retrieval-augmented generation), add these three additional steps:**

1. **Chunking for Structure and Clarity**: Ask the AI, *“Rewrite this text with chunking for AI. Use clear headings, subheadings, and bullet points. Highlight key points and add three illustrative examples.”* This improves navigation and comprehension for both humans and AI.
2. **Triples Format**: Ask the AI, *“Rewrite this in triples: subject, verb, direct object. Include three examples.”* Triples force concise, explicit statements that are easier for AI to process and cross-reference.
3. **Structured Output Conversion**: After chunking and triples formatting, ask the AI to, *“Convert this to XML”* (or *Markdown*, depending on your needs). Structured formats create consistent, machine-readable files that integrate well into context systems.

When preparing prompts for these transformations, **explicitly state the target audience** —e.g., *“The target audience is program officers at mid-size family foundations”* —so the AI shapes tone, examples, and emphasis appropriately.

### E — Engagement: Connecting AI Platforms to Your Context

The final phase connects your prepared organizational knowledge to multiple AI platforms, creating a seamless workflow where context is automatically transferred to the tool you're using.

Each major AI platform—ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini—offers different integration capabilities. Some allow direct file uploads, others work better with cloud storage connections, and emerging platforms provide increasingly sophisticated knowledge management features.

The goal is platform-agnostic access to your organizational context. Whether you're using ChatGPT for quick social media posts, Claude for complex grant narratives, or Gemini for data analysis, each tool should provide instant access to the same comprehensive understanding of your organization. Create platform-specific instruction sets: "When using ChatGPT for social content, prioritize voice-samples-casual.doc and recent-impact-stories.doc. When using Claude for grants, reference tier-1-programs.doc and funder-preferences.md."

Importantly, ensure that confidential information has restricted training access. Establish clear governance policies and access restrictions for who can read and modify your context system. Create training protocols for team members and measurement systems to track effectiveness and return on investment.

## Critical: Build Maintenance Into Your SCOPE Process

The most overlooked aspect of context engineering is systematic improvement. When AI generates content requiring significant editing, those edits reveal gaps in your context system, which need to be addressed.

Create a simple feedback protocol: If you spend more than 10 minutes editing AI output, document what information was missing or incorrect in your context files. Update your tier-1 documents monthly, archive outdated tier-2 materials quarterly, and conduct annual reviews of your relationship mappings.

Track performance metrics: time saved per content type, editing time required, and user satisfaction scores. This data helps you prioritize which context areas need the most attention.

## Real-World Transformation: Before and After SCOPE

The impact of context engineering becomes clear through specific examples. Let's trace the evolution of a hypothetical grant proposal creation across three stages:

**Stage 1 - Pre-AI Era:** Depending on the donor and the complexity of the request, writing a grant proposal could have taken anywhere from ten to forty hours. Research alone consumed hours as you gathered program data, impact metrics, organizational background, and funder priorities from scattered sources.

**Stage 2 - Basic AI with Good Prompting:** With solid prompt engineering skills, that timeframe dropped to roughly one-quarter of the original time. A proposal that once took twenty hours might now take five hours, but you still spent significant time explaining organizational context, correcting generic language, and ensuring accuracy.

**Stage 3 - Strong Context Engineering with Current Data:** With a comprehensive context system and integrated access to your organization's current knowledge base, proposal creation time is now measured in minutes rather than hours. **The same twenty-hour proposal from Stage 1 now may require just twenty minutes of focused work.**

Here's what that looks like practically: **Minute 1-2:** Upload funder guidelines and specify "Use tier-1-youth-programs.doc and foundation-x-preferences.md" **Minutes 3-7:** AI generates a complete first draft using your voice samples and relationship mappings. **Minutes 8-17:** Review for accuracy and funder alignment, minimal editing needed because AI already knows your voice and program details. **Minutes 18-20:** Final formatting and submission preparation. The AI immediately understands our programs, impact metrics, organizational voice, and strategic priorities without any explanatory prompting.

This efficiency gain multiplies across all organizational communications. Donor updates that previously required extensive prompting now generate automatically with accurate program details and authentic storytelling. Board reports that once demanded hour-long prompt sessions now emerge with complete context and appropriate messaging for governance audiences.

## Starting Your Context Engineering Journey (New to AI? Start Here)

If you're entirely new to AI, begin with basic prompting skills using the aforementioned Nonprofit AI Cookbook before implementing SCOPE. Explore the different outputs you get using level one, two, or three prompts. Customize or try writing your own prompts following these examples. Become proficient in prompt engineering before moving on to context engineering.

However, for those ready to advance, start with a pilot approach focused on your organization's most impactful communications. Identify the three types of content you create most frequently—perhaps grant proposals, donor updates, and program reports—and implement SCOPE for these specific use cases.

Start with Storage by creating a dedicated folder structure for these priority content types. Move to Cleaning by gathering all relevant historical documents and removing outdated or duplicate materials. Progress through Organization by establishing clear naming conventions and creating index files that explain content relationships.

The Preparation phase requires the most technical effort but delivers the highest impact. Focus first on converting your most important documents to AI-friendly formats, then gradually expand to your complete content library.

Preparation time varies significantly depending on your starting point. Individual practitioners might complete this phase in a few days, while teams could require several weeks, and agencies might need months, depending on the file volume and complexity. (Team and agency implementations also involve file permissions and security considerations that are beyond the scope of this essay, but critical for organizational success.)

Finally, implement Engagement by connecting your prepared content to your primary AI platform, then gradually expanding to additional tools.

Once your system is launched, expect a learning curve of perhaps four to six weeks for complete implementation, but with significant efficiency gains visible within the first two weeks. The initial time and financial investment pay dividends immediately and compound as your organizational knowledge base grows more sophisticated.

And remember the 20/20/60 adoption pattern. Your early adopters will champion the system, your resisters will need extra support and training, and your fence-sitters will convert once they see concrete results. Plan accordingly with change management strategies that address the needs and concerns of each group.
