It had been a decade since I last attended Skoll. Back then, there was so little focus on tech that it didn't seem like the right space for Fast Forward. Ten years later, that's changed — there's more tech, and a lot more curiosity and concern about AI and the future it portends.

I left with a lot to think about. Here’s what I’m taking with me. And what I left behind.

## What I’m taking with me: AI Readiness

Every funder is at a different stage in their AI journey. At the same party, I met the two archetypes in a single conversation. One told me AI scares her, so she's been “sticking her head in the sand” — she hasn't tried anything beyond searching for a cocktail party dress. The other had trained agents to scan her grants database, surfacing key insights across a 10-year portfolio and weaving them into her board docket — but hadn't told her boss, a proud luddite.

It’s clear that AI is surfacing gaps in funder infrastructure, not just grantee capacity. This moment calls for more than just money. It calls for philanthropists to upskill their knowledge of AI. We can't ask nonprofits to move fast, while philanthropic staff are still getting up to speed.

It’s time for philanthropy to invest in its own AI readiness. This is a true action item for funders. Get familiar with your organization’s AI policy. Test out one new AI tool per week. Share your findings with your colleagues. Take the first step.

(P.s. [Maggie Johnson](https://www.linkedin.com/in/maggie-johnson-9b1a97378/) and the [Google.org](http://google.org/) team just released a new [AI Readiness Playbook for Funders](https://www.google.org/ai-playbook-for-funders/). It’s a great place to start.)

## What I’m leaving behind: Aligning on “The What”

They are all looking at a blonde woman talking at the head up the table.](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D5612AQHl9X65qVsjSw/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/B56Z30AchnGsAQ-/0/1777915251357?e=1781136000&v=beta&t=cipCXdrWsaB6LoOh0bXv3YSh2CX5wyXTuLVuPavO8oM)

Put 20 philanthropists in a room. You'll get 20 silver bullets. 20 different and meaningful theories of change. Funders don’t need to agree on *what*. We need to agree on *how*.

I am on a mission to help funders integrate AI into existing strategies. That’s my how.

## What I’m taking with me: Fund Nonprofit Tech Capacity

The tension between big-picture questions and practical support is real. Organizations are clambering for support with AI credits, policy guidance, and product roadmapping all at once. Meanwhile, funders are still trying to determine their role in meeting these needs.

Here’s the tension: Many nonprofits are ready to build and use AI responsibly, but philanthropy’s funding models are lagging behind. Addressing this tension should be a top priority.

Nonprofits should have access to purpose-built technology, but they can only do what funders allow them to do with their budgets. Nonprofits need money for digital transformation. They also need permission from grantmakers to invest in tech as program strategy and not just administration. Funding and budget guidelines should match the support nonprofits actually need to be able to innovate.

If your foundation doesn’t have the resources, draw on others with expertise.

## What I’m leaving behind: Fear of the Hard

Last week, I heard one question from funders over and over and over. What am I going to do when I start getting 10x AI-generated grantee applications? How will I handle the volume?

This question needs reframing. We shouldn’t fear how AI usage will impact the day jobs of grantmakers. We should think of it as an opportunity for increased equity in grantmaking. Historically, writing quality has been a proxy for impact. That has put many proximate founders, especially those outside of the United States, at a disadvantage for grant opportunities. AI is already changing that.

If AI can help nonprofits write stronger grant proposals, that is a win for both nonprofits and funders. Funders need to stop fearing the unknowns around AI, and embrace new opportunities.

## What I’m taking with me: Partnership, Collaboration, and Community

![Treść artykułu](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D5612AQH2oc5CAI0DLQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/B56Z30AxeAHEAU-/0/1777915338261?e=1781136000&v=beta&t=HDJMq4iOpRu9cQulp172swhg-uqZr45WwQDRF7G9Sk4)

Words I heard on repeat at [Skoll Foundation](https://www.linkedin.com/company/skoll-foundation/), [TheSidebar](https://www.linkedin.com/company/the-side-bar/), and among [Fast Forward](https://www.linkedin.com/company/fast_forward/) ’s network. (Actually, it’s all I hear in meetings these days.) The greatest irony of the digital age is that it has brought us together and it has torn us apart. We see our peers tagging each other on LinkedIn, sharing similar resources, and attending events together. As leaders, we wonder, “Should I have been involved? Should my team be doing that too?”

My unsolicited advice is to use those moments as opportunities to create partnership. There is such a strong appetite for collaboration. We should build on the momentum.

For example, I heard a deep desire for shared tools. At Fast Forward, we're passionate about nonprofits licensing tools built by other aligned nonprofits — and we think the ecosystem needs a repository of AI tools that any mission-driven organization could put to work today. Not every organization needs to build tech, but every organization should be using it. A repository would be a great first step. Let's show what's out there and see what blossoms.
