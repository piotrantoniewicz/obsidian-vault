- **Nonprofit professionals are not leaving because they stopped caring.** Many are leaving because they are carrying too much responsibility with too little support.
- **Weak links are an underrated source of resilience.** Casual connections outside your immediate team can offer fresh perspective, new resources, introductions, encouragement, and a sense that you are not alone in the work.
- **Social capital is not evenly distributed in the nonprofit sector.** People who are newer to the sector, working remotely, outside major nonprofit hubs, or inside smaller organizations may have fewer natural pathways into the relationships where trust, opportunity, and support are built.

#### The attrition conversation keeps getting louder.

Every year, it seems like the nonprofit sector gets another report, another panel, another LinkedIn thread reminding us that nonprofit professionals are tired.

And not just “I need a nap” tired. **The deeper kind.**

The kind that comes from carrying too much responsibility with too little support. The kind that comes from caring deeply about the mission while quietly wondering whether the sector is built in a way that lets people stay in the work.

According to the *[latest Social Impact Staff Retention findings](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/638d02351c54f66542fa944f/t/6997e0690a3c861f2c349bc4/1771561065332/SISR+2026+-+Full+Report.pdf)*, 70% of nonprofit workers surveyed in the U.S. said they were looking for, considering, or expecting to look for a new job in the coming year. The top reasons were painfully familiar: too much responsibility without enough support, inadequate compensation or benefits, and unsupportive management or leadership.

And here’s the part that really matters: this is not a one-year blip.

Across the three-year SISR index, 71% of respondents said they were or would be looking for a new job. Only 34% said they planned to definitively stay working in the social sector. That should stop us in our tracks. Because nonprofit professionals are not leaving because they stopped caring.

**Many are leaving because they are tired of caring alone.**

And while internal organizational change is absolutely necessary — better management, better compensation, better professional development, better boundaries, better systems — I think there is another layer of support we do not talk about enough.

The people outside your immediate team. The people you sort of know. The people you meet once in a webinar breakout room, a LinkedIn comment thread, a conference hallway, or a 30-minute Hive Chat.

**The weak links.**

I believe they might be one of the most underrated sources of resilience we have.

## What are weak links, and why should nonprofit professionals care?

The “Weak Ties Theory” was first introduced back in the 70s by [Mark Granovetter.](https://sociology.stanford.edu/people/mark-granovetter) At its core Mark argued that:

*“While strong ties (i.e., close relationships with friends and family) are important for emotional support, weak ties (i.e., more distant or casual relationships) are more valuable in terms of providing access to new information and opportunities.”*

The weak tie theory suggests that our closest connections—those we interact with frequently and share a lot in common with—are often quite similar to us. These strong ties are great for support and shared understanding but they don’t usually introduce us to new ideas or perspectives because we all swim in the same informational waters.

### Strong links and weak links both matter — but they do different jobs

| **Aspect** | **Strong Ties** | **Weak Ties** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Frequency of Interaction | High | Low |
| Emotional Support | High | Low |
| Access to Diverse Information | Low | High |
| Innovation and New ideas | Low | High |
| Network Resilience | Medium | High |
| Job Opportunities | Medium | HIgh |

And while strong ties are essential, weak ties often give us access to something different: new information, new ideas, new opportunities, and new ways of seeing the world.

Strong ties are often swimming in the same water as us. Weak ties are the little bridges into a different pond. **For nonprofit professionals, that matters.** Because when you are working inside one organization, one department, one funding model, one crisis cycle, it is very easy to start believing that the way things are is the way things have to be.

Then you talk to someone in another city, another country, another part of the sector, and suddenly something opens.

“Oh wait, you do it that way?”

“You had that problem too?”

“You found a way around that?”

“Your board finally understood when you framed it like that?”

Sometimes the most useful conversation is not with someone who knows your exact context. Sometimes it is with someone who is just far enough away from it to help you see it differently. A healthy professional life needs both. You need the people who know the whole messy backstory. But you also need the person who comes in with no baggage, no organizational politics, no “we tried that in 2017 and it didn’t work” energy.

You need the colleague who will say, “That sounds impossible.” And you need the stranger who will say, “Actually, I have seen someone do that.” **Both are gifts.**

But in nonprofit work, I think weak links are especially powerful because so many of our organizations are under-resourced, internally stretched, and culturally cautious. Which brings me to something I have heard again and again through The Nonprofit Hive.

Sometimes people feel safer telling the truth to someone outside their organization than they do inside their own team.

## When nonprofit professionals feel safer talking to strangers

This is one of the strange and beautiful things I have watched happen through The Nonprofit Hive.

We take two nonprofit or social impact professionals who may have never met before. We give them a 30-minute conversation. No pitch. No performance. No pressure to prove anything.

And often, within minutes, people are talking about the real stuff. The burnout. The fundraising pressure. The board dynamics. The loneliness of leadership. The “I thought it was just me” moments.

The strange relief of being understood by someone who is not in your workplace, not in your reporting structure, and not personally invested in how the story looks on paper.

That is not a replacement for psychological safety inside nonprofit organizations. Let me be really clear about that. Nonprofit workplaces still need to become healthier places for people to ask questions, challenge old habits, share concerns, name capacity issues, and grow.

But while we work toward that, external peer connection can offer something deeply practical and human. A place to exhale. A place to hear, “No, you are not imagining it.” A place to collect new language, new examples, and new courage before bringing an idea back into your own organization.

Sometimes the weak tie is not weak because it lacks value. Sometimes it is weak because it is free from the weight of internal politics.

**And that freedom can make it powerful.**

## Weak links help nonprofit work escape the echo chamber

Every field has its patterns.

In nonprofit work, we have our own familiar ones: scarcity thinking, competition for funding, “do more with less,” program silos, board/staff disconnect, martyrdom disguised as commitment, and a lot of very tired people trying to make systems work with duct tape and hope.

Whether you are in marketing, fundraising, leadership or programs, we all need to have those “breath of fresh air” conversations that prevent our nonproft work from becoming an echo chamber of ideas and trends.

On a more personal level, these weak link connections provide us with cheerleaders – people who may not require the same level of close relationship that comes with obligations and continued maintenance. Instead these relationships can truly just exist in a time/place/space that can fade in and out without the same concerns of hurt feelings or absentee costs.

These are not always dramatic, fireworks-level moments. Sometimes the shift is small.

A phrase. A resource. A warm introduction. A “you should meet this person.” A little door opening somewhere you did not know there was a door.

That is the power of weak links.

They move ideas across the sector. They help people see beyond their own organizational walls. They create the conditions for collaboration before a formal partnership ever exists.

## Weak links are how nonprofit professionals build social capital

This is where I find the weak ties conversation gets even more interesting, because what we are really talking about is social capital.

Social capital is the value that lives inside relationships. It is the trust, goodwill, information, access, reputation, shared norms, and mutual support that move through a network. And in nonprofit work, social capital matters enormously.

It is often how someone hears about a job before it is posted. It is how consultants find aligned collaborators. It is how fundraisers learn what is working in a different shop. It is how executive directors quietly sanity-check a board challenge with someone who understands the strange complexity of nonprofit leadership. It is how a resource, opportunity, introduction, or bit of encouragement reaches the person who needs it at exactly the right moment.

But here is the part I do not think we say enough: nonprofit professionals do not all have equal access to social capital.

People who are newer to the sector, newer to a country, newer to leadership, working remotely, working in smaller organizations, working in underfunded issue areas, or outside major nonprofit hubs may have fewer natural pathways into the conversations where trust and opportunity get built. And that has real consequences, because in a sector that already runs on scarcity, isolation can become a professional disadvantage.

This is why intentional peer connection matters. Not as fluffy networking. Not as “nice to have” community vibes. As infrastructure for access, learning, resilience, collaboration, and belonging.

At its best, networking is simply the practice of staying connected to possibility. It is a way of saying, “I do not have all the answers inside my own organization.” It is acknowledging that we need peers, not just professional development. It is choosing to learn from people who are solving different versions of the same problems. It is believing that collaboration becomes easier when relationships already exist.

That is not self-serving. That is sector-strengthening.

Weak links help social capital move beyond the usual circles. They make the sector less dependent on who already knows whom. They create bridges between people who may never have met otherwise but still have something meaningful to offer each other. And over time, those bridges can change what is possible.

For individuals, social capital can mean support, opportunity, confidence, and fresh thinking. For organizations, it can mean better collaboration, stronger partnerships, faster learning, and more trust. For the sector, it can mean fewer people carrying hard work in isolation.

When nonprofit professionals are connected across organizations, geographies, roles, and issue areas, the whole ecosystem gets smarter. Resources move faster. Ideas travel further. People find support before they hit the breaking point. And maybe (just maybe) fewer nonprofit professionals feel like the only way to survive is to leave.

## How to build more weak links without adding another overwhelming thing to your plate

If you are already stretched thin, the last thing you need is someone telling you to “network more” in a way that sounds like another job.

So let’s keep this practical. You do not need to become a professional extrovert. You do not need to attend every conference. You do not need to turn every conversation into an opportunity.

You just need to create a few more openings for people outside your immediate circle.

### 1\. Say yes to one low-pressure conversation a month

One.

That could be a Hive Chat, a LinkedIn connection, a coffee conversation, or a peer intro from someone you trust. The goal is not to meet everyone. The goal is to keep your world from getting too small.

### 2\. Reconnect with someone you already sort of know

Weak links are often already there. A former colleague. A conference contact. A past collaborator. Someone who commented on your post six months ago.

A simple message can reopen the door: “Hey, I was thinking about your work recently and would love to hear what you’re learning these days.”

That is it. No big ask. No agenda. Just relationship maintenance without weirdness.

### 3\. Ask better questions in casual conversations

Instead of “What do you do?” try:

“What is feeling surprisingly hard in your work right now?”

“What is something your organization is trying to figure out?”

“What is giving you energy lately?”

“What is one thing you wish more people in the sector understood?”

Better questions create better weak links because they move the conversation from surface-level networking to actual connection.

### 4\. Make introductions generously

Weak links become powerful when we stop hoarding access.

If you know two people who should know each other, introduce them. Not because you need to manage the outcome. Not because there is a formal partnership ready to happen. Because strong ecosystems are built through small acts of connection.

### 5\. Join spaces designed for peer connection, not just content consumption

This distinction matters. A webinar can be helpful. A newsletter can be helpful. A resource library can be helpful. But if no one is actually connecting with anyone else, [that is not community](https://thenonprofithive.com/nonprofit-community-building-strategy/). That is content.

Look for spaces that create real interaction, especially smaller and more structured opportunities where people can be seen and heard.

### 6\. Follow the energy after a good conversation

Not every weak tie needs to become a strong tie. That is part of the beauty.

Some conversations are useful once. Some come back around months later. Some become collaborations. Some become friendships.

[You do not have to force every connection into an ongoing relationship.](https://www.nptechforgood.com/2026/04/03/community-as-infrastructure-why-nonprofits-must-start-designing-for-relationships/)

Just notice where there is energy, mutual curiosity, and shared values. Then follow that.

## The call to action: build the bridge before you need it

The power of weak ties is easy to underestimate because it often feels casual.

A short conversation. A kind message. A “you should meet…” A resource sent in a moment of generosity.

But over time, those little bridges become part of the infrastructure that helps people stay resourced, hopeful, and connected.

In a sector where so many people are considering leaving, we cannot afford to treat connection as extra. We cannot keep asking nonprofit professionals to sustain world-changing work with only internal support systems that may or may not be healthy enough to hold them.

We need strong teams, yes. We need better leadership, yes. We need better pay, boundaries, and professional development, yes. And we also need broader webs of peer support that remind people they are not alone in the work.

Because the next great idea, opportunity, collaborator, mentor, referral, or moment of relief might not come from your closest circle. It might come from someone you barely know. And that is exactly why the tie matters.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What are weak links in nonprofit networking?**

Weak links are the looser professional connections in your network. They might be people you have met once, past colleagues, LinkedIn connections, conference contacts, Hive Chat matches, or peers in other organizations. They may not know your whole story, but they often bring fresh ideas, resources, introductions, and perspectives that your closest circle may not have access to.

**Why are weak links important for nonprofit professionals?**

Weak links help nonprofit professionals move beyond their immediate organizational bubble. They can lead to new job opportunities, collaborations, funding ideas, professional support, referrals, and creative problem-solving. In a sector where many people feel stretched and isolated, these casual connections can also remind people that they are not alone in the work.

**How do weak links support social capital in the nonprofit sector?**

Weak links help social capital move across the sector. Social capital is the value that lives inside relationships, including trust, access, information, reputation, and mutual support. When nonprofit professionals build weak ties across roles, geographies, and organizations, they create more pathways for knowledge, opportunity, and encouragement to travel.

**What is the difference between networking and community building?**

Networking often focuses on making individual connections. Community building goes deeper by creating repeated, meaningful opportunities for people to connect, contribute, and support one another over time. A one-time conversation can build a weak tie, but a consistent relational system can turn many weak links into a stronger community ecosystem.

**How can nonprofits help staff build social capital?**

Nonprofits can support social capital by making peer connection part of professional development. This could include funding conference participation, encouraging cross-organization learning, creating peer circles, supporting staff involvement in communities of practice, or making space for staff to build relationships beyond the organization. The key is treating relationship-building as part of the work, not a distraction from it.
