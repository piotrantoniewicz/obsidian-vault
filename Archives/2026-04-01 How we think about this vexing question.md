As we integrate AI into our tech platform, we are neither all in favor, nor all opposed. The climate transition won’t happen without useful technology, but we need to channel it for good.

![](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/5f0c587ab32ca0e3d7078a8c/69cd8ac77e75c2bfcd4fd5a5_photo-1700150282220-d02cd4994cd5.jpeg)

There's a question we get asked more and more: **Does your organization use AI - and doesn't AI use a lot of energy?**

Yes. (And yes.)

The environmental cost of artificial intelligence is real and growing. Training large models consumes staggering amounts of electricity. Data centers are being built at a pace that would make a ‘90s mall developer blush. And the collective thirst for compute is straining power grids in ways that are genuinely concerning.

So can a climate nonprofit use it in good conscience? And how can we make responsible and purposeful integrations with AI where the benefits outweigh the costs?

##### Our position

We use AI. We measure the energy cost of doing so. We report those emissions honestly. And we hold ourselves to a simple principle: if using energy accelerates our mission and allows us to accomplish more than we would without it, we will use it, but we will use the least amount necessary to get there.

##### Leaf blowers and buses

Here's how we think about it: a leaf blower and a bus both use gasoline. But one is a terrible use: the blower is loud, inefficient, and exists primarily to move a problem from one yard to another. A city bus full of passengers, on the other hand, moves forty people to work, to school, to doctor’s appointments - efficiently, safely, and without the congestion that would come from 40 individual cars.

The question isn't whether energy is being used. The question is whether the **energy is doing beneficial work**.

Our beneficial work is to accelerate the transition to a low carbon future by giving companies the tools and incentives to find and fund climate solutions. When we use AI - to process data, to build tools, to work more effectively with a small team - we're putting literal energy toward that mission. We think that's a bus, not a leaf blower.

##### No easy answer

We'd love to tell you that AI is categorically good or categorically bad for the climate. That would make for a shorter blog post. But it's not that simple, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

The truth is that AI is a tool, and like every other tool, its impact depends on how it's built and used. A hammer can build a house or break a window. It can be manufactured in a facility that pays its employees a living wage or does not.

When it comes to AI, there are moral decisions baked into tools and their training data that impact their results, no matter how imperceptibly. We interact with AI tools in ways that seek to avoid the impacts of irresponsible training.

##### Measurement and accountability

The same rule applies to AI emissions as it does for any other business activity: don’t ignore it, and don’t pretend. Measuring and accounting for AI emissions is key to understanding its impact and being more intentional about its tradeoffs.

We use tools like Gemini, GitHub Copilot and Claude Teams - AI services, not our own GPU clusters. But "we didn't run the servers" isn't an excuse to ignore the energy behind them. We estimate the emissions associated with our AI usage using provider data and grid intensity factors, and we include those estimates in our own operational emissions reporting - the same way we include a commercial flight or our home-office energy usage.

This is the same standard we ask of the companies in our community. All of us need to account for the emissions that come from asking a language model to help us write code or draft blog posts like this one.

##### We use the smallest tool that does the job

There's a meaningful difference between running a lightweight model to parse a dataset and spinning up a Nano Banana process to generate a meme for social media. We try hard to do the former and avoid the latter.

In practice, this means we prefer computationally lighter approaches wherever they get the job done. We don't default to the biggest, most expensive model because it's flashy. We choose the right tool for the task. (More often than not, that's not using AI at all.) For small tasks, it's a smaller model like Gemini Flash. And sometimes, when the problem genuinely demands it, it's a larger flagship model like Opus 4.6 w/Thinking.

No extraneous meme-generating sessions. (Well, almost none.)

##### We’ll remain self-conscious, critical, and aware

AI’s effect on human civilization is evolving rapidly, and every day brings positive and negative surprises. The worst way to write a policy in this environment is to pretend that you have all the answers. No one does.

Our team has already made quick, responsive decisions such as abandoning our use of ChatGPT due to OpenAI’s lack of governance and ethical standards. We’ll continue to be self-conscious in our use of AI, critical of its net contributions to our work, and aware of both the downsides and the upsides.

For example:

On the downside: a lot of AI compute is being deployed for purposes that have nothing to do with solving hard problems. Generating photorealistic images of cats wearing hats is delightful, but it is not urgent. And when that workload sits on the same grid as hospitals and schools, the energy tradeoff matters.

On the upside: AI is genuinely accelerating work in climate science, materials discovery, grid optimization, and emissions measurement, including our own. The potential for AI to help accelerate solutions to the climate crisis is not hypothetical. Every day people are finding ways to use and deploy AI-assisted tools to do the work that’s worth it for all of us.

We have a small team with a big mission, and we are constantly seeking ways to increase our positive impact. As we integrate AI into our tech platform, we are neither all in favor, nor all opposed. The climate transition won’t happen without useful technology, but we need to channel it for good.

*For further reading, we highly recommend the work of one of our supporters – Fast Forward’s* [*AI for Humanity initiative*](https://www.ffwd.org/ai-for-humanity)*. No one is thinking more critically about the ethical choices facing the tech nonprofit sector when it comes to AI.*
