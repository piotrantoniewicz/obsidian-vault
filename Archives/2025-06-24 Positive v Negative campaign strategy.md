### Which do you prefer, and why would you even choose between them?

I’m increasingly noticing campaigners refer to their work as “positive” or negative”.

This either relates to the strategies and approaches they take to their campaigning, or the intended outcomes (aka the objectives) of the campaigns. Both are interesting topics and I’m going to write about the objectives of a campaign being considered positive or negative in a follow up post so I can focus on strategy in this piece.

In the strategic sense, “positive” tends to be code for campaigns that involve engaging nicely, building relationships and trying to work constructively with targets. “Negative” means naming, shaming, challenging and otherwise creating risk, awkwardness or difficulty for the target.

I want to distinguish between these labels and the concept of “inside track” and “outside track”, which seem to overlap substantially as they refer to the difference between engaging in dialogue and advocacy directly with a target, and public campaigning to get them to change. When I hear people talk in terms of “positive” or “negative”, it sounds a lot like inside / outside but with a distinct tone. Positive seems to involve being nicer towards the target, and negative is depicted as being meaner.

There’s a lot about these labels and the apparent dichotomy I find baffling and unhelpful, with my central question being: **why does this even have to be a choice?**

It’s possible to engage a campaign target in constructive dialogue, building relationships with its decision-makers or advisers, and creating a positive public narrative that opens up space for your target to gracefully agree to your asks, while also creating risk for your target through public naming, shaming, awkwardness and friction to pressure them.

I remember being able to deliver a speech to about a hundred people gathered in front of a bank’s offices, condemning its fossil fuel funding, before walking into the very same offices, going upstairs and having a perfectly respectful meeting with the bank’s sustainability team to talk policy and global trends by financial institutions to restrict fossil fuel investment. It takes a degree of empathy and careful managing of relationships, but this is more than doable.

By choosing to deliver either a “positive” or “negative” campaign, you’re missing out. In either case, the alternative has benefits you should want to make available to your campaign. A purely “positive” campaign is basically advocacy and you should only expect to get your outcome if the dynamics of power are already in your favour. On the other hand, a “negative” campaign strategy is likely to miss out on the opportunity to gather intelligence, build trust, ensure your target clearly understands what you want, and learn when your campaign is really starting to gain traction from direct interactions with your target’s representatives, or alienating them to the the point where it is becoming counterproductive. More importantly, if your campaign starts to gain traction you might not be in a position to take advantage because you’re not prepared to build a relationship with your target and negotiate an outcome that satisfies your objective, or know when it’s the right time to soften your public campaigning to give space for your target to do what you want.

If you engage sincerely and in a way that builds trust with your target’s representatives, but maintain the understanding that you still plan to campaign publicly to hold them accountable for their actions, and if that public campaigning doesn’t breach any trust or cross any other lines you might have established in your private dialogue, you can enjoy the benefits of both approaches to campaigning.

There will be some circumstances where a choice needs to be made. For instance, your target might tell you that unless you stop campaigning publicly, it won’t meet with you, or that your combative public campaigning is turning decision-makers away from giving you what you want. If that’s the case, you might need to decide which approach you value most (unless you think your target is bluffing). Are you better placed to get results from inside the meeting room, leveraging your relationships, and by presenting a comforting, opportunistic message to your target? Or is critiquing and challenging your target publicly going to be more valuable? Do you have allies who can handle more of the so-called “positive” or “negative” strategies, allowing you to choose the other?

I’ve never had to make this choice but always felt that if I was forced to, I’d probably give up dialogue in order to keep applying public pressure, based on where I felt the best opportunities were to get movement from my targets. But that’s my own judgement as to the best way for me to build and wield power in my own circumstances - it can vary for different campaigners and organisations, and even in different moments of campaigns.

From the way people talk about “positive” or “negative” campaigning there are a few reasons I can see people pigeon-holing themselves into one or the other:

- Delusions of access equalling influence. Access - the ability to have an audience with your target - can be very seductive. It can look and feel like like a breakthrough, and that you’re really being heard. Companies and governments have all sorts of reasons for giving you their time and while your ability to influence them is one possibility (and definitely the one you want), making you “feel heard” and placating you, satisfying key performance indicators of meetings with civil society (yes, these exist), and the need to demonstrate balance in their dealings are other reasons for engagement.
- Conflict aversion. Some people are just uncomfortable doing things that create tension or conflict between you and another entity, or are afraid of annoying someone or something with a lot of power. While I sympathise with these concerns, campaigners need to be ready to manage and even create conflict and tension. When you’re making governments and companies do things they don’t initially want to do, there’s going to be a degree of conflict that all campaigners must be ready to work with.
- Organisational style. Some organisations build their brand or identity around one or another style of campaigning, or a strategy. That’s understandable, but it’s important that these organisations realise the limitations of what they can get done if they shrink their strategies and styles to match their organisational profiles, and build alliances that bring in other necessary styles and strategies from other groups.
- Dismissiveness towards a target or it’s representatives. Some will rule out “positive” campaigning because they don’t want to engage with “corporate shills” who “should already know what they’re doing wrong and just need to fix it”. Frankly, I think part of this originates from uneasiness about the prospect of debating the nuances and detail of policies with our targets, or dealing with the hurdles that an institution would likely face in making decisions that will be risky and contested. But we need to have the empathy to be able to handle this. Empathy doesn’t necessarily means we come to like our targets, but it does allow us to see things from their perspective, realise the obstacles and challenges they would face if they were to deliver our asks, and design our campaign in a way that helps them navigate those challenges.

The other problem I have with referring to “positive” and “negative” campaigning is that the terms become pejorative, usually from the perspective of someone describing their campaigning as positive, implying it to be superior.

Here’s how you can tell whether a campaign is actually superior: it is successful.

There are plenty of ways to describe strategies without having to label them positive or negative. Advocacy, direct engagement, lobbying, building relationships, building alliances, leveraging relationships, acting as a source of information, providing incentives. Public naming and shaming, demonstrations and protests, brand-jamming, confronting and challenging representatives at public events. All of these sound like strategies I’d want on the menu, to use or discard based on what I think puts me in the best position to build and shift power, and win.

And that’s what matters most. As far as I’m concerned, **a positive campaign is one that wins and a negative campaign is one that fails,** irrespective of how it is delivered.
