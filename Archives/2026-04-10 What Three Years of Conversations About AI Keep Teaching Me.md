I’ve been talking with nonprofits about AI for about three years now. The settings change — conference stages, boardrooms, Teams calls squeezed between back-to-back meetings — but there’s one question that comes up almost every time, and it hasn’t changed: *Where do we start?*

What has changed is my understanding of the answer. Three years ago, I probably would have started with the tools. Now, after hundreds of these conversations across organisations of wildly different sizes, sectors, and levels of readiness, I’ve developed what I think is a pretty clear picture of how all the pieces fit together. I say “I think” deliberately — anyone claiming to be an expert in AI is naive at best. The landscape shifts every few weeks (or sometimes days). But the patterns I keep seeing in how organisations approach this? Those have been remarkably consistent, and they’re what I want to share here.

This is the zoomed-out view. The map I wish I could hand every nonprofit leader before we sit down and talk about any specific tool or tactic. Over the coming weeks, I’ll dig into each piece properly. But if you want to see how it all connects, start here.

## The gap that keeps showing up

There’s a moment I’ve watched play out dozens of times now. I’ll show something practical, like loading a nonprofit’s strategic plan and past fundraising emails into a ChatGPT project and watching it draft something 90% of the way there, and the room shifts. People aren’t amazed so much as they are struck by the gap between what they assumed AI required and what it actually took. No coding. No specialist. Just documents they already had and a tool that costs less than a monthly phone plan.

At the Fundraising Institute of Australia conference recently, that reaction was palpable. People were blown away by how simple it was. And then, almost on cue, the conversation moved to the same place it always does: *So where do we start?*

Here’s the pattern I’ve noticed across all of these interactions. The aha moment is never the problem. People get it. They see the value almost immediately. The problem is what happens between that moment and anything actually changing.

People go back to their desks. They’re busy, and in this sector, “busy” doesn’t begin to cover it. The insight fades. They might try a prompt or two, get a mediocre result, and quietly move on. Not because they don’t see the value, but because nobody has created the conditions for them to do anything with it.

That gap between the spark and the sustained change is where I’ve come to focus almost all of my attention. And it turns out it’s not a technology gap at all. It’s a leadership one.

## The thing most leaders don’t realise

Something that catches a lot of leaders off guard when I bring it up is that their team is almost certainly already using AI. They’re drafting emails with it, summarising documents, brainstorming ideas. They’re just not telling anyone.

This is shadow AI, and it’s far more common than most organisations realise. The irony is hard to miss. The organisations that haven’t developed a policy because they think “we’re not really using AI” are usually the ones where it’s most unregulated.

I heard of one nonprofit where a staff member was feeding confidential client information into ChatGPT, and because there were no guardrails in place, no policy, no guidance of any kind, the organisation’s response was to implement a blanket two-year ban on AI for the entire staff. One person’s misuse, and the whole organisation lost access to tools that could have been transforming their work for the next two years.

Think about what that actually costs. Not just in efficiency, but in learning, in capability, in the growing distance between what’s possible and what your team knows how to do. That distance widens every month right now, and a ban like that doesn’t solve the underlying problem. It just makes AI invisible again.

## Start with the conversation, not the tool

If I could change one assumption I keep running into, it would be this: the first step with AI is not signing up for ChatGPT. It’s having the conversation.

A policy might sound like bureaucracy, but in my experience it’s the opposite. It’s the single most important first move because it forces the discussion most organisations have been quietly avoiding. What do we actually think about this technology? How might we use it? How should we not? What are we comfortable with? What worries us?

That conversation does something no tool can do on its own. It makes AI visible. It brings the shadow use into the open, surfaces fears and assumptions and opportunities in the same room at the same time, and it means that when people do start using tools with intention, they have guardrails built from shared understanding rather than restrictions born from fear.

I keep coming back to a simple analogy here. You wouldn’t hand everyone in your organisation a power drill before figuring out whether you need one, what it’s for, and what the safety basics are. The conversation comes first. The tool comes second.

## The flywheel

Once the policy is in place and the conversation has happened, there’s a sequence I’ve seen work, and a sequence I’ve seen organisations skip, usually to their regret.

It starts with understanding where people actually are. Not everyone is in the same place. Some staff are already experimenting quietly, some are anxious about the whole thing, and some have never opened an AI tool in their life. You need to know this before you invest in training, or you’ll pitch it at the wrong level and wonder why it didn’t land.

From there, get people access to proper tools. Free tiers are fine for a first look, but they’re limited in ways that create a misleading impression of what AI can actually do. A paid subscription is less than most organisations spend on a single team lunch, and the return on that investment, even from basic use, is almost immediate.

Then train for where people are, not where you wish they were. Not a one-off workshop. Not a recorded webinar someone watches at double speed. Practical, hands-on training that gives people wins with work they’re already doing. Drafting that grant report, summarising board minutes, turning messy data into a narrative someone actually wants to read.

Getting those quick wins matters more than most leaders realise. Before anyone talks about transformation or strategy, people need to feel the difference in their own daily work. When someone saves two hours on something that used to take a full afternoon, they don’t need convincing anymore.

And then, only then, you create space for experimentation. This is where it gets genuinely interesting, because the time saved from those quick wins becomes the space for exploration. That’s where people start discovering uses nobody planned for. Things no training programme could have predicted, because they come from the people closest to the work.

This is the flywheel. Each step creates the conditions for the next. The wins create the time. The time creates the experimentation. The experimentation creates new wins. Skip a step, and the wheel stalls.

## The drill, the Plover, and why this matters

I think about all of this through a lens that might seem oddly domestic.

When I put together flat-pack furniture at home, I can use a screwdriver or I can use a power drill. The drill gets it done in a tenth of the time. That’s the efficiency argument for AI, and it’s real. If you’ve got access to power tools and you’re still reaching for the hand tools, you’ve got an opportunity cost whether you see it or not.

But the interesting part isn’t speed. It’s what happens once you’re comfortable with the tool and start seeing opportunities you wouldn’t have considered before. I’ve used my drill to put up cat shelves on the walls, mount a TV, build hammocks in the outdoor cat run. Projects I never would have attempted with a hand tool, not because they were impossible, but because the effort would have been prohibitive.

We had a Plover nest in the middle of one of our paddocks recently. Plovers are Australian birds, and this one had set up right where the sheep walk, completely exposed to wind and rain. So I grabbed some leftover wood, cut it with the power saw, screwed on a piece of perspex we had lying around, and made a little shelter over the nest. Twenty minutes, maybe. No budget, no plan, no trip to the hardware store. I just saw a problem, had the tools, had some scraps, and built the thing.

That’s what I want nonprofit leaders to understand about AI. Yes, it does your existing work faster. But once your team is fluent with it, they start seeing problems they can actually solve. Problems they would have walked past before because the cost of solving them felt too high. The fundraising series nobody had time to write. The onboarding guide that’s been on someone’s list for three years. The impact data that could tell a powerful story if anyone had the hours to shape it.

The tool doesn’t just make existing work faster. It makes new work possible. But only if people get the chance to reach that point.

## Where the leverage actually is

After three years of these conversations, and with the significant caveat that I’m still learning alongside everyone else, I’m more convinced than ever that the leverage point here isn’t the technology. It’s leadership making AI visible and creating the conditions for people to learn.

That means starting the conversation. Developing the policy. Understanding where your people are. Investing in tools and training. Protecting time for experimentation. Building a culture where people share what they’re discovering, the wins and the failures both.

None of that is expensive. Most of it isn’t even particularly hard. But it requires a decision, a deliberate choice to prioritise this now rather than next quarter or next year.

I want to be honest about why I think the timing matters. It’s not because AI is going to replace anyone’s job next Tuesday. It’s because the gap between what’s possible and what most nonprofits know is possible is growing wider every week. The organisations that start now, even imperfectly, even slowly, will be in a fundamentally different position a year from now than the ones that wait.

Your staff deserve access to these tools. Your mission deserves the multiplied impact. The people you serve deserve an organisation operating with every advantage available to it.

The power drill is sitting right there. Pick it up.
