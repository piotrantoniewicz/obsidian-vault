From Bots to “Weapons of Mass Destruction”

The attention-based business model has turned social platforms into curated, tilted speech environments that privilege extremity and manipulation over pluralism and deliberation, and that AI risks supercharging this trend. Sasha Havlicek calls for a decisive shift from censorship debates to mandated transparency, independent scrutiny of algorithms and data, and systemic interventions across the entire “information lifecycle” from production to amplification to consumption before the tools of mass manipulation become, in her words, “weapons of mass destruction.”

\-

A call from Sasha Havlicek

Today’s information ecosystem is what a democracy looks like when those checks fail to keep up with technology and capital. Citizens face a double deficit: insufficient protection from very real harms, and a steadily shrinking capacity to participate on equal terms in the public square.

For twenty years, the Institute for Strategic Dialogue (ISD), which Havlicek co‑founded and leads, has sat in what she called the “nether regions of the internet,” tracking extremist networks, hostile state operations, and youth exploitation at scale. That vantage point has left her with little patience for nostalgic liberalism. The internet did begin as “an immense opportunity for democratisation, for the free flow of information”; but the attention economy model that ultimately won has produced information environments where the “free and fair competition of ideas” has been replaced by algorithmic gatekeeping and behavioural engineering. What we inhabit now is not a free speech space, she argued, but “curated speech environments” that structurally privilege outrage, conspiracy, and extremism over accuracy, nuance, or civic value.

The stakes, in this telling, are not theoretical. They are measured in radicalised teenagers, intimidated politicians, and democratic institutions that struggle to function in the fog of synthetic noise.

#### When the feed radicalises your child

The most haunting part of Havlicek’s warning is not about geopolitics but about the everyday experience of young users. ISD’s monitoring work shows that minors who never set out to search for extremism or hate are nonetheless being “driven to ever more weird, extreme things” by recommendation systems optimised for engagement rather than wellbeing. She cited fresh research with the UK’s Antisemitism Policy Trust: a 15‑year‑old boy in Britain, merely interested in “male lifestyle influencers,” needed only an hour on TikTok before being served antisemitic conspiracy theories. Another, interested in left‑wing politics, was watching Hamas content and videos glorifying Iran’s Revolutionary Guard within ninety minutes.

The through‑line is not ideology but design. These boys did not go looking for antisemitism or militant propaganda; the system delivered it to them because it was sticky, high‑performing content. The same pattern appears in the supposedly “closed chapter” of ISIS propaganda. ISD recently found Islamic State supporters with over 10,000 followers on Facebook, generating more than 350,000 views in a single month content that is illegal in most jurisdictions, yet “again being algorithmically pushed to kids” who venture anywhere near the topic.

Havlicek connects this to a broader wave of youth radicalisation: nihilistic “no lives matter” subcultures, neo‑Nazi communities, school‑shooter fandoms, pornography pushed at boys and self‑harm content pushed at girls. The age of those appearing in the UK’s counter‑terrorism referral systems has dropped to as low as eight or nine. This is not, she stressed, the story of a few bad parents or reckless teenagers. It is the predictable by‑product of an industrial system in which “feeds” relentlessly test, learn, and push whatever holds attention longest regardless of how it corrodes the psyche, or the polity, downstream.

Her anecdote about her own son’s constant attempts to bypass parental controls was more than a moment of levity. It underscored the futility of placing the burden primarily on families, while the default architecture of the platforms pulls in exactly the opposite direction.

#### Silencing by swarm

If feeds pull vulnerable users towards radicalisation, they push others out of the public square entirely. Havlicek is blunt about the scale of harassment, abuse, and intimidation directed at politicians, judges, journalists, and activists especially women and minorities and the way platforms treat it as collateral damage rather than a systemic threat to democracy.

“The kind of thing that actually is illegal in the real world,” she said, is “waged at scale” online and “turns into violence in the real world,” but it also functions as “a massive silencing tactic.” Female public figures, in particular, are on the receiving end of such relentless, gendered abuse that “many women simply \[do\] not want to return into that public sphere.” The same is increasingly true for other targeted communities.

This is not just a safety issue; it is a representation issue. When certain groups are effectively chased out of online politics, democratic deliberation is skewed not by overt censorship but by who can bear the cost of speaking. A public square that looks formally open but is substantively dominated by those resilient or brutal enough to weather industrial‑scale abuse is already a distorted one.

In that context, the language of “participation” can become a fig leaf. Yes, more voices than ever can publish. But if the cost of entry for some is sustained character assassination, rape threats, or coordinated smear campaigns, then the promise of equal participation is hollow.

#### From bots to “weapons of mass destruction”

The conversation, moderated by former UK parliamentarian Damien Collins, shifted from human targets to the inauthentic ones, bots and fake accounts. Some estimates suggest that nearly 40 percent of global internet traffic is now driven by bots, a statistic that Havlicek links it to the extraordinary reach of far‑right figures like Nick Fuentes on X. When a platform architected around amplifying its owner’s voice is nonetheless delivering more engagement to a fringe extremist, she argued, “there’s something industrial going on” behind the scenes.

Elon Musk himself once framed tackling bots and algorithm transparency as prerequisites for improving free speech. Havlicek’s rejoinder is that the “inauthenticity problem” has only deepened, and it is not just a democracy issue but a consumer fraud issue: advertisers are paying to reach “humans,” and platforms are selling them a blended audience of people, bots, and state‑sponsored influence operations.

And then there is AI. Havlicek describes “LLM grooming” as systematic attempts by state and non‑state actors to flood large language models with propaganda or to prompt‑engineer them into repeating foreign talking points. In one ISD study across four major chatbots, roughly 20 per cent of responses on the war in Ukraine parroted Russian state media narratives. In an era where users increasingly treat AI outputs as neutral or authoritative, the prospect of hostile regimes “tilting the entire AI economy into this attention economy business model” is, for her, a civilisational risk.

That is why she escalated her metaphor: what are now “weapons of mass manipulation” could “with the onset of AI… become weapons of mass destruction.” The phrase risks sounding hyperbolic until one considers a scenario where bots, micro‑targeting, manipulated AI systems and synthetic media combine to systematically nudge elections, financial markets, and social cohesion in directions that no‑one voted for and no regulator can fully see.

#### Transparency as the democratic counter‑model

Faced with this arsenal, Havlicek is scathing about the current trajectory of platform governance. Over the last three to five years, she noted, the dominant trend has been not towards more accountability but towards less: “platforms have been progressively shuttering access to data for journalists, for third party researchers.” That is a direct hit to organisations like ISD, which rely on real‑time threat detection to disrupt extremist plots and foreign influence campaigns before they spill into the offline world.

The opacity is also a gift to conspiracy theorists. When outsiders cannot audit algorithms or understand why certain stories or parties trend, every grievance can be recast as a shadowy plot. As Havlicek puts it, people on “whichever loony end of the spectrum you might be” suspect bias at scale but have “no way of finding that out right now.” The result is a public square that is both actually tilted and widely perceived as rigged, a combination that corrodes trust in institutions and in the very idea of neutral enforcement.

Authoritarian regimes, she argued, have responded to digital threats by doubling down on censorship and control. Liberal democracies must “do the diametric opposite.” The answer is not more arbitrary content takedowns but mandated transparency: “We have to mandate transparency. We have to be able to say, okay, we’re going to get under the bonnet of this and understand, is there a systemic bias of these algorithmic pushes to one side or another?” Only then can societies have an honest fight about what to do next be it competition enforcement, design standards, or new obligations on recommender systems.

Crucially, Havlicek rejects the idea that platforms can “mark their own homework.” Internal research, however glossy, cannot substitute for an “ecosystem of scrutiny” in which independent academics, journalists, civil society groups and regulators all have some access to the data needed to test claims and spot harms.

#### Re‑balancing power, before it’s too late

The session ended with a question that crystallised the political dilemma: if democracies cannot protect women and children from abuses like deepfaked sexual images or extremist grooming, what is their legitimacy worth? That concern echoed in later interventions from MEP Alexandra Geese, who has warned that failures to enforce existing EU laws against platforms like X’s Grok “spicy mode” risk convincing citizens that democracy is “not efficient” and “not working” for them.

Havlicek’s answer avoids both fatalism and tech solutionism. Bans on youth access, she noted wryly as a mother of a 14‑year‑old, might be worth trying but “it shouldn’t stop there,” not least because they are easy to circumvent. Real progress, in her view, requires interventions across the entire “information lifecycle”:

- On production, where governments and platforms must do more to understand and disrupt hostile states, terrorist organisations, and organised harassers.

- On distribution, where engineers and designers in the private sector must rethink amplification models that reliably reward outrage and extremity.

- On consumption, where citizens children and adults need a much deeper literacy about how their data is used and why their feeds look the way they do.

Over‑concentration of power whether in markets, infrastructure, or information flows “leads ultimately to authoritarianism,” she warned, and authoritarians already “know that it is in the shaping of our digital information ecosystem that the battle between liberal democracy and authoritarianism is taking shape.” The challenge for those who care about “freedom, individual rights, democracy” is to “shape it in our image, which is transparency and accountability.”

That is an argument about power, not content moderation. If the last decade was about whether platforms should remove more posts, the coming one will be about whether democratic societies can meaningfully supervise the private infrastructures that now mediate almost every act of political participation. Havlicek’s core claim is that we no longer have the luxury of treating that as a seminar topic. The battle over power, protection, and participation online is already underway. The only real question is whether democracies are prepared to fight it on their own terms or continue to wage it on platforms designed to keep them in the dark.
