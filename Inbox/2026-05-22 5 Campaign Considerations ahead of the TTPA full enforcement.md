22.05.2026

Over the past few months, something in our sector has changed. The conversation is no longer *what is the TTPA?* The conversation is *how do we actually run campaigns under it?*

The Transparency and Targeting of Political Advertising regulation has been in full application since October 2025. The Dutch parliamentary election was the first visible stress test. Hungary is becoming the second.

For those still catching up: Regulation (EU) 2024/900 entered into force on 9 April 2024, with the bulk of its obligations applying from October 2025. It limits how political ads can be targeted, bans the use of special category data (political opinions, ethnicity, religion, health) for ad targeting without explicit consent, prohibits foreign sponsors in the three months before an election, requires record-keeping in machine-readable format for seven years, and threatens fines of up to 6% of global annual turnover. Meta, Google, Microsoft, Adform, Equativ, and several others ran the numbers and decided it was cheaper to ban political ads in the EU outright than to comply. TikTok had already done so globally years before.

But what matters politically is not the legal text. What matters is the behavioural response. Campaigns did what campaigns always do when one channel closes. They found another route. Not because professionals are uniquely cynical. Because elections still happen, candidates still need attention, and organisations adapt faster than regulation.

The result is what some of us have started calling the **Shadow Campaign**. A digital architecture built on Proxy Pages (lifestyle, "local news" and community pages that happen to favour a candidate), micro-influencers, organic virality, and AI-generated content that walks the edge of the regulation without crossing into formal political advertising. The Liberties.eu monitoring briefs from Hungary have documented all of these tactics in detail, and Brussels regulators are aware. The DSA team and the TTPA team are already discussing how to close the gaps.

#### What do we need to consider as professionals?

This piece is a description of what is happening, and what political professionals need to internalise to operate in the new environment. There is a real ethical conversation we need to have about the spirit of the TTPA, and I will come to that at the end. First, the field lessons.

#### Lesson 1: From Targeting Voters to Mapping Environments

- **The tech influence**: Under Article 18 of the TTPA, the use of special category data (as defined by Article 9 of the GDPR) for political ad targeting is prohibited without freely given, explicit consent. The platforms' answer was to remove the option entirely. The precision-guided era of campaigning, where you served a different ad to a 34-year-old undecided voter in a specific postcode based on their digital footprint, is over for paid placement in the EU.

- **The political lesson**: Campaigns are now buying environments rather than individuals. You are no longer buying a voter. You are buying a community context.

What this looks like in practice:

- **Build a psychographic map of digital communities, not voters.** Spend a week auditing Facebook groups, Subreddits, Discord servers, TikTok hashtag communities, and Telegram channels that map onto your target geography or demographic. Document each one with a one-page profile: tone, dominant topics, rhythms (when do people post most), key voices, taboos, in-jokes. This is the new equivalent of the canvassing map.

- **Train your content team to write inside the dialect of each community.** A foodie group does not want a candidate's policy memo. It wants a story about how a candidate's economic plan affects the cost of running a small restaurant. The message is the same. The grammar is different. If your team cannot tell the difference between the two, you are still operating on 2019 instincts.

- **Develop "issue carriers" instead of campaign ads.** An issue carrier is a piece of content (a meme, a short video, a community testimonial, a quiet observation) that carries a campaign theme inside a contextual wrapper. The wrapper is what travels through the algorithm. The theme is what lands in the audience's head. This is harder, slower, and less measurable than the targeting era. Get used to that.

#### Lesson 2: What Campaigns Learn When Advertising Stops Working

- **The tech influence**: Disguising campaign messaging as generic, community-centric content is currently the most common way to bypass the algorithmic filters and the self-imposed platform bans that catch traditional political ads.

- **The political lesson**: Modern campaign digital directors are starting to operate more like media holding companies than traditional advertisers. A word before the practical bit, because this is where ethics and tactics collide: building a hidden architecture of "neutral" pages that exist to launder partisan content is a strategy that wins short-term and corrodes the trust infrastructure of democracy long-term. The reason the TTPA exists is precisely because campaigns in 2016, 2017, and 2019 lost the public's permission to operate in the shadows. Doubling down on shadow tactics is a strategic dead end for the profession, even when it works for an individual race. That said, here is what professionals need to understand about how these networks actually work, because you will see them, you will be asked about them, and you will need to decide where your campaign sits.

- **The architecture is usually three layers.** A top layer of overtly partisan accounts and the official campaign. A middle layer of allied creators, columnists, and local community page admins who lean the same direction without coordination. A bottom layer of pages that present themselves as lifestyle, hyperlocal, or community-interest, which post content that frames issues in a candidate-friendly way without endorsements.

- **The middle layer is where most of the action sits.** Allied creators do not need to be paid in the way a contractor is paid. They are often offered access, exclusives, briefings, behind-the-scenes content, or attention from the candidate. Article 3 of the TTPA captures content "normally provided for remuneration" or as part of a political advertising campaign, which means in-kind benefits can fall within scope. A free lunch with the candidate, repeated weekly, can become a regulatory question.

- **If your campaign is using or considering proxy infrastructure, build a paper trail of your own decisions.** Document why each relationship exists, what your campaign's actual relationship with each page admin is, and what financial or in-kind transfers happen. When a regulator comes knocking, the absence of documentation hurts you more than the documentation will.

- **Decide where your line is, in writing, before the campaign starts.** Compliance is a question you answer in the planning phase, not in the middle of a crisis at 11pm three weeks before election day.

#### Lesson 3: Organic Reach Stops Being a Bonus and Becomes Infrastructure

- **The tech influence**: Without predictable paid reach on Meta and Google, campaigns are now entirely at the mercy of organic recommendation algorithms (TikTok's For You Page, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts).

- **The political lesson**: Content production has to prioritise high-engagement, shareable formats over polished, traditional campaign spots. Rapid-response memes, short-form video hooks, and micro-influencers are no longer fringe tactics. They are the primary engine for digital reach. If your content does not trigger algorithmic amplification on its own merits, your message effectively does not exist.

What this means for your structure and budgets:

- **Hire creators, not just communicators.** The classic campaign hire was a press officer or a digital comms manager. You now need at least one full-time short-form video editor on staff, ideally two. They should be able to turn a clip from a candidate's speech into a 25-second cut with text overlay within two hours of the speech ending. If they cannot, you are not yet operating at TTPA-era pace.

- **Build a content engine, not a content calendar.** A calendar is a list of posts. An engine is a process: capture (always-on filming of the candidate and the campaign), edit (rapid cuts within hours), test (small drops on secondary accounts before main ones), amplify (briefing allied accounts when something hits), retire (taking down underperformers). Aim for three to seven pieces of short-form content per day across platforms, not one or two polished posts per week.

- **Make your candidate filmable.** This is the unglamorous part. Most candidates over fifty hate being filmed casually. They want their suit, their lectern, their script. You now need them on a phone camera, in a kitchen, in a car, talking unscripted for ninety seconds at a time. If your principal refuses to do this, you have a strategic problem that no platform can fix and no consultant can compensate for.

- **Track what travels, not what you posted.** Set up listening on every platform for variations of your candidate's name, your key issues, and your opponents. The point is not vanity metrics. The point is detecting when something is moving on its own so you can pour fuel on it within the hour.

#### Lesson 4: Build for Scrutiny, Not Plausible Deniability

- **The tech influence**: Brussels regulators are not blind to the proxy page loophole. The TTPA was designed to work in tandem with the Digital Services Act. Articles 34 and 35 of the DSA require Very Large Online Platforms to assess and mitigate systemic risks to electoral integrity. The European Board for Digital Services coordinates cross-border cases. Each Member State has had to designate a competent authority for TTPA enforcement, and most have done so. Fines reach 6% of global turnover.

- **The political lesson**: Any digital architecture you build for a campaign should assume scrutiny, and any single page in your network should be treated as expendable.

What this looks like in practice:

- **Decentralise.** If your campaign's digital ground game depends on five large pages, the loss of two of them is fatal. If it depends on fifty small pages, an allied newsletter network, a Discord community, three messaging app groups, and a stable of micro-influencers, the loss of any single node is a setback, not a collapse. Build a hydra, not a hierarchy.

- **Keep the official campaign account impeccably clean.** The official campaign website and verified accounts should be model citizens of TTPA compliance. Transparency notices, sponsor declarations, the lot. Whatever happens in the wider ecosystem, the official asset should never be the regulatory case study.

- **Maintain an audit trail of editorial decisions.** If a content piece on an allied page is later flagged as coordinated political advertising, your defence depends on being able to show that editorial decisions were made independently. Verbal coordination leaves no audit trail. When a regulator asks "who decided what," that absence becomes a vulnerability rather than protection.

- **Budget for legal and compliance as a campaign function, not an afterthought.** A campaign without a compliance lead is making a strategic bet that no one is watching. Given the 6% turnover fine and the reputational damage of being made an example, that is a bad bet, particularly for parties that hope to govern next.

#### Lesson 5: Political Advertising Becomes Ambient

- **The tech influence**: The TTPA's definition of political advertising under Article 3(2) catches any message "liable and designed to influence the outcome of an election or referendum, voting behaviour or a legislative or regulatory process." This is intentionally broad. The Commission's July 2025 guidance widened the practical reading even further.

- **The political lesson**: Direct calls to action ("Vote for X on the 14th") are obviously in scope. So are issue ads on contested policy areas during election periods. The grey zone is content that creates a mood favourable to a candidate without naming them. Most modern campaigning will move into that grey zone, whether we like it or not.

What to actually do:

- **Sanitise your contractual relationships.** Anyone producing content for the campaign, even content that does not name the candidate, should be on a documented arrangement that makes the political nature clear. Trying to disguise the contract is worse than disclosing it, because the contract is the easy thing for an auditor to find.

- **Shift KPIs from persuasion to mobilisation.** Persuasion metrics ("are voters changing their minds because of our ads?") were always overstated and are now mostly unmeasurable. Mobilisation metrics ("are our voters showing up?") are what predict outcomes. Reframe your war room around turnout indicators, volunteer activation, event attendance, and small-dollar donation patterns.

- **Invest in mood, not just message.** Your digital outreach is now ambient. You are setting the temperature of the conversation, amplifying local grievances, framing the country's mood. This is less satisfying than landing a clean attack line, and it is also more powerful, because it is the substrate on which everything else lands.

- **Train candidates to be issue carriers themselves.** The most effective post in 2026 often comes from the candidate, on their own account, talking about a school in their district, filmed by a staffer on a phone. That kind of content is editorial speech in most readings, not regulated political advertising. The candidate is the medium. Your role is to make them comfortable being one.

#### So, What Should Campaign Professionals Actually Do on Monday Morning?

If you take only five things from this piece, take these:

**Tip #1: Run a TTPA audit of your current operation this week.** Map every paid relationship, every page you control or influence, and every piece of content under your campaign's banner. If you cannot draw the org chart of your digital ecosystem on a single page, you do not have an ecosystem. You have a liability.

**Tip #2: Hire or assign a compliance lead before you hire your next strategist.** Make this person a peer of the digital director, not a subordinate. They should have veto power on activity that creates regulatory exposure. If you do not have someone in that role by next month, your campaign is operating uninsured.

**Tip #3: Move budget from paid placement to in-house creators and rapid editing capability.** The money you used to spend on Meta ads has to go somewhere. The highest-return reallocation right now is on people who can turn raw footage into shareable short-form video within two hours.

**Tip #4: Decide your ethical line and write it down.** Will your campaign use proxy pages? Will you pay influencers without disclosure? Will you coordinate with allied accounts? Whatever you decide, put it in a one-page document signed by the campaign principal. When a junior staffer makes a borderline call at 11pm three weeks before the election, they should know what your campaign stands for without having to call you.

**Tip #5: Plan for the post-mortem before the campaign starts.** Whatever you do in the next campaign cycle will be reviewed, by regulators, by journalists, by historians of EU democracy. Choose now whether you want to be a case study in adaptation or a case study in circumvention.

***Is the TTPA going to clean up political campaigning in Europe? Probably not on its own.***

But it has done one useful thing. It has forced our profession to choose, openly, what kind of digital politics we want to practise. The shadow tactics work in the short term and erode public trust in the long term. The organic, contextual, community-anchored alternative is harder, slower, and less glamorous. It is also the only version of this job that is sustainable past the next regulatory round.
