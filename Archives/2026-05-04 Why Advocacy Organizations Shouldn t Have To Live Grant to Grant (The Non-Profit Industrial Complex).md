In 2007, a collective of women of colour and feminist organizations published an anthology titled *The Revolution Will Not Be Funded.* It introduced people to a term that had been circulating in activist circles for years: the nonprofit industrial complex.

> The argument they made was that the funding structures that underpin non-profit changemaking organizations redirect energy away from structural change and toward compliance, service delivery, and institutional survival.

Sound familiar?

**Look, the philanthropists and foundations funding this work are, by and large, people motivated by a strong desire to make the world better with the resources they have.** The organizations receiving that funding are full of talented, committed people doing hard work in really difficult conditions. Nobody intentionally designed this system to be self-defeating. And yet, in many ways it is.

Here's my analyis. Say a foundation wants to fund impact. Understandably, they want to measure it, and in fact are often required to measure it. So they ask for outcomes frameworks, evaluation reports, acquittals, strategic plans tied to grant cycles. None of this is unreasonable, in isolation. But the cumulative effect on a small or mid-sized advocacy org is significant. What tends to happen is that staff time is diverted to reporting rather than campaigning. Then, over time, programmes get shaped around what's fundable rather than what's needed or mission aligned.

**Leaders spend so much of their energy managing upward to funders rather than outward to the communities they serve.** And because the next grant depends on demonstrating the success of the last one, there's a structural incentive to overstate results and understate challenges.

Grant cycles also tend to be short, often one to three years, which is completely at odds with the time horizon of genuine social change. You can't build a movement in twelve months. You can't shift a policy environment in two years. But you can always produce a report that says you did, and that's what the non-profit industrial complex rewards.

So where does that leave us? We have organizations living grant to grant, in a permanent state of financial unease, unable to take the strategic risks required to actually win on their issues. Leaders who at their core are bold, are driven to become cautious. Boards become risk-averse, as organizational survival becomes the mission. The actual founding mission can fall through the cracks.

**This isn't anyone's fault. It's the incentive structure, and incentive structures shape our behaviour regardless of intention, and sometimes without our knowledge.**

The question I find more useful than blame is: what can organizations actually do about it, given that most can't simply opt out of the funding system they depend on?

A few thoughts...

**The first is to start building toward financial independence, even slowly, even partially.** This means diversified revuene! Grassroots small-dollar fundraising, owned media channels, membership models, subscriptions, even merchandise have all been used by progressive organisations to create new revenue streams that they themselves control. None of these is a silver bullet and the right model is different for every organisation. But every dollar of independent revenue is a little peice of strategic freedom.

**The second is to name this tension explicitly inside your organisation.** This is about surfacing a truth, as a strategic practice, ot about complaining for no reason. Ask yourself, and your team, what are we doing because it's right, and what are we doing because it's fundable? Where do those overlap, and where do they diverge? Most non-profits don’t have this conversation openly, which undermines strategic clarity and trust.

**The third is to have the same conversation with your funders**, if you have the kind of relationship that allows it (acknowledging that this conversaiton takes some preparation and fortitude!). Many philanthropists are more aware of this problem than you might assume, and there are some great funders trying to do this differently through longer grant cycles, and general operating budget support. But grantees rarely raise these underlying structural issues directly. Finding ways to have that vital conversation, carefully and with the relationship in mind, is an essential act of leadership.

I’m not naive enough to think any of this takes down the nonprofit industrial complex. This is a system, deeply embedded in culture, that been built over decades. It is self reinforcing. But starting to talk about it, and think about the ways it places a drag on the impact of advoacy organizations, is the first step.
