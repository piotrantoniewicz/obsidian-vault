A while back, I wrote an article titled, “ [There is No AI for Good.](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/ai-good-rev-tracy-kronzak-mpa--6rbsc/)” At the time, I acknowledged that this was a cynical perspective, and that I very much wanted to be wrong, but what I’ve seen since has only re-affirmed this personal sentiment. One thing I definitely got wrong in the first article was the rate of AI acceleration and development – what I pondered would take years has been condensed to months and shows no signs of slowing down. **I’m not afraid of AI, but I am afraid of its owners. I’m not anti-AI, but I am pro-Democracy.**

It bears describing what I mean when I say, “ *for Good.*” More than 25 years ago, during the first-ever Dot-Com boom, a line of thinking emerged that for organizations doing social good work, using the growth and development of the Internet and cloud-based technology would accelerate their ability to better understand constituent needs, reach people with personal and near real-time communication, and have a truly public domain in which information could be freely exchanged would lead to a stronger social safety net, and over time, bring people together around causes and needs while strengthening our democracy. **Does this feel like the era through which we are now living?**

The direction I’ve seen things moving feels very much like we are going further away from democratization of AI, and [I’m not alone in this opinion](https://www.techpolicy.press/the-trump-ai-action-plan-is-deregulation-framed-as-innovation/). Certainly, this is all in the context of the. I live with a professional marketer as my spouse, who frequently reminds me that if you must continuously hype your own output to get traction, maybe the output itself isn’t the best quality. The White House [literally refers to its own AI “action plan,” as “widely-acclaimed,” and “visionary.](https://www.whitehouse.gov/articles/2025/07/wide-acclaim-for-president-trumps-visionary-ai-action-plan/)” Reading through the list of endorsements is a veritable Who’s-Who of right-wing and Big Tech sycophants: Michael Dell, Anthropic, Meta, Palantir, Heritage Foundation, and many more. **Absent from this list are organizations and people whose genuine concern is the democratization of technology and making its access, ethics, transparency, and affordability universal in our country.** Further, the current Presidential administration is [removing guardrails](https://apnews.com/article/trump-ai-artificial-intelligence-3763ca207561a3fe8b35327f9ce7ca73) on the ethical use of AI, accelerating the buildout of AI data centers, and bludgeoning the hard-fought right for states to regulate AI by withholding federal funding.

Of particular note in the AI action plan is the twisted invocation of removing “woke” bias from AI, failing to recognize that how AI itself interprets and incorporates bias [is poorly understood at best](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/ai-models-can-secretly-influence-one-another-owls-rcna221583). Similarly, **the buildout of AI data centers is, by my own reasoning, one of the crucial environmental justice issues we will face in the 21st Century**. This was something that came to my personal attention [in late 2024](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbestechcouncil/2024/08/16/the-silent-burden-of-ai-unveiling-the-hidden-environmental-costs-of-data-centers-by-2030/), and has subsequently been [studied by MIT](https://impactclimate.mit.edu/2025/03/20/investigating-the-ecological-impacts-of-data-centers/), and [reported on](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/the-growing-environmental-impact-of-ai-data-centers-energy-demands) by the Public Broadcasting Corporation.

I currently live in a city that [lends its name to an element on the Periodic Table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livermorium), and is also the home to the [first successful nuclear fusion output greater than its input](https://www.llnl.gov/article/49306/lawrence-livermore-national-laboratory-achieves-fusion-ignition). Unlike the nuclear industry, which now has incredibly strong federal regulations and safety guidelines, we’re living through the “ [Demon Core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demon_core),” era of AI development **with the express desire to NOT learn from our mistakes for the betterment of AI**.

I’ve frequently called into question in my public statements the wisdom of imbuing AI into all of our daily interactions based on its [problematic effects on cognition](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/harnessing-hybrid-intelligence/202506/the-psychology-of-ais-impact-on-human-cognition), especially in an era when we’re *already* aware of the [deleterious effects of social media](https://www.publichealth.columbia.edu/news/just-how-harmful-social-media-our-experts-weigh). **I’m deeply troubled by the current turn of events related to AI because they represent a deliberate shunning of scientific inquiry, environmental impact, and transparent access to the core technology by its users.** The arguments for building AI data centers sound increasingly like the justifications we used for mining uranium and testing nuclear weapons just aside Native land, building oil and gas refineries to support the burgeoning auto industry, and foregoing auto safety measures to support “innovation.” A prelude of these consequences is already [playing out near Memphis](https://www.npr.org/2024/09/11/nx-s1-5088134/elon-musk-ai-xai-supercomputer-memphis-pollution).

Ask Ralph Nader about the process of getting “ [Unsafe at Any Speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unsafe_at_Any_Speed:_The_Designed-In_Dangers_of_the_American_Automobile) ” to print. Talk to [Downwinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Downwinders) about their experiences with the American nuclear program. Research racial justice and public policy and write policy briefs about the effects of redlining, locating refineries, and Jim Crow as I once did 20 years ago for my nonprofit organization. You’ll see stunning and disturbing parallels in our current arguments for AI “innovation” that are as real as the justifications used 40, 60, and 80 years ago that precipitated lasting health crisis and set entire communities apart from the so-called American Dream. **“Innovation” always comes at a cost, and history is repeating itself before our very eyes** – this is not a JFK-esque call to aspire to the best ourselves for the common goal of a shared dream. **This is an unregulated, uninformed, purposely obfuscated drive to achieve technological dominance that is expressly not a public good.**

Before the argument is made that the spoils go to the victor, and we’re in some kind of new "Space Race" for AI, and progress at any cost will ensure our country’s future in perpetuity, please [turn the page to the work being done in Norway](https://dig.watch/resource/norways-national-strategy-for-artificial-intelligence). We’ve only been told, repeatedly and loudly, that there is no other way forward for our country, **but there is clear evidence that there are better, more democratic, transparent, and yes, government-supported ways of achieving AI as a public good**. This is what the path forward for “AI for Good” looks like, and our country is shunning it with the deliberate turning of a blind eye by much of Silicon Valley, if not it's cooperation.

These are all, of course, macro critiques, and the challenges of ethics and policy we face. I’ve largely avoided micro critiques of AI, because I am a consumer of it. I’ve used it for mass dataset analysis, consuming disparate information sources for trendlines, predictive analytics, and even ski form improvement. **None of my words here should be interpreted as some kind of FUD (fear, uncertainty, doubt) that AI** ***as a technology*** **is somehow “bad,” but do ask yourself these questions**:

- For the parents out there, do you worry about your children’s use of social media and “smart” technology at large, and try to limit their consumption of it? **Are you doing the same with AI?**
- There are many excellent resources including those at [NTEN](https://www.nten.org/learn/resource-hubs/artificial-intelligence) and the [newly-published book](https://www.ai4np.org/) by [Cheryl Contee](https://www.linkedin.com/in/cherylcontee/) for understanding how to adopt AI, and the considerations in doing so. But we still lack any clear federal policy that unifies our approach as a country that creates consistency of adoption and use based on these principles. ***Why hasn’t Silicon Valley and its executive leaders rallied behind federal AI legislation that truly protects consumers and communities from the externalities of AI?***
- Sam Altman just stated that [there is no privacy when using AI as a therapist](https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/25/sam-altman-warns-theres-no-legal-confidentiality-when-using-chatgpt-as-a-therapist/) while giving a nod to the many who do, but hasn’t lifted a finger to actually prevent this use in his own product. Even the [State of Nevada takes gambling addition more seriously](https://www.nevadacouncil.org/). **Is this the kind of blind comfort you want for your use of any technology?**

In my first article, I posited that AI itself will never become a foundational technology because its current owners seek to maximize profit over public good. I cannot personally trust something as a resource for my decision-making when I know its only goal is to make money from me. **It is, however, a fundamental technology because we cannot avoid its use, and it is already changing how we behave and think – just like email and electricity.** But the protocols used for email sending are deliberately transparent and public, and this is not the case for most AI models and integrations. The state of AI, policy, and ethics – never mind conflicts of interest and all the other macro concerns I’ve outlined here feels much more like [breaking apart Ma Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakup_of_the_Bell_System) only so that it could reform under a new name.

Perhaps a better analogy for our current era might be drawn from my own time in Russia during 1993 and 1994: Every citizen was given a “ [Privatization Voucher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatization_in_Russia) ” in which they could theoretically choose to invest in former state-owned industries, but the existing conditions of poverty, inequitable access, and misinformation weren’t considered as part of the program, and because of that and many other factors today we have the Russian Oligarchs. I sometimes cynically joke that some of my favorite memories from that time in Russia include hiding under the dormitory windows with my friend Lisa while waiting for the riots and gunfire on the streets to stop. **But the magnitude of the impact of AI on our society, governance, and ownership of our own minds is equally as severe, and as a technological accelerant, will continue to press on all the current inequities in our country in an equally severe manner.** I can attest, firsthand, it is not a pleasant experience to live through a country that is tearing itself to pieces at an accelerated rate, as the privatization program once did in Russia.

**More than anything, I want you to leave this piece of writing asking if we’re taking the wrong path to potentially the right outcomes, and does the end truly justify the means?** If you live with these doubts, as I do, then *do something*.

- Speak up in your Boardrooms, demand that AI adopted, created, and used by your company is ethical, transparent, and manages its own externalities. We have Leed standards for buildings, for example, where are they for AI?
- Demand federal AI ethics legislation.
- Understand the true externalities of the technology you’re consuming, which isn't the same as opting out of its use.
- Demand federal data privacy legislation that protects people first.
- Pay attention to the efforts of the people and organizations who are taking AI’s possibilities as seriously as we do our nuclear programs.

There are paths forward in this era that are wholly-owned by consumers, or as I frequently consider, nonprofits as an impact economy – find them for yourself and for your organization, and **demand better and more of both Silicon Valley and the federal government.**
