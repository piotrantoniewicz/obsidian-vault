Power

20.04.2026

There is a question that every political journalist eventually has to sit with: who would benefit from reading everything on my phone or laptop right now? For a Brussels-based reporter chasing a corporate lobbying story, the answer in the worst case might be a law firm with a private investigator. For an exiled journalist covering a war launched by their own government, the answer involves an intelligence service with functionally unlimited resources. The problem is the same in structure, but the threat level is not.

The problem of cybersecurity is not limited to journalists. Anyone working in politics (including campaigners, digital leads, consultants, comms strategists, chiefs of staff) handles sensitive information that somebody else would like to see. The practices that keep journalists safe are directly transferable to anyone whose work involves sources, strategy, or opposition research.

\-

By Viktorya Muradyan

In order to get more insights how cybersecurity is handled by political journalists on a daily basis, I had a conversation with two journalists operating at different ends of the threat spectrum — an investigative reporter covering EU institutions, and an exiled Russian journalist now based in Europe.

#### Understand your threat model before choosing your tools

In cybersecurity, [a threat model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_model#cite_note-:1-1) asks simple questions: what are you protecting, from whom, and what can they realistically do to get it?

The investigative reporter I spoke with has a clear-eyed read on their own risk: "I mainly think about legal threats. It's possible that one of the companies I am writing about hires a private investigator. But beyond that, I don't really plan for more." While democratic practices are not perfect in all European countries, both public and private actors, mostly are constrained by rule of law.

The exiled Russian journalist operates in a world where those rules do not apply. Their adversary is a state security apparatus that can intercept SMS messages, manipulate mobile carriers, and deploy commercial spyware. "From the very start of my career I was cautious and vigilant," they told me. After a colleague working on a sensitive investigation linked to Russian domestic politics visited their apartment, they feared the security services would note the connection and target them — not because of anything they had done, but because of proximity. "I thought it was very irresponsible from my friend to put me in that situation." Even though the journalist left Russia and the zone of imminent danger, threat does not respect borders.

What makes this distinction between two political journalists increasingly uncomfortable is that the gap between these two worlds is narrowing. A quite recent research by [Garbe and Maerz](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13510347.2025.2579105) describes how surveillance practices are no longer confined to autocracies but creeping into democratic politics. Pegasus spyware has been found on journalists' phones across Jordan, Mexico, Serbia, Morocco, and the Baltic states. The Pegasus Project investigation in 2021 identified at least 180 journalists worldwide as possible targets. And spyware is only one of the possible cybersecurity risks political journalists face.

The bigger picture is significantly worse. Several studies from DC-based Center for News, Technology & Innovation (CNTI) mention [quite significant numbers of cyber risks](https://cnti.org/surveys/what-it-means-to-do-journalism-in-the-age-of-ai-journalist-views-on-safety-technology-and-government/security/), ranging from social media cloning and phishing to doxxing and DDoS attacks. CNTI also [claims that](https://cnti.org/surveys/what-it-means-to-do-journalism-in-the-age-of-ai-journalist-views-on-safety-technology-and-government/security/) *"Journalists in less democratic countries are more confident in their ability to respond to breaches, but not to recognise them."*

#### Protect your sources as much, if not more than you protect yourself

Source protection is one of the key priorities of political journalists, particularly because so much of our communication is happening across borders and online. Both journalists I interviewed agreed without hesitation: source protection comes at the top of their priorities, sometimes even before self-protection. But what that looks like in practice varies enormously.

The European investigative reporter I talked to mentioned that they never put clear names of sources on cloud-based story drafts and prefer to store them on a local hard drive. Besides more obvious missteps, journalists should also be careful about invisible signals. Sources are usually more careless in their communication than they should be, mostly because not every conversation starts from the intention to be a whistleblower. Sometimes, a very usual exchange can lead you to a public information that no one has looked at. Sources from the corporate world sometimes start conversations from their work email, unaware that their reply or input might even be used in an investigation eventually. *"I don’t want my source to be accidentally exposed just because we are connected on LinkedIn,"* says the investigative reporter.

#### Build your communications hygiene

The messaging app Signal is the baseline for secure communication at the moment as most of Europe it is already widely adopted among both journalists and officials. For anything more sensitive, encrypted emails provide another alternative, though as the European reporter noted, *"not many people know how to encrypt an email".* The knowledge barrier to use PGP (Pretty Good Privacy, providing end-to-end encryption for email) makes people rely mostly on messengers like Signal.

The exiled journalist operates with a more layered setup. As a first step, they switched from a Russian mobile carrier to an EU number immediately upon leaving Russia — a step many exiled colleagues still have not taken, even years after the Russian invasion to Ukraine. The reason is concrete: Russian security services have exploited telecom infrastructure to intercept SMS-based authentication.

When doing online investigations, the Russian journalist maintains multiple phone numbers and separate Telegram accounts under different names to move through chat groups without being identified. *"There is always a risk of exposure when you are batch texting potential sources on Telegram channels. It has happened to me during an investigation on the wives of Russian soldiers".* The journalist has managed to infiltrate the respective groups, but eventually was exposed by the admin.

The human layer is the gap that no technology can fully close.

#### VPNs, spyware, and passwords

Password hygiene sounds like the most boring advice in cybersecurity. It is also the thing that actually stops attacks. The exiled journalist described repeated attempts to reset their social media passwords — including after visiting the Russian embassy premises and after publishing their first byline for a major European outlet. Basic password managers and two-factor authentication were what kept them out. *"I use all the apps that generate all these long passwords,"* they said. *"And I have two-factor authentication everywhere."*

VPNs also have become an essential tool across the board for safer search and access to information. Outline VPN, an open-source tool built by Jigsaw (a technology incubator created by Google), lets you set up your own private proxy server, which makes harder for an adversary to block or monitor than commercial VPN alternatives. The exiled journalist uses it for their entire family. The European reporter uses a corporate password manager, but when it comes to a VPN, it’s used more "selectively".

For higher-threat contexts, where spyware might be involved, two specific tools deserve attention. [Apple's Lockdown Mode](https://support.apple.com/en-gb/guide/iphone/iph845f6f40c/ios) is an extreme protection optional feature for iPhone which is recommended to be used only if you believe you might be targeted by a highly sophisticated cyberattack, such as state-sponsored spyware. And one tip that sounds almost absurd but essential is restarting your phone regularly: [rebooting your devices’ system disrupts spyware that operates in volatile memory.](https://www.zdnet.com/article/rebooting-your-phone-daily-is-your-best-defense-against-zero-click-attacks-heres-why/)

#### Know what your adversary already knows about you

Beyond accessing general information you might have gathered on your adversary, a key aspect of cyber risk involves gaining access to sensitive data that is critical to your security.

Services like Probiv in Russia compile taxi rides, food delivery orders, passport numbers, and residential histories into searchable profiles*. "There is a whole service built to get all the info ranging from your taxi rides and food delivery orders,"* they said. Another service, Glaz Boga (Eye of God), a Russian partially-paid Telegram bot for obtaining personal data, sells packaged access to personal information for anyone who has been on Russian territory.

This is not a problem confined to authoritarian states. [Data brokers](https://netzpolitik.org/databroker-files/) operate globally, and personal information available through breached databases and commercial aggregators is growing everywhere. The European reporter described their own lighter version: *"I google myself once in a while. My home address, for example, is luckily not publicly available, but once in a while I check if it might be."*

For political professionals and journalists, this self-audit is one of the simplest and most valuable habits you can build. Google yourself, check what an adversary could piece together, review your publicly visible associations.

#### What you can do for your cybersecurity today

- **Assess your own threat model honestly**: Who is your likely adversary, what do they want, and what resources do they have?

- **Audit your source exposure:** Check your LinkedIn connections, your cloud-stored documents, your metadata. Keep source-identifying material on local, encrypted drives.

- **Move to encrypted communications by default:** Signal is the baseline. If sources or colleagues will not use it, at minimum enable disappearing messages.

- **Use a password manager and enable two-factor authentication everywhere.**

- **Run a regular self-audit** on your digital footprint: Google yourself, check whether any sensitive data about you is publicly available, review what a motivated adversary could learn about you.

- If you work from or report on **authoritarian contexts:** change your phone carrier, enable the iPhone Lockdown Mode, reboot your devices regularly.

- And finally, [**learn from your peers**](https://nieman.harvard.edu/digital-security-resources-for-journalists/)**,** check out [online training programmes](https://globalcyberalliance.org/work/gca-cybersecurity-toolkit/gca-cybersecurity-toolkit-for-journalists/) — one good tip might change the way you protect yourself radically.

To conclude, it is also important to highlight that in many cases, cybersecurity is treated as an individual responsibility rather than a structural one. That is why, it falls onto the shoulder of political journalists and other professionals to learn new habits, adopt new tools in order to protect their work and themselves better.
