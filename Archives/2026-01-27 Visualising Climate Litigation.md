**Photography** can be a powerful tool in helping audiences to engage with and understand complex, technical issues. In the context of climate litigation, images can help to build and tell a compelling narrative, engaging and informing audiences, playing an important communications role and driving public awareness of legal cases. Yet despite this potential, all too often, the imagery of climate litigation misses this opportunity.

Limited to familiar photographs of people on court steps, or stock images of corporate offices, the visual language of climate litigation is often restricted to depicting a technical process or functioning as surface-level illustration alongside text. But what about the realities of the human and environmental stories being fought in court? Photography can bring those realities into sharp focus for audiences, connecting technical details and context with relatable, engaging, human stories.

This resource, informed by conversations across the litigation, photography, journalism and NGO sectors, aims to provide practical guidance for making and distributing engaging visual storytelling of climate litigation, making the most of the opportunities that effective photography offers, and helping to get the right images into the right hands and in front of audiences.

## What is the story you want to tell?

Start by clearly identifying the story that you want to tell through photography. This process should happen as early on in the litigation proceedings as possible - the more time within a case you can involve and consider imagery, the more complete the story will be. Even if you expect to only need imagery towards the end of a case, start planning, and producing, early.

By identifying the story, you can begin to develop key visual priorities - people, places and activities that are important components of the complete narrative. Don’t limit images to the ***who*** and the ***where*** of the litigation, but consider how you can visualise the ***why.*** This is a key opportunity for photography, building understanding of the context and nuance of the proceedings, helping audiences to see for themselves why this case matters.

Use documentary photography to build a compelling visual case that illustrates the issue at the heart of the case - what is going on now? What has changed? What will happen if this carries on? From here, you can prioritise key information in the images, for example where possible introducing the viewer to the claimants, the location or environment at stake, and the activities the case is fighting against.

## Humanise the story

Highlighting the humanity at the heart of the story helps audiences to understand, engage and relate to the issues at hand. These people-centred stories can be effective ways to anchor what might otherwise be difficult to imagine or understand statistics and technical details.

Consider who is affected by the case, and how. Why are they involved in proceedings? What is their reality and how might a case affect them? Identify the key characters and perspectives involved. These could be claimants or maybe the legal team working on the case. Look for opportunities to visualise their story in relation to the case, using this to develop key details.

In doing this, don’t limit the imagery to posed portraits. Whilst portraiture is a useful and impactful component of visual storytelling, including more documentary imagery that shows the viewer a contextualised reality can be much more powerful. Instead of only telling them that someone is involved, an environmental portrait adds useful depth and context. When seeking media coverage of climate litigation cases, having a broader spread of imagery beyond posed portraits strengthens your media package and provides more opportunities for compelling image displays.

For a successful environmental portrait, make sure to use the space around the subject in the frame to communicate additional information to the viewer. This can be a useful tool in distilling ***why*** the person pictured is involved, and why the story is important. If you were to photograph someone on the steps of the court, you are limiting the information communicated to the viewer - that this person is involved and this is what they look like. However, if you were to photograph them in a context relevant to the case, and to frame the images in a way to keep that information in the image, you immediately distill valuable, engaging information and communicate this to your audience.

Photographing involved individuals in their home context can also be helpful to make the story relevant to specific audiences; a lot of climate litigation cases cross national borders, so the final courtroom events might take place in a country far from the home of the claimants. Think of the audience back home - they are more likely to be emotionally invested in a story illustrating people from their homeland, rather than on courtroom steps outside a building on the other side of the world. Images should be relevant to the people who are going to experience the outcome of the cases wherever possible.

A good example of this is photographs by Agence France-Presse (AFP) photographers Luka Gonzales and Patrik Stollarz of Saul Luciano Lliuya, a farmer and tourist guide in the Peruvian Andes, involved in a legal battle against German energy giant RWE seeking to hold them responsible for the melting of the Peruvian glaciers and rising threats from glacial lake outburst floods. Through a combination of scenes in the Andes in Peru and in a court in Essen in Germany, the images add vital visual information, helping the viewer to see a more complete picture of the story, and understand more about the person at the heart of the case by placing them at the heart of the story.

Photographing the people at the centre of climate litigation can also communicate a powerful message of solidarity and community. By representing the groups, NGOs, and individuals who are involved in cases, and photographing in a way which demonstrates and highlights their agency, images can act to empower those who are often dismissed as climate victims.

### Real stories of real people

By highlighting humanity in climate litigation cases, audiences can understand that these are not always superheroes, but are often ordinary people like them.

Photographing ordinary people in exceptional circumstances such as international courts can create a surprising and inspiring image. Think of the [Swiss women](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2024/apr/09/human-rights-violated-inaction-climate-echr-rules-landmark-case) at the European Court of Human Rights, or [images of very young people](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/sep/16/young-climate-activists-lawsuit-trump-fossil-fuels) testifying in Montana, for example.

Be careful not to exclusively rely on images defining people as ‘exceptional’, however. In some contexts, this can be an important way to gain media and public attention of climate litigation and raise awareness with eye-catching surprising imagery, but in other cases it risks implying only a small group of exceptional individuals are able or suited to take action - rather than litigation being a tool for society.

For example, be wary of the “David and Goliath” narrative - while it can be a powerful way to tell the story in some instances, remember that there are often many individual actors and faces behind both sides of a legal case, and depicting a lone plaintiff pitted against a nameless, faceless entity such as corporation or government can be problematic. For example, this implies no obligation or responsibility of the individuals who make decisions in the corporations or governments involved in the case, and gives no representation to the lawyers and other faces behind the court case itself. In general, it’s a more complex story than this framing implies.

## Understand your audience

Who is the intended audience of your photographs? What do they care about, and why will they be interested in this story?

These are essential questions to have in mind whilst planning your photography, which, when considered alongside the key elements of the story you’re looking to tell, can help you to develop and prioritise your imagery.

For example, you might choose to focus on photographs of the impacts being felt by an affected community, or highlight damage to the environment and nature. Your audience might be particularly interested in an effect on global supply chains, or maybe damage to culturally significant sites. Identifying these focusses of interest will help you to photograph stories that resonate with your intended audience.

For more general audiences, consider how you can use photography to capture interest and provide an entry point to a complex story - show emotionally powerful impacts and how they affect communities, wildlife and the environment, connect local issues with the global picture, show how communities have come together for a better future, and show what is being done to achieve this. In some contexts around the world, awareness of climate litigation is low, and engaging, impactful images can be an important way to draw people into these stories, making them aware of this global movement. Compelling imagery can also help make climate litigation reporting more likely to appear in mainstream or large news outlets by providing striking editorial material to support coverage.

It’s also useful to think about where your audience will see the images. For example, are you primarily targeting publishing by traditional news outlets, digital or print, or on social media? What format, shape and display constraints does your intended display bring? What accompanying information can you include? Thinking about these at the planning stage is a good way to prioritise the most effective imagery as it can dramatically affect images needs. For example, images aimed at a display across the pages of a magazine have the luxury of space for detail and nuance, along with additional explanations in accompanying text. You might need to leave space for headlines or a spine, or keep essential visual information to one side of a frame. Editors might need both portrait and landscape framing. Contrast this with a sequence of images intended for use on Instagram where all necessary visual information has to fit within the platform’s aspect ratios (16x9 for stories, 4x5 for posts) and images have to compete for users' attention in very short periods of time. Portrait framing will take priority, and you will need more obvious images, leaving much less opportunity for slower, more nuanced photographs. These constraints can define the images that you need, and how you are able to communicate the necessary information visually.

More information on [using images on social media](https://www.climatevisuals.org/images_on_social_media/).

Even where you don’t know the intended or likely audience and platforms for image use, make sure to intentionally cater for as wide a spectrum as possible - look for images that tell your intended story across a glossy double page spread, as well as those that tell it on the scroll of a phone, you can then make appropriate selections later on.

## Using photography to visualise complex, technical issues

Successful photographs can distill complex, technical, expansive narratives into engaging and relatable content. Images can make otherwise hard to imagine or grasp details real, showing audiences what they look like in practice and how they play out in real life, combining evidence with emotion and experience, to ground theoretical issues in reality.

Starting with the priorities you have already identified, think about how you can illustrate the complexity of a legal case in individual, or a sequence of, images. It might be helpful to think of images as ‘hooks’ for specific legal details - for example, you might show the viewer key elements of an environment at risk, or provide visual evidence of what pollution statistics look like in reality, on the ground. Look for ways to tie key case details and statistics to evocative, striking, relatable images.

Photography can be particularly impactful in showing the passage of time, and this can be a very effective tool in illustrating urgency. We are all familiar with ‘then and now’ image sequences such as those showing receding glaciers, changing landscapes, or growing developments. These sequences of images provide audiences with a tangible, immediately understandable record of change, and can bring home a reality far more impactfully than comparable statistics. By photographing the specific case, or also similar, related situations, you could use this within a variety of framings - as evidence and ‘proof’ of change up to this point, as an illustration of what inaction might look like, or, as a demonstration of what a future could look like were a case to be successful. All of which can help to build a deeper, more relatable understanding of the details of a case in the viewers’ mind.

Think about how you could combine new photography with archival imagery. Could you commission a photographer to repeat an existing image to show how a scene has changed over time? Consider this ahead of time and research existing imagery that you could supply a commissioned photographer with before the assignment - this will inform their photographic process and help them to build a comparative perspective into their work.

Photographs can also help audiences to understand the wider context of a legal case, for example by connecting different locations and building a more complete picture. In the example of ClientEarth et al’s case against the INEOS Project ONE development, photographs are an effective tool in making the link between the development in Antwerp, and the extraction of fossil fuels in the Permian Basin in Texas, highlighting the global nature, and impacts, of the issues at the heart of the case.

It is important to always think about the imagery from the perspective of the audience - do the images convey all the necessary information for the viewer to understand? Is a caption required to explain key context, and if so, can you guarantee that a caption will always be displayed alongside the image? Detailed, accurate and insightful captions are critical for making the most of the opportunities for engagement that photography can bring and ensuring that an audience more completely understands a photograph. See below for more on what makes a good caption and how to distribute them with your images.

When using images to represent broader narratives it’s important to avoid depending on visual cliches. Photographs should add detail to a story rather than reduce it to surface level illustration. For example, images of smoke stacks might signal to the viewer that this is a story about air pollution, but they don’t add vital context and detail to the story. Instead, consider how images can build on this illustration and communicate information in their own right - with the smoke stacks example you could show the impacts of this pollution on local communities, highlight the proximity of pollution to a children's play area, or make clear the impacts of industrial pollution on local wildlife. You could show images of air pollution monitors that might be contributing to an evidence base of pollution, or maybe medical responses for asthma sufferers or those acutely affected by the pollution. All of those images add to the visual representation of the issue and contribute to the information available to the viewer.

Whilst images can be used to represent and distill wider issues, it’s essential to be mindful of those featured in the photographs and ensure that their stories are told appropriately, respectfully and with empathy. Even when the details of one story might be relevant more broadly, it is vital to consider the ethics of using one story to represent a different one. This can be a challenge with both new photographs as well as archival and stock imagery.

### Visualising the legal process

Combining images that represent the legal process with images of the real-world situation central to a case can be an effective way to build a more complete visual narrative. Consider how you can visualise this link.

Rules for photography in and around court buildings vary by country and even within countries by type of courthouse. Lack of access for photographers creates a logistical barrier for photography documenting the process of the case. It can also lead to unequal access for smaller NGOs or groups without the expertise to understand how to navigate these restrictions. There is a risk therefore of the perspective of these groups being lost.

There are however opportunities outside of the courtroom for visualising the litigation process. For example, you could show the legal team at work with claimants or key individuals, visiting the locations at the heart of the story and placing them in the scene. You could sequence this with photographs of the team at work in the office, suggesting some of the work behind the scenes. Posed imagery, for example outside court locations, can be combined with more photojournalistic imagery, making a clearer connection between the technical, legal, context and the real-world situation at the centre of the case.

Images of activists or protests outside courtrooms are often used when images are not possible from within the court itself. This can be a helpful way of highlighting a legal moment or documenting the number of supporters, but also can risk falling into common tropes of protest imagery which may or may not be effective. Images of the legal process can be a powerful way in for audiences to engage with climate litigation and the reality of how cases work. See for example images from the [Held v. This helps to demystify what the legal process looks like and adds transparency, humanity and authenticity to what is often a complex technical process.

## Visualise alternative futures

Photographs have the natural limitation of only being able to depict something that has already happened - in a documentary sense you need something to be there to photograph it. In the case of climate litigation, this often takes the form of documenting impacts and the processes causing them, as well as the people and locations involved. But how can you use imagery to help audiences understand the different futures that are dependent on the outcome of a case?

One way to do this is by photographing a similar situation where comparable actions have continued unchallenged by a court case. This might show continued environmental degradation or long-term effects on communities that are as yet unseen in the location at the centre of the litigation. These images could help the viewer understand the significance of what’s at stake - and how the technical details at the centre of the case can play out over time.

However, you can also use photography to help the viewer visualise alternative, more positive futures. For example, if you are working on a case against an extractive industry and its effects on natural habitats, you could photograph a comparable example where ceasing extraction led to regeneration of habitats and the natural environment. In this role, the photographs can help to build a visual case for the potential positive impacts of the litigation. This again highlights to the viewer the significance of what’s at stake, but it does so from a constructive perspective.

Effectively combining these two approaches, particularly alongside images of the main case in question, can help the viewer to build up a more complete picture of the litigation. These images combine the realities at the heart of the case with compelling stories about what the future might look like. In this way you can build a greater understanding of the urgency and significance of the litigation.

## Remember the Climate Visuals principles

The Climate Visuals [principles](http://climatevisuals.org/evidence) are a practical starting point of key considerations when working with photographs of climate change and are useful pointers to apply to photographing climate litigation stories. They are:

1. Show real people
2. Tell new stories
3. Show climate change causes at scale
4. Show emotionally powerful impacts
5. Understand your audience
6. Show local (but serious) impacts
7. Be careful with protest imagery

In the context of climate litigation, aim for photographs that help ground the story in the experience of **real people at the heart of the case**. Try to avoid familiar visuals cliches and instead tell **new, engaging and inspiring stories** that won’t be familiar to your audience.

We know that photographs of climate impacts can be **emotionally powerful** and striking. The same can be true for large-scale climate change causes. Think about **who your audience is** and what is most relevant to them - how can you help them to **visualise a complex legal case in a way that resonates with them**?

Photographs depicting protestors, and visually similar scenes of participants holding placards on the courtroom steps, are a familiar staple of the visual coverage of climate litigation. But [our evidence](http://climatevisuals.org/evidence) suggests that images of protest can be met with cynicism by audiences, with responses varying based on the affinity the viewer felt with the demonstrators’ cause. Similarly, audiences favour authentic images over staged photographs. Whilst visualising the link between the courtroom setting and wider story can be an important tool, think about how you can do this in a way that would appeal to a wider audience - maybe by having a courtroom steps image as part of a wider collection of imagery, showing directly-affected individuals alongside imagery from court and considering avoiding more generic placards.

## Balancing the needs of litigation and the needs of photography

The legal case must always have priority, but compelling photography of climate litigation should be viewed as more than just a nice to have, and instead seen as a tool for engaging audiences more deeply on why the issues at stake are being fought in court.

Imagery can be an effective tool in driving awareness and understanding, communicating details, context and a sense of urgency and evoking an emotional response in audiences. Yet in order to make the most of this you need to balance the needs of the legal case with producing, and distributing, the right imagery.

Thinking about photography early in the process of litigation will give you the opportunity to explore its full potential. Avoid only starting conversations about images nearing the point of publication and instead begin developing your visual storytelling plan from the outset.

- Identify key points in the legal process where you need to have images ready for release
- Establish the story you want to tell at each point and what will need to be in the images. This might include key locations, individuals and actions.
- Understand any restrictions on who, and what, can and cannot be photographed, and be mindful that this might change as litigation progresses

Always seek the involvement of those participants to ensure their views on how their stories are told are at the heart of any decision making.

You can communicate the value of compelling photography to different stakeholders from a variety of perspectives, including by highlighting;

- that photography can drive public understanding and engagement with the issues at stake, building a case in the court of public opinion.
- that photography can help with securing editorial coverage of the litigation proceedings at key moments.
- that the images can contribute to a visual record and representation of climate litigation, helping audiences to understand more of the context, detail and humanity of cases and why they matter.

---

**Always check that imagery, at the point of being taken and published, will not affect legal proceedings, and keep legal teams involved in any discussions around active photography and image distribution.**

## Photographs as evidence

There is a long history of the evidentiary value of photography, both in the courtroom and in the court of public opinion. In the context of the climate and environment, the power of imagery in the court of public opinion is well known and explored, however this is less true in the courtroom. Might there be opportunities for images to be used more actively in this context within climate litigation too?

[Forensic Architecture](https://forensic-architecture.org/about/agency) uses photographs, alongside other visual mediums, to reconstruct and display incidents of state violence and human rights violations. Their approach sees compelling visual presentations for both the legal process and public communications.

There are many potential limitations and restrictions for the use of imagery in court, and these vary greatly depending on the location of the court in question. Yet that being said, other legal contexts have demonstrated the power of imagery as admissible evidence, suggesting it is worth exploring in conversations around how you might be able to use photographs in your case.

As a communications tool, consider how photographs can function as corresponding evidence in the court of public opinion, even if not as admissible evidence in court. By highlighting details such as what has happened or is happening, changes over time, the existence of developments or key sites, the presence of organisations or actors in key locations or examples of environmental damage, you can use photography as a visual record of the claims made in court. In this way images can go beyond basic illustration and instead provide evidence for why a case matters - even if only in the court of public opinion and not within the legal process itself. When releasing your images in this role, make sure to clearly communicate how the images accompany, or evidence, the claims made in court.

## Ethics and consent

Photography can be a powerful tool for telling compelling stories, and with this comes a responsibility to ensure that these stories are told ethically. This is only ever more true when working on sensitive, human-focussed stories.

Prioritising an ethical approach is important at all stages of image production and use - from story identification, through image production, to the distribution and use of the images.

### Protecting those in the images

Appearing in a photograph illustrating a piece of climate litigation has the potential to bring with it a variety of substantial risks to an individual or community. Involvement in the litigation itself does not mean photography is automatically possible, appropriate, or wanted.

Ensure that you identify and explore any potential risks to participants and that you discuss these potential risks at the outset, as well as throughout the photography process. Be mindful that you may not be able to give guarantees for how, or where, images will be used, and make sure that this is understood in any discussions. Consent for photography must not be given in false security of how images will be used - those participating in the photography must have a clear understanding of the purpose of the images, where they might be published and who might see them. If you are not able to specifically name or guarantee potential outlets - for example if you plan to widely distribute the images for general press use - it is essential that the participants understand this.

Anonymity in images might be a useful tool for mitigating against some of the risks associated with appearing in a photograph - this might be through selective framing or silhouettes, or post-processing techniques such as pixellation and blurring. However, be mindful of associated details that might allow for identification, for example recognisable location details, elements of stories that can be cross compared, or a combination of them all.

Climate litigation cases do not happen in isolation, and the wider context of a country, location or story may influence how appropriate it is to be photographed, for instance where there may be political, security or economic risks to participants. Be ethical in your practice, and think carefully of the risks and protections for subjects as well as photographers.

### Informed consent

It is essential that you prioritise developing informed consent with those featured in your photographs.

Any participants in the photography must be fully aware of the purpose of the photography, how their story will be told and how the images may be used - both intentionally and unintentionally.

Participants must be fully aware of any potential risks or consequences of being in the photographs and the decision to partake must be made on this basis.

Informed consent should include specifics around how participants are identified in images and accompanying text, how long the images and stories can be distributed for, and in what context the images and stories can be used (for example only in reporting specific updates on the legal case).

You must discuss this in detail with any participants before embarking on any photography and they should have free opportunity to refuse to take part and to withdraw consent at any point.

This is particularly relevant for photographing children and young people. Whilst it can make for very compelling imagery to photograph children involved in court cases, you must make sure participants, and those responsible for them, are aware of any risks, and how those images may be used in the future.

Details of consent given, including any restrictions or requirements, should be securely accessible from where any imagery is stored. Being able to access these details quickly, easily and accurately whenever images may be used is critical for maximising the potential impact of your images whilst ensuring those featured are protected.

### Agency

The photography process should empower those featured in the images to tell their story as part of the litigation. How do they want their story to be told through photographs? What do they want the images to show, or not show? Use photography as a tool for participants to communicate their reality and their motivations for participating in the litigation. This should be a discussion and collaboration between the commissioning and photography team and those featured in the images. Working with those featured in the images helps to ensure that they are depicted fairly and with agency. Avoid depictions that show participants only as victims and instead look to visualise their active participation in the story of the legal proceedings.

### Desire to be involved

Not all plaintiffs may want to be highly visible in media coverage surrounding their case. There is a risk of being typecast into an inspirational figure or a poster child for climate litigation or climate action, and it is important to remember this may not always be individuals’ intention. [For some](https://www.theatlantic.com/science/archive/2024/09/climate-change-human-rights-health-law/679727/), climate litigation cases are acutely personally motivated rather than by or alongside issues of global justice, and they may wish to protect their identity or private life through this process. This must be respected.

### Using specific stories to represent wider issues

Whilst photography can be an effective tool in communicating complex, nuanced issues to the viewer, there is also the potential for over-simplification and the inappropriate use of individual stories to illustrate broader issues. This is important to consider through the life cycle (from commissioning through to use and archive use) of an image and is true with both new and archive photography. It is essential to be mindful of this, particularly when dealing with imagery depicting people.

Ask yourself:

- Can I confirm that the person featured has given their consent for images to be used in this way?
- Is the person or location featured directly relevant to the story I am telling?
- If not, are there fair comparisons that can be made in caption information or accompanying text, and can I be sure that this will be alongside any use of the image?
- Could aligning the person featured in the image with the story in this way cause them any potential difficulties or harm?
- Does the viewer have enough information to understand the relationship between the image and the wider story?

It is common that individuals might consent to being photographed as part of editorial reporting of a specific piece of litigation, but not to their images being used outside of this context, for example in fundraising material, or more general communications. The consent that they have given may also be time limited. To protect the individuals involved in any photographs you are producing, you should discuss the potential uses for images throughout the image’s life cycle and gain informed consent from featured individuals across this timeline. This consent should be recorded and be accessible to the appropriate potential users. Being photographed as part of the coverage of litigation shouldn’t automatically mean that images can be used more generally or for fundraising purposes. Where this is the case, and it is likely that you might need relevant images for fundraising purposes in the future, think about how you can take anonymous or more generic images for this purpose - for example detailed shots of hands, medication or asthma inhalers, key texts or materials. As always, make sure to discuss these with those featured to ensure that they consent to this kind of imagery too and don’t assume that they are ok for this use.

## Ensuring you have the correct permissions for photography

Making sure that you have the correct permissions for taking photographs is an important consideration from story development through to production and publication. This is always the case, but ever more so in sensitive legal contexts. Taking images on private property, showing certain facilities (such as military or critical infrastructure) or flying drones are just a few examples of where restrictions could apply.

Before beginning the photography you should ensure that you have the necessary permissions to be in the location, to take imagery there and to show anything that will be in the images. **It is important to note that there is significant local, regional and national variation in any potential restrictions.**

Using drones for photography adds another level of complexity to the process. You must ensure that any drone flying is compliant with local regulations and assess whether you need further permissions to fly in any potential locations. Drone pilots will also need to have the necessary licences and insurance to comply with local requirements.

Check any photography you are planning at all stages to ensure you are complying with the relevant requirements - from assignment development through to pre-publication. Working with local photographers and fixers with trusted and detailed local knowledge can be very useful in navigating these issues.

Before releasing any images they should be checked for any potential legal issues as well as to ensure that they do not risk compromising the legal proceedings in any way. This includes caption information.

## Generative AI images

Over the last two years, generative AI tools for creating images from text prompts have developed exponentially. Increasingly, easily accessible tools such as DALL-E and Midjourney are able to generate photo realistic imagery, and images generated by these tools have proliferated.

It is tempting to see these new tools, and the images that they can create, as a great opportunity to visualise climate litigation in place of traditional, photographic, images. However, their use comes with significant potential [visual, ethical and moral pitfalls and implications](https://www.climatevisuals.org/on-ai-images-and-climate-change-photography/).

Photography’s role in telling the story of climate change litigation is strengthened by highlighting the reality central to cases. The use of generative AI images often misses this opportunity, restricting imagery to surface-level illustration of an imagined reality, frequently based on damaging or ineffective visual stereotypes, containing visual errors and contributing to a lessening of photography’s evidentiary value in society.

Alongside this, there are the moral and legal implications of how AI models are trained, the environmental issues as a result of the data processing, and the potential for [significant outcry at the use of generative AI images](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/may/02/amnesty-international-ai-generated-images-criticism) for reporting purposes.

Generative AI imagery brings many risks and issues when used as a shortcut, or budget-friendly option, for illustrating climate litigation stories. However, when combined with a detailed, empathetic and long form approach, it does open up the possibilities for new forms of visual storytelling, particularly when used more in the tradition of artists’ illustrations than photography. Law firm Maurice Blackburn worked with a creative agency to generate [visual representations of witness statements](https://www.exhibitai.com.au/home) from refugees detained on Naura and Manus Island off the coast of Australia. There is very little photographic imagery available of the islands, and, when the class action was discontinued following a change in Australian law, many witnesses still wanted their stories to be told. The images were created in collaboration with the witnesses, and AI was used in place of [commissioning artists to give them more control of the final images](https://edition.cnn.com/2023/06/25/australia/australia-ai-images-refugee-stories-intl-hnk-dst). The result is a compelling visual representation of the witnesses’ stories, crucially with their experiences at the heart of the images. When used, the images are accompanied by clear labelling informing the viewer of how the images were generated. This highlights the potential for complex, careful, use of generative AI images to accompany legal proceedings, however this is in stark contrast to the more common use of these images as a convenient stand-in for photography.

As the technology rapidly advances, and generated images become increasingly photo-realistic, the need to tell compelling, real stories through photography becomes ever more essential. Accurate, engaging images, with detailed accompanying caption information can bring home the reality of the real lives and events - generated images risk missing this opportunity to engage audiences, whilst also undermining the integrity of climate imagery more generally.

## Commissioning, contracting and copyright

Commissioning photographers to produce new imagery doesn’t need to be a complicated process, but there are a few key details that are important to get right.

### The photography brief

A detailed, clear and concise photography brief is an essential tool for successfully commissioning photographers. Think of it as the manual for the photography assignment - aim to have everything the photographer needs to know in one place, helping them to easily understand the story they’re working on and the images that you are looking for.

What should be in a photography brief?

- - **An overview of the story and case**, providing crucial information and context to inform the images. Remember, you want the photographer to have access to as much relevant information as possible, and the brief should make this easy to find and understand.
		- **Key themes for photography**. These are broader than specific images, instead highlighting important elements to be included in images.
- **Required images**. The photographs that are an essential deliverable from the assignment.
- **Ethical considerations and required approaches**. Be clear about the ethical priorities of the work and how you want to navigate them (more information on this below).
- **Stylistic requirements.** How do you want images framed? A mix of portraits and more fly-on-the-wall images? Colour or black and white? All landscape framing, a mix of portrait and landscape, or all portrait for social media?
- **Any legal requirements.**
- **Key contacts** on and off location.
- **Key locations** for photography.
- **A timeline**. Including pre-production through to image delivery.
- **Technical and submission requirements.** Including image files, caption information and delivery methods.

It’s good practice to discuss the photography brief with the photographer at the time of contracting, and to reference any image requirements contained in the brief in the contract.

### Contracting

When you are commissioning photographers to produce images it is essential to clearly and fairly contract them for their work at the outset. In order to help make the most of the commissioned images as a communications resource into the future, you need to set out clearly who has what rights to the images once delivered, where these rights apply, and how long for. The contract should include a detailed outline of the intended uses of the images, as well as clear timeframes and expectations around image licensing and ethical practices.

If you are planning on distributing images provided by non-professional photographers, for example claimants involved in the case, the same applies. Start by having detailed conversations with the image owner about how and where the images might be used, and draw up an agreement to confirm their permission, as well as the permissions of those featured if necessary.

### Copyright and licences

Clear agreements on image copyright and licensing are vital for ensuring that you can get the most use of any images whilst protecting those featured and the photographer. Expectations around copyright should be discussed with any photographers or image providers at the outset, and included in any contract discussions.

There are a number of possible ways to navigate copyright issues to ensure that the terms work best for all parties.

- **Full ownership of any images delivered by the photographer.** Often known as a ‘rights buyout’ or ‘work for hire’. Under these terms, the commissioning client owns all copyright to the images produced, not the photographer. Whilst there are some benefits to the simplicity of this agreement to the commissioner, it is in many cases not favourable to the photographer. You should expect to pay more for assignments like this, and for the terms to not be acceptable for some.
- **Exclusive license to the images.** This is where the photographer retains their copyright of the images, but grants the commissioning client exclusive rights to use them. This agreement could be in-perpetuity or time limited, and there might be mechanisms for agreeing future licences with other parties.
- **Non-exclusive licence to the images.** Similar to above in that the photographer retains copyright and grants the commissioning client the rights to use the images, but in this case the photographer is free to licence the images to other potential users as they wish.

Being clear on what you intend to use the images for is essential, for example - will they only be used by your organisation? Do you intend to distribute the images for editorial publication? How long do you want the images to be available for use? Ensuring you have the rights to distribute the images for free use in illustrating specific reporting can be a useful tool in securing coverage.

Be mindful that Western copyright frameworks may not be suitable for all imagery, particularly when working with sourced images and evidence material or photographs of indigenous communities, sites, artwork or objects, and that you may need to take specific advice. For further reading on navigating this, see [True Tracks: Indigenous cultural and intellectual property principles for putting self-determination into practice](https://www.terrijanke.com.au/terri-janke-phd-true-tracks).

## Working with archive imagery

Using images to effectively tell the story of climate litigation doesn’t necessarily require commissioning new photography. You might find that you can draw on existing archive sources to do this.

Archival imagery can be particularly useful in reminding the viewer of key historical events - for example industrial incidents or climate impacts. This can help the viewers to further understand the significance of the litigation, bringing home the impacts in question. You can develop this understanding by pairing the archive imagery with new photography, returning to a scene depicted in the photographs to help the viewer understand what has, or hasn’t, changed.

Archival imagery can also effectively illustrate climate litigation by geographically placing the story, highlighting key locations at the centre of the case. Look for images that do more than just reference the location in question - try to avoid generic images of a broad area and instead show details that add to the story and the viewer’s understanding of it. For example, for a case looking at river pollution, instead of only showing an aesthetically pleasing image of the river in question, can you show its course through areas of pollution? These might not be new photographs taken specifically for this purpose, but they could still add relevant, useful information.

When working with archive images it is essential to consider the connections you are making between the image and the litigation you are linking it to. Think carefully about any people featured in the images, are they involved or relevant to the proceedings, and is it appropriate to visually link them to the story? Does visually linking someone featured to the story bring any potential risks or consequences to them? Remember, you can’t guarantee that the viewer will be aware that the image is an archive image, or that they will understand critical nuance - what would the inferred meaning of the image be if it was displayed alongside a provocative headline?

Clear, transparent caption information is an important tool in helping the viewer understand the relevance of an image whilst making it clear when it was taken.

Try to avoid using generic stock or archive images of corporate buildings or logos. Whilst they might be relevant to a case, they don’t add to a viewer’s understanding of the story, and in reality might put potential audiences off engaging with it more deeply.

## Collaboration

Climate litigation is often a global effort. Consider how the images you take could be used to empower small NGOs, youth groups, groups in the Global South, or those with fewer resources. Many groups working on climate litigation are entirely volunteer-led, and often lack resources, time and expertise to produce their own professional quality photography. Getting news coverage of these cases, successful or not, is important for raising awareness and inspiring action around the world, and the presence of compelling imagery is a key part of this.

Well-funded and coordinated NGOs or other groups can produce high quality press packs or sets of photographs, and the effective sharing of these resources or imagery can contribute to a global effort of increasing awareness of climate action and empowerment of those working in this space.

You might also look for opportunities to collaborate with journalists and news organisations. By pitching potential stories early on in the process and enabling access you can seek to develop in depth coverage in partnership with editorial publications. Similarly, working with news agencies can be an effective way to reach wide ranging, global media outlets with material they produce distributed to their clients. A secondary benefit of this is that their photographs may be held in image archives, contributing to a longer term picture of climate litigation that potential image users can access.

Identify why the story might be of interest to your target organisations and develop a pitch that includes key details, wider context about why the story matters, and outline time frames. Make sure to consider the narrative from their perspective, and that of their audience. In many cases, publications and agencies will want their own photographers, and reporters, to cover the story - think carefully about how you can facilitate the kind of comprehensive access they will need to tell the story in detail.

## Image distribution: getting the right images into the right hands

### What will journalists and picture editors be looking for?

For your images to have the most impact you need to get them to the right people at the right time. Considering who your target audience is, and the associated publications might be, early on is helpful. Similarly, as a story develops, keeping in mind where the images are hopefully going to be seen—and how this might affect the content and style of the images—is too.

Editorial publications will be looking for engaging, striking imagery that catches the attention of their audience, adding context and detail to a story. Impactful images can do more than purely illustrate or fill space at the top of the page. Whilst a photograph on the court steps can literally show an audience that this is a story about a court case, images that go beyond this and add to reporting bring much more value to a publication, telling the viewer why a story is important and enhancing details contained in the text.

Providing publications with new imagery, that goes beyond familiar visual cliches, should be viewed as a useful tool in seeking greater coverage of a story - good photographs can help to increase prominence and space given to reporting by bringing opportunities for deeper audience engagement.

Journalists, editors and publications will be looking for images that:

- Show key individuals, locations and details and their relationship to each other.
- Humanise a story and help the viewer to understand why it matters.
- Are impactful and visually compelling.

Some publications will be looking for a single image that effectively communicates a wider story; others will have space for more images that can be used to tell more of a visual narrative. When producing photography, try to cater for both - a wide edit of strong images can make the difference between brief coverage and a story being given more space. Identifying key hero images and keeping these high in the edit helps with this - as does intentional sequencing that guides the viewer through a story.

Think closely about your intended publications and their audience and look for potential hooks that increase the relevance of your images, and story, to both. You can use these for more bespoke targeting of images and stories to potential outlets. For example, do any of the participants have geographical links that you could highlight to regional publications?

Clear accompanying information, including image captions, wider story context and usage rights and requirements is essential. Without this, images won’t be used, however strong they are visually. Prioritise detailed accompanying information that helps the recipient quickly understand what the images show, why they matter, and how they can be used.

### Distributing images

As you near your ideal point of publication, start building a list of potential contacts for distribution. Prioritise identifying why the images and story will be of interest, and consider how this will affect your pitch. As with written and other editorial content you could consider different or exclusive offers for specific outlets, and identify key ‘hooks’ for the images, these may be within the litigation or relevant external events.

Central to any pitch needs to be reasoning behind why the images, and the stories they tell, matter and why you think it will be of interest to the recipients’ audience.

Aim to:

- **Send a concise edit of photographs**, specifically targeted at covering the story in the most impactful way. Don’t send thousands of images, an edit of the 25 best is better.
- **Cover key elements of the story visually**. Referring to specific details in press releases? Make sure to have relevant imagery for any newslines and flag that these images are available in any releases.
- **Sequence the images in a way that makes sense to the viewer**. Don’t hide essential elements. Guide the viewer through the story you’re trying to tell.
- **Ensure all relevant information is accessible from the same place**. Where possible include caption and credit information in embedded metadata.

Within your edit of images, look for a lead image that sums the story up concisely and engagingly. Start with this in any selections and attach it to any emails to catch attention initially. It can also be useful to have a really tight edit (for example of 5 images) that highlight the themes covered by the rest of the images but allow for a quick assessment of what’s available.

When sending out images, make sure access to the images is as easy, clear and convenient for the recipient as possible.

File sharing platforms, such as Dropbox and Google Drive, are an effective way to create an easily accessible, sharable resource of images in a repeatable location. By using these, rather than download links such as WeTransfer, it is possible to edit and update the resource as required. Where possible, avoid sending large images via email attachments - often larger files are removed from emails on delivery, or cause emails to bounce, and are an unreliable way of sending imagery.

If you have existing relationships with editorial publications you may also be able to submit imagery for use via FTP (File Transfer Protocol). This would often mean that imagery is loaded directly into a Content Management System (CMS). If you are transmitting images in this way, it is essential that all relevant information is contained with each image’s metadata - this includes captions as well as contextual, contact information and usage rights. A good approach would be to also send the images and information via email alongside those sent via FTP.

### What should accompany images?

**Captions:**

Most people have heard the phrase “a picture is worth a thousand words”, but a picture with a good caption can be worth many more. All images that you distribute should be accompanied by detailed, contextual information in the image caption. If possible this should be contained within the image metadata, but it is also helpful to include a caption sheet when you send images out.

Start with Who?/What?/When?/Where?/Why? and include as much detail as you can. Use the caption to guide the audience as to why the image matters as part of the story, and add further contextual information and nuance. Don’t assume your audience understands critical details and context - help them out with this information but keep it as snappy and concise as possible.

If you require key details to be published in captions by a publication make sure to clearly mark what is mandatory as a condition for using any images.

A caption can be a great opportunity to take a viewer’s engagement with an image and draw them further into the story - don’t waste it!

**Credits and licence information:**

Make sure that you provide comprehensive credit information and clear licence terms. As with captions it is good practice to include this in image metadata as well as on a separate caption sheet. You want any potential users of the images to have the confidence that they have the permissions and licence to use the image - being clear about this when you distribute them helps with this.

Is there an embargo? Do you require specific details to be highlighted, or mandatory photography credits? Is there a time limit on when images are available for use? Make all these details as clear as possible, and include them in each images’ metadata as this is where many potential users will interact with it beyond an initial email conversation.

If they are, it’s always worth explicitly mentioning that the images are free to use.

This guide is designed to provide practical considerations for making the most of the opportunities for compelling climate litigation photography.

Strong photographs can engage audiences deeply, drive public understanding and concern, and demonstrate the urgent value of climate litigation. Each case will bring with it different opportunities along with accompanying constraints, but including photography in communications planning and discussions from the beginning should be seen as essential.
