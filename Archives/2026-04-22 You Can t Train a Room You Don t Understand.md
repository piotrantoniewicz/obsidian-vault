In the last [piece](https://kylebehrend.substack.com/p/what-three-years-of-conversations), I laid out the map. The zoomed-out view of how all the pieces of AI adoption fit together for nonprofits. Policy first, then understanding where people are, then training, then strategy, then the flywheel starts turning.

This is the step most organisations skip.

They write the policy (or they don’t, but let’s assume they did). Then they jump straight to training. They book a workshop, sign up for a webinar series, maybe bring someone in for a lunch-and-learn. And the training is fine. The facilitator is engaging. People nod along. But three weeks later, nothing has changed, and leadership wonders why adoption isn’t sticking.

Here’s what happened: they trained at the wrong level. They pitched to the middle of a room they’d never measured. Half the staff were already ahead of the material. The other half weren’t ready for it. And the people who might have been champions, the ones with a natural magnetic pull towards this technology, never got identified, so they went back to experimenting quietly on their own.

All of that is avoidable. But only if you start by finding out where people actually are.

---

### Why this matters more than it sounds

I know “do an assessment” isn’t the most exciting advice. It sounds like bureaucracy. Another form, another process, another thing on the to-do list for an already overstretched team.

But think about what you’re working with when you skip it. You don’t know who’s already using AI, and for what. You don’t know who’s curious but hesitant. You don’t know who’s been experimenting with tools the organisation hasn’t sanctioned. You don’t know who’s terrified of it. And you don’t know who’s quietly brilliant at it.

If you don’t know where people are and what they’re using, it increases your risk and can diminish what support you can provide. That’s not abstract. It means your training misses. Your policy has gaps you can’t see. Your champions go unnoticed. And the people who need the most support don’t get it because you didn’t know they were there.

The assessment isn’t paperwork. It’s the precondition for every other step actually working.

---

### What good assessment looks like

The most common starting point is a survey, and that’s a decent one. But not all surveys are equal. What you’re trying to do is measure people against some kind of maturity model, a framework that defines what capability looks like at different stages.

What does a level one user look like? Maybe they’ve tried ChatGPT a few times for personal use but haven’t applied it to their work. Level two might be someone using AI regularly for drafting or brainstorming but sticking to basic prompts. Level three could be someone who understands structured prompting, knows how to work with markdown outputs, can use conversational techniques to refine results, and has explored the deeper functionality of whatever tools the organisation provides.

You need a combination of quantitative and qualitative questions. The quantitative side gives you the data: specific skills, courses completed, tools used, terminology understood. The qualitative side gives you the texture. Questions like:

- How do you currently apply AI to your role?
- What opportunities do you think AI will present for your work in the future?
- Is there a tool or capability that’s piqued your curiosity, something that opens up ways of doing things that weren’t possible before?
- How would you identify which parts of your work could potentially be automated?

That blend, the measurable and the open-ended, gives you something you can actually work with. You get both a snapshot of current capability and a sense of where people’s heads are at.

I’ve got a downloadable [maturity model](https://aiimpacthub.com/free-resources/) on my website that you can use as a starting framework, or you can build your own based on the specific tools your organisation uses and what advancement looks like in your context.

### Spotting your champions

One of the most valuable things assessment reveals is who your AI champions are, or could be.

A champion isn’t necessarily in a specific role. They’re not always in IT or operations. They’re the person who takes the initiative without being prompted. The one who’s been learning about AI on their own time, who gets genuinely excited about what’s possible, who’s already helping colleagues figure things out. They have a natural drive towards this technology and towards helping others use it.

You can’t always manufacture that. Sometimes nobody pulls ahead, and that’s a different situation entirely. In those cases, you’ve got three options: wait and see if someone emerges, hire specifically for an AI-focused role, or have someone in leadership step forward and take the reins.

Of those three, there’s no universal right answer. It depends on your size, your budget, your capabilities. But I’ll say this: it’s always helpful to have someone in leadership actually learning and getting excited about this technology, because that has a genuine flow-on effect through the organisation. Show, don’t tell. When staff see a leader who’s visibly curious and experimenting, not just issuing directives about innovation, it shifts the culture. People feel permission to explore. That’s not structural, it’s cultural. And culture is where adoption actually lives.

### What you do with the data matters more than collecting it

Data is only as good as what you do with it. That might sound obvious, but I’ve seen enough surveys disappear into shared drives to know it needs saying.

Once you’ve got the results, you should be using them to make informed decisions. Who needs foundational training? Who’s ready for advanced capability building? Who should be given room to lead? Where are the gaps between what people know and what your policy expects of them?

Store the data somewhere accessible, because if you run a follow-up assessment in six months (and you should) you want to be able to track movement. Progress becomes visible. Gaps that persist become priorities.

And here’s a practical note: you can use AI to analyse the results. Anonymise the names, feed the data in along with your policy and any strategic goals, and ask for a proposed set of next steps. It won’t give you a perfect plan, but it’ll give you a starting point that’s grounded in your actual data rather than generic advice.

### Reimagining the form

This is where I want to push a little further, because I think we’re leaving opportunities on the table.

A survey doesn’t have to be a form. It could be an interactive chatbot, a text-based experience that adapts as someone answers, adjusting the complexity of its questions in real time and landing the user at a capability level by the end. It could be a dynamically generated assessment app where the questions change based on previous answers. It could be a mini hackathon where you hand teams your AI policy and challenge them to transform it into different media: a song, an infographic, a video, an interactive web app. That exercise reveals capability levels without anyone having to self-report, because you see where people are by watching what they build.

I’ll be honest. This is the advanced end. If your organisation is just getting started, a well-designed survey is the right move. But if you’ve identified a champion or you’re ready to invest, these experiential approaches are the direction worth heading in. We have mediums available to us now that were previously gatekept behind the expense of developers. Why should an assessment look the same as it did five years ago?

I don’t think this space is thoroughly enough explored yet. **But that’s exactly why it’s worth experimenting with, even if the experiment itself is the point.**

---

### The smallest viable step

Here’s what I’d actually like you to do after reading this. Not just nod along and file it away for later.

Open up whatever AI tool you have access to. Describe your organisation: your size, your sector, your current level of AI adoption, what tools you’re using. Tell it what you want to measure and why. Ask it to help you frame an assessment that fits your context.

This is a genuinely useful exercise for two reasons. First, you’ll end up with a draft assessment you can actually use. Second, the process of building it is itself a practical exercise in using AI, which means you’re developing your own capability at the same time as designing a tool to measure everyone else’s.

It’s one thing to read about this. It’s another to actually make it happen.
